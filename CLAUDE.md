## 1. PROJECT OVERVIEW

**Working Title:** The House of Paper
**Series:** Four books, each a standalone story within a larger arc
**Genre:** Commercial historical fiction — propulsive, literary, Shogun-meets-Medici pacing
**Comp Titles:** *Shōgun* (Clavell), *The House of Niccolò* (Dunnett), *Pillars of the Earth* (Follett), *Pachinko* (Lee)
**Setting:** The Abbasid Caliphate, 770–814 CE

**Logline:** A tilemaker's son grows up in the shadow of Baghdad's Khorasan Gate, apprentices in the empire's finest paper mill, journeys to the edges of the known world, and builds a revolutionary financial house — only to discover that the cost of power is everything he once loved.

**Series Arc:** Yusuf ibn Ali al-Khorasani rises from craft to commerce to finance to statecraft, building the most powerful banking house in the Islamic world. Each book asks a different question about the price of ambition.

| Book | Title | Years | Ages | Central Question |
|------|-------|-------|------|------------------|
| 1 | The House of Paper | 770–794 | 0–24 | Will Yusuf choose the safe inheritance or bet everything on a world his father cannot imagine? |
| 2 | The House of Silver | 791–803 | 21–33 | Can a man build an empire on trust in a world where trust is the first casualty of power? |
| 3 | The House of Gold | 803–809 | 33–39 | What does it cost to become indispensable to the most dangerous man alive? |
| 4 | The House of Wisdom | 809–814 | 39–44 | Can the next generation prove worthy — and can the old man let go? |

**Ending:** Happy — Khalid succeeds Yusuf, the House endures, but at significant personal cost. Not a fairy tale. A happy ending earned through sacrifice.

**Intended Audience:** Adult readers of literary historical fiction who want immersive settings, morally complex protagonists, and propulsive plotting.

- Book 1 is in active chapter writing
- Five chapters drafted (see §5 for status)
- Extensive research, world-building, and character documentation complete

## 2. NARRATIVE VOICE AND STYLE RULES

This section is derived from the existing drafted chapters (Chapters 1–5), which establish the voice. All new writing must match this voice exactly.

### Point of View

- **Third-person limited**, locked to Yusuf in Books 1–3. Book 4 expands to include Khalid, Zahra, and Omar's perspectives in designated chapters.
- **One POV per chapter.** No head-hopping. If information exists outside Yusuf's perception, he cannot know it unless he observes it, is told, or infers it.
- Yusuf's interiority is the engine of the prose. Every scene should be filtered through his perception, his associations, his emotional state. He notices what matters to him — patterns, people's hands, the mathematics of commerce, gaps between what people say and what they mean.

### Tense

- **Past tense** throughout. No exceptions.

### Prose Style

The voice is literary but muscular. Sentences are concrete, sensory, and precise. The prose earns its emotional weight through accumulated physical detail, not through abstraction or sentimentality.

**Key characteristics observed in the existing chapters:**

- **Concrete and physical.** Abstractions are grounded in objects and sensations. Not "he felt sad" but a specific image: a fragment of tile, a handful of sand, the smell of lye.
- **Similes are earned and original.** "A smile that lasted approximately as long as a match flame and gave off about as much warmth." "The flat, immovable patience of a creature that had existed long before human ambition." Similes must arise from the world of the story — craft, commerce, the desert, animals, materials — never from modern life.
- **Sentences vary in rhythm.** Long, accretive sentences for description and reflection. Short, blunt sentences for emotional impact and turns. A paragraph might build through three complex sentences and then land on a single short one.
- **Paragraphs are frequently short** — sometimes a single sentence — for pacing and emphasis. This is a distinctive feature of the voice.
- **Em dashes** are used freely for parenthetical interjections, appositional detail, and mid-thought pivots. This is part of the rhythm.
- **Section breaks (marked with `* * *`)** divide scenes within chapters. Each scene is a discrete unit with its own micro-arc.
- **The narrator is close to Yusuf but not identical.** The narrator occasionally pulls back to a wider frame — "He was seventeen years old, and until this morning, he had never slept outside the walls of Baghdad" — providing the reader with perspective Yusuf himself would not articulate.

### Dialogue Style

- **No modern idioms.** Dialogue should feel period-plausible without being stiff or archaic. Characters speak naturally, but the diction is rooted in the world — references are to God, to trade, to the desert, to craft.
- **Dialogue tags are minimal.** Prefer "said" and "asked." Use action beats instead of tags where possible. Never use adverbs in dialogue tags ("he said angrily"). The dialogue itself and the surrounding action must carry the tone.
- **Characters speak distinctly** (see §4 for individual voices). A merchant does not sound like a craftsman. A Bedouin does not sound like a bureaucrat.
- **Arabic and Persian terms** are used sparingly and always in context that makes their meaning clear without footnotes: *khan*, *suq*, *ustad*, *sarraf*, *mudarib*, *rabb al-mal*, *suftaja*, *adhan*, *fals*, *dirham*, *dinar*. Italicise on first use in a chapter; thereafter no italics.
- **No dialogue in quotation marks for remembered speech or reported speech.** Direct dialogue uses standard quotation marks. Interior thought is rendered without quotation marks, woven into the narration.

### Things to Always Do

- **Open every scene in a specific physical setting.** The reader must be grounded in place, time of day, and sensory environment within the first few sentences.
- **Use sensory detail in every scene.** Smell is particularly important — this is a world of spices, dung, lye, paper pulp, cooking fires, perfume, dust, and river water. Sound is the second priority. The sensory palette anchors the historical world.
- **Show character through specific action.** How a person handles a sheet of paper, how they pour tea, how they stand — these reveal more than any description of personality.
- **Let commercial and craft detail do narrative work.** The mechanics of paper-making, the structure of a *mudaraba* partnership, the logistics of a caravan — these are not background; they are the substance of the story. Treat them with the same narrative attention as sword fights in other novels.
- **Ground thematic ideas in concrete scenes.** The theme of "paper as power" must be felt through specific moments — a watermark held to lamplight, a forged contract exposed, a suftaja honoured across a thousand miles — not stated as abstractions.
- **Give every chapter a "stealth thesis" — a quiet promise** in its opening paragraphs about what the chapter will explore. The reader should sense, even unconsciously, what this chapter is about.
- **End chapters on a beat of change** — a realisation, a departure, a decision, an image that carries forward.

### Things to Never Do

- **Never use modern slang, idioms, or metaphors.** No "ticking time bomb," "level playing field," "pushing the envelope," "red flag." If a metaphor could appear in a 21st-century email, it does not belong here.
- **Never use anachronistic technology or concepts.** No printing press, no compass, no gunpowder, no coffee (tea and date wine are fine), no tomatoes, no potatoes. Verify any material culture detail against the 8th-century CE setting.
- **Never summarise what can be dramatised.** If a scene is worth mentioning, it is worth writing. "Over the next few weeks, Yusuf learned the trade" is forbidden. Show the learning through specific scenes.
- **Never break the fourth wall.** The narrator does not address the reader, does not comment on the story as a story, does not wink.
- **Never use "suddenly" or "he realised that."** Show the moment of realisation through action, perception, or a shift in the prose rhythm.
- **Never have characters deliver exposition through dialogue.** No "As you know, the Barmakids control the treasury..." Characters speak to communicate with each other, not to inform the reader.
- **Never make Yusuf too competent too easily.** For every two successes, there must be a genuine failure with lasting consequences. Failure is where character is revealed. (See Critical Analysis in project files.)
- **Never reduce supporting characters to functions.** Every named character has their own desires, fears, and agency. If a character exists only to serve Yusuf's plot, they need interiority or they need to be cut.
- **Never use the word "suddenly."**
- **Never write "he couldn't help but" or "he found himself."** These are evasions of agency. Yusuf acts; he does not find himself acting.

### Pacing Rules

- **Action and tension scenes:** Short sentences. Short paragraphs. Rapid scene cuts. Sensory detail becomes selective — only what the character would notice under stress. Dialogue is clipped.
- **Reflective and descriptive scenes:** Longer sentences. Accretive detail. The prose breathes. Interior thought mingles with observation. Allow pauses.
- **Commercial/craft scenes (negotiations, paper-making, accounting):** Medium pace. The detail itself creates tension — the reader must feel the stakes embedded in a handshake, a number in a ledger, a sheet of paper held to light. Do not rush these.
- **Transitions between locations or time periods:** A single scene break (`* * *`) and a strong opening image are sufficient. Do not use "Three weeks later..." preambles. Drop the reader into the new scene and let context orient them.

### Chapter Structure

- **Average chapter length:** 5,000–7,000 words based on existing drafts.
- **Chapters have titles,** rendered in italics below the chapter number: `CHAPTER THREE` / `*The Wounded Heart*`
- **Each chapter contains 3–6 scenes** separated by `* * *` section breaks.
- **Each chapter has an emotional arc** — it begins in one state and ends in a different one. The reader should feel changed by the end of each chapter.
- **Time compression within chapters is acceptable** — a chapter can span days or weeks — but each scene is grounded in a specific moment.

## 3. HISTORICAL SETTING AND WORLD-BUILDING

### Time Period and Locations

**Primary:** Baghdad (Madinat al-Salam), 770–814 CE. The Abbasid Caliphate at its zenith under Caliph Harun al-Rashid (r. 786–809) and the civil war between al-Amin and al-Ma'mun (809–813).

**Secondary locations across the series:**
- Kufa — the old capital, a city of scholars and grievance (Book 1)
- Wasit — garrison town on the Tigris (Book 1)
- Rayy (modern Tehran) — Silk Road crossroads (Books 1, 3, 4)
- Merv — Yusuf's ancestral homeland in Khorasan (Book 1)
- Constantinople — the Byzantine rival (Book 2)
- Damascus — the desert crossing (Book 1)
- Mosul — northern trade route (Book 1)
- Basra — maritime hub (Books 2, 3)
- Fustat (old Cairo) — Nile grain trade (Books 2, 3)

### Historical Accuracy Rules

- **Real historical events are fixed points.** The Barmakid purge (January 803), Harun's death (March 809), the siege of Baghdad (811–813), al-Amin's execution (September 813) — dates, outcomes, and key figures cannot be altered.
- **The spaces between events are fiction.** Yusuf is a fictional character navigating real history. His actions must be plausible within the historical record but do not need to be documented.
- **Material culture must be accurate.** What people eat, wear, use, build with, travel in, trade — all must be verified against the period. When uncertain, use a `[RESEARCH NEEDED]` tag rather than guessing.
- **Creative licence is acceptable for:** private conversations between historical figures, the inner lives of real people, the specific mechanics of trade and finance (which are poorly documented for this period), and the day-to-day texture of ordinary life.
- **Creative licence is NOT acceptable for:** the outcomes of battles, the fates of historical figures, the existence of technologies or crops, the basic structure of Islamic law and governance, or the geography of the caliphate.

### Material Culture — What Exists and What Does Not

**Exists in this period:**
- Paper (recently introduced from Samarkand; Baghdad's first paper mill c. 794–795)
- Suftajas (letters of credit / bills of exchange)
- Mudaraba partnerships (profit-sharing investment contracts)
- Date wine, *nabidh* (fermented drinks — legally ambiguous)
- The oud, the ney, drums
- Camels, donkeys, horses, mules
- Irrigation canals, *shadufs*, *norias*
- Astrolabes (early astronomical instruments)
- The postal system (*barid*)
- Soap, perfumes, dyes (indigo, saffron, henna)
- Cotton, linen, silk, wool
- Flatbread baked in tandoor ovens
- Oil lamps, candles, braziers

**Does NOT exist yet:**
- Printing press (not until 15th century Europe; woodblock printing exists in China but not in the Islamic world)
- Coffee (not documented until 15th century)
- Gunpowder weapons
- Magnetic compass (not until 11th-12th century)
- Distilled spirits (early alchemy exists but not drinking spirits)
- Potatoes, tomatoes, maize, chocolate, tobacco (all New World)
- Eyeglasses
- Mechanical clocks
- Standing armies in the modern sense (troops are paid seasonally or per campaign)

### Social Structures

- **The Khorasaniyya** — Persian/Khorasani elite who powered the Abbasid revolution and form the military-administrative backbone of the state. Yusuf's family belongs to this class.
- **Arab aristocracy** — tribal families who resent Khorasani dominance. The tension between Arab and Persian-Khorasani factions is a central political dynamic.
- **The *mawali* system** — non-Arab Muslims who attached themselves to Arab tribes as clients. Social mobility exists but is structured by patronage.
- **Religious diversity** — Baghdad is home to Muslims (Sunni and Shi'a), Nestorian Christians, Jews, Zoroastrians, and others. Nestorian Christians are prominent in medicine, translation, and finance. Jews serve as money-changers (*sarrafs*) and bankers (*jahbadhs*). Religious identity shapes social networks but does not preclude commercial partnership.
- **Slavery exists** — domestic slavery is common. Yusuf's mother Layla is a free Bedouin woman, but enslaved people are part of the background world. Treat this historically, without anachronistic moralising or avoidance.
- **Women's lives** — women in elite households have significant influence but operate through different channels than men. Zahra manages the household economy, maintains social networks, and builds her own intelligence system. Women are present in markets, in domestic production, and in intellectual life (though access is structured by class and family).
- **The *ayyarun*** — Baghdad's urban underclass, gangs of young men who become a significant force during the civil war.

### Language and Terminology

**Prefer these period-appropriate terms:**
- *Khan* (caravansary/inn), not "hotel" or "inn"
- *Suq* (market), not "bazaar" (which is Persian and acceptable) or "marketplace"
- *Ustad* (master craftsman/teacher)
- *Sarraf* (money-changer)
- *Jahbadh* (banker/financial agent of the state)
- *Suftaja* (letter of credit / bill of exchange)
- *Mudaraba* (profit-sharing partnership)
- *Rabb al-mal* (capital investor in a mudaraba)
- *Mudarib* (working partner in a mudaraba)
- *Barid* (postal/intelligence service)
- *Fals* (copper coin), *dirham* (silver), *dinar* (gold)
- *Adhan* (call to prayer)
- *Khabir* (experienced guide)
- *Badraqa* (armed escort for caravans)
- "Commander of the Faithful" for the Caliph (not "king" or "emperor")
- "Peace be upon him" after mentioning the Prophet (characters would say this)
- *Siyaq* (the accounting script)

**Anachronisms to specifically avoid:**
- "Okay," "alright," "fine" (as affirmations)
- "Interesting" (as a response)
- "Basically," "essentially," "literally"
- Any sports metaphor
- Any mechanical or industrial metaphor
- "Network" used as a verb
- "Leverage" used as a verb
- "Impact" used as a verb

### Sensory Palette by Location

**Baghdad — Khorasan Gate neighbourhood:**
Dust (always), camel dung, spices from the road (cumin, cardamom), the mineral smell of fresh tile glaze, bread baking in tandoor ovens, the call to prayer competing with camel groans at dawn, hammers on stone, the Tigris in the distance (milky brown, warm).

**Abu Said's paper workshop:**
Wet, sour tang of lime, the earthy reek of soaking rags, the faint sweetness of drying paper in the loft, the rhythmic THUMP of pulping hammers, steam, the canal running behind the workshop, fine white dust from the finishing room.

**Kufa:**
Softer noise than Baghdad. Worn. Sounds smoothed by repetition. Baked earth, old stone, old mortar. A heaviness in the air, a city holding something in its chest. The Great Mosque's columns casting long shadows.

**Caravan camp:**
Pre-dawn silence. Camel groans. The *adhan* from somewhere in the dark. Smoke from acacia wood. Cumin, cardamom, dates in burned sugar. Meat cooking in fat. Dust stirred by a thousand travellers.

**The Suq al-Warraqin (Paper Sellers' Market):**
Ink, old parchment, the faintly sweet grassy scent of papyrus, the clean chemical smell of fresh paper, the scratch of reed pens, low voices of scholars arguing.

## 4. CHARACTER BIBLE

### Yusuf ibn Ali al-Khorasani — Protagonist

**Full name:** Yusuf ibn Ali al-Khorasani
**Born:** 770 CE, Baghdad
**Role:** Protagonist across all four books

> **NAMING NOTE:** Some project documents use "Idris" for the father. The canonical name is **Ali** (full name Ali ibn Suleiman). Yusuf is **Yusuf ibn Ali al-Khorasani**. If you encounter "Idris" as the father's name in source documents, read it as "Ali."

**Physical description:** Lean, dark-eyed, with hands roughened by years of paper-making. Moves with contained energy — still when observing, quick when acting. Not physically imposing. His most distinctive feature is his eyes — watchful, assessing, seeing more than they reveal.

**Personality and psychology:**
- Defining trait is *perception* — he reads people the way his father reads tile grain, structurally, looking for the flaw that will eventually crack.
- This perception makes genuine human connection difficult. He sees everyone as a system to be understood.
- Fatal flaw: by the time he realises some people deserved to be loved rather than analysed, he has already betrayed them.
- Driven by the memory of the Sogdian merchant in Merv — a competent man destroyed by proximity to power. Everything he builds is a fortress against that vulnerability.
- Capable of genuine warmth (with Musa, with his children) but has learned to override his feelings when survival demands it.

**Character arc across the series:**
- Book 1: Formation. The education of a dangerous man. The reader should finish thinking "I love this person, and I'm slightly afraid of him."
- Book 2: Ambition. The Barmakid Gamble reveals his capacity for ruthlessness. The cost is his relationships with Ishaq, Omar al-Dimashqi, and Zahra's full trust.
- Book 3: Entrapment. Proximity to absolute power becomes a prison. He discovers that the summit of achievement is a cage.
- Book 4: Release. He must surrender control — the hardest thing he's ever done — and let Khalid prove himself.

**Voice in dialogue:** Precise, economical, occasionally sharp. He asks questions more than he makes statements. When he does speak at length, it's usually to reframe a situation — the speech to Harun after the Barmakid purge is characteristic. He uses craft metaphors (glazes, foundations, cracks) inherited from his father.

### Ali ibn Suleiman — Yusuf's Father

**Born:** ~720 CE, Merv. **Dies:** ~808 CE, Baghdad.
**Role:** Moral anchor of Book 1, recurring presence through Book 3.

**Physical description:** Broad-shouldered, weathered hands permanently stained with glaze chemicals. In his later years, frail but sharp-eyed.

**Personality:** Quiet genius, deep principle. Believes a craftsman's integrity is his only true wealth. Represents the path not taken — honourable, modest, safe.

**Key relationship with Yusuf:** A father who loves his son but cannot understand his hunger, and a son who loves his father but cannot accept his smallness. Central emotional drama of Book 1. Their argument about paper instruments vs. physical craft ("Paper is real... you can touch it") is the central philosophical argument of the series.

**Voice:** Slow, deliberate, speaks in craft metaphors. "A beautiful thing must also be strong, and a flaw in its foundation will eventually lead to its ruin."

### Layla bint Hakim — Yusuf's Mother

**Born:** ~740 CE (Banu Asad, Bedouin). **Dies:** 782 CE, Baghdad.
**Role:** Spiritual and emotional foundation. Her death in Book 1 is formative.

**Personality:** Source of Yusuf's emotional intelligence. Teaches him the Bedouin arts of observation — reading character from hands, posture, treatment of animals. Her stories provide an alternative moral framework to the city's corruption.

**Key legacy:** The fox-and-lion story ("The fox who becomes a lion is still a fox — he has only traded one danger for another"). The pouch of desert sand she gives Yusuf before dying — a motif that recurs at key moments throughout the series.

**Voice:** Direct, warm, speaks in stories and proverbs. "An Arab's contract is his word. A Persian's soul is in his poetry. You must understand both to survive."

### Omar ibn Ali — Yusuf's Half-Brother

**Born:** 756 CE, Merv. Older than Yusuf by 14 years.
**Role:** Emotional foil. The soldier to Yusuf's merchant. Their relationship is the emotional backbone of the series.

**Physical description:** Lean, scarred, military bearing. Ages visibly across the series.

**Character arc:** Leaves for the army at 18 (774 CE). Returns periodically throughout. Retires in Book 3 to a modest farm outside Baghdad. Fights to defend his neighbourhood during the siege in Book 4.

**Key relationship with Yusuf:** Love and mutual incomprehension. Omar serves power with his body, Yusuf with his mind. Omar is the only person who can make Yusuf feel like a boy again, and the only person whose respect Yusuf craves but can never quite earn. Omar's pride clashes with Yusuf's instinct to control.

**Voice:** Blunt, military. Says hard truths plainly. "You sit in the Caliph's palace and move gold with a pen. I marched for months and bled for miles to protect the roads your gold travels on."

### Abu Said al-Samarqandi — Yusuf's Master

**Role:** Father figure who complements Ali. Where Ali teaches integrity, Abu Said teaches excellence.

**Physical description:** Not large, but occupies a room. Stillness, compressed attention. Beard neatly trimmed, hands clean, eyes the colour of strong tea.

**Personality:** Demanding but not cruel. Teaches through demonstration and silence. Assigns tasks, waits for failure, shows how it should be done — once. Never explains why. A Sogdian — one of the legendary merchant-craftsmen of Central Asia.

**Key speech:** "You have the hands of a craftsman and the eyes of a merchant. The hands will make you prosperous. The eyes will make you dangerous."

**Voice:** Sparse, authoritative. Questions rather than lectures. Silences are louder than his words.

### Omar al-Dimashqi — Workshop Friend

**Role:** Yusuf's first real friend, eventually master of production at the House of Paper.

**Personality:** Steady where Yusuf is restless, content where Yusuf is hungry. True craftsman. Wants to make great paper, not run an empire.

**Key dynamic with Yusuf:** The only friendship built entirely on genuine affection. Omar is the person Yusuf trusts with his craft but not his secrets — a distinction that eventually wounds Omar. After the Hamid al-Qurtubi destruction (Book 2), Omar's respect cools: "You didn't just beat him. You erased him."

**Voice:** Dry, practical, rare smiles. "Pull from the side. Not from the front. You are having a conversation with the animal."

### Umar (Ustad Umar) — Journeyman/Teacher on the Road

**Role:** Yusuf's guide and teacher during the caravan journeys in Book 1 (Chapters 1–4).

> **NOTE:** This character is named "Umar" (with a 'u') in the drafted chapters, distinct from "Omar al-Dimashqi" (Yusuf's workshop friend). Do not confuse them. Umar is a senior journeyman who accompanies Yusuf on trade journeys as Abu Said's representative.

**Personality:** Private, practical, gruff exterior concealing deep feeling. A man who keeps his warmth behind a wall. Has personal connections to Kufa (a dead uncle, Shi'a sympathies hinted at). Teaches by letting Yusuf fail, then offering quiet correction.

**Voice:** Spare, instructive. "I could have told you everything you just told me about Kufa in an afternoon. You would have nodded and said *yes, Ustad*, and understood nothing. You had to go there."

### Musa ibn Tariq — Childhood Friend / Musician

**Role:** The series' conscience. Counterpoint to Yusuf's ambition.

**Personality:** Spontaneous, emotional, alive to beauty for its own sake. What Yusuf might have been without ambition. A childhood friend from the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood who fell in love with the oud.

**Character arc:** Joins Yusuf's caravans as entertainment, eventually becomes a successful musician and patron in his own right. Connects to the historical Ziryab through mentorship. Builds something that endures through beauty rather than power.

**Key function:** Also serves as an intelligence asset on caravans — people tell things to a musician they would never tell a merchant. Yusuf recognises this, and the recognition both pleases and shames him.

**Voice:** Warm, spontaneous, speaks in musical metaphors. "Not everything in the world can be weighed on a scale."

### Ishaq bar Yohannan — General Manager / Nestorian Scribe

**Role:** Yusuf's most important professional partner. The series' deepest friendship, built on mutual respect and shared loneliness.

**Personality:** Brilliant, sardonic, haunted by marginality — a Christian in a Muslim empire, indispensable but never fully belonging. Private dream is to fund a great library of Syriac translations.

**Key dynamic:** Something breaks between Ishaq and Yusuf after the Barmakid Gamble. "Every document is perfect. Every contract is legal. Every signature is witnessed. And it is the most dishonourable thing I have ever been party to." He does not resign, but the easy trust is gone.

**Voice:** Precise, sardonic, legally-minded. The sharpest dialogue in the series.

### Zahra bint Abbas — Yusuf's Wife

**Introduced:** Book 2 (married ~795 CE).
**Role:** The person who sees through Yusuf's self-justifications. Partner, not ornament.

**Personality:** Sharp-eyed, direct, formidable. Runs the household with the same precision Yusuf runs his business. Quietly builds her own network of contacts and her own emergency preparations — insurance against the day Yusuf's plans fail.

**Key speech to Yusuf:** "If you want a wife who decorates your house, find someone else. If you want a partner who tells you the truth, I'm interested."

**Character arc:** Their relationship is deep respect that slowly, grudgingly, becomes love — though neither would use that word. She sees the Barmakid Gamble for what it is: "The man I married would have tried to save them."

### Khalid ibn Yusuf — Yusuf's Eldest Son

**Born:** ~800 CE. **Role:** The heir. Deuteragonist of Book 4.

**Personality:** Quiet, scholarly, deceptively passive. Inherits his father's analytical mind but masks it behind an unassuming demeanour. At ten, he asks: "If two men both claim to be Caliph, who is right?" — and his father has no answer.

**Arc:** Must prove he can preserve and adapt what was built for him — in some ways harder than building from nothing.

### Khalid's Key Question (from the Tutor Scene)
"If a merchant has ten dinars and lends five to a man who will repay six, and uses the other five to buy goods worth eight — what is his profit? ... Three dinars and the goodwill of the man who borrowed five. The goodwill is worth more than the dinar of interest, but only if the man is the right man. So the real question is: how do you know who the right man is?"

### Historical Figures

**Ja'far al-Barmaki** — The historical vizier. Brilliant, charming, generous, fatally blind to his own vulnerability. Becomes Yusuf's most important patron and eventually his most important victim. Their relationship is the moral crux of Book 2.

**Harun al-Rashid** — The Caliph. Not the legendary figure of the Thousand and One Nights but a complex, terrifying man: deeply pious, intellectually voracious, capable of great generosity and sudden violence. His night walks through Baghdad are a key narrative device in Book 3.

**Abu Nuwas** — Court poet. Delivers the thematic verdict of Book 2: "You are a man who sells what he does not believe in. That is either wisdom or tragedy."

**Al-Fadl ibn al-Rabi'** — Vizier after the Barmakids. Antagonist of Book 3. Brilliant politician, no financier.

**Tahir ibn Husayn** — Al-Ma'mun's general in the civil war. Military genius. Books 3–4.

**Rule for historical figures:** They serve the story, not the other way around. Every encounter must change Yusuf's understanding of himself or his world. If it doesn't, cut it.

## 5. PLOT STRUCTURE AND CHAPTER OUTLINE

### Overall Structure

**Book 1: The House of Paper (770–794 CE, story begins 787 CE)**
Five parts, 43 chapters: The Road (Kufa journey, Chs 1–9) → The Mountain and the Market (Zagros/Rayy, Chs 10–19) → The Desert, the North, and the Ghost (Damascus, Mosul, Merv, Chs 20–33) → The Choice (founding the House, Chs 34–38) → The Byzantine Education (Constantinople, Chs 39–43).

The novel opens in media res with Yusuf already on his first overland trade journey at age 17. Childhood, apprenticeship, Layla's death, and the Wasit river journey are all backstory, revealed organically through memory and conversation. The series overview (`series-overview.md`) and Book 1 plot (`book-1-plot.md`) outline 43 chapters across five parts, of which the first three are drafted. Constantinople has been moved from Book 2 into Book 1.

### Recurring Motifs and Foreshadowing

- **The Sogdian in Merv (Dastan)** — appears first when Yusuf witnesses him destroyed at the governor's asset auction (age 19, Ch 30). Recurs at every moral crisis, each time meaning something different: motivation (I must never be that vulnerable) → justification (I built my fortress) → accusation (in the Barmakid Gamble, Yusuf becomes the system that destroyed Dastan) → reconciliation (Yusuf honours all commitments, refusing to let power destroy those who trusted him).
- **The pouch of desert sand** — Layla's gift. Carried unopened for decades. Opened for the first time at the crisis of Book 3.
- **"Paper is real"** — Ali's argument against abstract finance. Echoes throughout as Yusuf's empire becomes increasingly abstract.
- **The Khorasan Gate tiles** — Ali's masterpiece. Visible from the family rooftop. Survive the siege in Book 4. The endurance of physical craft vs. the fragility of financial systems.
- **The fox and the lion** — Layla's story. The fox who becomes a lion is still a fox.
- **Abu Said's parting speech** — "I taught you to make paper that endures. You have just shown me that you are willing to burn everything for a single profit. Reconcile those two things, or you will destroy yourself." Yusuf spends the series trying to obey this.

## 6. THEMES AND MOTIFS

### Central Themes

**Paper as Power:** The literal commodity, the financial instruments written on it, the contracts that bind men, the records that outlast them. Paper is fragile, flammable, and yet it is the medium through which empires are administered, wealth is transferred, and knowledge is preserved.

**Trust as Currency:** Yusuf's business is built on trust — the watermark, the honoured suftaja, the reliable partnership. The series asks whether a man who weaponises trust (the Barmakid Gamble) can ever rebuild it.

**The Cost of Ambition:** Each book raises the price. Book 1 costs Yusuf his master's respect. Book 2 costs him his integrity. Book 3 costs him his freedom. Book 4 costs him control — which turns out to be the price of redemption.

**Craft vs. Commerce:** Ali makes things that endure. Yusuf moves things that multiply. The series does not resolve this tension — both are valid, both are limited.

**Inheritance and Legacy:** What can a father pass to a son? A trade? A fortune? A set of values? The series argues that character is the only true inheritance — but character is formed by hardship, and a successful father's greatest achievement (security for his children) may also be his greatest failure (depriving them of the forge that made him).

### Thematic Questions

- Is it possible to build something lasting on morally compromised foundations?
- Does perception — the ability to read people as systems — make genuine love impossible?
- Can the next generation surpass the one that built the empire, or are they inherently diminished by inheriting what they did not earn?
- Is survival a sufficient justification for betrayal?

### Recurring Symbols

- **The watermark** — trust made visible. The wave pattern (the Tigris) pressed into paper, visible only in strong light.
- **The cedarwood chest** — the *Daftar al-Asrar* (Book of Secrets). Yusuf's intelligence ledger. His most valuable and most dangerous asset.
- **The Khorasan Gate** — facing east, toward origin, possibility, and danger. The first beautiful thing travellers see from the east. Yusuf's father built it. Yusuf grows up facing the same direction.
- **The tile fragment** — picked up during the siege when a tile falls from the Khorasan Gate. Kept on Yusuf's desk. His father's craft, broken, still beautiful.

## 8. WORKFLOW INSTRUCTIONS

### Source Authority Hierarchy

When project documents conflict, authority flows in this order:
1. **Drafted chapter text** — what is actually written is ground truth.
2. **Continuity ledger** — the record of what was drafted. Must match the text.
3. **Chapter brief** — the plan for what should be written. If drafted, the brief should have been updated to match; if not, the drafted text and ledger take precedence.
4. **Book plot file** (e.g. `book-1-plot.md`) — the detailed chapter outline. May be overridden by a brief that reflects a more recent creative decision.
5. **`series-overview.md`** — the high-level series arc. Rarely overridden.
6. **CLAUDE.md §4/§7** — character bible and timeline. Should be updated when chapters are drafted but may lag.

When two briefs for undrafted chapters contradict each other, flag the contradiction for the author. Do not silently resolve it.

### When Writing a New Chapter

1. **MANDATORY:** Read the chapter brief at `chapters/book-1/briefs/ch{NN}-brief.md`. This is your primary context — it contains character states, continuity notes, voice reminders, and sensory palette for this specific chapter. If additional research depth is needed, consult `context-manifest.md` for this chapter's research file list. Then read the continuity summary at `chapters/book-1/continuity-summary.md` for an overview of all previous Parts, then the current Part's detailed ledger (e.g. `chapters/book-1/continuity-part-1.md`) for the chapters closest to the one being written. Then read `series-overview.md` for the series arc and the relevant book plot file (e.g. `book-1-plot.md`) for the chapter's place in the overall arc.
2. **Read the previous chapter's final scene** for continuity of setting, mood, and any unfinished action.
3. **Check this file (§4)** for every character appearing in the chapter — voice, current emotional state, what they know.
4. **Check the timeline (§7)** — what year is it? What has happened recently in the world? What is Yusuf's age and current state?
5. **Write the chapter in a single markdown file**, following all voice and style rules in §2.
6. **After writing, append a continuity ledger entry** to the appropriate Part's ledger file (e.g. `chapters/book-1/continuity-part-1.md` for Ch 1-8, `continuity-part-2.md` for Ch 9-18, etc.) following the established format (~300 words: Facts, Objects, Threads planted, Character states, Physical descriptions introduced, Commercial/financial facts, Deviations from brief). Update the continuity summary (`chapters/book-1/continuity-summary.md`) if this chapter completes a Part or significantly changes the state. Then update the chapter brief at `chapters/book-1/briefs/ch{NN}-brief.md` with any details that changed during writing, and update the threads ledger at `chapters/book-1/threads-ledger.md` with any new threads planted. Flag any details needing historical verification with `[RESEARCH NEEDED]` tags.

   **Ledger corrections:** The ledger is append-only for new entries but may be corrected in place when errors are discovered. When correcting: re-read the relevant chapter text to verify the correction, edit the entry directly, and add a `[CORRECTED: date, description]` annotation at the end of the corrected field.
7. **Do not add meta-commentary within the chapter text itself.** No "[Author's note]" or "[TODO]" inline. Keep those in the continuity note.

### When Editing or Revising a Chapter

1. **MANDATORY:** Read the chapter brief at `chapters/book-1/briefs/ch{NN}-brief.md` AND the existing chapter draft. The brief contains continuity details, enhancement priorities, and voice reminders specific to this chapter. For deeper research, consult `context-manifest.md`.
2. **Assess across these dimensions** (present findings before editing):
   - Prose quality and voice consistency (does it match §2?)
   - Pacing (are action/reflection scenes using the right rhythms?)
   - Historical accuracy (any anachronisms?)
   - Continuity (consistent with previous chapters and the timeline?)
   - Dialogue authenticity (does each character sound like themselves?)
   - Sensory detail (is the reader grounded in place?)
   - Emotional arc (does the chapter start in one state and end in another?)
   - Agency (is Yusuf an active agent, not a passive observer?)
3. **Make edits in-place** but describe what was changed and why.
4. **Never rewrite the author's voice into a generic style.** Preserve the established voice. When in doubt, match the existing chapters.

**Prose-only edits:** If a revision changes only prose quality (word choice, rhythm, sentence structure) without altering any facts, character states, objects, or threads, no downstream updates to the ledger, briefs, or other tracking files are required.

### When Making a Cascading Revision

A cascading revision is any edit that changes a character name, an object description, a timeline fact, or a planted thread that other chapters reference.

1. Before editing, grep the project for the detail being changed across: all chapter drafts, all briefs, all continuity ledgers, context-manifest.md, the book plot files, CLAUDE.md (§4, §7).
2. List all files that reference the changed detail.
3. Make the edit in the chapter text.
4. Update all downstream files in this order: continuity ledger entry → continuity summary → CLAUDE.md §4/§7 → affected briefs → threads ledger → book plot file → context manifest.
5. Note the cascading change in the commit message listing all files modified.

### When Splitting, Merging, or Reordering Chapters

These operations affect the numbering and structure of the book. Follow this procedure:

**Splitting a chapter:**
1. Identify the split point (usually a scene break).
2. Create two new chapter files with updated numbers and titles.
3. Update the brief for the original chapter — split it into two briefs.
4. Update the continuity ledger — split the entry if the chapter was drafted.
5. Renumber all subsequent chapters: files, briefs, ledger entries, manifest entries, threads ledger references, relationship tracker references.
6. Update the book plot file and CLAUDE.md §5 chapter table.

**Merging chapters:**
1. Combine the two chapter files.
2. Merge their briefs into one.
3. Merge their ledger entries if both were drafted.
4. Renumber all subsequent chapters as above.

**Reordering chapters:**
1. Verify no continuity dependencies are broken (a chapter cannot reference events from a later chapter).
2. Rename files to reflect new order.
3. Update all cross-references: briefs, ledger entries, manifest, threads ledger, relationship tracker, book plot file, CLAUDE.md §5.

**Moving a scene between chapters:**
1. Move the text.
2. Update both chapters' ledger entries to reflect what each chapter now contains.
3. Update both briefs.
4. Check the threads ledger for any threads planted in the moved scene — update the "Planted" column.

### When Starting a New Book

1. Create the directory structure: `chapters/book-N/`, `chapters/book-N/briefs/`.
2. Create the book's continuity files: `continuity-summary.md`, `continuity-part-1.md` (and additional parts as the book's structure requires).
3. Create the book's threads ledger: `chapters/book-N/threads-ledger.md`.
4. Create the book's relationship tracker: `chapters/book-N/relationship-tracker.md`.
5. Reference the previous book's final continuity summary for character states and unresolved threads that carry forward.
6. Create `book-N-plot.md` from the relevant section of the series outline.
7. Update `context-manifest.md` with the new book's chapter-to-research mappings.
8. Update CLAUDE.md §5 with the new book's chapter status table.

### When Doing a Continuity Check

1. Read all existing chapters in sequence.
2. Cross-reference against the timeline (§7), character tracker, and plot outline.
3. Produce a continuity report listing issues by severity:
   - **Critical:** Character knows something they shouldn't. Timeline contradiction. Dead character appears alive.
   - **Moderate:** Physical description inconsistency. Object in wrong location. Tone shift.
   - **Minor:** Spelling of names. Arabic term inconsistency. Sensory detail clash.

### When Doing Research or Fact-Checking

1. **Flag any historical claims in the text** that may need verification.
2. **Clearly distinguish** between: documented historical fact, reasonable extrapolation from known sources, and pure invention.
3. **When uncertain, say so.** Use `[RESEARCH NEEDED: brief description]` tags. Never fabricate historical details and present them as fact.
4. **Consult project knowledge first** — extensive research documents exist in the project files covering: Baghdad's geography, the paper trade, Abbasid politics, caravan logistics, Islamic finance, social structures, and more.

### When Conducting Research for the Project

This workflow applies when the user asks Claude to research a topic for use in future chapters.

1. **Search existing research first.** Read `research-index.md` for an overview of available sources. Grep `research/` and `research/digests/` for keywords related to the topic. If the topic is already covered, point to the existing file(s) and ask whether the user wants deeper investigation.
2. **Conduct research** using web search, synthesis, and project knowledge. Clearly distinguish between documented historical fact, reasonable extrapolation, and pure invention. Use `[RESEARCH NEEDED]` tags for any claims that could not be verified.
3. **Write the research file** as narrative prose in the scene-ready style of existing research documents. File it in the appropriate subdirectory:
   - `research/world-building/` — places, material culture, society, politics, religion
   - `research/characters/` — character backstories, historical figure profiles
   - `research/finance/` — financial instruments, deals, economic mechanics
   - `research/journeys/` — route logistics, itineraries, travel conditions
   - `research/craft/` — scene-level research, specific locations, walks
   - `research/plot/` — plot outlines, chapter breakdowns, structure analysis
4. **Name the file** using descriptive kebab-case: `nestorian-christians-in-baghdad.md`, not `research-topic-1.md`. The filename should be grep-friendly — someone searching for "Nestorian" should find it by filename alone.
5. **Update context files:** After updating `research-index.md`, also update `context-manifest.md` to add the new file under relevant chapters. If the research changes continuity or character state, update the affected chapter briefs in `chapters/book-1/briefs/`.
6. **Include a "Works cited" section** at the end with numbered references, following the format used in existing research files.
7. **Update `research-index.md`** — add the new file to the appropriate topic section and to any relevant Book/Chapter rows.

### When Digesting a Reference Book

This workflow applies when the user adds a book (PDF, EPUB, or other format) to `reference/` and wants it made searchable. The user may say "digest this book" or "I've added N books, digest them."

1. **Read the book.** For PDFs, read using the Read tool (20 pages at a time) — start with the table of contents to identify which chapters are relevant, then read only those sections. For EPUBs, unzip and read the HTML content. For other formats (MOBI, DJVU), attempt conversion or ask the user to provide a PDF/EPUB version.
2. **Extract material relevant to the project's needs:** Baghdad geography, Abbasid politics, material culture, commerce, paper trade, social structures, military campaigns, daily life, sensory details, and any historical figures in the character bible (§4).
3. **Write a digest file** at `research/digests/{author-surname}--{short-title}.md` with this structure:
   - **Header:** title, author, source file path (`reference/filename.pdf`), date digested, one-line relevance summary
   - **Overview:** 2–3 paragraphs on the book's scope, argument, and value to this project
   - **Key Extracts:** topic-organized, scene-ready prose with page/location references. Include direct quotes where the language is vivid enough to use in fiction. Each extract should have a **"Scene potential"** annotation — one sentence on how this material could be used in the novel.
   - **Facts and Dates:** bulleted, grep-friendly list of specific data points (dates, names, measurements, prices) with page references
   - **Relevance to Chapters:** which chapters or plot beats this book informs, keyed to the book plot files (e.g. `book-1-plot.md`) and existing chapters. Use consistent format: `Book 1, Ch 1-3 (Kufa journey)` for grepability.
   - **[RESEARCH NEEDED] flags:** any claims that conflict with other sources or existing research files
4. **Target 2,000–5,000 words** per digest regardless of book length. Apply the iceberg rule: extract what matters to this project, not everything. A 300-page book rarely has more than 100 pages of material relevant to this specific novel.
5. **Update `research-index.md`** — add the digest to the Book Digests table and to any relevant topic and chapter rows.

### When Creating or Updating a Chapter Brief

After writing or significantly revising a chapter:

1. Create or update the brief at `chapters/book-1/briefs/ch{NN}-brief.md`.
2. Follow the format established in existing briefs: When/Where, Characters Present, What This Chapter Must Accomplish, Continuity, Voice Reminders, Deep Research links.
3. Update character states, continuity notes, and planted threads based on what was actually written.
4. Check the briefs of adjacent chapters (N-1 and N+1) — if continuity details changed, update those briefs too.
5. Flag any `[RESEARCH NEEDED]` items discovered during writing.
6. Update `context-manifest.md` if new research files were consulted or created.
7. If this is a newly drafted chapter, also append an entry to the appropriate Part's continuity ledger (e.g. `chapters/book-1/continuity-part-1.md`) recording what the chapter actually established.

### When Expanding World-Building

1. Always ground additions in the established historical setting.
2. Cross-reference with existing world-building documents to avoid contradictions.
3. Present new additions for approval before incorporating them into chapter text.
4. Prefer specific, concrete detail over general description. Not "the market was busy" but the particular smells, sounds, and textures established in §3.

## 10. PROJECT-SPECIFIC RULES

### Historical Verification
- If unsure about a historical detail, leave a `[RESEARCH NEEDED: description]` tag rather than guessing. Getting it wrong is worse than flagging it.
- The project files contain extensive research. Always search them before doing external research.

### Character Safety
- Never kill a character that isn't marked for death in the outline without explicit approval.
- Never introduce a new named character without considering their arc — every named character must have a reason to exist beyond a single scene.

### The "Too Competent" Rule
- Before writing any scene where Yusuf succeeds, ask: has he failed recently enough? "Competence porn" is the biggest threat to the reading experience. Yusuf must struggle, misjudge, lose, and learn. His victories must be earned through prior failure.
- Specific mandate: every trade journey should include a meaningful setback. Not every negotiation should go well. Not every plan should work as designed.

### The "Medici Scaffolding" Rule
- The Medici Bank was an inspiration for the financial mechanics, but the story must grow from its own world. Do not use Medici-era terminology or make explicit parallels. The financial structures emerge from Sogdian merchant practices, Byzantine banking, and the empire's own commercial traditions. If a plot beat feels like it was imported from Florence, rework it until it feels native to Baghdad.

### Expansion vs. New Beats
- When asked to "expand a scene," add sensory detail, interiority, and character interaction — do **not** add new plot beats unless explicitly requested.
- When asked to "develop a character," show their inner life through action and thought — do **not** add backstory dumps or explanatory narration.

### Word Count
- Maintain awareness of chapter length. Target 5,000–7,000 words. If a chapter is running significantly longer, consider splitting at a natural scene break.

### The Iceberg Rule
- The research depth of this project is enormous. Apply the iceberg rule: for every ten pages of research, one page appears on the surface. The other nine inform the texture, confidence, and authenticity of the writing without being explicitly stated. Never let research overwhelm narrative.

### Research Conflict Resolution
When historical research contradicts authorial intent in a brief: flag with `[RESEARCH CONFLICT: description]`, present the conflict to the author with both the research finding and the brief's creative intent, and wait for a decision. Never silently alter a brief to match research — the author may prefer creative licence over historical accuracy.

### File Reference Integrity
When encountering a broken file path in any project document: flag with `[BROKEN REF: path]`, search for the file under possible new names, and update all references if found. When renaming any research file or chapter file: grep the entire project for the old path and update all references before committing.

### Reading Order for New Context
If you need to orient yourself quickly in the project, read these files in this order:
1. This file (`CLAUDE.md`)
2. `series-overview.md` — the series arc and cross-book structure
3. `book-1-plot.md` — the Book 1 chapter-by-chapter outline (or whichever book is active)
4. `chapters/book-1/ch05-the-suq-al-warraqin.md` — the most polished example of the voice
5. `chapters/book-1/ch03-the-wounded-heart.md` — the longest and most textured chapter
6. `chapters/book-1/ch04-the-poisoned-vat.md` — strong example of workshop/craft scenes
7. `chapters/book-1/continuity-summary.md` — compressed state at each Part boundary
8. `context-manifest.md` — maps chapters to research files
