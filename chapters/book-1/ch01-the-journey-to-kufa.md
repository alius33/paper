**CHAPTER ONE**

*The Journey to Kufa*

There are two kinds of silence. The first is the quiet of rest, of the world finished with its noise and settling down. That kind Yusuf knew well. The paper workshop at the end of a long day, the vats stilled, the drying frames empty, his master's voice gone hoarse and finally, mercifully, stopped.

This was the other kind.

The pre-dawn cold of the caravan camp held a silence that waited. Vast and brittle, like a sheet of ice over deep water. Into it the world leaked: the snores and grunts of a thousand sleeping men, the shifting of animals in the dark, the low moan of wind across open ground. But the sky was enormous here, and it swallowed every sound before it could take hold.

Then the silence broke. The *adhan* rose from somewhere in the camp, thin and clear as a blade, and it launched the complaints. Not of men. Of camels. A deep, wet, protesting groan from a hundred throats, a sound like the earth itself objecting to the dawn. They were the ships of the desert, indispensable and prized, and they hated the morning.

A bubbling, liquid belch from the animal beside him jolted Yusuf fully awake. He lay still, eyes half-open. Through his lashes he watched Ustad Umar rise from his mat, wrap his shawl tightly around his shoulders, and begin picking his way toward the prayer area. Yusuf kept very still. If the ustad turned and saw his eyes open, there would be no escaping it---the cold water, the ablutions, the prayers. He had done his share of praying in Baghdad. God would understand.

Umar did not turn. Yusuf pulled the blanket over his head and breathed out.

What woke him next was the smell.

It came in layers. First the spices---cumin, then cardamom, then a sweetness that might have been dates in burned sugar. Then smoke, acacia wood, sharp and resinous. Then, beneath it all, cutting through the thick animal reek of the camp, the unmistakable smell of meat cooking in fat. His stomach answered before his mind did. He sat up.

All around him the camp was stirring. The great field outside Baghdad's Kufa Gate was filled with a city's worth of travellers, and now every one of them was doing the same thing: finishing whatever task was at hand and turning their face toward the cook fires. The biggest group were the Hajj pilgrims, hundreds of them, bound for the holy cities. They had come from every corner of the Caliph's empire---from Raqqa and Mosul, from Isfahan and Rayy, from the Khorasani cities Yusuf knew only from his father's stories: Merv, Nishapur, Nasa. There were even men from Kabul who spoke their Arabic with a thick accent and prayed with their hands held differently. They had been talking last night of friends who had sailed from a place called Karachi, a city Yusuf had never heard of, in a land he could not quite imagine.

He was seventeen years old, and until this morning, he had never slept outside the walls of Baghdad.

\* \* \*

He found Sayeed and Sa'dan already at work.

The two merchant's assistants were breaking down their master's tent with the practised efficiency of men who had done this a hundred times. Sa'dan, the elder, was directing the labourers on the right side, his voice low and precise. Sayeed, who was closer to Yusuf's age, was overseeing the left, one eye on the fold and the other on Sa'dan, checking that he was doing it right. They worked without hurry, their hands quick, their conversation easy.

"You have to do this every morning?" Yusuf asked, stepping up beside Sayeed. He already knew the answer. But he needed to say something, because he had been standing at the edge of their campsite for a full minute and the silence was becoming conspicuous.

"Almost every morning." Sayeed didn't look up. "Past Kufa, there are some bigger *khans* with rooms to rent. The master sleeps indoors. We sleep outside as always."

"That doesn't seem fair."

Sayeed shrugged. "I like the open sky. Camp life is good once you're used to it." He glanced at Yusuf and grinned. "You'll see."

Sa'dan came around to check the left side. He was perhaps twenty, lean and weathered in a way that made him look older, with the kind of calm that comes from knowing exactly what to do at every moment of the day. He looked at Yusuf briefly, took in the boy's shifting feet and the way his eyes kept drifting toward the cook fires, and smiled.

"Salaam, Yusuf. Mirza can finish loading. I can see our little brother has a growling stomach. Let's go eat."

\* \* \*

The cook fires were a world unto themselves.

A row of massive, blackened cauldrons balanced over smoky pits. Steam rose in thick, fragrant clouds, momentarily hiding the faces of the men who stirred them with paddles the size of oars. Around them pressed a mass of humanity---pilgrims in simple white *ihram*, merchants in dyed wools arguing with porters, hard-faced caravan guards with scarred hands resting near the hilts of their swords. The noise was tremendous. Men shouting in half a dozen accents, haggling over portions, laughing sudden belly-laughs. All of it underscored by the hungry crackle of the fires.

Yusuf spotted Ustad Umar already waiting in one of the long queues and moved to join him, but Sa'dan caught his shoulder.

"We don't cut in line. Core rule of the caravan."

"I'm just going to---"

"Doesn't matter. Come with me. I know a fire that's less busy."

He moved through the crowd with a shuffling, practised gait, finding gaps where Yusuf saw only a wall of bodies, turning his shoulders to slide through. Yusuf and Sayeed followed in his wake. He led them to a smaller fire where the men ladling portions looked less harried.

"Two choices," Sa'dan said, pointing with his chin. "*Harīsa* you know. The other is *Sikbāj*. Too rich for a morning meal, but it's departure day. They always cook something special."

Yusuf looked at the cauldrons. The *Harīsa* was a familiar comfort---wheat and meat, slow-cooked to a thick pale porridge, beaten with a wooden paddle until it became a wholesome, filling paste. His mother made it on cold mornings. The other cauldron held something else entirely. Chunks of lamb swimming in a dark, glossy sauce that smelled sharply of vinegar and sweetly of dates. He could see plump apricots like sunken jewels, a scattering of toasted almonds. *Sikbāj*. A dish he'd heard of but never tasted. A rich man's dish.

"I know for a fact," Sayeed said, leaning in conspiratorially, "that Sa'dan will have the *Harīsa*. Boring, tasteless, perfect for a man of no imagination. I've learned the best way to enjoy life is to do the opposite of what Sa'dan does."

"It will give you a reflux," Sa'dan said flatly. "But if you want to walk six hours with your heart on fire, go ahead."

They reached the front of the queue. The servers looked at them. Sayeed stepped forward to the *Sikbāj* without hesitation. Sa'dan moved to the *Harīsa*. Yusuf stood between them, frozen.

It was a small choice. A nothing choice. But something turned in him---something stubborn and hungry and tired of the familiar. He grabbed a bowl and followed Sayeed.

The *Sikbāj* tasted strange. Not bad. But overwhelming. The deep fragrance of the lamb, the tang of vinegar, the sweetness of dates, the occasional burst of apricot---each flavour arrived at full strength, one after another, holding nothing back. Yusuf was used to food that was one thing or another, sweet or savoury, and this was everything at once. He ate it slowly, frowning in concentration, aware that Sa'dan was watching him with amusement.

"Verdict?"

"I don't know yet," Yusuf said honestly.

Sa'dan nodded, as if this were the right answer.

\* \* \*

A low, booming sound rolled across the camp. A ram's horn, deep and mournful. Yusuf felt it in his ribs before he heard it in his ears.

"Prepare the animals," Sa'dan said, already on his feet. "We move soon."

The change was instant. The communal ease of breakfast vanished as if it had never existed. Men who had been sharing jokes were now barking orders. The sound of the camp being dismantled was a cacophony: the heavy *thwack* of mallets knocking tent pegs from hard earth, the snap of canvas being folded, the clatter of cooking pots scoured with sand and nested together. *Yallah! Yallah!* The shouts came from every direction.

Sa'dan and Sayeed were calm. They had taken their tent down before breakfast---Yusuf understood why now. While the rest of the camp erupted, their little group moved with unhurried purpose.

Ustad Umar was already at their belongings, checking the packs. "Personal rolls first. Then the packs. Then the chest."

The chest. Small, bound in iron, it held the entire reason they were here: a dozen samples of their master's finest paper. Not the standard stock that the merchant Ibn Zubair carried in his bulk shipment. These were the best pieces Ali ibn Suleiman could produce---sheets so smooth and white they seemed to glow. Meant for the scholars and noblemen of Kufa, men who knew quality when they held it. Yusuf checked the leather straps and the heavy lock, ran his fingers along the seams. Sound.

"Good," Umar grunted. "Now stay with me. Observe everything. Learn."

\* \* \*

As Yusuf helped secure the last of their packs, he saw Ibn Zubair for the first time.

The merchant stood apart from the sweating labourers. He wore a fine dark-blue *jubbah* of wool so closely woven it shimmered, and he tapped a short riding crop against his boot with the impatience of a man who believed the world moved too slowly for him. A young *kātib* with ink-stained fingers stood beside him, holding a writing board and nodding rapidly as the merchant dictated instructions. Ibn Zubair did not raise his voice. He did not need to.

Then the true roar began. Not of men, but of camels.

The trumpet had been a call to the men; what followed was a call to the animals, and the animals did not want to go. The groans of dawn had been a whisper compared to this---a deep, bubbling, guttural concert of a thousand complaints. The camel drivers, the *jammālūn*, moved among the kneeling beasts, cursing and cajoling in equal measure. *Hut! Hut!* they cried, hauling on nose ropes. One driver, a dark-skinned Bedouin, wrestled a pair of heavy boxes onto a pale-grey beast. The camel twisted its long neck and spat a glob of green cud that just missed the man's head. He didn't flinch. He cursed, slapped the animal hard on its flank, and shouldered the load into place.

Yusuf watched, and began to notice the differences among the animals. Most were dromedaries, the familiar one-humped workhorses of the road. But the string being loaded with Ibn Zubair's personal cargo were different---taller, with heavier shoulders and thicker coats.

"*Bukhtī* hybrids," Sayeed said at his elbow. "Bactrian sire, dromedary dam. They carry twice what ours do. Cost a fortune."

Past the camels stood the horses. Bays, deep chestnuts, and one striking black that seemed to drink the light. The men grooming them were not drivers. They were the caravan's guards---hard-faced men with chainmail under their tunics and the curved sheaths of Khorasani swords at their hips. Their horses were their weapons, and they treated them accordingly.

\* \* \*

The trumpet sounded a different call. A long, sustained note. The chaos did not stop, but it narrowed, like a river finding its banks.

"Who is the caravan master?" Yusuf asked. "Shouldn't he be directing all this?"

Sa'dan pointed with his chin to a man on a dust-grey horse. He was not the largest man in the camp, nor the most richly dressed. He wore a plain dark-brown *aba* and a tightly wound turban. But the entire operation seemed to pivot around him. He was in quiet, intense conference with the captain of the guards and a wiry man in Bedouin dress.

"That is the *Khabīr*," Sa'dan said, his voice dropping. "The master of the caravan. His name is Al-Muin. From this gate to Mecca, his word is the law."

As Yusuf watched, a smaller group of riders broke away from Al-Muin. They were Bedouin, but their robes were of fine cloth and their horses were pure white. The sheikh at their head wore a silver-worked dagger. They rode away with an unhurried, satisfied air.

Umar leaned close. His voice was barely a breath.

"Do you see that? That is the real business of the morning. The *khuwwa*."

"Protection money?"

"That's the word that's used. The *Khabīr* just paid that sheikh a good amount of gold. Not for protection. To keep his tribe from being the threat. The guards around us with their swords are for show. That negotiation is what keeps us alive."

Yusuf looked at the departing horsemen, then at the imperial road stretching south, wide and well-maintained, the milestones carved with the Caliph's seal. The state had built this road. But the Bedouin owned the desert it crossed.

He had grown up in Baghdad believing that the Caliph's word was the final authority in all things. It had taken him less than one morning outside the walls to learn that this was not true.

\* \* \*

Three sharp trumpet blasts.

"Mount up," Sa'dan said. "The column is forming."

The camel assigned to Yusuf was a grumpy beast the drivers called One-Eye, though he had both eyes and used them mainly for glaring. He was kneeling, and Yusuf scrambled into the wooden saddle---a simple frame with two upright posts.

"Hold the front post," Sayeed called. "Both hands. And lean back!"

"Why would I---"

The camel surged upward. Back legs first, pitching Yusuf so violently forward that he nearly sailed over the beast's neck. He yelped. Then the front legs straightened with a second lurch that cracked him back into the saddle hard enough to jar his teeth.

Sayeed was laughing. "See? Always lean back when they stand. You'll learn."

From up here the world looked different. The caravan was forming not as a mob but as a long, segmented serpent. Guards and scouts fanned out at the front. Behind them rode Al-Muin, his green banner held by a rider at his side. The wealthy merchants occupied the protected centre, surrounded by guards. Behind them, Yusuf's group, with the high-value cargo.

"The belly of the beast," Sa'dan said, manoeuvring his mule between Yusuf's camel and Umar's. "The safest place."

Behind them stretched the great mass of pilgrims, a caravan within the caravan, and at the very end a rear guard of horsemen trailing a utility train of spare camels, fodder, and a small flock of goats.

A final, long blast of the trumpet.

A thousand camels groaned.

The serpent began to move.

\* \* \*

Yusuf's world became a chaos of sensation.

He was high up, swaying, and the camel's gait was not a walk but a rolling, lurching plod that immediately threatened the *Sikbāj* in his stomach. The sound was immense: the rhythmic *plod-plod-plod* of thousands of soft camel feet, the shouts of drivers in a dozen accents, the bleating of goats, the creak and groan of leather and rope. And the smell---the sharp ammonia of camel urine, the musk of unwashed bodies, the dust rising in a great choking cloud.

He looked back. Baghdad. The morning sun had caught the Green Dome of the Palace of the Golden Gate, and it flashed a brilliant, heartbreaking emerald above the walls. He thought of his room. The smell of rosewater and sizing paste in the workshop. His mother's face. They seemed impossibly far away, though he could still see the dome.

Then they cleared the last of the suburbs, and the world changed.

The green of the Tigris basin fell away as if cut with a knife. The road became a wide, pale, sun-baked track. The land was flat, brown, and empty to every horizon.

Ustad Umar pointed a long finger at the road. "Look, boy. This is no goat track. This is the *Barīd*. The Caliph's artery. See how it is cleared of stone? How it is wide enough for ten men abreast?" He gestured to a squat carved milestone at the roadside, then to a small square tower shimmering in the heat. "A fire-signal tower. This is the hand of the state, the power of Baghdad, reaching all the way to the holy cities. You are riding on the Caliph's will."

Yusuf felt a swell of pride. He was part of this. This great, organised, powerful world.

"The state builds the road, Ustad," Sa'dan's voice came from below. He had guided his mule alongside. "But they do not own it. Out there---" he gestured at the vast emptiness on either side---"the road belongs to the Banu Asad. The Caliph's milestone tells us the distance. The *Khabīr's* gold buys the miles."

Umar grunted. "The sword and the dinar. Both are the will of God."

The sun climbed. The heat, still mild in the city, became a physical weight---a hot breath that sucked the moisture from Yusuf's skin. His lips cracked. He reached for the waterskin tied to his saddle.

Sa'dan's switch tapped his hand.

"No. Not yet."

"But I'm thirsty."

"We are all thirsty. That skin must last you. You drink when the *Khabīr* halts the column. Not before. A sip now is a sip you'll beg for at sundown."

Yusuf's hand fell. Nearby, a pilgrim in white was drinking deeply from his own skin. Sa'dan saw him too.

"That one will be begging by midday."

The warning about the *Sikbāj* had been a joke. This was not.

\* \* \*

Hours passed. Or perhaps it was one hour. Yusuf could not tell. The sun was a white, merciless eye and the road stretched on, featureless, the same brown emptiness repeating itself to the horizon. His back ached. His legs cramped against the saddle. The camel's gait was a kind of torture---rhythmic enough to be maddening, irregular enough to prevent sleep.

Sa'dan had warned him about this. *It's boredom, Yusuf. That's the real enemy. Hours and hours. First you count your steps. Then the bumps on the camel's backside. Then you run out of things to count.* Yusuf had thought the older boy was exaggerating. He was not.

Beside him, Sayeed was riding a small donkey and humming a tune. He reached into a pouch at his belt and pulled out something dark and brittle. He popped it into his mouth and chewed with a loud, cheerful crunch.

"What is that?" Yusuf asked, his voice hoarse.

Sayeed held one up. It was a dried locust, legs and wings still attached.

"*Jarād*," he said. "Traveller's food. Good protein."

Yusuf's stomach turned. But Sayeed's face was open and earnest, not teasing, and Sa'dan was watching from his mule with an expression that was not quite a challenge but was certainly an assessment.

"They're clean," Sa'dan said. "The Prophet ate them, peace be upon him. When you're two days from water and your rations are gone, these are the sweetest meat you'll ever taste. They fly in clouds that block the sun. We catch them at dawn when the dew weighs their wings, throw them in brine, roast them in a pan. The sun and salt cure them."

Sayeed held one out. Roasted dark, its wings removed, coated in salt and cumin.

Yusuf looked at it. He thought of the *Sikbāj* that morning---how he'd chosen the unfamiliar dish on impulse, and how the taste had been too much and not enough at the same time. He thought of how he'd frozen at the front of the queue and Sa'dan had stepped forward to spare him. He did not want to be spared again.

He took the locust. It was light as air, rough against his fingers. Before he could lose his nerve, he put it in his mouth and bit down.

The crunch was enormous, shockingly loud inside his own skull. Then it dissolved into a dry, salty, faintly nutty dust. Like roasted chickpeas, but lighter. Not bad. Not bad at all.

Sa'dan smiled---a real smile, rare on that weathered face---and clapped him on the shoulder hard enough to nearly unseat him.

"There. You'll be a merchant's man yet, boy."

The caravan plodded on. The sun climbed higher. The only sounds were ten thousand footfalls, the creak of harnesses, the shouts of the drivers---*Hut! Hut! Yallah!*---and the hot, empty wind.

Yusuf held on to his saddle and stared at the road ahead. Brown, flat, endless. Behind him, the Green Dome was gone. Baghdad was gone. There was only the road, and the heat, and the smell of the camel, and a faint, lingering taste of salt and cumin on his tongue.

He had been outside the walls for less than half a day. He had learned that the Caliph's road was not the Caliph's. That silence and chaos could trade places in the space of a trumpet blast. That food could be a thing you did not understand. That thirst was not a problem to solve but a discipline to learn.

He had thought he was going to Kufa to sell paper. He was beginning to suspect the paper was beside the point.
