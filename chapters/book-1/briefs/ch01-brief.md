## BRIEF: Chapter 1 — The Journey to Kufa

**When/Where:** Caravan camp outside Baghdad's Kufa Gate, then the imperial road south. Early spring 787 CE. Yusuf is 17.
**Arriving from:** This is the opening chapter. Yusuf has never slept outside Baghdad's walls. He has been an apprentice at Abu Said's paper workshop since age 9. His mother Layla died when he was 12. The Wasit river journey (his first trip outside the city) happened earlier in 787 but is backstory, not dramatised.

### Characters Present
- **Yusuf:** Awake, alert, hungry, overwhelmed. First morning on the road. Watches everything, reads everyone. Oscillates between confidence and uncertainty. Chooses the unfamiliar (Sikbaj, locusts) but freezes at small decisions.
- **Ustad Umar:** Gruff, private, authoritative. Rises for dawn prayer. Gives orders in compressed phrases. Points out the *barid* road and its meaning. Not yet emotionally open.
- **Sa'dan:** Lean, weathered, about 20. The caravan veteran who teaches through quiet competence. Corrects Yusuf's water discipline. First to approve him (the locust smile). Knows camp hierarchy instinctively.
- **Sayeed:** Close to Yusuf's age. Cheerful, aspirational. Offers the locusts. Dreams of owning his own caravan. The emotional warmth to Sa'dan's practicality.
- **Ibn Zubair:** Seen from a distance. The wealthy merchant whose caravan they travel with. Fine blue *jubbah*, riding crop, *katib* beside him. Represents the mercantile class Yusuf will eventually enter.
- **Al-Muin:** The *khabir* (caravan master). Seen negotiating the *khuwwa* with Bedouin scouts. Quiet authority. His word is law on the road.

### What This Chapter Must Accomplish
- Establish the sensory world of caravan travel: pre-dawn silence, cook fires, camel complaints, dust, heat, the immensity of the sky
- Show Yusuf's first steps outside his known world; register the psychological shift
- Introduce the *khuwwa* system: Bedouin power vs. the Caliph's road. First lesson that the state's authority has limits
- Plant the caravan hierarchy: *khabir*, guards, merchants, pilgrims, drivers
- Demonstrate Yusuf's capacity for observation (his mother's Bedouin training, unnamed but felt)
- Stealth thesis: "He had thought he was going to Kufa to sell paper. He was beginning to suspect the paper was beside the point."

### Continuity
- The iron-bound chest holds samples of the Caliph's Cream (Abu Said's finest paper). Yusuf checks the leather straps and lock.
- Yusuf's mother referenced as memory ("his mother's face") when he sees the Green Dome from the departing caravan
- *Bukhtit* hybrid camels identified by Sayeed. Yusuf's camel is called One-Eye.
- The Sikbaj meal and the locust are small arcs of adaptation that recur as emotional shorthand for road life
- Sa'dan and Sayeed are introduced here; their farewell happens in Ch 2/3
- Plants for future: the locust familiarity grows (Ch 2), the Green Dome disappears (never seen again until return), the *khabir's* navigation style observed (echoed in Ch 2 route diversion)

### Voice Reminders
- Sensory lead: SMELL dominates (cumin, cardamom, burned sugar, acacia smoke, camel urine, meat in fat). Sound second (camel groans, trumpet, *adhan*).
- Prose rhythm: Long, accretive sentences for the cook-fire scene and the departure. Short, blunt sentences for moments of impact ("This was the other kind." / "He was seventeen years old.").
- Dialogue: Sa'dan is flat and practical. Sayeed is cheerful and confiding. Umar is compressed. No modern idioms.
- The narrator pulls back for two key perspective sentences: the "two kinds of silence" opening, and "He was seventeen years old, and until this morning, he had never slept outside the walls of Baghdad."

### Enhancement Priorities
- Differentiate the pilgrims as a cultural map: give one or two groups a vivid distinguishing detail (prayer style, food, garment) rather than listing origins
- Use the landscape's archaeology: mention a Sassanid ruin or abandoned caravansary as the caravan passes, grounding the world in deep time
- Add a brief exchange with one of the *jammalun* (camel drivers) to show that the lowest-status workers hold the most essential knowledge
