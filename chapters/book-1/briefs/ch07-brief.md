## BRIEF: Chapter 7 — The Tilemaker's Son

**When/Where:** Ali ibn Suleiman's tile workshop and home, near the Khorasan Gate, Baghdad. Late autumn/early winter 787 CE. Yusuf is 17.
**Arriving from:** Chapter 6 — Yusuf has been deepening his workshop skills and commercial understanding. He has noticed Abu Said undercharges for premium paper. The workshop world is intensifying; this chapter pulls Yusuf out of it and back to his father's world.

### Characters Present
- **Yusuf:** Between two worlds now — the paper workshop and his father's tile craft. He visits Ali with new eyes, shaped by the suq experience and Abu Said's teaching. He loves his father but is beginning to outgrow the scale of his father's ambitions. This tension is felt, not spoken.
- **Ali ibn Suleiman:** In his late sixties, still working, still precise. A man of quiet genius whose craft philosophy ("a beautiful thing must also be strong") is the moral foundation Yusuf is building on — and will eventually build away from. He asks about Kufa with genuine interest and offers his perspective on honest failure. He does not lecture; he demonstrates through craft.
- **Omar ibn Ali (via letter):** Present only through a letter from Mosul, where he is stationed. The letter carries news of military life, the northern frontier, and a brother's gruff affection. It is the first direct communication from Omar since the story began, giving the reader a voice for this absent but important character.

### What This Chapter Must Accomplish
- **Plot:** Yusuf visits Ali at the tile workshop. Ali explains glaze chemistry — the parallels and differences between tile-making and paper-making. They eat together. Ali asks about Kufa and offers his perspective on honest failure ("A flaw in the foundation will eventually lead to ruin, but a man who has never found a flaw has never tested his foundations"). A letter from Omar arrives — stationed near Mosul, he mentions the army's pay difficulties and the roads being dangerous. Yusuf on the family rooftop at sunset, looking east through the Khorasan Gate.
- **World-building:** Ali's tile workshop rendered with full sensory depth — a different craft world from Abu Said's paper mill. Glaze chemistry (mineral pigments, kiln temperatures, the difference between turquoise and cobalt). The Khorasan Gate neighbourhood as a community — the evening rhythms, neighbours, the domestic texture of life near the gate. The rooftop view east, toward Khorasan and the wider world.
- **Character:** The father-son relationship at its warmest and most intimate. Ali is not diminished — he is shown as a master of his own domain, deeply knowledgeable, philosophically grounded. But through Yusuf's eyes, the reader should sense the gap opening between the father's contentment and the son's hunger. Backstory surfaces naturally: Layla's death (the pouch of sand, briefly referenced), Khorasani family roots, Ali's philosophy of craft.
- **Thematic:** Craft vs. commerce in its gentlest form — not yet an argument, but two ways of seeing the world coexisting in the same family. Ali's tiles are physical, permanent, visible from the rooftop. Yusuf's emerging world is abstract, portable, invisible. The chapter's stealth thesis: what does a father pass to a son, and what must the son find for himself?

### Continuity
- **From Chapters 1–5:** The Khorasan Gate tiles have been visible in every chapter — from the rooftop at the end of Ch 4, in the final paragraphs of Ch 5. This chapter finally enters the world that makes those tiles. Ali has been referenced (Ch 4 rooftop, Ch 5 closing image) but never appeared on stage. Layla's death (782 CE, five years ago) is part of this household's emotional landscape — her absence should be felt in the domestic space without being belaboured. The pouch of desert sand (established in CLAUDE.md backstory) can be briefly referenced — Yusuf notices it in the house, or Ali mentions Layla in passing.
- **Plants for future:** Omar's letter mentioning dangerous roads and pay problems plants the seed for the military dimension of the story (Omar's career, the army's logistical challenges that suftajas could solve). Ali's glaze philosophy ("a flaw in the foundation") becomes a recurring motif — it is the craft version of Abu Said's later warning. The rooftop view east through the Khorasan Gate is the image that opens the Rayy journey (Ch 9–10).

### Voice Reminders
- **Sensory lead:** Visual — this is the chapter where colour dominates. The turquoise and cobalt of the tiles, the mineral pigments in Ali's workshop, the amber light of sunset on the Khorasan Gate. Smell runs second: kiln smoke, mineral dust, glaze chemicals, the familiar cooking smells of the family home.
- **Prose rhythm:** Slow, reflective, warm. Longer sentences. This is one of the novel's breathing spaces — the pacing should allow the reader to settle into the domestic world before the next journey begins. Short sentences reserved for moments of quiet emotional weight (Layla's absence, the gap between father and son).
- **Dialogue:** Ali speaks slowly, deliberately, in craft metaphors. He does not argue; he observes. Omar's letter voice is blunt, military, affectionate beneath the gruffness. Yusuf is quieter here than in the workshop — in his father's house, he listens more than he speaks.
