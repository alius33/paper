## CHAPTER FOUR

*The Poisoned Vat*

The workshop smelled the same.

That was the thing that stopped him in the doorway — not a thought but a physical fact, the wet sour tang of lime and the earthy reek of soaking rags and the faint sweetness of drying paper in the loft above hitting him all at once, a wall of scent so familiar it made his chest tighten. He had been gone ten days and the air had not noticed. The hammers had not paused. The vats had not asked where he was.

He set the iron chest down in the corner of the courtyard.

The apprentice boys were at work in the soaking pit, stripped to their waists, hauling sodden rags from the lye with long wooden poles. Their skin was slick with it. He knew those boys. He had been one of them, blistered and aching, learning by repetition what his hands refused to learn by instruction. But they seemed smaller to him now, their world bounded by the courtyard walls, and the feeling that came was not pity — it was the quiet vertigo of having seen a larger room and being asked to step back into a smaller one.

Omar was the first to find him. The head journeyman came out of the finishing room wiping his hands on a cloth, his face breaking into one of his rare, brief smiles — a smile that lasted approximately as long as a match flame and gave off about as much warmth.

"The traveller returns," he said. "Still in one piece?"

"Mostly."

Omar looked at the iron chest. Then at Yusuf. He did not ask about the sales. That would come later, from the Ustad, in the Ustad's time. Instead he said, "You're thinner. Did they not feed you on the road?"

"They fed us. It was the walking that ate it all away."

"Good. Soft hands are a poor advertisement for hard work." He jerked his chin toward the kitchen. "Wash up. Eat. The Ustad will see you this afternoon. He is with a customer."

Yusuf washed at the courtyard well, scrubbing the road dust from his hands and arms. The water was cool and familiar, drawn from the same deep channel that fed the paper-making vats. He watched the dust cloud off his skin in brown swirls and disappear into the drain, and he thought of Sa'dan — *keep your hands away from Biter's mouth* — and of Sayeed's rough, quick grip, and of the Hajj column vanishing south in its own dust, and the thought sat inside him like a coin in a pocket: small, heavy, already worn smooth by handling.

He ate a bowl of lentils and bread in the apprentices' kitchen, sitting alone at the long table while the younger boys worked. The food was plain and good. The bread was not as good as the flatbread from the vendor at Kufa's gate, the one that had smelled of char and yeast and had been snatched from his hand before he had finished tearing the first piece. He thought of Tariq's thin fingers leaving grey prints on the surface. He thought of the boy's bones standing out like knots in a reed.

He ate, and the strangeness of being back settled over him like a garment he had outgrown but was being asked to wear again.

Umar — his Umar, the man who had walked beside him for ten days on the Kufa road — had already gone to the Ustad's office. Yusuf had seen him disappear through the inner door without looking back. The teacher would give his report first, privately. Yusuf's turn would come after.

He spent the waiting hours in the drying loft, where the air was warm and still and smelled of clean paper. Sheets hung on horsehair ropes in long, pale rows, like washing on a line. He ran his fingers along one — a standard grade, smooth enough but without the luminous finish of the Caliph's Cream. Serviceable. The kind of paper Khalid ibn Dawud's customers were perfectly content with. The kind that sold because it was known and trusted, because the man who supplied it had supplied it for twelve years and would supply it for twelve more.

He was still in the loft, turning a sheet to the light the way Abu Yaqub the grammarian had turned it — slowly, with attention, as if listening to something in the surface — when a boy came to fetch him.

"The Ustad wants you."

* * *

Abu Said al-Samarqandi's office was a small, clean room at the front of the workshop, separated from the production areas by a heavy curtain and the permanent smell of ink. Yusuf had entered it perhaps a dozen times in four years, and never without a quickening of his pulse — the same feeling he imagined a soldier had when summoned to his commander's tent. The walls were lined with shelves that held not paper but ledgers, heavy cloth-bound books filled with the Ustad's close, precise handwriting. A single latticed window admitted a square of afternoon light that fell across the desk like a judgement.

Abu Said sat behind the desk. He was not a large man, but he had a way of occupying a room that made it feel smaller — some quality of stillness, of compressed attention, that drew the walls in. His beard was neatly trimmed, his hands clean, his eyes the colour of strong tea. Umar sat on a cushion to one side, his expression giving nothing.

"Sit," Abu Said said. He did not say *salaam*. He did not ask about the journey. He said: "How many?"

Yusuf had rehearsed this on the road. He had considered different ways to present the number — framed by context, cushioned by explanation, softened by the story of Abu Yaqub and the promise of future orders. He had rejected all of them. A number did not need a story. A number was its own story.

"Thirty-seven sheets sold. Twenty-four on consignment to a grammar school. The rest to individual scribes and scholars."

Abu Said's face did not change. He looked at Yusuf the way the *sarraf* at the khan had looked at a suspect coin — assessing, weighing, reading the metal through the surface.

"And the price?"

Yusuf named it. It was lower than the workshop's standard rate. Abu Said knew this — Umar would have told him already.

"So. Thirty-seven sheets, at a price below our margin. The caravan cost us forty dirhams. The samples were worth ninety at Baghdad rates. The total return is —" He paused, not because he needed to calculate, but because he wanted Yusuf to.

"Approximately twelve dirhams of profit, after costs. Before accounting for the time and the loss of my labour here."

"Which makes it a loss."

"Yes, Ustad."

Abu Said picked up a reed pen and turned it between his fingers — a habitual gesture, the pen dry, the motion automatic, the way another man might stroke his beard when the thinking required his hands to be doing something. He said: "Tell me about the first stationer. Khalid ibn Dawud."

Yusuf told him. He kept it brief. The admiration for the paper. The refusal based on existing relationships. The sentence Yusuf should not have said — *Because this paper is better* — and the silence that had followed it.

Abu Said nodded. "And what did you learn?"

"That 'better' is not an argument. That a man who has trusted his supplier for twelve years will not change because a boy from Baghdad tells him to."

"Correct. What else?"

The Ustad's eyes were steady on him, waiting. Not for the obvious answer.

"That he was not rejecting the paper. He was rejecting Baghdad."

The pen stopped turning. Abu Said set it down.

"Go on."

"Kufa was promised the khalifate. They raised the black banners, fought the revolution, and when it was won, the capital went to Baghdad instead. The paper from Baghdad is not just paper to them. It is a reminder. A grammarian named Abu Yaqub told me directly — he said the men of Kufa do not forget."

Abu Said leaned back. Something shifted in his face — not a smile, but a loosening, the way a knot gives when you find the right thread.

"Umar," he said, without looking at his journeyman. "What did I say when I sent them?"

Umar's voice came from the side, dry and level: "'Send the boy to Kufa. Kufa will teach him what Baghdad cannot.'"

Yusuf stared. Then, slowly, understanding arrived, and with it a complicated feeling — part relief, part indignation, part the queasy recognition of having been managed without knowing it.

"You expected us to fail."

"I expected you to sell very little paper," Abu Said said. "I did not expect you to fail. Those are not the same thing." He picked up the pen again. "I have been selling paper for twenty years, Yusuf. I could have told you everything you just told me about Kufa in an afternoon. You would have nodded and said *yes, Ustad*, and understood nothing. You had to go there. You had to stand in that man's shop and hear the silence after you spoke."

He opened one of the ledgers on his desk and turned it so Yusuf could see the pages. Dense columns of numbers in the *siyaq* script — compressed, angular symbols that looked more like decoration than arithmetic.

"Can you read this?"

Yusuf looked. He could recognise a few of the symbols — Umar had taught him the basics — but the full pages were impenetrable.

"No, Ustad."

"This is the book of the workshop. Every dirham that comes in, every dirham that goes out. Every sheet of paper sold, every bale of rags purchased, every payment to the canal inspector for water rights, every bribe to the tax assessor —" He caught Yusuf's expression. "Yes. A business that does not account for its bribes is a business that does not understand its own costs." He tapped the page. "This is where the truth lives. Not in the suq, not on the road. Here. In the numbers."

He closed the ledger.

"Your apprenticeship has three phases. The hands, the road, and the numbers. You have done the hands — four years of rags and pulp and burnishing. You have had your first taste of the road. Tomorrow, you begin the numbers."

"The accounting?"

"The accounting. The costs. The contracts. The art of knowing, when a man walks through your door, whether he is a customer or a catastrophe." The pen turned once more, then stopped. "And you will begin by entering the Kufa trip into this ledger. Every cost. Every sale. The caravan fee, the khan, the meals, the twelve dirhams of profit that do not cover the cost of the samples you carried. You will write down, in numbers, the shape of your failure."

"You said it was not a failure."

"It was not. But the numbers do not know that. The numbers say what they say. The wisdom is in knowing what the numbers do not say." He gestured toward the door. "Go. Rest. Tomorrow is the first day of your real education."

* * *

The weeks that followed were a different kind of apprenticeship.

Each morning, before the other boys stirred, Yusuf sat cross-legged in Abu Said's office and struggled with the *siyaq*. The lamp burned low on the desk. The practice sheet — the cheapest stock the workshop produced, the kind sold for wrapping fish at the market — filled with clumsy symbols. The number three looked nothing like what three should look like. The symbol for a thousand could be mistaken for a hundred by anyone who had not spent months learning the difference. He copied and copied and Abu Said watched and said nothing, or said only "Again."

"Why is it so difficult?" Yusuf asked, after a morning spent on a single number that refused to sit on the page the way it was supposed to.

"Because it is meant to be difficult. The *siyaq* exists so that a stolen ledger is useless. A thief who takes this book from my desk will see only marks. He will not know if I am rich or poor, if I owe money or am owed. This script is a wall. On one side are the men who know. On the other side are the men who do not." He tapped the page. "You are crossing from one side to the other."

In the afternoons Yusuf returned to the workshop floor, to the hammers and the vats and the familiar labour of making paper. And it was here, in the space between the morning's numbers and the afternoon's craft, that he began to notice what he should have noticed sooner.

Jabir was watching him.

Not openly. Jabir ibn Malik was not a man who stared. He was a large boy, the son of a cloth merchant in the Karkh district, with thick hands and a face that settled naturally into an expression of mild dissatisfaction, as if the world were a meal that had arrived lukewarm. He had been the Ustad's most promising apprentice before Yusuf entered the workshop. He was still good — his sheets were clean, his burnishing adequate, his knowledge of the processes sound. But "adequate" is not "excellent," and in four years the gap between them had opened like a crack in a wall: slowly, silently, and then impossible to ignore.

The resentment was not in anything Jabir said. It was in his posture — the turned back when Yusuf entered a room, the tightness of his jaw when Abu Said praised a sheet of Yusuf's work. It was in his silence about the Kufa trip. He asked no questions about the road, the sales, the caravan. His silence on the subject was so complete it became its own kind of statement, a refusal to acknowledge that Yusuf had been given something he had not.

Yusuf had been aware of this before Kufa. After Kufa, he could read it. He had spent three days in a city that hid its wounds beneath a surface of politeness, and he had learned — from Abu Yaqub, from Khalid ibn Dawud's unfinished sentence, from a barefoot boy who could summarise a grammar lecture better than the students who were paying for it — that the things people did not say were often more important than the things they did.

And now Yusuf was disappearing into the Ustad's office every morning and coming out with ink on his fingers and a look the other boys could not read. Everyone in the workshop knew what the *siyaq* meant. Learning the numbers meant being brought inside the wall.

Jabir saw this. Jabir understood exactly what it meant.

Yusuf told himself it was not his problem. The numbers occupied him — the ledger was absorbing, a puzzle that rewarded patience. But in the afternoons, working beside Jabir at the burnishing bench, he felt the other boy's silence the way you feel a change in air pressure before a storm. Something was building. Something was accumulating, day by day, grain by grain, in the dark.

Three weeks passed in this way.

Then the smell came.

* * *

It was the third watch of the night. The workshop was dark, the hammers stilled, the fires banked. Yusuf was asleep on his mat in the apprentices' quarters when a hand closed around his shoulder and shook.

"Get up." Omar's voice, low and tight.

Yusuf was awake in an instant. On the road, Sa'dan had taught him to sleep lightly and wake fast — the old caravan master's grip, his instructions delivered in the dark, the understanding that a man who woke slowly in the desert might not wake at all. He had not expected to use the lesson here.

"What is it?"

"Come. Quietly."

Omar led him through the dark workshop. The shapes of the hammers stood against the faint grey of the windows like sleeping animals. The vats dripped. Their footsteps on the packed earth were soft and careful, and Yusuf's body was awake before his mind was — the hairs on his arms prickling, his breath shallow, something in the air that was wrong before he could name what.

Then they reached the sizing room, and he could name it.

The smell hit him like a hand across the face. The sizing solution should smell of cooked wheat — bland, faintly sweet, the smell of a kitchen on baking day. This was different. Gritty. Mineral. The sharp, dry tang of stone dust, of something ground fine and scattered into a place where nothing ground should be.

"Smell it," Omar said.

Yusuf did not need to be told. He bent over the vat and inhaled. Then he plunged his hand into the mixture.

Sand.

Not much. A handful, perhaps two — enough to be invisible in the cloudy starch. But sand in the sizing would embed in the surface of every sheet it touched, a grit so fine you would not feel it when you ran your palm across the page. The flaw would only appear when a scribe put pen to paper and felt the surface drag, the fibres catch and tear, the ink bleeding into the wound. By then the entire batch would be ruined. A week's production. Forty reams of the mid-grade, already promised to the court scribes on an order that Abu Said had spent two years cultivating.

"When did you find it?" Yusuf whispered.

"An hour ago. I came to check the temperature — it has been cooling unevenly." Omar's face, in the lamplight, had the drawn, hard look of a man holding something back. "Sand does not fall into a covered vat, Yusuf. The door was latched."

They looked at each other. The thing between them did not need to be spoken.

"Who?" Yusuf said, though he already knew.

"There are six people in this workshop with a key to this room."

Six. Abu Said. Omar. Yusuf. Jabir. And two junior apprentices who slept like the dead and had no reason to be here at night.

"What do we do?"

"We fix it." Omar was already pulling the drain plug from the base of the vat. The contaminated sizing began to flow, a pale, gritty stream running into the waste channel. "We clean this, cook a fresh batch, and have it ready before the morning call to prayer. Then we tell the Ustad."

"Why not tell him now?"

"Because if the Ustad comes in tomorrow and finds his sizing ruined and his order destroyed, he will be a man responding to a catastrophe. If he comes in and finds the problem solved and the evidence preserved, he will be a man making a judgement." Omar looked at him. "Which do you think produces the better outcome?"

They began.

* * *

Brutal, precise, exhausting work. They drained the vat and scrubbed it with lye, the sharp alkaline smell burning Yusuf's nostrils. They rinsed it three times — Omar insisted on three — running clean water from the channel until the runoff was clear. Then Omar began cooking a new batch from scratch, measuring the wheat starch and alum with the care of an apothecary, his hands steady, his lips moving silently as he counted the proportions.

Yusuf tended the fire. He kept it low and even, feeding it chips of wood to maintain a steady heat. Too hot and the starch would cook unevenly, leaving lumps that would mark the paper. Too cool and it would not thicken. The fire, like everything else in paper-making, was a matter of patience and attention.

They did not speak for a long time. The only sounds were the crackle of the fire, the slow thickening of the starch, and the drip of water from the scrubbed vat drying on the stone floor.

Yusuf thought about Tariq.

It came unbidden — the bread thief from Kufa, the barefoot boy at the edge of the study circle, his lips moving in silent repetition, holding a sheet of the Caliph's Cream like it was the most valuable thing he had ever been given. Which it probably was. Tariq had stolen because the gap between what he had and what he needed was a canyon, and hunger does not wait for a bridge. The theft was simple and defensible and almost honest in its directness. He needed food, he saw food, he took food. The mathematics were immediate and human.

This was not that.

Whoever had poisoned the vat — and Yusuf found he could not yet bring himself to think the name, as if the naming would make it final — had done it not from need but from something else. Something slower and colder. The accumulated bitterness of being adequate in a room that valued excellence. The daily erosion of a position that had once been secure and was now, sheet by sheet, lesson by lesson, morning by morning in the Ustad's office, being taken away.

"Yusuf." Omar's voice. "The fire."

He adjusted it. Too high. He had not been watching.

They worked on in silence. The new sizing thickened in the vat, pale and clean, the faint smell of cooked wheat replacing the mineral wrongness. Outside, the pre-dawn dark was beginning to soften — not yet grey, but the black had lost its weight.

The batch was ready as the first light touched the high windows of the sizing room. Omar tested it — dipping a strip of paper, lifting it out, running his finger across the surface. He held it to the lamp. Felt the edge. Held it again.

"Good," he said. "It will do."

They cleaned the tools, banked the fire, and stood for a moment in the quiet of the room. Omar had set aside a single sheet from the contaminated batch — the sand still gritty in its surface, invisible to the eye, devastating to the pen. Evidence.

"Now we tell him," Omar said.

* * *

Abu Said arrived with the dawn prayer, as he always did. He moved through the production rooms with his usual unhurried precision — checking the vats, inspecting the drying loft, exchanging a word with the apprentices who were already at their posts. He reached the sizing room and stopped.

He stood in the doorway for a long moment. His nostrils moved, once, a small involuntary thing — the way a dog's ears turn toward a sound before the dog itself has decided to listen. Twenty years of paper-making had trained that nose. The fresh batch lacked the faintly stale undertone of the aged solution it had replaced, and Abu Said caught the difference the way a *sarraf* catches a clipped coin.

"The sizing is new," he said. Not a question.

"Yes, Ustad." Omar stepped forward. "We remade it in the night."

"Why?"

Omar told him. Briefly, factually. The smell. The sand. The impossibility of accident. Abu Said listened without expression. When Omar finished, the Ustad walked to the vat and dipped his own finger in the new sizing. He tested a sheet. He held it to the light from the window.

Then he took Omar's evidence — the contaminated sheet — and went to his office and sent for Jabir.

* * *

Yusuf had imagined shouting. Accusations. The theatre of justice, the wronged master and the guilty apprentice. Abu Said would rise from his desk, his voice would carry, the workshop would hear and know and the righteous order of things would reassert itself.

What he witnessed was worse. It was quiet.

Abu Said did not accuse Jabir. He placed the contaminated sheet on the desk and asked Jabir to examine it. Jabir took the sheet, and his hands were steady, and his face showed nothing.

"Feel the surface," Abu Said said.

Jabir ran his finger across the paper. His expression did not change.

"There is sand in the sizing. This sheet, and every sheet in the batch, would have been ruined. The order for the court scribes — forty reams — would have been destroyed. Our reputation with the court would have been damaged." He paused. "Perhaps permanently."

Silence. The kind that has a texture to it — dense and heavy, pressing against the walls.

"I am not going to ask you how the sand got into the vat." Abu Said's voice was level, unhurried, the voice of a man stating facts in the order he has already arranged them. "I am going to tell you what I know. The sizing room was locked. Six people have keys. Omar found the contamination at the third watch. He and Yusuf remade the batch overnight."

He let the silence do its work.

"I know who did this, Jabir. I have known for some time that you were unhappy. I have watched you. I hoped the unhappiness would pass — that you would find contentment in your own work." His voice had not risen. It had not needed to. "You are a capable paper-maker. Your technique is sound. But capability and contentment are not the same thing, and a man who cannot find contentment in good work will eventually destroy the work of others."

Jabir said nothing. His jaw was tight. His hands, which had been steady, were trembling now — a fine tremor, visible only because the room was so still and the light from the window so precise.

Abu Said reached into a drawer and took out a half-finished sheet of paper. Rough, lumpy — a failed experiment. Yusuf understood, with a cold clarity, that the Ustad kept it for exactly this purpose.

"Your work is not worthy of this house," Abu Said said. "Go to your father. Tell him I have taught you all that I can."

Jabir stood. He looked at the sheet in Abu Said's hand — the emblem, the proof, the sentence written in pulp and fibre instead of ink. For a moment Yusuf thought he would speak. Would argue, deny, explain. Would say the thing that everyone in the room knew but that, once spoken, could never be taken back.

The moment passed. Jabir turned and walked out of the office, through the workshop, past the vats and the hammers and the apprentice boys who had stopped their work to watch, and out through the courtyard door.

He did not look at Yusuf. He did not look at anyone.

The door closed behind him. The workshop was silent except for the dripping of the vats.

* * *

That evening, Yusuf climbed to the roof.

The Khorasan Gate was below him, his father's tiles glowing amber in the last of the light. He could pick out the pattern from here — the geometric interlocking that his father had spent a lifetime perfecting, the blues and greens and golds that decorated the arch through which the road to the east began. Beyond the gate, the road stretched into the distance, carrying its traffic of camels and men and the small, persistent hopes of commerce toward the darkening horizon.

The air smelled of cooking fires and river mud and the faint tang of lime from the workshop below.

He had not spoken to anyone since the morning. The workshop had resumed its rhythm — the hammers, the boys, the paper being made — but the rhythm felt different, as if a string had been cut from an instrument. Not a melody lost. A note. Jabir's absence was a silence where a voice had been, and the silence was more noticeable than the voice had ever been, the way you become aware of a sound only after it has stopped.

He did not feel victorious. He felt — what? Not sadness exactly. Something more complicated. The weight of watching a man walk out of a room for the last time, knowing you were part of the reason he was walking.

He thought about Kufa. About Tariq at the edge of the study circle, his lips moving, pressing the sheikh's words into memory because he could not afford the paper to write them on. About Tariq's thin fingers closing around the sheet of the Caliph's Cream — careful, precise folds, the folds of a boy who understood that some things, once creased wrong, could not be uncreased.

Tariq had stolen his bread. Jabir had poisoned a vat.

Both had looked at the distance between what they had and what they wanted, and both had reached.

* * *

Omar found him on the roof as the last light faded.

"Thinking?" The journeyman settled himself against the low parapet wall with the ease of a man who had sat in this exact spot many times before. His legs stretched out, his back against the brick, his face turned toward the darkening sky.

"Yes."

"About Jabir?"

"About what I should have done differently."

Omar said nothing for a while. The evening call to prayer rose from the mosque near the gate, thin and clear, the muezzin's voice carrying across the rooftops like a thread pulled taut between the earth and whatever lay beyond it.

"I will tell you something about this workshop," Omar said. "I have been here since I was twelve years old. Fifteen years. I have seen six apprentices dismissed. Three for incompetence, two for theft, and now Jabir. Every time, the Ustad handles it the same way. Quiet. Direct. No shouting, no public shame. He gives them a failed sheet and tells them to go." He paused. "Do you know why?"

"Because he is merciful?"

"Because he is practical." Omar picked at a loose thread on his sleeve — a small, human gesture that made the words land harder. "A dismissed apprentice who is humiliated becomes an enemy. An enemy talks. He goes to the other workshops and says, 'Abu Said's sizing formula uses this much alum,' or 'his supplier for rags is that man in the Karkh quarter.' But a dismissed apprentice who is treated with dignity goes home to his father and says, 'The Ustad taught me well, but I was not suited to the work.' The father respects this. The father does not become an enemy. The father might even, one day, send another son."

"So it is strategy."

"It is always strategy. But that does not mean it is not also kindness. The two are not enemies, Yusuf. A man can be kind for strategic reasons and still be kind."

The prayer ended. The muezzin's voice faded, leaving the evening sounds of the city to fill the space — the distant murmur of the river, the creak of a cart on the road below, a woman calling a name from a rooftop somewhere to the south.

"You are thinking you should have spoken to him," Omar said. "That you saw it coming and did nothing."

"I did see it coming. I did nothing."

"You are probably right. But you are also seventeen, and it is not your job to manage the feelings of a man who has decided to hate you for being better than him. That is the Ustad's job." He paused. "And he failed at it too. He saw the same thing you saw, and he hoped, as you did, that it would resolve itself."

"It did not."

"No. Some things do not resolve. Some things break."

Below, a dog was barking. The sound carried up to them, sharp and regular, the protest of an animal that had found something it did not understand and could not leave alone.

"In Kufa," Yusuf said, "I met a boy. A thief. He stole my bread the day we arrived. I found him later, at the mosque — sitting outside a study circle, listening. He could not afford to be a student. He knew more grammar than the students inside."

"What did you do?"

"I gave him a sheet of the Caliph's Cream."

Omar said nothing for a moment. His mouth made a shape that was not quite a smile.

"The Ustad would say you gave away product."

"The Ustad was not there."

"No." Another pause. The dog had stopped barking. "You are thinking about the difference."

"Between the boy who stole bread and the man who poisoned a vat."

Omar waited.

"Tariq had nothing. He stole because the gap between what he had and what he needed was too wide to cross any other way. He was hungry and he saw food and he took it. It was desperate, but it was honest — you could understand it, you could almost respect it." Yusuf paused. He was working his way toward something, feeling for its shape the way he had felt for the shape of things at the khan gallery in Kufa — watching the *sarrafs* and the merchants, sensing the pattern without being able to name it. "Jabir had a place in this workshop. A skill. A future. He had everything he needed except the one thing he wanted, and because he could not have it, he tried to destroy it."

"And what does that tell you?"

"That need and want are different things." The words came slowly, each one tested before it was placed. "Need is a wound. It hurts, but it is clean. You can see it, you can tend it. Want is — want is a poison. You can carry it for years without knowing it is killing you."

The stars were appearing. One by one, then in clusters, sharp and clean in the dry Baghdad air. Below, the workshop was dark and quiet. Somewhere in the city, the faint sound of music — a stringed instrument, something low and circling.

"Your mother," Omar said, "told you to watch what people do, not what they say."

Yusuf looked at him. "How do you know that?"

"Because you do it. You have been doing it since you arrived in this workshop four years ago. You watch. You measure. You file things away." Omar stood, slowly, brushing the dust from his trousers with a deliberateness that told Yusuf the conversation was ending and the next words would be the ones that mattered. "Most apprentices learn the craft. You are learning the people. The Ustad sees it. I see it." He paused at the top of the stairs. "Jabir saw it too. And it frightened him. Because a man who watches that carefully will eventually see everything — including the things that others wish to keep hidden."

He started down the stairs. At the third step, he stopped and turned.

"Sleep, Yusuf. The Ustad wants you in the office at dawn. He is going to teach you the full sizing formula — the real one, with the proportions he has never shared with any apprentice." Half a beat. "He says you have earned it."

The door closed. The footsteps faded.

Yusuf sat on the roof for a while longer, alone with the sky and the smell of cooking fires and the distant, patient sound of the Tigris moving through the dark.

Tariq was somewhere in Kufa tonight, with a sheet of the Caliph's Cream folded inside a patched robe. Jabir was somewhere in the city, carrying a failed sheet of paper as the proof of his dismissal. Two boys, each holding a single sheet from the same workshop, the same master's hand.

He did not try to draw a lesson from it. The lesson was not ready. It was forming, the way paper forms — slowly, in water, the fibres finding each other, settling, pressing together into something that could hold weight. It would take time. The understanding would come with the numbers, as Abu Said had said, and the numbers would come with the practice, and the practice would come with the mornings.

Below, the Khorasan Gate stood dark against the sky. His father's tiles were invisible now — the blue and green and gold swallowed by the night. But they were there. They were always there.

He went to bed. Tomorrow, the Ustad would give him the formula.

It was a beginning.
