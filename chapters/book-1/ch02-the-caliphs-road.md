## CHAPTER TWO

*The Caliph's Road*

The first day ended with his legs.

Not all at once. The pain had been building since midday — a deep, hot ache in his inner thighs where the saddle's wooden frame pressed into muscle that had never been asked to grip anything for hours at a stretch. By the time the sun began to drop toward the western horizon, painting the flat brown earth in shades of copper, the ache had become a burning, and the burning had become a thing with its own life, pulsing with every step of the camel beneath him.

When the ram's horn finally sounded — three short blasts, the signal to halt — Yusuf tried to dismount and his legs simply refused. They had locked into the shape of the saddle. He sat there, high above the ground, while around him men slid from their animals with the ease of water pouring from a jug, and he could not move.

Sayeed appeared below, grinning up at him. "Lean forward. Let the camel kneel first."

"I know that."

"Then do it."

Yusuf leaned. The camel, with a groan of theatrical displeasure, folded its front legs and pitched forward. Yusuf's body followed, and for one lurching moment he was certain he would sail over the beast's neck and land face-first in the dust. Then the back legs folded, and the world levelled, and he was close enough to the ground to swing one dead leg over the saddle and slide off.

His knees buckled the instant he took his own weight. He caught himself with one hand on the camel's flank — warm, coarse hair, the smell of animal sweat and dust — and stood there, swaying, while the blood returned to his legs in hot, prickling waves.

Sa'dan walked past without stopping. "First day is the worst. It gets easier." He paused and looked back. "A little."

The khan appeared out of the flat landscape as if the earth had simply decided to produce a building. A squat, rectangular fortress with high walls of mud-brick and a single gate wide enough for two loaded camels to pass abreast. The Caliph's banner hung from a pole above the gate — black, with white script — and below it two guards sat on stools with the particular stillness of men who had been sitting on stools for a very long time.

Inside, a central courtyard opened up, already filling with the controlled chaos of a caravan making camp. Camels were being unloaded along the far wall, their drivers cursing them with a fluency that suggested long practice. The storerooms on the ground level were dark, cave-like spaces into which bales and chests disappeared. Above, a gallery of small rooms ringed the courtyard, each with a wooden door and a single window no larger than a man's head.

"The merchants sleep up there," Sa'dan said, nodding at the gallery. "We sleep down here, with the cargo. The *khabir* sleeps in the gate room, so he can hear if anyone tries to come in or out."

"Where do the pilgrims sleep?"

"Outside the walls. Their own camp. There are too many of them for any khan."

The thought of sleeping outside the walls made the small, dark storeroom suddenly seem inviting. Yusuf helped Umar carry the iron-bound chest of paper samples to their assigned space — a corner near the stables, screened from the animals by a low wall that did nothing for the smell but at least blocked the sight of them. The straw on the ground was old but dry. Umar laid out their sleeping mats without comment.

"Rest," the teacher said. "Eat when the fires are ready. Tomorrow will be the same."

The same. Yusuf lay on his mat and felt every muscle in his body announce its displeasure in turn, from his neck to the arches of his feet. Outside, the sounds of the camp being made were a diminishing roar — shouts becoming murmurs, the clatter of unloading giving way to the quieter sounds of men settling in. Somewhere, a donkey brayed. Somewhere else, the evening *adhan* rose from the courtyard, thin and wavering.

He did not pray. He did not eat. He slept.

* * *

What woke him was the sound of money.

A bright, clear ringing — the sound of silver coins being poured from a height onto a hard surface. It carried through the open doorway of the storeroom and pulled him from a sleep so deep it took him several breaths to remember where he was. The straw. The smell of the stables. The ache in his legs, which had not improved.

He sat up. Through the doorway he could see the courtyard, lit now by oil lamps and the glow of cook fires. Night had fallen while he slept, and the khan had transformed. The working chaos of the evening had given way to something else — a murmur of voices, the clink of cups, the warm, shifting light of flames on mud-brick walls. Men sat in clusters, cross-legged on mats or on the low stone ledges that lined the courtyard. The air smelled of grilled meat and woodsmoke and something sweet, perhaps tea.

The ringing of coins came from a group near the gate. Three merchants were sitting around a low table, and one of them had just emptied a leather purse onto its surface. Silver dirhams caught the lamplight, scattering it in small bright flashes. The man who had poured them was leaning forward, his voice too low for Yusuf to hear, but his hands were active — sorting, stacking, pushing coins toward the man across from him, who was shaking his head.

Yusuf's stomach growled. He stood, wincing, and limped toward the cook fire where a pot of something was still simmering. A boy younger than him, one of the khan's servants, ladled out a bowl of lentils and rice without being asked. Yusuf took it to the edge of the courtyard and sat on the stone ledge, close enough to the three merchants to hear but far enough to be ignored.

He ate slowly and listened.

"...thirty-two dirhams the *mann* in Basra, I'm telling you. Thirty-two. And that was a month ago. By now it could be thirty-five."

"For indigo? That's robbery. I paid twenty-six in Baghdad not ten days ago."

"In Baghdad, yes. In Baghdad you have six suppliers fighting each other for your business. In Basra you have two, and one of them is the governor's cousin. The price is whatever the governor's cousin says it is."

The third man, who had been quiet, stirred his tea. "And in Kufa?"

"Kufa doesn't buy indigo. Kufa buys nothing, if it can avoid buying from Baghdad." This produced a low laugh from the others.

"Kufa buys parchment," the quiet one said. "And ink. And reed pens. The scholars use a river of ink. If you have good ink, real Khorasani ink, not the Egyptian rubbish, you'll sell it in Kufa faster than you can unload it."

The first man leaned forward. "How do you know this?"

"Because I was there four months ago, and I watched a man sell forty jars of lampblack ink in two days. Two days. He could have sold a hundred if he'd had them."

"And the price?"

"Eight dirhams the jar. He bought them in Baghdad for three."

A silence. The men looked at each other. Yusuf could see the calculations moving behind their eyes — not written down, not spoken, but there, as real as the coins on the table. The distance between three dirhams and eight dirhams. The cost of transport. The weight of forty jars on a camel's back. The risk of breakage, of rain, of a buyer who changed his mind.

"Who is your source for this?" the first man asked.

The quiet one smiled. "My source is my own eyes. I don't sell what I haven't seen."

"And how much would you want for an introduction to the ink seller?"

The smile widened. "Ah. Now we are talking about something else entirely."

Yusuf finished his lentils. He was watching the quiet merchant — the way the man sat, the way he held his tea, the way he had waited while the other two argued about indigo before dropping his piece of knowledge into the conversation like a stone into a pond. The man had not shouted. He had not boasted. He had simply known something the others didn't, and that knowledge had changed the shape of the table.

The ink seller's prices. The scholars' appetite. The route, the timing, the margin. None of it was written down. All of it was worth something.

He thought of the paper in the iron chest, the Caliph's Cream, smooth and white and perfect. The Ustad had taught him how it was made — the pulp, the pressing, the burnishing. Umar had taught him what it cost and what they needed to sell it for. But neither of them had taught him *this*: that before you sold anything, before you loaded a single camel, you sat in a courtyard and listened, and the listening was worth more than the goods.

He limped back to the storeroom and lay on his mat. Umar was asleep, or seemed to be, his breathing slow and even. The courtyard sounds filtered in — the murmur of deals being made, prices being traded, information changing hands in the warm, smoky dark.

Yusuf lay awake for a long time, thinking about the distance between three dirhams and eight.

* * *

The second day was easier on the body and harder on the mind.

His legs had loosened overnight. The ache was still there — would be there for days, Sa'dan told him — but it had dulled from a scream to a mutter, and by the time the caravan reformed at dawn he could mount without help. The pride he felt at this was out of all proportion to the achievement. Sa'dan noticed and said nothing, which was Sa'dan's way of saying *well done*.

The landscape had changed, though Yusuf could not have said when. The irrigated green of the Tigris basin was gone. The road ran through a dry, tawny plain that stretched to every horizon without a single feature to break it. No tree. No hill. No building. Just the road, straight as a bowstring, and the pale sky pressing down on the pale earth, and the caravan between them like a thread pulled taut.

The monotony was extraordinary. Yusuf had been warned about it, and still it stunned him. An hour felt like a morning. A morning felt like a lifetime. The camel's gait was a metronome — *rock, sway, rock, sway* — and the sound of hooves and harnesses was a white noise that ate other thoughts the way the sky ate sound in the desert. He tried counting milestones. He lost count. He tried watching the other riders. They were all doing the same thing he was doing, which was nothing.

Sayeed rode up beside him on his small donkey, its legs working twice as fast as the camel's to keep pace. "You have the face," he said.

"What face?"

"The face of a man who has realised the road is boring."

"It's not boring. It's —"

"It's boring," Sayeed said cheerfully. "It's the most boring thing in the world. I've done this road nine times, and every time I think I'll be ready for the boredom, and every time the boredom surprises me. It's bigger than you remember."

"How do you stand it?"

"I sing. But Sa'dan hates my singing, so mostly I think." He paused. "I plan what I'll do when I have my own caravan."

"Your own?"

"Why not? Sa'dan thinks I'm foolish, but someone has to own the camels. Why not me?" He said this lightly, but there was something underneath — not boastfulness, but a quiet, private certainty, the way a man holds a coin he doesn't want to show. "I've been watching the *khabir* since I was fourteen. I know the routes. I know the tribes. I know which merchants pay on time and which ones need a reminder. All I need is capital."

"How much?"

Sayeed laughed. "More than you and I will see in our lifetimes. But a man can dream on the road. The road is good for dreaming."

He reached into his pouch and held out a dried locust. Yusuf took it without hesitation. The taste was familiar now — salt, cumin, the dry crunch. It was, he had to admit, growing on him.

The hours passed. The sun moved. The road went on.

* * *

They stopped at a second khan that evening, smaller than the first, its walls crumbling in places and patched with newer brick that was already beginning to crack. The cistern in the courtyard was low — the water had a greenish tint and a smell that suggested things living in it that shouldn't have been. Sa'dan told the water-carriers to boil everything twice.

Yusuf was helping unload the chest when he saw the riders.

They came from the south, five of them, riding fast on horses that looked too fine for the dusty road. They wore robes dyed a deep indigo, almost black, and carried long lances held upright, the steel tips catching the last of the daylight. They did not slow as they passed the khan. They did not look at the caravan. They rode past in a thunder of hooves and dust and were gone.

"Banu Asad," Sa'dan said, watching them disappear. He had stopped what he was doing — the only time Yusuf had seen him stop in the middle of a task. "Scouts."

"Scouts for what?"

"For their sheikh. They're counting us. How many camels, how many guards, how much cargo. They'll report back, and their sheikh will decide whether to come and collect his *khuwwa* or whether we've already paid the right tribe."

"Have we?"

"The *khabir* paid the Banu Kalb at the last khan. But the Banu Asad are a different tribe with different territory. It depends on where exactly the borders are this season, and the borders move with the grazing." He paused. "The *khabir* knows what he's doing. But those riders weren't smiling, which means the negotiation hasn't happened yet."

He went back to unloading. His hands were steady, his movements unchanged. But Yusuf noticed that Sa'dan had positioned himself so that he could see the southern road, and he did not stop watching it until the light was gone.

That night, the *khabir* doubled the watch.

* * *

On the third morning, Yusuf woke to find Umar already up and sitting in the courtyard with a cup of tea, watching the eastern sky lighten. This was unusual. Umar was a man who slept until he had to wake and rose with purpose, not contemplation. The sight of his teacher sitting still, doing nothing, was strange enough to make Yusuf pause in the doorway.

"Sit," Umar said, without turning.

Yusuf sat. A servant brought him tea — weak, sweet, flavoured with something he didn't recognise. Cardamom, perhaps, but heavier. He drank and waited. Umar was not a man you hurried.

"How far are we?" Yusuf asked, when the silence had stretched long enough.

"Two days. Perhaps a little more, if the road is clear."

"And if it isn't?"

Umar looked at him. "You've been talking to the caravan men."

"Sa'dan says there are riders from the Banu Asad watching us."

"There are always riders watching. That is the nature of this road. The Caliph builds it, the Caliph marks it with his milestones, the Caliph sends his postal riders galloping down it as if speed were proof of sovereignty. But the land on either side belongs to the tribes, and always has, and the tribes take their share of what passes through." He drank his tea. "It is the oldest bargain in the world. Older than the Caliph. Older than the Prophet, peace be upon him. The man with the goods pays the man with the sword. The names change. The arrangement does not."

A pause. The sky was brightening, pale gold along the horizon, the stars fading one by one.

"Ustad," Yusuf said. "Why did the master send us? Why not just let Ibn Zubair sell the paper with the rest of his goods?"

Umar set down his cup. "Because Ibn Zubair sells paper the way he sells everything else — by the bale, at a fixed price, to men who will sell it again at a higher price. The Ustad does not want his finest work sold that way. He wants it placed with the men who will use it. Scholars. Calligraphers. Men who will hold a sheet up to the light and understand what they are holding."

"And those men are in Kufa?"

"Some of them. Kufa has the oldest schools in the empire. Grammar, law, theology. They were teaching there before Baghdad existed." He said this with a weight that made Yusuf look at him. "My own father studied there. Before the war."

"The war?"

"The revolution. The Abbasid revolution. Thirty years ago. The men of Kufa fought for it. They raised the black banners. They bled for the promise that the family of the Prophet would rule." His voice had changed — not louder, but lower, pressed down, the way a man speaks when he is holding something in place. "And when the war was won, the new Caliph built his round city on the Tigris and moved the capital away from Kufa. The scholars stayed. The power left."

He picked up his cup again, though it was empty.

"My father did not fight. He was a student, not a soldier. But he was there. He saw what happened to the city afterward. How the money dried up. How the young men left for Baghdad, chasing the new capital's promise. How the schools that had been the pride of the Islamic world were slowly emptied of their best teachers, because the best teachers followed the money, and the money was in Baghdad."

"Is that why you became a paper-maker? Because your father —"

"My father was a scholar who could not afford to remain a scholar. He came to Baghdad and became a merchant's clerk." Umar's mouth thinned. "A very good clerk. He kept the books for a silk trader for twenty years. When the trader died, the trader's sons let my father go and hired someone cheaper. He died two years later. I was fifteen."

The courtyard was filling with the sounds of the caravan preparing to move — the shouts, the groaning of camels, the clatter of harnesses being checked. But here, in this small pocket of quiet, the sounds felt distant.

"I tell you this," Umar said, "not because I enjoy speaking of it. I tell you because we are going to a city that remembers what it lost. The men we will try to sell paper to are scholars in a city that the empire forgot. They will be proud. They will be suspicious. And if you walk in with the confidence of a boy from Baghdad who thinks new is always better, they will eat you alive."

He stood, brushed the dust from his robe, and looked at Yusuf.

"The Ustad did not send us just to sell paper, boy. He sent us to learn whether we could sell paper in a place that has every reason to refuse it. If we can do that, we can sell it anywhere."

He walked toward the storeroom to collect the chest, and the morning swallowed the rest of whatever he might have said.

* * *

The fourth day, the caravan stopped.

Not gradually, not with the trumpet and the orderly dismounting that marked the regular halts. One moment they were moving, the long column swaying and creaking in its familiar rhythm. The next, the column compressed — a shudder running back through the line the way a shudder runs through a rope when someone jerks the far end. Camels bunched against each other, drivers shouted, and a murmur passed through the ranks, man to man, mouth to ear, gaining urgency as it travelled.

Yusuf stood up in his saddle — his legs had learned to grip by now, at least well enough for that — and looked toward the front of the column. He could see nothing unusual. The same flat, brown plain. The same pale road stretching ahead. The same colourless sky.

Then Sa'dan was there, on his mule, his face set in an expression Yusuf had not seen before. Not fear. Something harder. The expression of a man who is calculating.

"Riders on the eastern ridge," he said. His voice was level. "Eight, maybe ten. They've been paralleling us since the last milestone."

"Banu Asad?"

"Probably. Or someone worse. The *khabir* has sent scouts." He looked at Yusuf. "Stay in the column. Do not ride ahead, do not fall behind. If you hear the horn — three long blasts — dismount, get under the camel, and stay down."

"Under the —"

"The camel is your shield. Its body will stop an arrow. Yours will not." He said this with the same flat practicality with which he discussed the loading of cargo, and then he was gone, riding forward along the column.

Yusuf sat very still on his camel and tried to see the eastern ridge. It was low — barely a ridge at all, more a gentle swell in the earth, the kind of feature you would not have noticed if you were not looking for it. But now that he was looking, he saw them. Small, dark shapes against the pale sky, moving at the same pace as the caravan. Not approaching. Not retreating. Keeping a distance that was close enough to watch and far enough to run.

His mouth had gone dry. Not the dry of thirst — the dry of something else, something that tasted of copper and made his hands want to grip something.

The column had stopped. Ahead, Yusuf could see a cluster of men at the front — the *khabir*, the chief of the guards, and two others he didn't recognise. They were in conversation, heads close together, hands gesturing. One of the men pointed east. The *khabir* shook his head. Another pointed south, tracing a line with his finger. The *khabir* considered this, then nodded.

Time passed. The sun beat down. The camels groaned and shifted. The pilgrims, who had been told nothing, were growing restless — Yusuf could hear their voices rising in complaint, querulous and afraid, and the guards moving among them, saying things that were meant to reassure and did not quite manage it.

Then the *khabir* sent two riders out.

They went east, toward the ridge, moving at a deliberate walk — not fast enough to be threatening, not slow enough to be hesitant. Yusuf watched them cross the open ground between the road and the swell of the ridge. They grew smaller. The riders on the ridge did not move.

"They're going to talk," Umar said. He had come up beside Yusuf, riding his donkey, his face calm but his eyes sharp. "The *khabir* is offering terms. The question is whether the men on the ridge want money or trouble."

"What's the difference?"

"About a hundred dead men. If they want money, this is a toll. We pay it and move on, and the cost is added to the merchants' accounts and passed on to the buyers in Kufa. If they want trouble —" He left the sentence unfinished. He did not need to finish it.

They waited. The sun moved. The two riders reached the base of the ridge and stopped. After a long moment, three of the riders on the ridge descended to meet them. The groups converged. Yusuf could see nothing — they were too far away, and the heat haze made the figures shimmer and waver, as if they were melting.

Sayeed appeared, on foot, leading his donkey. His face was pale under the dust.

"The pilgrims are terrified," he said. "Two of the women are crying. Sa'dan is trying to keep them calm, but —"

"Sa'dan will manage," Umar said.

"What if they attack?"

"They won't attack. There are eight of them and three hundred of us, with thirty armed guards. They are not here to fight. They are here to be paid."

"How do you know?"

"Because if they wanted to fight, they would not have shown themselves on a ridge in full daylight. A man who wants to fight comes in the dark, from behind, and you never see him until it's too late." He looked at Yusuf. "Remember that. The man who shows you his sword wants you to pay him. The man who hides it wants to use it."

They waited. The heat was enormous. Yusuf's waterskin was almost empty, and he did not dare drink.

Then the two riders turned and came back. They rode at the same deliberate walk, but something in their posture had changed — a looseness, a settling. The *khabir* met them at the head of the column. There was a brief exchange. Then the *khabir* raised his hand and signalled the column forward.

But not forward. South-west. Away from the ridge, away from the road they had been following.

"We're changing the route," Umar said, frowning. "The *khabir* is taking us around. Through the lower ground."

"Why? I thought you said they wanted money."

"They did. But perhaps the price was too high. Or perhaps the *khabir* does not trust them to honour the agreement once we're in narrow ground." He looked toward the ridge. The riders were still there, watching. "This will cost us time. Half a day, perhaps more. We'll approach Kufa from the west instead of the south."

"Half a day," Yusuf repeated. It did not sound like much.

"Half a day for three hundred men, a thousand animals, and all the cargo they carry. Half a day of water. Half a day of food. Half a day of wages for the guards, the drivers, the cooks. Half a day of interest on every merchant's investment, because every day on the road is a day the goods are not being sold." Umar looked at him. "Trade is time, boy. And time is the one thing you cannot buy back."

The column turned. The ridge fell behind them. The riders watched for a while, and then, one by one, they turned their horses and disappeared over the far side.

Yusuf rode in silence. He was thinking about the *khabir* — a man who had looked at eight riders on a ridge and calculated not the danger to his body but the danger to his schedule. A man who had weighed the cost of paying against the cost of going around, and chosen the extra half-day because the risk of the narrow ground was not worth the savings in time. A man who thought in distances and dirhams simultaneously, measuring both against the same scale.

The caravan plodded on through the unfamiliar ground, picking its way over terrain that had no road, no milestones, no fire-towers. Just the flat earth and the pale sky, and the *khabir* out front, navigating by the sun.

* * *

That evening, they made camp in the open — no khan, no walls, no cistern. The water-carriers distributed what they had. It was not much. The cook fires were smaller than usual, the food simpler: dried bread soaked in broth, a handful of dates, tea so weak it was barely coloured.

Yusuf sat with Sa'dan and Sayeed near one of the fires. The mood of the camp was subdued. The pilgrims had recovered from their fright but were sullen, and several merchants had been heard complaining loudly to the *khabir* about the diversion. The *khabir* had listened to each one with the same expression — patient, closed, unyielding — and then walked away.

"He made the right decision," Sa'dan said, as if continuing a conversation no one had started. "The Banu Asad wanted too much. Or they wanted something the *khabir* couldn't give — safe conduct all the way to Kufa in exchange for exclusive rights to supply the next caravan. The *khabir* is an al-Muin man. He doesn't sell his future seasons for one safe passage."

"You sound like you know him," Yusuf said.

"I've watched him for three years. He's the best *khabir* on the southern road. He loses fewer men and fewer animals than anyone else, and that's not because he's brave. It's because he's careful." Sa'dan broke a piece of dried bread and dipped it in his broth. "Bravery is cheap. A brave man will fight his way through an ambush and lose half his cargo. A careful man will walk an extra half-day and lose nothing."

"Umar said the same thing. About the cost."

"Your ustad is a smart man." Sa'dan looked at him. "Listen to him. And listen to the road. The road teaches you things that no master can, because the road doesn't care whether you learn or not."

Sayeed, who had been quiet, looked up from his bread. "Sa'dan, do you think they'll follow us?"

"No. We're past their territory now. Tomorrow we'll rejoin the main road south of the old canal. After that, it's two hours to Kufa."

"Two hours," Sayeed said, and grinned. "I'll be glad of walls."

"You said you liked the open sky."

"I like the open sky when no one on the ridge is watching me sleep under it."

Sa'dan's mouth twitched, and for a moment the fire caught his face in a way that made him look younger — closer to the boy he must have been when he first joined the caravan, before the road had weathered him.

"Get some sleep," he said. "Both of you. Tomorrow we deliver our merchants to Kufa, and after that —" He looked south, toward the darkness where the pilgrimage road ran on into the desert. "After that, Sayeed and I have a long walk ahead of us."

It was the first time either of them had acknowledged the parting that was coming. In Kufa, the caravan would split. The merchants would stay. The pilgrims — and their escorts — would continue south toward Najaf and eventually into the Nefud, that vast ocean of sand that lay between Iraq and the holy cities. Sa'dan and Sayeed were going with the pilgrims. Their road and Yusuf's road diverged at the gate of a city he had not yet seen.

Yusuf looked at Sa'dan — his lean, weathered face, his steady hands, the quiet competence that made everything around him feel a little more ordered and a little less frightening. He looked at Sayeed — the grin, the ambition tucked behind it, the pouch of locusts, the dream of his own caravan. He had known them for five days. It felt longer.

"Will I see you again?" he asked.

Sa'dan gave him a long look. "God willing. The road is not so big. The same men walk it, season after season. If you stay in the paper trade, and your master sends you out again, you'll find us."

"And if you eat my locusts without complaint next time," Sayeed added, "I'll even save you the good ones."

Yusuf laughed, and the sound surprised him — how easy it was, how natural, sitting by a fire in the open desert with two men he would not have known a week ago. The stars were enormous overhead, thicker than he had ever seen them, and the fire cracked and spat, and somewhere in the darkness a camel groaned in its sleep.

He did not want to say goodbye. So he didn't. He lay down on his mat and listened to the camp settle around him, and he thought about the road.

Five days. He had learned that his body could endure more than he had asked it to. That information was traded in courtyards the way silk was traded in markets. That the Caliph's road was the Caliph's in name only, and the real price of passage was paid to men on horseback who owned nothing but the right to be in the way. That time was money, and not as a saying but as a fact — that every hour of delay was a cost that someone, somewhere, would pay.

And he had learned, from Umar's careful, compressed words over morning tea, that the city they were approaching was not an empty market waiting to be filled. It was a wound. And you could not sell anything to a wound until you understood what had caused it.

* * *

He woke before the horn.

The sky was still dark, but the eastern edge had begun to lighten — not with colour yet, but with a faint lessening of the black, a suggestion that the night was thinning. He lay on his mat and breathed the cold desert air and felt, for the first time on the journey, something he had not expected.

He did not want the road to end.

The horn sounded. The camp stirred. He rose, rolled his mat, splashed water on his face from the last of his waterskin, and went to help load the camels. His legs ached, but the ache was familiar now, almost welcome, the way the ache of a day's work at the paper vats had become familiar — proof that the body was doing what it was asked.

The caravan reformed. The *khabir's* green banner caught the first of the light. They moved south-west for an hour in the grey pre-dawn, and then the road appeared — the real road, the Caliph's road, wide and pale and marked with milestones, emerging from the left as they descended a gentle slope. The column turned onto it with the relief of water finding a channel.

The last stretch was quiet. Even Sayeed had nothing to say. The camels walked with their heads low, sensing something — a change in the air, perhaps, or the faint smell of a city carried on the morning wind. Irrigated fields appeared on either side of the road, green and startling after days of brown. A canal glinted in the early light. A farmer looked up from his work and watched the caravan pass without expression.

Then Umar, riding beside him, pointed.

A low, brown line on the horizon, wavering in the heat that was already building. Mud-brick walls. No dome. No minaret. Just a city the colour of the earth it sat on, pressing itself flat against the ground.

"Kufa," Umar said.

Yusuf looked at it. Five days ago, he would have been disappointed. He would have expected something grander — something that matched the weight of the name, the pride and the grievance he had heard whispered along the road.

He was not disappointed. He was not anything, yet. He was watching, the way Sa'dan watched — taking in the shape of the walls, the height of the gate, the movement of people at the entrance, the lay of the road ahead. Measuring. Not judging.

The caravan moved toward the city, and Yusuf moved with it, and the road that had taught him so much in five days was about to teach him something else entirely.
