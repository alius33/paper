# Madinat as-Salām, 146 AH (763 CE): A Portrait of the Round City in its First Year

## Introduction: The City of Peace, A World in the Making

In the year 146 AH (763 CE), the city that would become the wonder of the world was not yet a gleaming metropolis of legend. It was a monumental work-in-progress, a landscape of raw potential and relentless creation. Officially named *Madinat as-Salām*, the City of Peace, its reality was one of profound tension between the perfect, geometric vision of its creator, the Caliph al-Mansur, and the chaotic, human reality of its birth. By this year, the great enterprise had reached a pivotal moment. The caliphal Palace of the Golden Gate and the main mosque at the city\'s heart were deemed complete, allowing al-Mansur to officially move his court and residence into the unfinished capital . This act transformed the sprawling construction site on the banks of the Tigris into the functioning heart of the vast Abbasid empire.

Yet, beyond the newly occupied central sanctum, the city was a scene of organized chaos. The air was thick with the fine, pale dust of Mesopotamian earth, a powder that coated everything from the half-finished mud-brick walls of the residential quarters to the timber scaffolding that rose like a skeletal forest against the sky. The sounds were a ceaseless cacophony: the rhythmic clang of hammers on stone, the shouts of foremen in a dozen languages, the groan of barges laden with materials navigating the Tigris, and the roar from the massive kilns firing millions of uniform bricks.^1^ For a child growing up in this environment, like Yusuf ibn Ali, the city was not a historical marvel but a dynamic, evolving home. His world was defined by the sharp contrasts between his father\'s meticulous, colorful craft on a finished gate, the raw materials of the city\'s creation all around him, the martial swagger of his Khorasani half-brothers, and the quiet stories his mother told of an entirely different world---the open, wind-scoured desert.

## Section I: The Blueprint of Power - Architecture and Symbolism

Every wall, gate, and avenue of *Madinat as-Salām* was a deliberate statement of Abbasid power, ideology, and social control. The city was not built to grow organically; it was conceived and imposed upon the landscape as a complete system, a physical manifestation of a new world order.

### The Perfect Circle: A New World Order

The city\'s design was the singular vision of the Caliph al-Mansur. His involvement was absolute, from personally selecting the strategic site on the west bank of the Tigris for its command of both river systems and trade routes , to overseeing the final plans. A legendary account tells of the Caliph having the city\'s outline drawn in ashes on the ground. He then walked its imaginary streets and courtyards before ordering oiled cotton seeds to be spread along the lines and set ablaze, allowing him to see his vision as a whole in flickering firelight before a single brick was laid .

This choice of a perfect circle was a radical and deeply political act. The Umayyads, the dynasty the Abbasids had violently overthrown, ruled from Damascus, an ancient city that had grown over millennia. By creating a new capital *ex nihilo* with a perfect, top-down geometric plan, al-Mansur was visually and symbolically rejecting that past. The design was a direct reflection of traditional Persian Sasanian urban planning, seen in cities like Gur (modern Firouzabad), signaling a decisive shift in the empire\'s cultural and political center of gravity from the Arab-Syrian focus of the Umayyads to a new, Persian-influenced Mesopotamian base.^3^ The very men hired to draw the plans---Naubakht, a former Zoroastrian, and Mashallah ibn Athari, a Persian Jewish astrologer---cemented this connection.^5^ The city\'s form was propaganda written in brick and mortar. It could be read as a cosmogram, with the Caliph at the center of the universe, a physical representation of the Qur\'anic paradise, *Dar al-Salam* (the House of Peace), which was one of the city\'s official names.^1^ For a resident like Yusuf, this meant growing up in an environment where power and order were not abstract concepts; they were physically inscribed into the very landscape of his home.

### The Three Rings of Society: A Fortified Hierarchy

The city was, above all, a fortress, its social hierarchy rendered in concentric layers of defense.

- **The Outer Defenses:** The design began with a deep moat, filled with water from the Tigris, which encircled the entire city.^7^ Rising from the moat\'s inner bank was a massive outer wall, constructed of sun-dried mud brick. It stood approximately 60 feet high and was punctuated by 113 defensive bastions.^2^

- **The Main Wall and the *Fasil*:** Inside this first barrier was a wide, empty expanse known as the *fasil*, or intervallum . This was a deliberately designed killing ground, ensuring any enemy who breached the outer wall would be exposed. Beyond the *fasil* rose the main wall, an even more formidable structure of baked brick reaching a height of 90 feet, with a foundation 105 feet thick.^2^

- **The Residential Annulus:** The space between the main wall and a third, innermost wall formed the residential annulus, the ring designated for habitation. This was where the bulk of the city\'s privileged residents---the victorious Khorasani soldiers, essential craftsmen and their families, merchants, and government clerks---were granted plots of land. This ring was partitioned into four distinct quadrants by the city\'s main avenues .

- **The Inner Sanctum:** The third and final wall cordoned off the vast central area, the exclusive domain of power . This was the administrative and spiritual heart of the caliphate. Its exclusivity was absolute; al-Mansur decreed that no one but himself could enter this central enclosure on horseback, a rule that famously applied even to his frail and gout-ridden uncles, forcing them to dismount.^3^ Within this sanctum stood the Caliph\'s Palace of the Golden Gate, the Great Mosque, residences for his sons, the key government offices (*diwans*), and the barracks for his elite personal horse guard .

### The Four Gates to the World

The four gates piercing the walls were not merely entrances but monumental structures, each a symbolic portal connecting the capital to a key region of the empire.^1^ Named for their destinations, they were Bab al-Kufa (southwest), Bab al-Basra (southeast), Bab al-Sham (northwest, for Damascus), and Bab al-Khorasan (northeast).^8^ Each gate was a complex defensive structure with a bent entrance, a vaulted inner chamber, and a domed audience hall (*majlis*) on its second story, where official business could be conducted . The massive iron doors for these main gates were themselves legendary, reputed to have been crafted by *shaytans* (demons) for King Solomon and later brought by river from the city of Wasit at al-Mansur\'s command .

#### The Bab al-Khorasan: The Gate of the Revolution

Of the four, the Khorasan Gate was paramount. It faced northeast, toward the vast province of Khorasan, the revolutionary heartland that had birthed the Abbasid dynasty and provided the soldiers who brought it to power.^1^ The main caliphal palace, the Khuld Palace, was situated near this gate, and the Great Khorasan Road, the empire\'s vital artery to the east, began here.^5^ The gatehouse itself was a magnificent structure, its upper chamber a favorite resting place for Caliph al-Mansur, from which he could survey his growing capital and the bustling Tigris.^3^ The gate\'s outer iron door was said to be of ancient Egyptian design, brought from Syria .

For the young Yusuf, this gate was more than a public monument; it was a deeply personal landmark. It was here that his father, the master craftsman Ali ibn Suleiman, created his masterpiece of turquoise and lapis lazuli tilework.^10^ In a city still raw and dun-colored with construction, this brilliant facade would have been one of the first finished, beautiful things, a dazzling splash of divine color. The gate was a source of immense family pride, the tangible proof of his father\'s genius and the reason for their place within the city\'s walls. Yet it also marked a point of difference, a reminder that his family\'s status was earned through craft, setting them apart from the world of soldiers and courtiers to which his half-brothers belonged.

## Section II: A Walk Through the Unfinished City (c. 763 CE)

To experience the Round City in its first year is to journey through a series of sharply defined zones, moving from the chaotic periphery of creation to the awe-inspiring, exclusive center of power.

### Outside the Walls: The Chaos of Creation

Beyond the half-finished ramparts lay a vast, temporary city of its own. Here, in sprawling encampments, lived the immense workforce of reportedly 100,000 men---engineers, surveyors, diggers, masons, carpenters, and blacksmiths---drawn from every corner of the Abbasid empire.^1^ The air would be a cacophony of Arabic, Persian, Syriac, and other tongues, mixed with the constant noise of construction. The Tigris river and the network of canals would be choked with barges delivering timber from the north, marble, and reeds for reinforcing the mud-brick walls . The landscape, once the site of a small Aramaic-speaking village on the fertile Mesopotamian plain ^12^, was now transformed into a massive industrial zone. It was scarred by pits where mud was dug for bricks and dominated by the smoke from the kilns where millions of massive, 18-inch square baked bricks were fired .

### Entering the Bab al-Khorasan

Approaching the city, the first sign of order would be the Khorasan Gate, manned by the elite Khorasani soldiers who were the backbone of the Abbasid army. Their armor, their bearing, and the Persian they spoke amongst themselves would set them apart, a physical embodiment of the new dynasty\'s power base---the very world to which Yusuf\'s half-brothers, Omar and Bakr, belonged.^2^ Passing through the gate was a sensory transition. The bent entrance forced a moment of darkness and disorientation before opening into the ordered space of the city proper . For Yusuf, looking up, the sight would be breathtaking: his father\'s brilliant tilework, a patch of perfect, finished beauty against the raw brick and dust. He might hear the murmur of officials conducting business in the audience hall above, their voices a distant echo of the power that shaped his world.^3^

### The Arcades and Avenues

From each of the four gates, a wide, straight avenue ran directly toward the city\'s center. These avenues were designed to be lined with vaulted arcades containing shops and bazaars . In 763 CE, this grand commercial vision was still taking shape. Many arcades would have been a forest of timber scaffolding, while others, recently completed, would be empty shells, their spaces echoing with the footsteps of soldiers and officials. Although the Caliph would later decree that the main markets be moved to the Karkh district outside the walls to maintain order and security within his administrative capital , in this first year, commerce was already asserting itself. Enterprising merchants, keen to serve the new elite, would be setting up temporary stalls, selling essentials like grain and oil, as well as textiles, spices, and other goods for the newly settled households.

### The Residential Quadrants: Planned but Unsettled

Branching off the main avenues, a grid of narrower streets led into the residential quarters.^4^ The layout was a perfect, pre-planned system, a testament to the top-down vision of the city\'s founder.^11^ The houses, primarily made of mud brick, were new and uniform . For a family like Yusuf\'s, their home was a direct reward for his father\'s service to the Caliph.^10^ While the streets were laid out with geometric precision, the social fabric was just beginning to be woven. This created a friction between the rigid plan and the messy reality of human settlement. Neighbors from different parts of the empire---a Khorasani soldier, a Syrian clerk, an Iraqi craftsman---found themselves living side-by-side, forging new connections. People would begin to personalize their identical homes, and informal social networks based on common origin or trade would start to form. The city\'s design notably lacked public gardens or recreational spaces in these quarters; it was a city for governance and habitation, not public leisure . This tension between the imposed order and the organic growth of a real community would have been a defining feature of daily life.

### Approaching the Center: The Aura of Power

The journey inward was a journey into increasing exclusivity. The third, innermost wall was a formidable barrier, its gates manned by the Caliph\'s most trusted personal guard . To pass through was to enter another world entirely. The area immediately surrounding the central palace and mosque was a vast, open plaza, deliberately kept clear of any buildings.^3^ This was not wasted space; it was a tool of power. The great void created a physical and psychological distance between the ruler and the ruled, amplifying the scale, grandeur, and untouchable nature of the structures at the empire\'s heart.

### Glimpsing the Sanctum: The Heart of the Empire

From the edge of the central plaza, an ordinary resident could only gaze in awe. The Caliph\'s residence, the Palace of the Golden Gate, was a colossal structure of 360,000 square feet.^1^ Its most iconic feature, visible from miles around, was the *Qubbat al-Khadra*, the great Green Dome, which soared 120 feet into the air.^1^ Atop it stood the figure of a horseman with a lance, a weathervane that symbolized the Caliph\'s ceaseless vigilance and military might.^3^

Adjacent to the palace stood the Great Mosque. Its construction, with a hypostyle prayer hall and wooden columns supporting a flat roof, showed strong Sasanian architectural influence.^5^ Yet, this symbol of perfection contained a famous flaw: because the palace was built first, the mosque attached to it was slightly misaligned with the *qibla*, the direction of Mecca . This small, humanizing error in the otherwise perfect city served as a quiet reminder of the fallibility inherent in even the most absolute plans. For a boy like Yusuf, this central area was a place of profound exclusion, the source of all authority, yet a world he could only ever glimpse from a distance.

## Section III: The People of the Round City

The architectural shell of *Madinat as-Salām* was brought to life by a diverse and rigidly stratified population. The city\'s circular design was a map of its social hierarchy, with one\'s proximity to the center defining one\'s status and power.

### The Social Tapestry: A Hierarchy in Rings

At the absolute center of the universe was the Caliph al-Mansur and his immediate family. Surrounding them were the powerful Persian Barmakid family, who served as viziers and were instrumental in establishing the sophisticated, centralized Abbasid bureaucracy.^3^

The city was, in its essence, a Khorasani victory prize. The Abbasid revolution had been carried to success on the backs of soldiers from the eastern province of Khorasan, and Baghdad was their reward. The army was dominated by these troops, who were settled in their own cantonments, such as the Harbiyya district.^2^ They enjoyed immense privilege and prestige as the martial foundation of the dynasty. This was the world of Yusuf\'s entitled half-brothers, Omar and Bakr, who saw themselves as the city\'s rightful inheritors.^10^ The new administration was likewise staffed by Persian scribes and officials, who brought Sasanian models of governance to the heart of the Caliphate.^16^

Master craftsmen like Yusuf\'s father, Ali, held a respected but distinct position. They were essential to realizing the Caliph\'s vision and were rewarded with status and housing within the walls.^10^ However, they stood outside the primary circles of military and political power. Crafts were organized into guild-like structures, fostering a strong sense of community and hierarchy from master to artisan to apprentice.^17^ The common saying, \"Industry is a lineage,\" speaks to this powerful professional identity that was passed down through generations.^17^

Beyond these groups, the city was a magnet for a diverse populace. While the elite was Persian-dominated, the general population was a mix of Arabs from various regions (including settled Arabs from the nearby city of Kufa), the local Aramaic-speaking Nabatean farmers, and other Persians who came seeking economic opportunity.^12^ The city was also religiously diverse. It was home to large and active communities of Christians (Nestorians and Jacobites) and Jews, who had their own quarters and learning centers and would play a crucial role in the translation movement that would soon make Baghdad the intellectual capital of the world.^5^

The city\'s very existence created a powerful, symbiotic tension with the world just outside its walls: the desert. A massive, non-agricultural urban center like Baghdad was utterly dependent on the surrounding countryside and the nomadic Bedouin tribes for its survival. These tribes were the primary suppliers of camels for transport, as well as horses, sheep, goats, and leather. They would bring their goods to the burgeoning, informal markets sprouting up outside the city gates. This dynamic created a fascinating interplay of mutual dependence and cultural friction. The Bedouins represented freedom, mobility, and a connection to the caliphate\'s Arab past. The city represented order, confinement, and a Persian-influenced future. Yusuf\'s mother, Layla, a woman of a Bedouin family, embodied this tension.^10^ Her marriage to Ali, the master builder of the city, was a union of these two opposing worlds. Yusuf, their son, was the living synthesis of this divide, caught between the rigid geometry of the city\'s walls and the boundless horizons of the desert stories his mother told him.

## Conclusion: A City of Contradictions

In its first year, Baghdad was a city built on powerful contradictions---the very forces that would shape the life of a boy like Yusuf ibn Ali. It was *Madinat as-Salām*, the City of Peace, that was in fact a formidable fortress designed for absolute control. It was a symbol of a unified Islamic *Ummah* that was rigidly segregated by ethnicity, class, and proximity to power. It was the new heart of a historically Arab caliphate, yet its design, culture, and ruling elite were deeply and deliberately Persian. It was a place of perfect, abstract geometry, yet its daily life was one of organic, chaotic, and vibrant human interaction. For Yusuf, a boy of mixed heritage---the son of a master city-builder and a woman of the desert, the younger half-brother of the privileged Khorasani elite---this city was more than a home. It was the central conflict of his existence, a world of impenetrable walls and distant horizons that he was destined to navigate.
