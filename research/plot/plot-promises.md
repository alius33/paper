The timing for making plot promises depends on whether you are referring to the small, ongoing conflicts (little p plot) or the overarching story goal (big, or capital P plot) \[1, 2\].

Here is a breakdown of when you should make these promises:

\### Little P Plot Promises

Promises regarding the \*\*little p plot\*\*---the smaller, chapter-by-chapter events that keep the reader engaged---should be made frequently:

\* \*\*Every Chapter Generally Has a Promise:\*\* Every chapter in a story generally has one of these promises \[3\].

\* \*\*Chapter Structure:\*\* Oftentimes, the best chapters start with a stealth thesis paragraph that introduces what the chapter is going to be about and what the reader will learn \[3\]. These resolved small problems build toward larger problems in the narrative \[2\].

\### Big (Capital) P Plot Promises

The \*\*big P plot\*\* is the core, overarching conflict of the book, which you can boil down to a sentence or two (e.g., destroy the ring in \*Lord of the Rings\*) \[1\].

While you might make this promise in the opening scene \[4, 5\], you generally do not have to promise the big P plot until near the \*\*end of Act One\*\* \[4\].

\* \*\*End of Act One:\*\* This point is known as \"Plot Point One,\" which separates Act One from Act Two. It marks the moment when \"life can never be the same\" for the character \[6\].

\* \*\*Reason for Delay:\*\* Sometimes the main conflict won\'t come until much later in the structure, perhaps not even until the end of Act Three \[7\].

\* \*\*Pacing in the Opening:\*\* If you delay the capital P plot promise, you should use the opening sections of the story to give promises of tone, character conflict, or a smaller narrative in microcosm (a small snapshot of the larger picture) \[7, 8\]. For instance, you could use the beginning to introduce a metaphor for the main conflict \[7\].

\### Importance of Promises

Promises are crucial because the first chapter or few pages are where a reader decides whether or not to put your book down \[9\]. Once you know the core of your book, rewriting the first few chapters to nail your promise is relatively easy, unlike throwing away 40 chapters in the middle \[10\].
