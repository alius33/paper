## Overview

Oran's study (2009, available via ResearchGate) investigates the institutional structural framework of the Abbasid state, examining how its "advanced, relative to that era, administrative as well as socio-economic institutions" produced notable economic performance in money and finance, internal and external trade, and agriculture -- though not, notably, in industry. The paper is particularly useful for our project because it maps the *machinery* of the Abbasid economy: the diwans that collected taxes, the *muhtasib* who regulated markets, the *Bayt al-Mal* that managed the treasury, and the monetary system that underpinned all commerce. Where Shatzmiller provides the economic indicators, Oran provides the institutional plumbing.

For a novelist, this is the bureaucratic world Yusuf must navigate. Every transaction he conducts exists within a framework of state regulation, taxation, and currency management. When he becomes Chief *Jahbadh* in Book 2, he is stepping into a role defined by these institutions. Understanding them is essential to making his financial manoeuvres feel historically grounded rather than improvised.

## Key Extracts

### The Bayt al-Mal: Structure of the State Treasury

The *Bayt al-Mal* ("House of Wealth") was the central public treasury, founded on the principle that state revenue belonged to the community, not the ruler. Under the Abbasids, it evolved into a sophisticated institution managing multiple revenue streams. Revenue flowed from: *kharaj* (land tax, the primary source), *jizya* (poll tax on non-Muslims), *zakat* (mandatory charitable levy), customs dues, and a levy on cattle, gold, silver, and commercial wares. Under Harun al-Rashid, the treasury reportedly held 900,000,000 dirhams -- an extraordinary sum reflecting the empire's fiscal reach.

Provincial governors collected revenue locally, paid local officials and designated beneficiaries from local income, and remitted only the surplus to the central government in Baghdad. This decentralised model meant that the provinces had significant fiscal autonomy, creating opportunities for both corruption and enterprise.

**Scene potential:** The Bayt al-Mal is the institution Yusuf eventually serves as *jahbadh*. When he manages the Caliph's finances, he is interacting with this system -- tracking provincial remittances, assessing the real versus reported surplus, and understanding that the gap between what a province *owes* and what it *sends* is where power lies. The decentralised structure also explains why financial intermediaries like Yusuf are needed: someone must bridge the gap between distant provinces and the central treasury, and paper instruments (suftajas) are the technology for doing so.

### The Diwan System: Specialised Bureaucratic Departments

The Abbasids inherited and expanded a system of specialised administrative departments (*diwans*), each managing a distinct function of state:

- **Diwan al-Kharaj:** Bureau of land tax collection and revenue assessment -- the most important revenue department.
- **Diwan al-Jaysh:** Military finance department. The army consumed roughly 80% of state expenditure (documented for the province of Sistan).
- **Diwan al-Nafaqat:** Managed pensions and stipends for officials and soldiers.
- **Diwan al-Barid:** Postal and intelligence network -- communications infrastructure across the empire.
- **Diwan al-Rasa'il:** Chancery for official correspondence and decrees.
- **Diwan al-Zimam:** Accountancy department.
- **Diwan al-Istifa':** Department tracking officials' accounts, responsible for detecting embezzlement and arrears.

Accounting followed strict procedures: taxes were paid in instalments and entered into registers as received. Monthly and annual balances were prepared and presented to fiscal authorities. Taxpayers received a certificate of quittance (*bara'a*) upon payment.

**Scene potential:** The diwans are the corridors of power Yusuf learns to navigate. When he becomes *jahbadh*, he is interacting primarily with the Diwan al-Kharaj and the Diwan al-Zimam. The accounting procedures -- registers, instalments, certificates of quittance -- are the documentary infrastructure that makes suftajas possible. When Yusuf introduces paper-based financial instruments, he is building on an existing culture of written records and bureaucratic accountability. The Diwan al-Barid (postal/intelligence service) is also significant: Yusuf's intelligence network in Book 3 parallels the state's own.

### Taxation: Kharaj, Jizya, and the Abu Yusuf Reforms

The *Kitab al-Kharaj* of Abu Yusuf (731-798 CE), written at Harun al-Rashid's personal request, is the foundational text on Abbasid fiscal policy. Abu Yusuf served as the first *Qadi al-Qudat* (Grand Qadi) in Islamic history -- the highest judicial appointment. His key recommendations:

- **Proportional over fixed taxation:** Abu Yusuf advocated charging *kharaj* as a proportion of harvest rather than a fixed sum, arguing that this would encourage cultivation and increase state revenue over time. Proposed rates: 40% from naturally irrigated land; 15-30% from artificially irrigated land, depending on effort.
- **Jizya scaled to wealth:** The poll tax on non-Muslims was graduated: 48 dirhams for the wealthiest (moneychangers), 24 for those of moderate means, 12 for craftsmen and labourers. Payment could be made in kind -- cattle, merchandise, even needles.
- **Fair administration:** Abu Yusuf insisted on appointing "knowledgeable, decent, devout, and fair" tax collectors and centralising tax administration to reduce corruption.

**Scene potential:** Abu Yusuf is a contemporary of Ali ibn Suleiman (Yusuf's father). The *Kitab al-Kharaj* was written around 786 CE -- the year Harun becomes Caliph, when Yusuf is sixteen. The proportional taxation principle is directly relevant to how agricultural profits are calculated in mudaraba partnerships. The graduated jizya rates are socially revealing: a moneychanger (48 dirhams) versus a labourer (12 dirhams) is a 4:1 ratio that maps wealth stratification. When Yusuf deals with Nestorian Christian partners like Ishaq bar Yohannan, the jizya is an ever-present cost of doing business for the non-Muslim partner -- a structural disadvantage that explains why Christians gravitate toward high-value professions (medicine, translation, finance) where the tax burden is proportionally smaller relative to income.

### The Muhtasib: Market Regulation and the Hisba System

The *muhtasib* (market inspector) was the central figure in Abbasid market regulation. The term was first used during the caliphate of al-Mahdi (775-785 CE) -- precisely the years of Yusuf's childhood. The institution expanded under the Abbasids from a simple market oversight role into a comprehensive system of commercial regulation:

**Core duties of the muhtasib:**
- Control of weights and measures -- preventing fraud in scales and volumes.
- Prevention of fraud and forgery in production and sale.
- Monopoly and price control -- intervening when hoarding or collusion distorted markets.
- Prevention of counterfeiting.
- Ensuring street order and cleanliness, preventing traffic obstruction.
- Broader moral oversight of public spaces.

Each major city had only one *muhtasib*, assisted by a deputy (*naib*) and helpers (*a'wan*), who could number more than ten. During the Abbasid dynasty, the *muhtasib* received a salary of approximately 430 dinars -- a substantial income indicating the position's importance.

The Islamic market ideology, as Oran describes it, was "founded on the ideals of economic justice," emphasizing fairness as a moral duty enforceable by the state. Individuals had the right to engage in exchange and earn fair rewards, but when individual self-interest jeopardised public welfare, the state was "obliged to give public welfare precedence." Pricing was generally left to market conditions, but state intervention was permissible when necessary.

**Scene potential:** The *muhtasib* is a critical background figure in every market scene. In Chapter 5, when Yusuf sells paper in the Suq al-Warraqin, the *muhtasib* or his *a'wan* would be present, checking weights, inspecting goods, and ensuring fair dealing. When Jabir sabotages the sizing vat in Chapter 4, this is precisely the kind of fraud the *hisba* system is designed to punish -- and Abu Said's decision to handle it privately (expelling Jabir rather than reporting to the *muhtasib*) reveals something about the workshop's preference for self-governance over state intervention. In Books 2-3, when Yusuf's operations grow, his relationship with the *muhtasib* of Baghdad's financial district becomes politically significant. The salary of 430 dinars also provides a benchmark: this is a very well-paid official, roughly equivalent to a senior merchant's income.

### The Monetary System: Dinar, Dirham, and the Bimetallic Standard

The Abbasid monetary system rested on a bimetallic standard: gold dinars and silver dirhams. The dinar (4.22-4.28 grams of gold) served as the high-value unit; the dirham (variable weight, approximately 2.2-4.3 grams of silver depending on era and region) as the everyday commercial currency. Copper *fals* served for small daily transactions.

Coins were inscribed with simple Kufi script, bearing the names of caliphs, crown princes, ministers, and commanders. The standardisation of weights and measures across the empire reduced transaction costs and facilitated trade integration.

A critical issue: the silver content of the dirham was not always stable, introducing uncertainty into price data and long-distance transactions. This instability is precisely what made financial intermediation valuable -- a skilled *sarraf* could assess the true silver content of dirhams from different mints, and a skilled *jahbadh* could arbitrage the difference.

**Scene potential:** The instability of the dirham's silver content is a gift to the novelist. When Yusuf learns to assess coins -- weighing them, testing their ring, comparing mints -- he is developing a skill that has genuine commercial value. When he later introduces suftajas denominated in dinars (more stable) rather than dirhams (variable), he is solving a real problem for merchants. The inscriptions on coins also serve as a political barometer: when a new caliph's name appears on the coinage, the transition of power is literally being stamped into everyday commerce.

### Trade: Internal and External Networks

The paper documents several key features of Abbasid trade:

**Internal trade:** Considerable quantities of mass-consumption goods -- especially textiles and foodstuffs -- were exchanged between distant provinces. Baghdad's suqs were organised by trade specialisation, with each occupation occupying its own market or street. This specialisation enabled efficient matching of buyers and sellers.

**External trade:** The Abbasid commercial world extended from the Atlantic to the South China Sea. Maritime trade through the Persian Gulf reached India, Southeast Asia, and China. Overland routes connected Baghdad to Central Asia, the Caucasus, and Eastern Europe. Arabic silver dirhams circulated as far as Scandinavia and sub-Saharan Africa.

**State support for trade:** The Abbasids invested in roads, bridges, and irrigation channels that improved trade efficiency. The postal system (*barid*) served dual purposes as communication network and intelligence service, and its routes doubled as commercial arteries.

**Scene potential:** The internal trade routes are the geography of Yusuf's caravan journeys. The specialisation of Baghdad's suqs is the landscape of his daily commercial life. The external trade networks provide the long-distance opportunities that his suftaja system will exploit. The dual-use of the *barid* as postal and intelligence service foreshadows Yusuf's own intelligence network in Book 3 -- he is, in effect, building a private *barid*.

### The Role of State Intervention

Oran's analysis reveals a nuanced position on state economic intervention. Under normal circumstances, prices should "depend on the prevailing market conditions" -- the Abbasid state was broadly market-friendly. However, "when the individual's pursuit of self-interest puts the overall public welfare into jeopardy, the state is obliged to give public welfare precedence over individual self-interests." The state could and did intervene in pricing, regulate monopolies, and enforce fair dealing.

The first recorded loan from money lenders in Islamic history came during the ministry of Ali ibn Issa (912-916 CE) -- after our period, but suggesting that state borrowing from private financiers was a later development. During Harun's reign, the state was a *creditor* (lending to farmers) rather than a borrower, which changes the power dynamic Yusuf navigates.

**Scene potential:** The tension between market freedom and state intervention is politically charged in our story. When Yusuf becomes Chief *Jahbadh*, he straddles both worlds -- he is a private merchant who serves the state, and his power derives from occupying the gap between the two. The Barmakid purge in Book 2 is, among other things, a massive state intervention in the financial system: the seizure of private assets by sovereign authority.

## Facts and Dates

- **775-785 CE:** Caliphate of al-Mahdi; first use of the term *muhtasib* for market inspectors.
- **786 CE:** Abu Yusuf's *Kitab al-Kharaj* completed for Harun al-Rashid; Abu Yusuf appointed first *Qadi al-Qudat*.
- **786-809 CE:** Harun al-Rashid's reign; treasury reportedly holds 900,000,000 dirhams.
- **Kharaj rates (Abu Yusuf's recommendation):** 40% of harvest from naturally irrigated land; 15-30% from artificially irrigated land.
- **Jizya rates:** 48 dirhams (wealthy), 24 dirhams (moderate), 12 dirhams (labourers/craftsmen).
- **Muhtasib salary:** c. 430 dinars per year.
- **Military expenditure:** c. 80% of state spending (documented for Sistan province).
- **Dinar weight:** 4.22-4.28 grams gold. Dirham weight: 2.22-4.28 grams silver (variable).
- **State structure:** 24 provinces, each with fiscal autonomy; surplus remitted to central Bayt al-Mal.
- **Key diwans:** al-Kharaj (land tax), al-Jaysh (military), al-Nafaqat (pensions), al-Barid (post/intelligence), al-Rasa'il (chancery), al-Zimam (accounts), al-Istifa' (audit).

## Relevance to Chapters

**Book 1 (770-791):**
- The *muhtasib* is present in every market scene (Ch. 5, Suq al-Warraqin). His authority shapes how Yusuf can and cannot sell paper.
- Abu Yusuf's *Kitab al-Kharaj* (786 CE) is being written during Yusuf's apprenticeship. The fiscal reforms it recommends form the legal environment in which Yusuf's earliest business decisions occur.
- Taxation structures explain the economics of Ali's tile workshop and Abu Said's paper mill -- both are subject to *kharaj* or commercial levies that constrain margins.
- The diwan system provides the administrative vocabulary for the bureaucracy Yusuf encounters when Abu Said fulfils government commissions.

**Book 2 (791-803):**
- When Yusuf becomes *jahbadh*, he enters the world of the Diwan al-Kharaj and the Bayt al-Mal. The institutional structures described here define his professional environment.
- The graduated jizya system shapes Ishaq bar Yohannan's experience as a Nestorian Christian in the financial system -- a structural tax burden that makes his partnership with Yusuf economically rational (Yusuf provides the Muslim legal standing; Ishaq provides the expertise).
- The muhtasib's anti-monopoly powers are relevant to the Hamid al-Qurtubi rivalry -- Yusuf must destroy his competitor without triggering regulatory intervention.

**Book 3 (803-809):**
- After the Barmakid purge, the treasury's structure becomes politically fraught. The new vizier al-Fadl ibn al-Rabi' inherits fiscal institutions he may not fully understand, creating opportunities and dangers for Yusuf.
- Harun's death and the succession crisis threaten the fiscal architecture: two competing caliphs means two competing claims on the Bayt al-Mal.

**Book 4 (809-814):**
- The civil war disrupts tax collection across the provinces. Trade routes collapse. The diwan system fractures along factional lines. Yusuf's decision to honour obligations to both sides is a financial act conducted against the backdrop of institutional collapse.
