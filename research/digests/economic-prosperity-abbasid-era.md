# Digest: Economic Performance and Economic Growth in the Early Islamic World

**Author:** Maya Shatzmiller
**Source:** `reference/economic-prosperity-abbasid-era.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** Empirical evidence for the Abbasid economic boom (c. 750-1100 CE) -- urbanization rates, wage data, trade volumes, and commodity prices that ground the material world of Yusuf's Baghdad.

---

## Overview

Shatzmiller's study, published in the *Journal of the Economic and Social History of the Orient* (JESHO, Vol. 54, 2011, pp. 132-184), is a landmark empirical challenge to the prevailing institutional-pessimist school that treated Islamic economic institutions as inherently stagnant. Working from numismatic evidence, wage records, price data, and urbanization statistics, Shatzmiller demonstrates that the Abbasid Caliphate experienced sustained, measurable economic growth between roughly 750 and 1100 CE -- precisely the period of our story. The paper is invaluable because it provides *numbers*: dirham wages, grain prices, mint production volumes, and urbanization comparisons that transform the world-building from atmospheric to material.

The core argument is that there was "nothing intrinsic to Islamic institutions that impaired economic growth." On the contrary, the caliphate's fiscal apparatus, legal frameworks for property rights, monetary supply expansion, and commercial infrastructure collectively produced an economy that outperformed both its Roman predecessor and its Byzantine contemporary by most measurable indicators. For a novel about a man who builds a financial empire in this world, Shatzmiller provides the economic weather -- the rising tide that makes Yusuf's ambitions possible.

## Key Extracts

### Urbanization and Baghdad's Scale

The Arab-Islamic world was "much ahead of Western Europe in the first half of the millennium" in terms of urbanization. Baghdad itself, founded in 762 CE at a construction cost of 4,883,000 dirhams employing 100,000 labourers over four years, had grown by the 9th century to cover over 25 square miles with a population approaching half a million -- possibly exceeding one million by some estimates under Harun al-Rashid. This makes it the largest city in the world outside China during Yusuf's lifetime.

**Scene potential:** The sheer density and scale of Baghdad is not backdrop -- it is the engine of Yusuf's commercial education. A city of half a million people generates demand for paper, creates markets deep enough to absorb speculative trades, and produces the anonymity that allows a tilemaker's son to reinvent himself. Every crowd scene should feel this density. Every market description should convey a city that *consumes* on an industrial scale.

### Wages and Living Standards

Urban wages were substantially higher than rural incomes. The average agricultural family of four subsisted on roughly 1,000 kg of grain per year, costing approximately 154 dirhams (about 13 dirhams per month). By contrast, a soldier in the early 9th century earned roughly 60 dirhams per month, and scattered data suggests craftsmen in Baghdad earned comparable amounts. Pamuk and Shatzmiller (2014) found that Middle Easterners in this period had incomes "equal to 2-3 times subsistence level," higher than those in the Babylonian period, the Roman Empire, and the Byzantine Empire at their respective peaks.

**Scene potential:** These numbers anchor the economic stakes of the story. When Yusuf earns his first profit as a paper seller, the reader should feel the gap between 13 dirhams (a farmer's monthly struggle) and 60 dirhams (a soldier's wage). When Abu Said pays his apprentices, the amounts should feel real. When a caravan journey generates 500 dirhams in profit, the reader should feel the magnitude -- roughly three years of a farmer's income. The wage gap between urban and rural life also explains *why* people flood to Baghdad and why the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood hums with ambition.

### Monetary Expansion and Mint Activity

The number of active mints, minting activities, and mint production volumes rose significantly across nearly every region during the first three centuries of Islamic rule. Coins from the 8th and 9th centuries found in hoards -- minted in places as distant as North Africa -- demonstrate wide-ranging monetary circulation. The supply of silver dirhams deposited in Scandinavian and Eastern European hoards increased at an accelerated rate across the 9th century, and by the 10th century, the quantity of dirhams found in hoards increased by 75% over the previous century. Early hoards contained North African dirhams, but from 825 CE onward these were replaced by dirhams struck in Iran, Iraq, and Central Asia.

**Scene potential:** The expanding money supply is the hidden current beneath Yusuf's commercial success. More coins in circulation means more liquidity, more transactions, more demand for financial intermediation. When Yusuf introduces suftajas and mudaraba partnerships, he is not inventing demand -- he is meeting a demand created by an economy bursting at the seams of its coin-based infrastructure. The shift from North African to Iranian/Iraqi minting also maps onto the political geography -- Khorasani power literally stamping itself onto the empire's coinage.

### Commodity Prices and the Cost of Living

Wheat held the largest share in the basic consumption basket, with its cost ranging from 30% of total expenditure in the 8th century (when wheat prices were "exceptionally low") to 60% in the 10th and 11th centuries. Many prices and nominal wages are recorded in silver dirhams, though the silver content of the dirham fluctuated, introducing uncertainty. Researchers have reconstructed price indices using gold dinars as a more stable unit of account, drawing on price data from Baghdad (wheat) and Fustat-Cairo (a broader basket of goods).

**Scene potential:** The low wheat prices of the 8th century -- precisely when our story begins -- mean that food is cheap and disposable income is relatively high. This is the economic condition that makes luxury goods viable, that makes paper a growth industry, that makes a tilemaker prosperous enough to have ambitions for his son. When prices begin rising in later books, Yusuf will feel the squeeze from a different direction.

### Trade Volumes and Market Specialization

Economic growth was driven by multiple reinforcing factors: increased monetary supply, efficient tax collection, legal institutions upholding property rights, demographic growth, increased manufacturing output through division of labour, and expanded trade volumes with efficient markets and commercial techniques. The existence of quarters and markets bearing the names of Sogdian cities in Baghdad confirmed transcontinental trade immediately following the Abbasid takeover in 750 CE. Industrial production was "growing steadily, prices and salaries were rising, the demand for skilled labour was considerable, and the population of urban areas increased more and more."

Rising demand for income-elastic goods helped the emergence of new tastes and expanded urban demand for new products, supporting growing specialization in manufacturing -- food, textiles, ceramics, leather, metal, paper, and other industries. Shatzmiller's compilation of trade names and occupations from the period reveals an economy of extraordinary specialisation.

**Scene potential:** The Sogdian quarters in Baghdad connect directly to Abu Said's Sogdian heritage. The specialization of the economy means the Suq al-Warraqin (paper sellers' market) is historically plausible as a dedicated commercial district. When Yusuf walks through Baghdad's markets, each suq should feel like a distinct world -- the paper sellers, the money-changers, the spice merchants -- because the economy is specialised enough to support that granularity.

### Trade Networks: East and West

Under Harun al-Rashid, maritime trade through the Persian Gulf thrived, with Arab vessels reaching Madagascar, China, Korea, and Japan. Merchants returned with finished goods (ceramics, silk, paper, and ink from China) and raw materials (spices from India, teakwood from Southeast Asia). The markets of Baghdad sold goods from Russia, Africa, China, and Europe. A dedicated section of the East Baghdad suq handled Chinese goods. Paper came from Egypt, Samarkand, and China; ink from China; reed pens from Raqqa; glass from Baghdad and Syria; porcelain from China.

**Scene potential:** The global reach of Baghdad's trade is the canvas for Yusuf's caravan journeys. When he travels to Damascus, Mosul, and Merv, he is moving along arteries of an integrated commercial world. The specific trade goods listed here -- Chinese porcelain, Indian spices, Egyptian papyrus -- are the inventory of the suqs he walks through. The fact that paper already arrives from multiple sources means Yusuf's local production must compete on quality and price, not merely on availability.

## Facts and Dates

- **762-766 CE:** Construction of Baghdad; cost 4,883,000 dirhams; 100,000 labourers employed.
- **c. 750-1100 CE:** Period of sustained economic growth identified by Shatzmiller.
- **9th century:** Baghdad population approaches 500,000-1,000,000. Soldier's wage approximately 60 dirhams/month. Agricultural family subsistence approximately 154 dirhams/year.
- **Income levels:** Middle Easterners earned 2-3x subsistence, exceeding Babylonian, Roman, and Byzantine peaks (Pamuk & Shatzmiller 2014).
- **Dirham hoards:** Quantity increased 75% between the 9th and 10th centuries.
- **825 CE:** Shift in hoard composition from North African to Iranian/Iraqi dirhams.
- **8th-century wheat prices:** "Exceptionally low" -- wheat comprised only 30% of household expenditure (vs. 60% by the 10th century).
- **Urbanization:** The Arab world "much ahead of Western Europe" in the first half of the millennium.

## Relevance to Chapters

**Book 1 (770-791):**
- Chapters covering Baghdad life (Khorasan Gate neighbourhood, paper workshop, Suq al-Warraqin) should reflect an economy in its expansion phase: cheap food, rising wages, growing demand for luxury goods and paper, and a city swelling with migrants and ambition.
- Trade journey chapters (Kufa, Wasit, Rayy, Damascus, Mosul, Merv) map onto documented trade routes. Commodity lists from these cities are historically grounded.
- The silk gamble in Merv gains plausibility from documented transcontinental trade volumes and Sogdian merchant networks.

**Book 2 (791-803):**
- Yusuf's financial innovations (watermark, suftaja system, mudaraba partnerships) emerge naturally from an economy where monetary circulation is expanding faster than the coin supply can keep up -- paper instruments fill the gap.
- The Barmakid connection is economically motivated: the state needs efficient financial intermediation, and Yusuf provides it.

**Books 3-4 (803-814):**
- The Barmakid purge disrupts a mature economic system. The civil war threatens trade routes and fiscal stability. Yusuf's survival depends on the resilience of trust-based financial instruments in a period of political chaos.
