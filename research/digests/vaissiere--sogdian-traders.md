# Digest: Sogdian Traders: A History

**Author:** Etienne de la Vaissiere (trans. James Ward)
**Source:** `reference/Sogdian Traders_ A History (Handbook of Oriental Studies) -- Etienne de la Vaissiere [...] -- Anna's Archive.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** The definitive scholarly history of Sogdian merchant networks from antiquity through the Islamic period. Covers the social structures of Sogdian commercial society, their caravan practices, trade goods, monetary systems, legal frameworks, the Sogdian ethic of hospitality, and -- most critically -- the fate of Sogdian merchants under Islamic rule in the 8th-9th centuries. Directly informs Abu Said al-Samarqandi's character, the Sogdian paper-making tradition, and the broader world of Central Asian merchants in Abbasid Baghdad.

## Overview

De la Vaissiere's central argument is that the Sogdians -- an Iranian-speaking people of the Zarafshan valley centred on Samarkand -- were the dominant overland merchants of Eurasia from roughly the 2nd century BCE to the 10th century CE. Their commercial network stretched from China to Byzantium, from the Upper Volga forests to the Indian Ocean. Unlike other merchant peoples, the Sogdians were simultaneously carriers and traders: they organised and led the caravans themselves rather than relying on intermediaries. Their success rested on a diaspora structure -- permanent communities settled along trade routes, linked by family ties, shared language, and a distinctive commercial ethic.

For this novel, the book provides three categories of essential material. First, it establishes what a Sogdian merchant *was* -- his social status, his commercial practices, the family-based structure of trading enterprises, the goods he dealt in, and the ethic of hospitality and obligation that held the network together. This is Abu Said's inheritance: not just a craft, but an entire civilisational tradition of commerce. Second, it documents what happened to the Sogdian merchant class under Islamic rule -- the disruptions of the Arab conquest, the migration of Sogdian financiers to Merv and eventually Baghdad, and the gradual dissolution of Sogdian identity into the broader Islamic world during the 9th century. Abu Said, arriving in Baghdad in the late 8th century, stands at the hinge of this process: a man from the old Sogdian commercial world operating in the new Islamic one. Third, the book reveals the Sogdian role as *financiers of the Arab armies* -- Sogdian merchants at Merv lent money to Arab generals for military expeditions, imprisoned debtors, and profited from conquest. This detail resonates powerfully with Abu Said's practical, unsentimental character and with the novel's larger theme of commerce entwined with power.

The book is an academic monograph drawing on Chinese, Arabic, Sogdian, and archaeological sources. It is strongest on the pre-Islamic period (Chapters 1-8) and more speculative on the Islamic period (Chapters 9-10), where the documentary record thins dramatically. De la Vaissiere is scrupulous about distinguishing what is proven from what is inferred.

## Key Extracts

### The Sogdian Merchant Class: A Society Built on Commerce (pp. 160-164)

Multiple independent sources -- Chinese, Armenian, and Arabic -- converge on the same portrait of Sogdian society. The Tang annals state that Sogdians "excel at commerce and love profit; as soon as a man reaches the age of twenty, he leaves for the neighbouring kingdoms." The Armenian geographer Ananias of Shirak calls them "wealthy and industrious merchants." When the Abbasid caliph al-Mahdi (r. 775-785) debated with the poet Bashshar about whether Sogdians or Tukharistanians were braver, Bashshar answered bluntly: "the Sogdians are merchants." The caliph did not contradict him.

This merchant identity was not merely an outsider's perception. When the Arab armies captured Sogdian rebels at Khujand in 722, they separated the captives into two groups -- nobles and merchants -- and executed only the nobles. In the Mount Mugh documents (the archives of the last independent Sogdian king), the population of besieged Khujand is enumerated as "noblemen, merchants, and workmen" -- three distinct social orders.

The merchants occupied a high place in Sogdian society but were not identical with the landowning aristocracy (*dihqans*). The Kashkathun clan at Bukhara were described as "not originally dihqans, but of foreign origin" -- yet "a good family, traders, and rich," possessing great "power and dignity." The distinction between merchant and noble families was sometimes thin: at Panjikent, aristocratic houses integrated shops into their exterior walls from the moment of construction, and the sole identified merchant residence has the same architectural grandeur as noble houses. The aristocracy was not purely agrarian; it participated in urban commercial life.

**Scene potential:** Abu Said's social identity -- proud Sogdian, master craftsman, man of commerce but not of the sword -- is grounded in this tradition. When he tells Yusuf that the Sogdians were already trading while the Arabs were herding goats, he is drawing on a real and deep cultural memory. The Kashkathun detail -- traders who were not nobles but commanded enormous respect -- is a direct parallel to Abu Said's position in Baghdad: not an Arab, not a Persian noble, but a man whose craft and commercial acumen place him among the powerful.

### Sogdians as Financiers: Moneylenders at Merv (pp. 273-276)

A passage in al-Tabari's *History* describes an Arab expedition against Bukhara in 699 CE. The Arab general Bukayr prepared his campaign by "going into debt with the Soghdians and their merchants" at Merv. When one of Bukayr's lieutenants, Attab al-Liqwah, borrowed money for the expedition and then failed to join it, the Sogdian creditors had him imprisoned. The general had to pay off the debts to free his own officer.

De la Vaissiere concludes that the Sogdians were the dominant moneylenders in the market of Merv by the late 7th century -- and that they had sufficient power to imprison an Arab military officer for defaulting on a debt. The Sogdian merchants of Merv financed Arab military operations against their own homeland, likely calculating that the conquest would reduce commercial taxes from 10% (the rate for goods from enemy territory) to 5% (the rate for conquered territory).

**Scene potential:** This is the deepest historical resonance for Abu Said's character and for the novel's central tension between commerce and conscience. The Sogdians financed the conquest of their own people because the numbers made sense. Abu Said, who arrived in Baghdad from Samarkand, is the descendant of men who calculated that co-operating with conquerors was more profitable than resisting them. This puts a fine edge on his later relationship with Yusuf: when Abu Said warns Yusuf about the cost of ambition, he speaks from a tradition that has already made that bargain and knows its price.

### The Sogdian Ethic of Hospitality (pp. 190-194)

The 10th-century geographer Ibn Hawqal devoted a long passage to the distinctive commercial hospitality of Transoxiana. Across the region, any person with an estate applied himself to hosting travellers. The competition to provide hospitality was so fierce that it "leads to the disappearance of fortunes and the ruin of properties." Ibn Hawqal personally saw the remains of a Sogdian country house whose entrance had been propped open for over a century -- never once closed to passing travellers. Caravans of one or two hundred people with animals and servants could arrive without warning and find forage, food, and bedding in sufficient quantity.

De la Vaissiere argues that this ethic was pre-Islamic in origin and that it solved a fundamental problem of caravan commerce: the need for reliable shelter along trade routes. In Sogdiana proper, the courtyards of aristocratic castles served as informal caravansaries. The formal Islamic caravansary (the *ribat*) likely emerged in the 9th century when this pre-Islamic Sogdian ethic was married to the new architectural form of the square Muslim fortification. Before the Islamic period, the Sogdians had no dedicated caravansaries -- they did not need them, because the obligation to shelter travellers was woven into the aristocratic code.

**Scene potential:** Abu Said's workshop ethic -- demanding but generous, expecting absolute standards but providing for those in his care -- has roots in this tradition. A Sogdian master was not merely a craftsman; he was the head of a household that functioned by the same rules as the great estates: hospitality to those under your roof, generosity as a display of status, and an unspoken expectation of reciprocal loyalty. When Abu Said feeds, houses, and trains his apprentices, he is enacting a specifically Sogdian form of patronage. When Yusuf eventually breaks with him, he is violating something deeper than a master-apprentice contract.

### Caravan Commerce: Small Groups, Not Armies (pp. 186-190)

The reality of Sogdian commerce was more complex than the classic image of vast camel caravans. Business documents reveal small groups -- at most forty people, often far fewer -- travelling with donkeys, mules, or horses. A Sogdian caravan from Turfan in 732-733 consisted of four people (including a labourer and two slaves) and ten mules. However, literary and diplomatic sources describe caravans of several hundred merchants with hundreds of camels. De la Vaissiere resolves this by noting that merchants travelled in small groups through safe territory but congregated into large caravans for dangerous crossings -- desert stretches, war zones, or territories controlled by hostile nomads.

Pack animals varied by terrain: camels for the desert (old camels could sense approaching sandstorms and gave warning by snarling and burying their mouths in the sand), mules and horses for mountain passes, yaks for the high Indus routes, and sometimes human porters on the cliff-face "suspended paths" of the Hindu Kush.

**Scene potential:** This directly informs the caravan scenes in Book 1. Yusuf's caravans would be small groups most of the time -- four or five men with mules and donkeys -- swelling into larger convoys at dangerous junctions. The detail about old camels sensing sandstorms is precisely the kind of caravan lore that Umar would teach Yusuf, and that Abu Said would expect him to learn on the road.

### Sogdian Products: Trading Everything of Value (pp. 174-176)

The Sogdians did not specialise in a single commodity. They traded "everything that could have value in Inner Asia." The Register of the Customs of Turfan (early 7th century) records Sogdian merchants dealing in gold, silver, brass, ammonia, saffron, silk thread, medicinal plants, "stone honey" (sugar candy), and perfumes. Only silk came from the east; all other goods were western. In China, the Sogdians were known for importing young female musicians, singers, and dancers. They dealt in musk (documented from the 4th century). They traded silver tableware and precious stones (carnelian, rock crystal, lapis lazuli). In the northern forests near the Volga, they exchanged silver dishes for Baltic amber, furs, and slaves.

Silk held a special status because it functioned as money in China. Sogdian merchants benefited from the Chinese state's massive westward shipments of silk to pay soldiers and administrators in the Tarim basin -- this halved the Sogdians' transportation costs, since the Chinese treasury moved the silk halfway for them.

**Scene potential:** Abu Said's knowledge of trade goods -- and his insistence that Yusuf learn to assess the quality of diverse commodities, not just paper -- reflects this Sogdian tradition of omnicompetence. A Sogdian merchant who only knew one product was not a merchant at all. When Yusuf later builds a diversified trading house, he is unconsciously following the Sogdian model Abu Said instilled in him.

### Sogdians in the Islamic World: The 8th-Century Transition (pp. 263-290)

The Arab conquest of Sogdiana (705-715 CE) was followed by decades of instability. Samarkand fell through treachery in 712; the initial tribute was two million dirhams and three thousand slaves. The Sogdian towns were wealthy enough to recover quickly from pillage -- Paykent, destroyed by the Arabs, was rebuilt within a generation because its merchant population had been abroad in China during the sacking and returned with enough capital to ransom their families and reconstruct the city.

Under the Abbasids, a Sogdian military and administrative presence in Baghdad is well documented. The geographer Ya'qubi (writing in 889 CE) describes a suburb of Baghdad near the Syria Gate inhabited by peoples from "Balkh, Merv, Khuttal, Bukhara, Asbashab, Ishtakhandj, Kabul-Shah, Khorezm" -- each regional group with its own military or civil leader. However, the documented Sogdian presence at Baghdad was primarily military, not commercial. After the reign of al-Ma'mun (811-833), Sogdian nobles served as military officers, but were progressively replaced by Turkish slave soldiers.

During the siege of Baghdad in 813, a Samarqandi distinguished himself as a specialist in catapults and siege engines before being captured and crucified by the city's defenders. A Bukharan colleague had served as a catapult specialist during the Abbasid revolution at Merv in 745-746.

**Scene potential:** The Ya'qubi passage describing Central Asian communities in Baghdad, each with its own leader, provides the social world Abu Said inhabits. He is part of a recognisable Sogdian/Central Asian diaspora in the city -- not isolated, but embedded in a community that retains its own internal structures. The catapult specialist at the siege of Baghdad in 813 is a small but resonant detail for Book 4: a man from Samarkand, fighting on the wrong side, crucified. The war does not spare anyone based on origin.

### The 9th-Century Rupture: Sogdian Identity Dissolves (pp. 284-290)

The 9th century was a turning point. The Sogdian military elites who had flourished under al-Ma'mun "seem to have spent their vital energies with their transfer to Baghdad and Samarra." The caliph al-Mu'tasim (833-842) preferred Turkish slaves to Sogdian officers. The nobleman Afshin, the most powerful Sogdian-descended general, was executed (starved to death) after pagan idols and books were found in his palace -- evidence that Islamization was still superficial among the elite. The old Sogdian noble families, with the exception of a few (the Samanids, the rulers of Chaghanyan), declined into obscurity.

De la Vaissiere tracks the formation of new religious elites in Sogdiana through statistical analysis of biographical dictionaries. At Nishapur -- the bottleneck through which all Central Asian scholars passed on pilgrimage to Mecca -- names from Sogdian cities begin appearing significantly only in the 9th century. Samarkandis at Nishapur numbered 6 before 850, then 35 between 850 and 950, then 54 between 950 and 1050. The pattern indicates that Sogdian society's integration into the Islamic scholarly world was a 9th-century phenomenon -- a full century after the political conquest.

The Sogdian language survived as a written medium into the 10th century but was being replaced by Persian and Arabic. Sogdian culture as a distinct entity was absorbed, not destroyed: its commercial ethic, its craftsmanship, its practical wisdom survived in the new Islamic forms of Transoxiana, even as the Sogdian name disappeared.

**Scene potential:** Abu Said, working in Baghdad around 780-790 CE, is a man from the generation just before this dissolution. He still knows what it means to be Sogdian -- the language, the commercial ethic, the craft traditions, the memory of Samarkand before the conquest. His apprentices, a generation later, will not. This gives his character a particular gravity: he is transmitting not just a craft but a dying civilisation's knowledge. His insistence on standards, his compressed silences, his refusal to explain -- these are the behaviours of a man who knows that what he carries cannot be put into words, only into hands.

### The Sogdian Oligarchy: Merchants and City-States (pp. 167-169)

Sogdiana was not unified. Several city-states shared the Zarafshan valley. Within each state, the king was "first among equals." At Samarkand, the record shows two father-son successions, one deposition by the people, and two elections. At Panjikent, there was no father-son succession among three known rulers. The urban community (*naf*) had its own legal personality and could rent out public properties -- the bridge at Panjikent was leased for 150 silver drachms per year.

De la Vaissiere compares the Sogdian political structure to "the Italian mercantile republics of the late Middle Ages." The summit of Sogdian society was occupied by an oligarchy combining landowning nobles (*dihqans*) and great merchant families. At Paykent, the "city of merchants" par excellence, no sovereign is ever named -- the merchants seem to have acted collectively.

**Scene potential:** Abu Said comes from a society where merchants governed cities and kings were elected. His egalitarian bearing -- he treats aristocrats and apprentices with the same compressed attention -- reflects a political culture fundamentally different from the Arab tribal hierarchy or the Persian court. When he sizes up Yusuf, he is applying Sogdian criteria: not birth, not tribe, but capacity.

## Facts and Dates

- **654 CE:** First Arab incursion into Sogdiana (p. 265)
- **699 CE:** Sogdian merchants at Merv finance Arab expedition against Bukhara; imprison Arab officer for debt default (p. 274)
- **706 CE (88 AH):** Paykent captured and sacked by Qutayba b. Muslim; plunder included 50,000-150,000 mithqal (220-660 kg) of precious metal; merchants abroad in China returned and rebuilt the city (pp. 268-270)
- **712 CE:** Samarkand taken by treachery; surrender price: 2 million dirhams plus 3,000 slaves; annual tribute: 200,000 dirhams (p. 271)
- **712 CE:** Conversion rates from Samarkand treaty: 1 slave = 200 dirhams; 1 large garment (brocade) = 100 dirhams; 1 small garment = 60 dirhams; 1 roll of silk = 28 dirhams; 1 mithqal gold (4.4 g) = 20 dirhams (p. 271)
- **Price of silk doubled** between Dunhuang and Samarkand in the 8th century: 14 dirhams to 28 dirhams per roll (p. 271)
- **722 CE:** Sogdian rebels captured at Khujand; nobles executed, merchants spared (p. 161)
- **740s CE:** Panjikent partially rebuilt after 15-year abandonment; proportion of noble-style houses dropped from 42% to 24% (p. 272)
- **760s-770s CE:** Panjikent's noble houses abandoned; site occupied by lower social strata (p. 272)
- **806 CE:** Revolt of Rafi' b. Layth at Samarkand; set all Sogdiana ablaze; quelled under al-Ma'mun from Merv (p. 266)
- **813 CE:** Siege of Baghdad: a Samarqandi catapult specialist fights and is crucified (p. 281)
- **889 CE:** Ya'qubi describes Baghdad suburb near Syria Gate with communities from Balkh, Merv, Bukhara, Khuttal, Khorezm etc., each with its own leader (p. 283)
- **9th century:** Sogdian names at Nishapur: 6 before 850; 35 between 850-950; 54 between 950-1050 -- indicating gradual integration into Islamic scholarly world (p. 286)
- **Sogdian caravans:** Business documents show small groups (4-40 people) with mules and donkeys; literary texts describe large caravans of 200-600 with camels -- both real, for different conditions (pp. 187-188)
- **Sogdian coins:** Intentionally debased; local tokens of account, not for great commerce; Sassanid drachms used for large transactions (pp. 172-174)
- **Sogdian products:** Gold, silver, brass, ammonia, saffron, silk thread, medicinal plants, stone honey (sugar candy), perfumes, musk, silver tableware, precious stones, slaves, female musicians (pp. 174-176)
- **Pack animals:** Camels for desert; mules, horses, donkeys for mountain passes; yaks for Upper Indus; human porters for cliff paths (pp. 188-190)
- **Sogdian word for "inn"** borrowed from Chinese (*tym* < Chinese *dian*), indicating no native institution of commercial hospitality buildings (p. 191)

## Relevance to Chapters

### Book 1: The House of Paper (770-791 CE)

**Apprenticeship (ages 13-21):** The entire portrait of Abu Said -- his Sogdian identity, his compressed attention, his teaching through demonstration and silence, his expectation of hospitality and loyalty -- is grounded in the Sogdian social structures described here. The Sogdian ethic of the master's household as a place of obligation and mutual duty (the caravansary ethic applied to the workshop) directly informs the apprenticeship scenes. Abu Said's parting speech about endurance versus profit echoes the Sogdian tradition of merchants who built for generations, not for a single deal.

**Journey 6: Merv (age 19):** The Sogdian presence at Merv -- as financiers, as military auxiliaries, as a distinct community -- is directly relevant. If Yusuf visits Merv, he may encounter remnants of the Sogdian merchant community that once financed Arab armies. Abu Said's family connections to Merv are plausible.

**Journey to Kufa, Wasit, and other cities (ages 17-19):** The caravan details -- small groups, varied pack animals, pragmatic regrouping at danger points -- directly inform these chapters. The description of Sogdian caravaneers who could sense weather from camel behaviour provides texture for road scenes.

### Book 2: The House of Silver (791-803 CE)

**Founding the House:** Yusuf's commercial model -- a diversified trading house built on trust, family connections, and omnicompetent agents -- is the Sogdian model. The historical pattern of Sogdian merchant families operating across vast distances through settled communities linked by kinship is the template for what Yusuf builds, whether or not he consciously acknowledges it.

**The Barmakid connection:** De la Vaissiere notes that the Barmakids may represent "another example of people from these aristocratic circles of Khurasan who were able to achieve positions of dominance over the conquerors" (p. 276). The Barmakids' Central Asian roots place them in the same broad world as Abu Said.

### Book 4: The House of Wisdom (809-814 CE)

**Siege of Baghdad (812-813):** The Samarqandi catapult specialist crucified during the siege is a vivid detail for the war chapters. Central Asians fighting on both sides of the civil war.

## [RESEARCH NEEDED]

- **Sogdian communities in Baghdad specifically:** De la Vaissiere finds only one text (Ya'qubi, 889) documenting Central Asian communities in Baghdad, and notes the Sogdian presence was primarily military, not commercial. Abu Said's presence as a *commercial* Sogdian in Baghdad is historically plausible but not specifically documented. The novel should not overstate the size of the Sogdian merchant community in Baghdad itself.
- **Sogdian paper-making tradition specifically:** De la Vaissiere does not discuss paper production. The link between Sogdian Samarkand and paper-making is documented elsewhere (Bloom's *Paper before Print*; Hunter's *Papermaking*), but this book does not address it. The Sogdian merchant-craftsman identity described here must be combined with the paper-specific sources for Abu Said's full characterisation.
- **Sogdian women's roles in commerce:** The book mentions women only peripherally (a Sogdian woman in Dunhuang writing letters, female slaves traded). No information on whether women participated in commerce or craft production. Relevant if Abu Said's household is depicted.
- **Religious identity of Sogdians in Baghdad:** The book documents Zoroastrianism, Buddhism, Manichaeism, Nestorianism, and Islam among Sogdians, but does not specify which religion a Sogdian merchant in late 8th-century Baghdad would most likely profess. Abu Said's religious identity needs careful handling. By the 770s-780s, conversion to Islam was accelerating but Afshin's case (executed in 841 for possessing pagan books) shows it remained superficial among some elites.
