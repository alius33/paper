# Digest: Islamic Calligraphy

**Author:** Sheila S. Blair
**Source:** `reference/islamic-calligraphy--blair.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** Comprehensive scholarly treatment of Arabic script, writing materials, and the transformation of calligraphic practice under the Abbasids -- providing scene-ready detail on reed pens, inks, paper preparation, and the revolution in script that accompanied the shift from parchment to paper in Yusuf's lifetime.

## Overview

Blair's *Islamic Calligraphy* (Edinburgh University Press, 2006) is a 680-page survey spanning fourteen centuries, from the origins of Arabic script through modern calligraphic art. The book is organized in six parts: an introduction covering principles of script and materials; the development of Arabic script in early Islamic times; the pre-eminence of round scripts in the early middle period; the emergence of regional styles; dynastic styles in the age of empires; and modern faces. For this project, Parts I and III are the most valuable -- Part I for its detailed treatment of writing materials (Chapter 2: papyrus, parchment, paper, pens, inks, inkwells), and Part III for its analysis of the transformation from angular to cursive scripts that occurred precisely during the decades in which the novel is set (Chapter 5: the adoption of round scripts).

Blair approaches calligraphy as material culture, not merely aesthetic history. She tracks how changes in writing surface -- from papyrus to parchment to paper -- physically altered what scribes could do with a pen, which scripts they favoured, and how bureaucratic and commercial writing practices evolved. This materialist perspective aligns perfectly with the novel's central argument that paper is power: Blair demonstrates that the introduction of paper to the Islamic world did not merely replace an older surface but catalysed a transformation in how knowledge was recorded, government was administered, and culture was transmitted.

The book draws on an extraordinary range of surviving manuscripts, documents, and material evidence, supplemented by Arabic literary sources on calligraphic practice. Blair is particularly strong on the physical archaeology of writing -- how a pen was cut, how ink was mixed, how a sheet was ruled and burnished -- the kind of granular craft detail that translates directly into fiction.

## Key Extracts

### The Importance of Writing in Islamic Culture

Blair opens by situating calligraphy within the broader Islamic reverence for the written word. The Koran itself commands literacy: Sura 96 begins with the instruction to "recite" and Sura 68 opens with "the letter Nun, by the Pen." Verses 17-18 of Chapter 50 mention two recording angels who sit on each person's shoulders recording every action and thought. The importance of writing thus has Koranic sanction at the deepest level. Blair notes that writing decorated buildings and objects across the Islamic world in a way that had no parallel in other civilisations -- inscriptions appeared on architecture, metalwork, ceramics, glass, textiles, coins, and tombstones, giving the Arabic script a public and monumental presence unique among writing systems (pp. 3-6).

The practical consequence was that calligraphy became the supreme Islamic art form. Blair compares its status to that of Chinese and Japanese calligraphy, noting that in both traditions the art was considered supreme and practitioners maintained a similar tension between the form of the message and its content. But in the Islamic world, the script's public and monumental presence -- on buildings, coins, official documents -- gave calligraphers a social importance that went beyond the literary (pp. 6-7).

**Scene potential:** The Koranic framing of writing as sacred reinforces the thematic weight of Yusuf's trade -- papermaking is not merely a craft but participation in one of Islam's most valued activities. A scene where the young Yusuf hears a sermon on the recording angels, then returns to the workshop where he is learning to make the very surface on which such records are kept, could connect the sacred and commercial dimensions of his world.

### The Reed Pen: Cutting, Shaping, and Use

The traditional implement for writing Arabic script is the reed pen, or *qalam*. Blair provides extensive detail on its preparation. Reeds were cut from beds in marshy or wet areas -- the marshes of Iraq and the swamps of Egypt and Fars were renowned for producing firm reeds that did not wear quickly and had straight fibres. The pen was made from a tube of reed cut between two knots. Like most artisans, calligraphers lavished great care on the materials of their art, and the desire for a durable material for the pen led calligraphers in modern times to explore new materials. Calligraphers in the early seventeenth century, for instance, had Java pens sent from overseas. Inverse pilgrims to Mecca introduced pens made from the bast, straight and slender, of a palm tree native to Makassar and Malaysia. Ottoman calligraphers eventually adopted "Java pens" which were extremely durable and had little need of resharpening (pp. 57-58).

The process of cutting a pen was considered half the art. To be used for writing, the fresh reed had to be seasoned. Literary sources provide somewhat generalised descriptions which may be apocryphal. One instruction states that the reed should be left very steep in hemp seed submerged in water. Others report that the reed was buried, traditionally for a period of four years, in horse manure which maintains a constant warm temperature. In both cases the reed hardened and darkened to reddish, light, or dark brown, or even black (p. 59).

The actual nib-cutting was precise: the calligrapher cut the nib by first laying the reed against a small flat slab -- bone, ivory, or horn -- that had a small groove to secure the tip. Using a special sharp knife with a long handle, the calligrapher then slits the point with a sharp transverse cut. This creates a reservoir containing a small store of ink which flows down to the nib as the calligrapher writes. Preparing the nib was considered half the art of calligraphy. The shape and width of the nib determined the character of every stroke (pp. 59-60).

Close examination shows how frequently a calligrapher had to lift his pen to retranscribe a single folio of early Kufic script. In early Koran manuscripts, the scribe wrote words on the page from the so-called *Ansatz* point -- the pen touches the surface and begins the stroke. The variations in density of the ink show that he wrote the previous syllable with the two bottom lines last, then went back to the top. To write large or thick letters, the scribe used extended nib strokes, dipping the pen between strokes to maintain ink supply (pp. 59-60).

**Scene potential:** The cutting of a reed pen is a scene waiting to happen in the workshop chapters. Abu Said demonstrating the precise angle, the importance of the reservoir slit, the feel of a correctly seasoned reed -- this is exactly the kind of craft detail that does narrative work. The pen-cutting vocabulary (*qalam*, the slit, the Ansatz point) gives the scene authentic texture.

### Inks: Carbon and Iron-Gall

Two kinds of dark ink were traditionally used in the Islamic lands: carbon-based (*hibr*) and metal tannin inks (*midaad*). In early Islamic times two different words were used for the two types. *Midaad*, from *madda*, to stretch out (*i.e.* the ink), was used for a paint-type of ink whose most important ingredient is soot or carbon mixed with oil or plant gums. *Hibr*, from *habara*, to write, referred to an ink that reacts chemically with the support. It was typically made from gallnuts (oak galls) and ferrous compounds that produced a dark brown colour. Over time the two terms became interchangeable and confused, but at least through the eleventh century each seems to have maintained its specific meaning of either carbon or metal tannin ink (pp. 61-62).

Carbon ink sits on the surface; it is water-soluble and erasable with water, making it poorly suited to documents that needed permanence on paper, since the ink could smudge. Iron-gall ink, by contrast, bites into the paper fibre and becomes virtually permanent, though it can corrode the support over time. The practical consequence was significant: carbon ink was preferred for Koran manuscripts (where errors could be scraped away and corrected on parchment) while iron-gall ink became increasingly important for official documents and correspondence on paper, where permanence and tamper-resistance mattered (pp. 61-62).

Eventually the two types of carbon and tannin ink were combined. In the developed formula, soot was mixed with gum arabic and gallnut extract, and lampblack was added to iron-gall ink to help stabilise the colour. Most recipes also became elaborate confections -- Salamander and Nightingale recipes were like alchemy, with mislaid and false steps intermingled. The Fatimid calligrapher Qadi Ahmad gives many recipes for preparing ink. The most developed formula involves soot, dissolved gum arabic, and distilled water, mixed and ground together. Ottoman calligraphers were also said to have collected the soot from the oil lamps of a particular mosque. This ink was considered not only high quality but also efficacious in improving one's spiritual blessing (pp. 62-63).

**Scene potential:** The distinction between carbon and iron-gall ink is directly relevant to Yusuf's commercial world. A merchant sending a suftaja across a thousand miles needs ink that cannot be washed away or altered -- iron-gall ink on paper, not carbon ink on parchment. A scene where Yusuf first understands why permanence of ink on paper matters for financial documents would connect the craft to the commerce.

### Inkwells and Pen Cases

The calligrapher stored his ink in an inkwell called *dawat* or sometimes *mihbarah*. Inkwells came in a variety of materials and shapes. Small glass pots were used in early Islamic times, but by medieval times the small glass receptacles for the ink were often encased in cylindrical containers made of glazed ceramic or inlaid wood or metal, and elaborately decorated with figures and inscriptions. These containers could be round, square, or domed, sometimes resembling small buildings. The inkwell was often attached to or incorporated in the pen case, and the casket also often accompanied a drawer. The domed cylindrical type of inkwell came with an attached upright cylinder for the pens. Large rectangular boxes measuring some 30 cm long had separate compartments for different pens, an inkwell, and a sandbox. Grand scholars often had such pen cases with their names and titles inscribed across the lid; they seem to have been official insignia of office. Mu'izz al-Mulk al-Muzaffar, grand vizier of the Khwarazmians, for example, had his lengthy titles inscribed in *nastaliq* around the lid of an inlaid brass example (pp. 63-65).

**Scene potential:** The pen case as insignia of office is a potent detail for the novel. When Yusuf becomes Chief Jahbadh, an ornate pen case -- inscribed, inlaid -- could serve as a visible marker of his new status, connecting the craft of writing to the power of administration.

### Paper's Impact on Writing Practice

Blair's most significant contribution to this project is her analysis of how the introduction of paper transformed calligraphic practice in the Abbasid period. Under the Abbasids in the ninth century, paper replaced papyrus and parchment for record-keeping in the chancery. The shift was rapid: paper was both cheaper and easier to use. Blair notes that it also received the most elaborate preparations, and the development of specially prepared papers designed as supports for fine calligraphy became an art form in itself. The nature of the support affected the type of pen and inks used and therefore the development of different scripts (pp. 41, 45-46).

The key passage concerns the Abbasid chancery. Scribes who penned official letters and correspondence in early Islamic times had used a round script, for it was faster and therefore more efficient than the stately Kufic reserved for monumental inscriptions and Koran manuscripts. Legibility was essential for official correspondence, and so letters were generally penned in words, and important or difficult terms might be marked with dots. Blair notes that early Islamic times also used a round hand to transcribe codices in every subject other than the Koran. The second-oldest dated Arabic manuscript on paper is a treatise on unusual terms in the Traditions of the Prophet, copied at Dhu'l-Qa'da 252 (November-December 866). The text was commissioned by the grammarian Abu'l-Husayn al-Qasim ibn Sallam (d. 224/838). This manuscript exemplifies the cursive book script used in Abbasid times, possibly called *warraq*: the scribe was intent on legibility and therefore carefully pointed letters and spaced words. As opposed to Koran manuscripts, this was functional writing meant to be read, not contemplated (pp. 145-146).

**Scene potential:** The existence of a script called *warraq* -- literally the bookseller/paper-dealer's script -- links the craft of paper directly to the craft of writing. If Abu Said's workshop produces paper and the warraqin of the Suq al-Warraqin write on it in warraq script, the entire chain from raw material to finished book is unified by a single word-root. This is exactly the kind of linguistic connection that the novel can exploit.

### The Shift from Angular to Round Scripts

Chapter 5 details the transformation that occurred in Yusuf's lifetime. Virtually all Koran manuscripts made in the ninth century or earlier were calligraphed in some variant of a rectilinear script. During the tenth and possibly even the eleventh century, copyists continued to transcribe Koran manuscripts in this style. But the most important innovation of the middle period was the refinement of round scripts and their transformation from chancery hands into artistic styles suitable for calligraphing the Koran (p. 143).

Blair identifies a key mechanism: scribes working on paper in early Islamic times apparently continued the practice of those working on parchment and did not rule their manuscripts. The oldest complete dated Arabic manuscript on paper was in fact a Koran manuscript penned in a round book hand corrected in 905 CE. The script shows features characteristic of round book script: little spacing between letter groups, variation in the thickness of strokes, diagonal or curving ascenders, and angular ligatures and looped forms for certain letters (pp. 148-149).

The adoption of round scripts for copying the Koran came about in the context of a larger cultural shift. These Koran manuscripts copied in round scripts show that the late ninth and early tenth centuries were a time of experimentation in transcribing the scripture. They are parchment codices, and their copyists had already begun to adopt paper for non-Koranic texts. In a few instances, these manuscripts are transitional -- they show the features of broken Kufic overlaid on round script characteristics (pp. 149-150).

**Scene potential:** The coexistence of angular Kufic for sacred texts and round cursive for commercial writing creates a visual and social hierarchy of scripts that Yusuf would navigate daily. In the workshop, paper destined for Koran copies might be prepared differently (heavier, more carefully burnished) than paper for correspondence or account books -- a distinction that reveals the sacred-commercial tension at the heart of the novel.

### What Caused the Canonization of Round Scripts?

Blair surveys three explanations for why round scripts rose to dominance. First, Deroche, reflecting his training as a palaeographer, proposed that the answer may have something to do with the type of pen used for the new style of writing -- a differently cut nib could have been a new type of reed pen or a new method of cutting the pen. Both Ibn Muqla and Ibn al-Bawwab commented on the importance of preparing the nib correctly, since the pen determines the handwriting. Second, scholars like Whelan proposed a historical explanation, suggesting that the canonisation of round scripts reflected the new role of the chancery secretary as copyist. When Kufic Koran manuscripts were transcribed by members of the *ulama* who did not sign their work, manuscripts in round scripts included colophons signed by people bearing the epithets *warraq* (bookseller or copyist) or *katib* (scribe/secretary). The adoption of round scripts was thus the triumph of the secretarial class -- scribes who had long used round scripts for secular purposes now claimed the sacred text as well (pp. 173-174, 177).

Third, Tabbaa, as part of his broader argument for the revival of Sunni Islam, suggested that political motivations lay behind these calligraphic innovations. He argued that the Abbasid caliph al-Muqtadir, as vicar of the Abbasid caliphate, adopted the new proportioned script to assert Sunni orthodoxy. Tabbaa argued that the geometric mode, which the calligrapher drew from the Frankish word for a letter, was an emblem of the Abbasid caliphate itself, reflecting Sunni orthodoxy against Shi'a and other rivals (pp. 174-175).

Blair's own view synthesises these positions: the adoption of round scripts for calligraphic and religious texts represents the triumph of the secretarial class. The scribes who had long used round script, paper, and black ink for commercial and administrative purposes now transferred their expertise to sacred and literary texts. This new class of copyists was, if anything, rather lax in its knowledge of the Koran but highly skilled in the physical craft of writing (p. 177).

**Scene potential:** Blair's argument that the rise of round scripts represents the triumph of the secretarial/commercial class over the religious elite is a direct analogue to Yusuf's own rise. The men of the pen -- the *kuttab*, the scribes, the merchants -- displacing the men of the sword and the men of religion as the true administrators of empire. This is the world Yusuf is building into. A conversation between Ishaq (the scribe) and Yusuf (the paper-maker) about the changing nature of who writes and what they write could carry this theme.

## Facts and Dates

- Arabic script has 28 letters; 22 basic shapes, with dots distinguishing otherwise identical forms (p. 8)
- Arabic is written from right to left; the codex (*daftar*) opens in the opposite direction to Western books (p. 7)
- *Qalam*: the Arabic word for pen, from the Greek *kalamos*; also a unit of measurement for letter proportions (p. 7, glossary)
- *Mashq*: a technique of stretching certain letters horizontally, used in early Kufic manuscripts to fill the line; the flat, immovable patience of bureaucratic script (p. 84, glossary)
- Papyrus: used as early as 22 CE in the Islamic lands; papyrus rolls (*tumar*) typically composed twenty sheets; bilingual Greek-Arabic protocols introduced under Umayyad caliph Abd al-Malik (r. 685-705) (pp. 41-43)
- Parchment: made from animal skin (sheep, goat, calf, gazelle); tanned with plant substances; the largest examples are the *Blue Koran*, written in gold on indigo-dyed parchment (p. 44)
- Paper: introduced to the Islamic world c. 751 CE via Samarkand; Arabic word *kaghad* from Sogdian/Chinese; standard Arabic word *waraq* (pp. 45-46)
- The Abbasid vizier Ja'far al-Barmaki reportedly ordered the transition from papyrus to paper in government offices; Harun al-Rashid decreed paper use because writing on it was harder to erase without detection (p. 45, cross-referenced with Gacek digest)
- Oldest complete dated Arabic manuscript on paper: a Koran in round book hand, corrected in Sha'ban 292/June 905 (p. 148)
- Second-oldest dated Arabic manuscript on paper: the *Gharib al-Hadith*, copied at Baghdad in Dhu'l-Qa'da 252/November-December 866 (p. 146)
- Ibn Muqla (272-328/886-940): Abbasid vizier and calligrapher who systematised proportioned script; developed the system of measuring letters by dots and circles (p. 157)
- Ibn al-Bawwab (d. 413/1022): refined Ibn Muqla's system; his Koran (Chester Beatty Library 1431) is the earliest surviving complete Koran in round script (p. 160)
- Reed pens: cut from marsh reeds of Iraq, Egypt, and Fars; seasoned by burial in horse manure or steeping in hemp seed water for up to four years (pp. 57-59)
- Carbon ink (*midaad*): soot or carbon mixed with oil or plant gums; sits on the surface; erasable (p. 61)
- Iron-gall ink (*hibr*): made from gallnuts and ferrous compounds; bites into the paper fibre; permanent but corrosive (p. 61)
- *Warraq*: the cursive book script used in Abbasid times for non-Koranic texts; named after the booksellers/copyists who used it (pp. 145-146, glossary)
- *Dawat*: inkwell, often cylindrical, sometimes incorporated into a pen case; grand versions served as insignia of office (pp. 63-65)
- *Mastara*: a set of strings stretched over a pasteboard frame, used to rule lines on paper by pressing the strings into the sheet (p. 49, glossary)
- The ruling device (*mastara*) became popular only in medieval times; calligraphers working on paper in early Islamic times apparently continued the practices of those working on parchment and did not rule their manuscripts (p. 49)
- *Abriq*: literally "having shine" -- a term for the sheen given to well-burnished paper (glossary)
- *Siyaq*: a cursive accounting script used in the Ottoman and later chanceries; from Arabic *siyaqa* meaning "driving" or "arranging" -- related to the marshalling of financial records (glossary)
- Pen cases: rectangular boxes ~30 cm long with separate compartments for pens, inkwell, and sandbox; official insignia inscribed with owner's titles (pp. 63-65)

The warraqin Yusuf encounters are not merely retailers but practitioners of a writing tradition that is, in Blair's analysis, displacing the old Kufic religious establishment.

Different regions produced different reeds, inks, and papers, giving each city Yusuf visits a distinctive calligraphic character.
- **Book 2, Watermark innovation:** Blair confirms that Arab paper lacked watermarks, reinforcing the fictional innovation. Her analysis of paper's role in displacing parchment provides context for why a superior, authenticated paper product would command a premium.
- **Book 2, Barmakid connection:** Blair's note that Ja'far al-Barmaki ordered the transition from papyrus to paper in government offices directly connects the Barmakids to Yusuf's trade. The Barmakids were not merely patrons but the political force behind the paper revolution.
- **Books 2-3, Financial documents:** The distinction between carbon ink (erasable) and iron-gall ink (permanent) is directly relevant to the security of suftajas and financial instruments. Yusuf would understand that the combination of good paper and permanent ink creates a tamper-resistant document.
- **Books 2-3, Rise of the secretarial class:** Blair's argument that round scripts represent the triumph of the *kuttab* (scribes/secretaries) over the *ulama* parallels Yusuf's own rise as a man of commerce displacing older forms of authority.
- **Book 3, House of Wisdom:** Blair's treatment of the monumental growth in book production under the Abbasids provides context for Yusuf's patronage of translation and scholarship.

## [RESEARCH NEEDED]

- Blair mentions *siyaq* in the glossary as a script developed in Iran by the thirteenth century and used for documents, characterised by numerous ligatures between letters. This conflicts with the novel's placement of *siyaq* in the eighth century (Chapter 4 introduces it). The term may have existed earlier in a proto-form, or the novel may need to adjust its terminology. Cross-reference with Gacek digest and further research on early Abbasid accounting scripts is needed.
- Blair's date for the oldest complete dated Arabic manuscript on paper (905 CE) and the second oldest (866 CE) establishes that very few paper manuscripts survive from the period 750-850 CE. This has implications for what Abu Said's workshop would have been producing in the 780s -- most early paper was likely used for administrative documents and correspondence, not books. The novel should reflect this: paper for government use first, literary/religious use later.
- The *warraq* script name is attested in Blair but the precise dating of when this term came into use for the script (as opposed to the profession) needs further verification. If the term was only applied retrospectively, Abu Said's contemporaries might have called it something else.
