# Digest: The Arabic Book

**Author:** Johannes Pedersen (trans. Geoffrey French; ed. Robert Hillenbrand)
**Source:** `reference/the-arabic-book--pedersen.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** The single most comprehensive study of book production, the book trade, scribal culture, and writing materials in medieval Islam. Directly informs Yusuf's apprenticeship at the paper workshop, his selling in the Suq al-Warraqin (Ch. 5), the economics of the warraq's profession, and the cultural status of paper in Abbasid Baghdad.

---

## Overview

Johannes Pedersen (1883-1977) was a Danish Orientalist of extraordinary range whose scholarship spanned Hebrew, Islamic studies, and comparative religion. First published in Danish in 1946, *The Arabic Book* remained the only comprehensive treatment of book production in the Islamic world for decades. Robert Hillenbrand's introduction to the 1984 English translation notes that the core chapters on composition, transmission, and bookselling have never been superseded.

The book covers ten chapters: pre-Islamic writing, the Qur'an's influence on literary culture, the composition and oral transmission of books, scribes and booksellers, writing materials (papyrus, parchment, paper), Arabic script and calligraphy, book painting, bookbinding, libraries, and printed books. For this project, the most valuable chapters are 3 (Composition and Transmission), 4 (Scribes and Booksellers), 5 (Writing Materials), and 9 (Libraries).

Pedersen's central argument is that the Islamic book was shaped by the deeply oral character of Islamic intellectual culture. Books originated as dictated lectures in mosques, were authenticated through chains of personal authorization (*ijaza*), and were disseminated through a professional class of copyist-booksellers (the *warraqin*) who served as publishers, printers, and retailers in a single person. The shift from papyrus to paper, accomplished during the Abbasid period, was the material revolution that made possible the vast expansion of Islamic literature. This is the world Yusuf enters when he apprentices at Abu Said's workshop and later sells paper in the Suq al-Warraqin.

## Key Extracts

### The Warraq: Copyist, Bookseller, Publisher

The *warraq* (from *waraq/waraqa*, "a sheet") was the central figure of Islamic book production. The term designated someone who made a profession of transcribing -- as a scribe he was also called *nassakh*, "a copyist." Many important scholars carried the description *al-warraq* appended to their names. Ibn al-Nadim, who wrote the *Fihrist* -- the great tenth-century catalogue of Arabic books -- was himself a warraq. The Christian Arab philosopher Yahya ibn Adi (d. 974) made his living as a copyist; he twice transcribed al-Tabari's Qur'anic commentaries, which fill thirteen printed volumes in modern editions (p. 43).

The warraq had his stall or booth (*hanut*, *dukkan*) where copying went on and book business was conducted. Al-Sha'ubi, who lived in Baghdad under Harun al-Rashid and al-Ma'mun, was a copyist at the Bayt al-Hikma who also maintained his own booth where he employed people to transcribe for him. When a rich patron hired him to work in his house, a breach occurred because al-Sha'ubi refused to accept instruction in manners, declaring he had come to execute a commissioned task, not to beg (pp. 49-50). The warraq's sense of professional dignity as an independent businessman is notable.

An independent warraq performed a function that in the modern West is shared between printer, bookseller, and publisher. An author's warraq, having the first copies, was the natural person approached by anyone wanting the work. A warraq of the mid-tenth century described how a poet approached him, saying he had composed a lengthy *qasida* and wanted the warraq to write it in his own hand "so that I may have it published" (p. 50).

**Scene potential:** This is Yusuf's world when he sells paper in the Suq al-Warraqin. The warraqin are not just paper buyers -- they are the literary establishment's infrastructure. Yusuf selling them paper is selling the raw material of knowledge itself. Every sheet he carries will become a copy of the Qur'an, a legal commentary, a collection of poetry, or a merchant's ledger.

### The Suq al-Warraqin in Baghdad

Pedersen provides the key physical description. The booksellers' booths clustered in a particular quarter, as was customary in Near Eastern cities. In Baghdad, the "bazaar of the warraqs" (*suq al-warraqin*) was situated a little outside the Round City, toward the southeast, by one of the canals. There were about a hundred stalls (p. 52, citing Le Strange). The "books' bazaar" (*suq al-kutub*) was situated in the vicinity of the goldsmiths' quarter. In Damascus, Ibn Battuta described the corresponding quarter in 1327 as lying near the Umayyad mosque, with booths selling paper, pens, ink, and other articles associated with books (p. 52).

Literary folk frequented these quarters, sitting in the booths and whiling away the hours with literary gossip and discussion of the day's issues. This milieu forms the setting for many anecdotes in scholarly biographies, affording glimpses of daily life (p. 52).

Like other professions, the "bookmen" were organized in a guild headed by a *shaykh*, who occupied a position of standing. The historian Ibn Iyas records the death of Shams al-Din, the shaykh of the Aleppo booksellers' quarter, as an important event of September 1516, noting that this man belonged to the sultan's social circle (p. 52).

**Scene potential:** This is the setting for Chapter 5 of Book 1. The hundred stalls, the canal nearby, the proximity to the goldsmiths' quarter, the literary gossip, the guild structure with its shaykh -- all of this is direct material. Yusuf's donkey loaded with paper navigates through this world. The warraqin who buy from him are scholars, copyists, publishers, and booksellers all at once.

### How Books Were Published: Dictation in the Mosque

The starting point of Islamic literary activity was the mosque, where teachers sat surrounded by a circle (*halqa*) of students. Publication (*akhraja*, "let go out") was accomplished by reciting a work and having it written down to dictation (*imla*), usually in a mosque. Even poets published their works this way. One minor poet from the beginning of the tenth century described sitting in the principal mosque of Kufa dictating a work while "people wrote it down after me" (p. 25).

The philologist al-Farra (d. ca. 822) was asked to compose a book on Qur'anic interpretation. He announced in Baghdad that he would recite such a work in the mosque, and so the work was produced. His two copyists then withheld the book from the public to make a profit, releasing it only at one dirham for every five pages. When people complained, al-Farra threatened to dictate an expanded version. The copyists yielded and agreed to supply ten pages for one dirham (pp. 44-45).

Dictation was often delivered from memory. Al-Nisaburi (d. 1066) dictated his great Qur'anic commentaries from memory; at his death only four volumes were found in his library. The philologist al-Bawardi (d. 957) dictated 30,000 pages on linguistic topics from memory. Abu Bakr ibn al-Anbari (d. 939) is said to have dictated 45,000 pages of prophetic traditions from memory and claimed he could recite thirteen chests of books by heart (pp. 26-27).

**Scene potential:** The world of dictation and publication is the world Yusuf's paper serves. When he sells a ream of paper, it goes to a warraq who will carry it into the mosque to transcribe a scholar's dictation. Understanding this chain -- paper mill to warraq's booth to mosque circle to finished book -- is essential for grounding the paper trade in lived reality.

### The Authorization System (Ijaza)

The authorization of a book was called *ijaza*, meaning "to make lawful." The author placed his *ijaza* on copies he approved, signifying permission to transmit the work in the approved form. Anyone so empowered could empower others, provided he first assured himself that their transcripts agreed with his own. The guarantee of a copy's genuineness rested upon there being an unbroken chain of *ijazas* going back to the author himself (pp. 31-32).

The collation (*muqabala*) of manuscripts was a necessary stage. Abu Hayyan al-Tauhidi, who grew sick of transcribing in old age, burned his books and cried: "Why should my eye strain itself any more with ink and paper and parchment and reading and collation and correction and draft and fair copy?" (p. 47).

A hundred dinars seems to have been the price for a check-reading of Sibawayh's grammar, the most important work of Arabic linguistics. Al-Mazini refused a Jew's request for such a check-reading at that price, not wanting to allow a non-Muslim to recite his work's many Qur'anic passages (p. 42).

**Scene potential:** The *ijaza* system explains why authenticated copies of important texts commanded premium prices. Yusuf, as a paper seller and eventually a financier, would understand that the value of a book depended not just on its contents but on its chain of authorization -- an exact parallel to the chain of trust underlying a *suftaja*.

### Paper: From China to Baghdad

Paper was first made in China, traditionally dated to 105 CE. The raw materials were mulberry bark, bamboo shoots, rags, fishing line, and hemp fiber. Chinese paper was sold in Samarkand. In July 751 CE, the Arabs defeated Turkish and Chinese forces near Samarkand. Among the prisoners were Chinese papermakers, who introduced the manufacture of paper by the Chinese method. Since the mulberry tree did not grow in the region, recourse was had to linen rags. "As to the Khurasan paper, that is made of linen," says Ibn al-Nadim. Thus rag paper was invented (pp. 60-61).

Under the name "Samarqand paper" or "Khurasan paper," the new writing material gradually spread over the whole Islamic world. During the time of Harun al-Rashid, paper was introduced as an article of use in Iraq by Ja'far ibn Yahya (the Barmakid). Al-Maqrizi records this, and Ibn Khaldun says it took place at the prompting of Ja'far's brother al-Fadl, who was governor of Khurasan in 794-795 (p. 61).

Al-Istakhri, writing in the mid-tenth century, stated that paper was not produced in any Islamic lands save Transoxania. If correct, paper manufacturing remained confined to its first Islamic home for over a century and a half, even though raw materials were easily available everywhere. By the late tenth century, however, al-Maqdisi (c. 985) mentions paper as an export commodity in Damascus and Tiberias, and there may have been papermaking even in distant San'a in Yemen (pp. 63-64).

**Scene potential:** The Barmakid role in introducing paper to Iraq is directly relevant. Abu Said's workshop, established around 794-795, would be among the very first paper mills in Baghdad -- a pioneering enterprise made possible by Barmakid patronage. This strengthens the Barmakid connection that later becomes central to Yusuf's story.

### The Papermaking Process

Paper was made from flax or hemp fibers, not raw but in the form of linen, cordage, and other already-processed materials. Cotton was never used, despite earlier scholarly assumptions. The process: rags or cords were first sorted, then unraveled and softened by combing. They were steeped in lime water, then the pulp was kneaded by hand and bleached in the sun. This was repeated several times. Scissors cut the fibers of the pulp, which was washed in clean water changed daily for a week. The lime having been removed, the hydrated pulp was pounded in a mortar or ground between millstones until comminuted and even (p. 65).

The mold consisted of a frame with a screen stretched across it, like a sieve. Pulp was placed on the screen and smoothed to even thickness, then transferred to a board and placed against a smooth wall to dry. The sheets were rubbed with a mixture of fine meal and wheaten starch, which "filled out" the sheet and whitened it. Sheets were then piled, pressed, and individually polished on a board using agate, onyx, or sometimes glass as a polishing stone. Cockleshells were originally used for polishing (pp. 65-66).

The final stage was sizing (*ilaj*): immersing the paper in or rubbing it with wheaten starch paste to prevent ink from running. Rice water was also employed -- a viscous decoction strained through clean linen. Less often, gum tragacanth was used (p. 66).

Paper could be manufactured in many types. The usual colour was white, but sheets could be dipped in or rubbed with tinted substances: blue from indigo or aloe, yellow from saffron and lemon, red from the dissolved wax of a mealy bug, olive green from blue with saffron, green from saffron with verdigris, violet from blue and red (pp. 66-67).

Ibn al-Nadim gives six named varieties: *al-fir'awni* (the Pharaonic, imitating Egyptian papyrus), *al-sulaymani* (probably named after Harun al-Rashid's comptroller of finance in Khurasan), *al-ja'fari* (after Ja'far ibn Yahya the Barmakid), *al-talhi* (after Talha ibn Tahir, governor of Khurasan 822-828), *al-tahiri* (after Tahir ibn Abdallah, governor 844-862), and *al-nuhi* (after Nuh ibn Nasr of the Samanids, r. 942-954). The varieties, apart from the first, are named after rulers or officials in the province where Islamic paper was produced (p. 67).

Sheets were sold in formats as they came from the frame. The sheet was folded to size. Twenty-five sheets were called in Persian *dast* ("hand"), translated into Arabic as *kaff*. Five "hands" (125 sheets) constituted a *rizma* ("bundle") -- the origin of the English word "ream." For book use, sheets were laid inside one another to form a fascicle (*kurrasa*), usually of eight folded sheets (p. 67).

**Scene potential:** The sizing process -- specifically the *ilaj* involving lime water and starch paste -- is the process sabotaged in Chapter 4 ("The Poisoned Vat"). The named varieties of paper (especially *al-ja'fari*, the Barmakid brand) give Yusuf something to discuss with customers. The ream and fascicle measurements give him the language of his trade.

### The Reed Pen and Writing Equipment

The writing instrument was the reed pen (*qalam*, from the Greek *kalamos*). Reeds grew abundantly along the Nile and in Iraq. Abdallah ibn Tahir, governor of Khurasan in 828-844, complained that good reeds were rare in his region and asked that some be sent from Iraq (p. 69).

The reed had to be hard, neither too thick nor too thin, best when it had the brown colour acquired from lying in water. The reed stem must not be cut at a knot and must not be curved; the length about a hand's width. One end was sharpened to form a sloping plane surface, hollowed slightly but not too deeply. The sides were cut away to form a point, and from the point an incision was made inward along the centre line. The point was cut with a sharp knife (used for nothing else) in a downward direction onto a piece of bone or similar material (*miqatta*) (p. 69).

The scribe kept equipment in a receptacle consisting of a box with an inkwell (*dawa*) at one end. These writing sets were often beautifully decorated -- copper with inlaid patterns, or cardboard embellished with paintings in Persia. They were portable, carried in the waistband. Yaqut mentions a penman of the twelfth century whose writing set was sold for 900 dinars (p. 70).

The class of scribes and officials was called "The People of the Pen," contrasted with the military caste, "The People of the Sword" (p. 70).

**Scene potential:** The reed pen, its preparation, and the writing set are objects Yusuf handles and sells alongside paper. When he enters the Suq al-Warraqin, these are the tools of the trade on display in every booth -- the pens, the inkstands, the bone cutting blocks. The ink recipe (vitriol and gall-nuts, sometimes with ashes of burnt papyri as carbon) is part of the sensory world of the market.

### Book Auctions and Prices

Book sales were conducted like all trade: buyer and seller bargained. In an age when every book was produced individually, fixed prices were unknown. A fine copy of the "Book of Songs" was procured for 10,000 dirhams. The purchasing prince upbraided his agent for treating the warraq badly, declaring the book was worth over 10,000 dinars (p. 51).

Book sales were sometimes conducted by auction (*nida'*, "call"). People sat in a circle and the book was called out, bid following upon bid (p. 51).

Al-Jahiz (b. 767), a great bookworm, hit upon the idea of hiring booths from the warraqin and spending the nights in them reading; it was cheaper than buying the books (p. 50).

**Scene potential:** Al-Jahiz hiring booths to read overnight is a marvellous detail for the Suq al-Warraqin scene. If Yusuf visits the market in the evening, he might see lamps burning in booths where scholars sit reading -- a city that never stops consuming the written word.

### The Barmakids and the Bayt al-Hikma

The Barmakid Yahya ibn Khalid, Harun al-Rashid's tutor and minister, initiated the translation of Ptolemy's Almagest. He first had the work translated independently by several scholars, then appointed supervising editors to correct and improve the text. This system of using multiple translators and supervising editors was general practice (p. 114).

Al-Ma'mun's Bayt al-Hikma ("House of Wisdom") employed large numbers of copyists. Its bookbinder was named Ibn Abi al-Harish. The institution was a combined translation bureau, library, and academy (pp. 103, 113-114). Three sons of al-Munajjim paid five hundred dinars a month to a group of translators (p. 115).

The caliph al-Mu'tadid (892-902) planned a new palace with residential and study accommodations for leading scholars, with board and lodging to free them for their studies (p. 115).

**Scene potential:** Abu Said's paper workshop supplies the Bayt al-Hikma. The volume of paper consumed by a translation bureau employing dozens of translators and copyists would be enormous -- and the Barmakid connection to both the translation movement and the paper trade is not coincidental.

### Bookbinding in the Early Abbasid Period

The production of bookbinding was part of the warraq's art. Specialist trades emerged: calligrapher (*khattat*), painter (*musawwir*), sketcher of ornaments (*tarrah*), gilder (*mudhahhib*), cutter (*qati'*), and bookbinder (*mujallid*). Ibn al-Nadim could name the bookbinder who worked in al-Ma'mun's library, Ibn Abi al-Harish (p. 103).

Al-Maqdisi, the great tenth-century traveller, was himself a skilled bookbinder. In Yemen, he was offered two dinars -- a very large sum -- to bind a Qur'an for the amir. He notes technical differences: flour paste was employed in Yemen for gluing and lining covers, whereas in Palestine he used asphodel glue (p. 103).

The earliest surviving Muslim book cover is a fragment of cedar wood from the Tulunid period (ninth century) in Egypt, inlaid with ivory, bone, and multicoloured wood mosaic (p. 104).

**Scene potential:** Bookbinding is a natural extension of the paper trade. If Abu Said's workshop produces finished paper, some of it goes directly to bookbinders. The specialist trades listed here -- calligrapher, gilder, cutter, binder -- represent the full supply chain that depends on Yusuf's paper.

### Libraries of the Abbasid Period

The Abbasid library in Baghdad was associated with al-Ma'mun's Bayt al-Hikma, or possibly even Harun al-Rashid's earlier academy. When the Mustansiriya college was founded in 1234, some 80,000 volumes were transferred to it from the caliph's library (p. 115).

In Rayy, the Buyid wazir Ibn al-Amid had a library so large that it comprised over a hundred camel loads. During a Khurasani army's sack of the city in 996, the wazir was concerned only for his book collection, which he managed to rescue (p. 122).

Isma'il ibn Abbad (d. 995), another wazir in Rayy, refused an invitation to become wazir in Khurasan on the grounds that moving his library would be too much trouble. Its catalogue alone filled ten volumes (pp. 122-123).

The wazir Sabur ibn Ardashir founded a large public library in Baghdad around 993, in the street called Bayn al-Surayn ("between the two walls"). It was destroyed when the Seljuks conquered Baghdad in 1055 (p. 123).

In Basra, Ibn Sawwar established a *dar al-ilm* ("house of learning") with a large library and bursaries for students. The library gave money and paper to indigent students (p. 124).

**Scene potential:** The demand for paper from these enormous libraries is the economic engine driving Yusuf's business. A library of 80,000 volumes, each hand-copied on paper, represents an astonishing quantity of raw material. The detail about libraries giving paper to students -- the commodity Yusuf sells -- directly connects his trade to the intellectual life of the empire.

## Relevance to Chapters

- **Book 1, Ch 4 (The Poisoned Vat):** The sizing process (*ilaj*) described here -- immersion in starch paste, the role of lime water in pulp preparation -- provides the technical backdrop for the sabotage scene. The named paper varieties (*al-ja'fari*, etc.) give the workshop's products specificity.
- **Book 1, Ch 5 (The Suq al-Warraqin):** This is the chapter most directly informed by Pedersen. The hundred stalls, the canal location, the guild structure, the warraqin buying paper for transcription and resale, the literary gossip in booths, the book auctions, al-Jahiz reading overnight -- all of this is scene-ready material.
- **Book 1, Ch 1-3 (Kufa journey):** The mosque at Kufa as a site of dictation and literary discourse. The minor poet who sat in "the principal mosque of Kufa" dictating while people wrote it down -- this is the kind of scene Yusuf might witness.
- **Book 1 (apprenticeship, all chapters):** The papermaking process, the shift from papyrus to paper, the Barmakid role in introducing paper to Iraq, the economic chain from mill to market to mosque.
- **Book 2 (Barmakid connection):** The Barmakid patronage of translation and papermaking. Ja'far's role in introducing paper to Iraq makes the House of Paper's Barmakid dependence historically grounded. The named paper variety *al-ja'fari* means Barmakid patronage was literally stamped into the product.
- **Book 3 (House of Wisdom):** The Bayt al-Hikma as a major consumer of paper. The translation system, the army of copyists, the library. Yusuf as supplier to this institution.
- **Books 1-4 (thematic):** The relationship between oral culture and written culture -- between trust in the spoken word and trust in paper -- mirrors the series' central tension between tangible craft and abstract finance.

## [RESEARCH NEEDED]

- Pedersen says paper manufacture remained confined to Transoxania for over 150 years after 751 CE, citing al-Istakhri (mid-10th century). But the novel assumes Baghdad had paper mills by 794-795. Cross-reference with Jonathan Bloom's *Paper before Print* (in `reference/`) for a more nuanced chronology of when paper manufacture actually began in Iraq. The Barmakid introduction of paper *use* in Iraq (794-795) is well attested, but paper *manufacture* there may be slightly later.
- The exact location of the Suq al-Warraqin relative to the Round City needs verification against Le Strange's *Baghdad during the Abbasid Caliphate* (already digested). Pedersen says "southeast of the Round City, by one of the canals." Does Le Strange give a more precise location?
- Pedersen's paper-manufacturing description comes from an anonymous tract finalised "around 1200" -- three to four centuries after the novel's setting. The process may have been simpler in the late 8th century. Bloom's book may provide earlier evidence.
- The guild structure of the warraqin under a *shaykh* is attested only from the 16th century (Ibn Iyas). Whether such organisation existed in the late 8th century is unclear. The novel should be cautious about implying a formal guild in the 780s-790s.
