# Digest: The Camel and the Wheel

**Author:** Richard W. Bulliet
**Source:** `reference/The Camel and the Wheel (Morningside Books) -- Richard W Bulliet [...] -- Anna's Archive.pdf`
**Date digested:** 4 March 2026
**Relevance:** Camel transport technology, why wheeled vehicles disappeared from the Middle East, saddle types, caravan economics, pack-camel logistics, urban design consequences -- essential background for all caravan and trade scenes.

## Overview

Bulliet's central argument is that a vast stretch of civilisation -- from Morocco to Afghanistan -- abandoned the wheel as a transport technology sometime between the third and seventh centuries CE, and that this was not a regression but a rational economic response to the superiority of pack camels. The book traces the domestication of the camel, the invention of various saddle types, the economic competition between camel and wagon, and the consequences of the wheel's disappearance for urban design, road infrastructure, and social organisation. Originally published in 1975 (Harvard), the Morningside Edition (Columbia, 1990) adds a preface with updated scholarship.

For this project, the book provides the material foundation for understanding how Yusuf's world moves goods. Every caravan scene, every trade journey, every mention of Baghdad's narrow streets or absent roads operates within the wheelless transport economy Bulliet describes. The book also illuminates the social relationship between camel-breeding nomads and settled urban merchants -- exactly the interface Yusuf navigates on his journeys.

## Key Extracts

### Why the Wheel Disappeared

Bulliet marshals three types of evidence for the total absence of wheeled vehicles in the medieval Islamic Middle East: traveller accounts, pictorial evidence, and linguistic analysis.

The linguistic evidence is particularly striking. The Arabic word for all wheeled vehicles is *'ajala*, derived from a root connoting swiftness -- and it appears only rarely in medieval texts, usually to describe foreign or extraordinary vehicles. No word for cart or wagon passed from Aramaic or Syriac into Arabic, despite direct continuity between those speech communities. Persian shows the same blank. The medieval period word *gardan* suffices for the rare instances when wheeled transport is mentioned. After the fourteenth century, when Turkish influence reintroduces wagons, borrowed foreign words flood in: *karrisa* from Italian *carrozza*, *qarita* from Italian *carretta*, *hantir* from Hungarian *hinto*. The vocabulary gap confirms that the wheel had vanished from daily experience long before the Arab conquests (pp. 10-14).

**Scene potential:** Yusuf would have no mental frame of reference for wheeled transport. A wagon seen in a Byzantine border town would be as alien to him as an elephant -- worth staring at and describing to friends back home.

### The Economic Case: Pack Camel vs. Wagon

The core economic argument rests on a comparison of two ancient sources. A Palmyrene fiscal law gives a tax ratio of four to one between a cartload and a camel load. Diocletian's edict on prices (301 CE) specifies 20 denarii for carrying a wagonload of 1,200 Roman pounds one mile, and 8 denarii for a camel load of 600 pounds the same distance. The camel carried half the weight at 40 percent of the cost -- a 20 percent saving per unit of goods (pp. 20-21).

But the raw cost advantage understates the camel's superiority. Bulliet enumerates the further advantages documented by nineteenth-century military transport officers who compared camels directly with oxen and mules:

1. A camel can carry or draw twice as much as an ox.
2. Faster, covering more ground daily.
3. Can travel 20 to 25 miles in one stretch.
4. Makes many more journeys per year and across its longer lifetime.
5. Traverses ground where a wagon would stick.
6. Fords rivers without trouble, where wagons must be unloaded.
7. Lives and works four times as long.
8. Greater powers of abstinence from food and water.
9. Greater tenacity and endurance.
10. Wagons are liable to break down, upset, or stick -- causing time loss and repair expense.
11. The dead weight of a wagon itself is considerable, at least a ton.
12. A single person can manage a string of three to six camels, while a wagon requires one driver per two animals (pp. 22-25).

Roman oxcarts travelled only six to nine miles per day, compared to fifteen to twenty for a pack camel. The cost of a wagon and draught harness far exceeded that of a camel saddle, particularly in wood-poor regions. And the camel needed no road at all (p. 24).

**Scene potential:** When Yusuf calculates the economics of a trade journey, these are the numbers in his head. A caravan of thirty camels managed by five or six men replaces a wagon train that would need fifteen drivers, a road, and twice the time. This is why paper -- light, high-value, compact -- is the perfect caravan commodity.

### The North Arabian Saddle

The critical technological innovation was the North Arabian saddle, invented between 500 and 100 BCE. It consists of two large arches or saddle bows shaped like inverted V's, one placed on a pad in front of the hump and the other on a pad behind it. The two bows are connected on the sides by crossed sticks or straight sticks, forming a rigid square frame that converges toward the top, with the hump rising in the middle. A pad is placed over this frame for the rider, whose weight bears not on the hump itself but is distributed by the framework to the camel's rib cage (p. 87).

When used as a pack saddle, the load is divided into two parts of equal weight and tied to each side of the saddle. The Arabic name comes from *shadid*, meaning hard or firm -- stability is its defining quality (pp. 87, 96).

The North Arabian saddle was a military as well as a commercial innovation. Earlier cushion saddles gave riders no stability for weapons work. The rigid frame of the North Arabian saddle allowed camel-mounted warriors to fight effectively with sword and long spear from their elevated position -- four cubits above the ground. The saddle's invention correlates directly with the rise of Arab camel-breeding nomads as a military and economic force, beginning with the Nabataeans at Petra (pp. 91-96).

**Scene potential:** Every camel in Yusuf's caravans wears a North Arabian saddle. The physical description of the frame -- the twin inverted-V arches, the crosspieces, the pad over the top -- is what Yusuf sees when he watches the camel drivers load up at dawn. The balanced loading, equal weight on each side, is a craft in itself.

### Caravan Economics and the Single Handler

One of the most consequential details for fiction: a single cameleer can manage a string of three to six camels. This is because camels can be tied nose-to-tail in a line and led by one person on foot or mounted on the lead animal. A wagon, by contrast, requires one driver per vehicle and at least two draught animals per wagon. The labour savings alone made camel transport vastly cheaper (p. 25).

**Scene potential:** The caravan scenes in Chapters 1-3 should show this in action -- camel strings led by single handlers, the rhythmic procession of loaded animals nose-to-tail, the handlers walking alongside or riding the lead camel. The caravan master coordinates the strings, not individual animals.

### A Society Without Wheels: Urban Design

The most vivid consequence of the wheel's disappearance for this project is its effect on city design. Bulliet argues that the winding, narrow streets characteristic of medieval Islamic cities are not the product of religious ideology or cultural preference but the direct physical result of a society that had no wheeled vehicles to accommodate. With only pedestrians and pack animals to serve, streets could follow the lay of the land, narrow to provide shade, widen into cul-de-sacs giving access to residences, and accommodate the encroachment of buildings and merchants' displays. No minimum width was required, no constant grade, no paving. Corners could be sharp, dead ends abundant (pp. 224-226).

This was not disorder but a different kind of order -- one optimised for human and animal foot traffic in a hot climate. Narrow winding streets diminish winds, provide shade, permit higher density of habitation, facilitate social relationships, are easily defensible, and allow for careful regulation of water consumption. As late as 1845, when a major new street was built in Cairo, its width was determined by measuring the combined width of two loaded camels (p. 226).

**Scene potential:** Baghdad's streets in the novel are shaped by this reality. When Yusuf navigates the suqs and residential quarters, he moves through a city designed for pedestrians and pack camels. A loaded camel brushing against shop fronts, the impossibility of turning a cart in an alley, the shade cast by overhanging upper storeys -- all of this is the physical world of the novel. The Round City's broad avenues are the exception that proves the rule: a planned imperial space imposed on the organic city.

### Roads in a Wheelless Society

Governments in the Islamic Middle East invested in bridges and caravansaries rather than road surfaces, because these were the only infrastructure that mattered for camel transport. Camels, donkeys, and pedestrians do not need paved roads. References to road upkeep are almost nonexistent in Islamic sources, while bridge and caravansary construction is a major form of royal and charitable patronage (pp. 227-228).

A regular daily stage of travel for a caravan does not exceed twenty miles, and a good road provides a stopping place at the end of every stage -- a town, village, or caravansary. Beyond bridges and stopping places, physical road maintenance is negligible. This is a transport economy that runs on animal endurance and human knowledge of routes rather than on engineered infrastructure (p. 227).

**Scene potential:** Yusuf's caravan journeys are structured by the spacing of caravansaries and water sources, not by the condition of roads. The road itself is a track worn into the earth by centuries of camel traffic, not a paved surface. When the caravan crosses a bridge, it is a notable event -- a piece of infrastructure worth remarking on.

### Camel Load Capacity

A standard camel load in the Roman period was approximately 600 Roman pounds (roughly 430 pounds avoirdupois / 195 kg), and Bulliet notes this is consistent with medieval and modern camel loads. The figure appears stable across two millennia (p. 20).

**Scene potential:** When Yusuf loads paper bales onto camels, he is working within this constraint. A ream of handmade paper is light relative to its value -- paper is the ideal caravan commodity precisely because it maximises value per unit of camel-load weight. A camel carrying 400 pounds of fine paper carries a fortune; a camel carrying 400 pounds of grain carries a modest sum.

### Camels and Water

Among the camel's advantages enumerated by military officers: greater powers of abstinence from food and water compared to any other pack animal (p. 22). This is the quality that makes desert crossing possible and that gives the camel its economic dominance in the arid zone from Morocco to Afghanistan. A camel can travel several days without water in moderate conditions, far longer than an ox or mule team that would need daily watering.

**Scene potential:** The water discipline of caravan travel -- rationing, knowledge of wells, the anxiety of dry stretches -- is driven by the needs of the humans and the more delicate pack animals (mules, donkeys), not the camels themselves. The camels' endurance is what makes the route viable; the humans are the weak link.

### The Camel as Cultural Marker

An important social distinction: throughout history, some Arabs bred camels while others did not. Those who bred them held them in high esteem; those who did not -- the urban, cosmopolitan population -- tended to regard them with indifference or mild contempt. The camel was not a symbol of backwardness but of nomadism. One noble Iranian in the ninth century declared he had adopted everything Arab except the riding of camels (pp. 218-220).

The most famous literary work on animals from this period, al-Jahiz's *Kitab al-Hayawan*, mentions the camel only incidentally, in the context of discussing other species. Pigeons receive far more attention. In pictorial art the camel is usually a bystander (p. 219).

**Scene potential:** Yusuf, a city-bred Khorasani, would not romanticise camels. He would regard them as useful, impressive in their endurance, but fundamentally as working animals -- tools of commerce. The Bedouin camel handlers on his caravans would have a completely different relationship with the animals, one of deep familiarity and respect. This difference in attitude is itself a marker of the social boundary between nomad and townsman that Yusuf must learn to navigate.

## Facts and Dates

- **Camel load standard:** ~600 Roman pounds / ~430 lbs / ~195 kg, stable from Roman period through modern era (p. 20)
- **Cost advantage:** Pack camel transport roughly 20% cheaper than ox-wagon per unit of goods, based on Diocletian's price edict of 301 CE (p. 20)
- **Daily march:** Pack camel caravan covers 15-20 miles/day; Roman oxcart 6-9 miles/day (p. 24)
- **March stretch:** Camels can do 20-25 miles in one stretch without rest (p. 23)
- **Handler ratio:** One person manages 3-6 camels in a string; wagons require one driver per two animals (p. 25)
- **Camel longevity:** Lives and works four times as long as an ox (p. 23)
- **North Arabian saddle:** Invented between 500 and 100 BCE; two inverted-V arches connected by crosspieces, frame sits over the hump (p. 87)
- **Name etymology:** *shadid* = hard, firm (Arabic), referring to the rigid saddle frame (p. 96)
- **Wheel disappearance:** Complete across the Middle East by the time of the Arab conquests (7th century CE); the process began centuries earlier (pp. 14-16)
- **Arabic wheel vocabulary:** *'ajala* (from root meaning swiftness) is the only native Arabic word; all other cart/wagon terms are post-14th-century borrowings from Italian, Hungarian, Russian (pp. 10-14)
- **Street width standard:** As late as 1845, Cairo street width measured by the span of two loaded camels (p. 226)
- **Infrastructure priority:** Islamic governments invested in bridges and caravansaries, not road surfaces (pp. 227-228)
- **Stage length:** Standard daily caravan stage does not exceed 20 miles; stopping places (town, village, caravansary) required at each stage end (p. 227)
- **One-humped vs. two-humped:** *Camelus dromedarius* (one hump) dominated the Middle East; *Camelus bactrianus* (two humps) used in Central Asia and Iran's eastern regions (p. 6)
- **Palmyrene tax ratio:** Cart taxed at 4:1 ratio over camel load, reflecting Palmyra's caravan merchants protecting their camel-based trade against wheeled competitors (pp. 20-21)

## [RESEARCH NEEDED] Flags

- **Camel breeds by region:** Bulliet discusses one-humped vs. two-humped distribution but does not detail breed variations within the dromedary used in Iraq and Syria. Specific breed names used in 8th-century caravan trade would enrich the caravan scenes. Existing journey research files may partially cover this.
- **Loading procedure:** Bulliet describes the balanced-load principle (equal weight on each side of the North Arabian saddle) but does not give a step-by-step description of how bales were actually lashed to the saddle frame. Travel literature or ethnographic sources may provide this.
- **Camel sounds and behaviour:** The book is an economic and technological history, not a natural history. Sensory details of camel behaviour -- the groaning when made to kneel for loading, the chewing of cud, the distinctive gait -- will need to come from other sources (travel accounts, veterinary literature, or Thesiger's *Arabian Sands*).
