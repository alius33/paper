# Digest: Jews in Iraq after the Muslim Conquest

**Author:** Michael G. Morony
**Source:** `reference/Jews in Iraq after the Muslim Conquest -- Morony, Michael G_ -- Analecta Gorgiana [...] -- Gorgias Press, LLC -- 9781463216054 [...] -- Anna's Archive.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** The definitive scholarly treatment of the Jewish community in Iraq from the Sasanian through early Abbasid periods -- covers communal organisation, the exilarch and gaonate, legal autonomy, economic roles, dhimmi precedents, and interfaith social boundaries. Essential for understanding the religious minorities alongside whom Yusuf builds his commercial empire, and for grounding the character of Ishaq bar Yohannan (Nestorian Christian) through comparison with parallel minority institutions.

## Overview

This monograph is Chapter 11 of Morony's larger work *Iraq after the Muslim Conquest* (2006), published separately by Gorgias Press. Morony argues that the Jews were the oldest and best-organised religious community in Iraq, and that their communal institutions under the Sasanians served as prototypes for other minority communities -- including the Nestorian Christians whose institutional structures closely paralleled the Jewish model. The central narrative traces how the exilarchate (the hereditary leadership claiming Davidic descent) was gradually eclipsed by the gaonate (the scholarly-legal leadership of the rabbinical academies), a process that accelerated under Islamic rule precisely because the Muslim government declined to intervene in internal communal affairs.

For the novel, Morony is valuable on three fronts. First, he provides the institutional scaffolding for how non-Muslim communities actually functioned in Abbasid Iraq: their courts, tax collection, market supervision, charity systems, and internal governance. These structures operated in parallel to the Islamic state, creating the layered, multi-confessional commercial world through which Yusuf navigates. Second, Morony establishes the deep Aramaean cultural substrate shared by Jews and Nestorian Christians in Iraq -- they spoke the same language (Aramaic), inhabited the same cities, and operated analogous communal institutions, making interfaith commercial partnerships both natural and bounded by clear social limits. Third, he documents the specific economic roles of Jewish communities in early Islamic Iraq, including the transition from agricultural to commercial occupations that positioned Jews as merchants, dyers, tanners, and money-changers by the Abbasid period.

The text is heavily footnoted and draws primarily on the Babylonian Talmud, Syriac Christian chronicles, Arabic historical sources, and archaeological evidence (particularly the Nippur incantation bowls). Morony writes for academic specialists but his prose is clear.

## Key Extracts

### The Oldest Organised Religious Community (pp. 306-307)

Morony establishes that Jews were concentrated in the districts between Hira and Mada'in in the Sawad of Kufa -- an area that was "almost entirely Jewish" by the middle of the Sasanian period. Major settlements existed at Pumbaditha, Nehardea, Mahoza, Kutha, Sura, and Neresh. Outside this central concentration, smaller Jewish communities were scattered across Iraq at Hira, Nippur, in Maysan, at Sinjar, and around the confluence of the Greater Zab and the Tigris in Adiabene. After the Muslim conquest, Jews survived at approximately the same locations and also appeared at the new garrison cities of Basra and Kufa.

The Jewish population peaked at perhaps two million around 500 CE (following centuries of conversion among the native Aramaean population), then declined slightly through the late Sasanian period as conversions to Christianity increased at the margins.

**Scene potential:** The Kufa journey (Chapters 1-3) passes through territory that was heavily Jewish for centuries. Yusuf could observe Jewish communities along the road, noting their synagogues, distinct quarters, and the Aramaic spoken alongside Arabic -- establishing the multi-confessional texture of the Iraqi landscape.

### Aramaean Cultural Substrate: The Language of Commerce (pp. 309-310)

Jews in Sasanian and early Islamic Iraq were linguistically and culturally Aramaeans. Aramaic was the language of the Talmudic academies, legal documents, and daily speech. Hebrew scriptures were translated into Aramaic so congregations could understand them. The same dialect of Aramaic appears on incantation bowls commissioned by Jews at Nippur. Morony documents extensive cultural mixing: Jews bore Persian theophoric names (Bahmanduch, Hormizduch, Ispandarmed, even "Sardust bath Shirin" -- a feminine form of Zarathustra), were familiar with Persian festivals and legal traditions, and shared demonological beliefs with their pagan Aramaean neighbours.

This Aramaean base was shared with Nestorian Christians, who also used Syriac (a dialect of Aramaic) for liturgy, scholarship, and legal documents. The two communities inhabited a common linguistic world, which facilitated commercial interaction even as religious boundaries remained firm.

**Scene potential:** Ishaq bar Yohannan, the Nestorian scribe, and Jewish sarrafs would share Aramaic as a working language for commercial documents. A scene where Ishaq drafts a contract in Syriac script for a Jewish merchant -- or where Yusuf observes that Jews and Christians can read each other's documents because the underlying language is the same -- would ground the novel's interfaith commerce in historical reality.

### Jewish Social Stratification (pp. 310-312)

Morony documents a sharp social divide within the Jewish community. The wealthy Jews of Mahoza were the aristocracy -- descended from converts, known for luxury, intelligence, and charity. Their Persianized notables at Nehardea wore tall hats, maintained mounted retinues, and could influence officials. Wealthy Jews lived in mansions with columned reception halls and gardens surrounded by retinues of servants.

But the great majority were labourers, peasants, and slaves. They worked in grain, wool, linen, and wine production. Slaves were common -- female domestic slaves who baked, spun, and wove; skilled craft slaves who perforated pearls or worked as tailors. By the fourth century, investment in commerce brought better returns than agriculture, and small Jewish farmers were being displaced by large estates. By the eighth century, Jewish peasants near Sura and Pumbaditha owned no land at all.

The Arab author al-Jahiz, in a characteristic stereotype, says Jews in the Islamic period tended to be dyers, tanners, barbers, butchers, and tinkers.

**Scene potential:** The detail about Jewish pearl-perforators, tailors, dyers, and tanners populates Baghdad's suqs with specific tradespeople Yusuf would encounter. The Jewish dyers in the Karkh market could be neighbours of Ali's tile workshop. The stereotype from al-Jahiz -- that Jews were dyers and tanners (low-status crafts involving bad smells) -- could surface in dialogue as prejudice Yusuf observes but does not share.

### Communal Self-Governance: The Template for Dhimmi Autonomy (pp. 313, 316-318)

The Jewish community under the Sasanians developed the most fully articulated system of minority self-governance in Iraq. Each community had a council with a chairman and seven advisers responsible for alms, the synagogue, communal property, and schools. Officers checked weights and measures, maintained flood watches, and kept order. A three-member rabbinical court handled personal status (marriage, divorce, inheritance), commercial transactions, land disputes, and property documents. The court had its own scribe to draw up documents.

The exilarch -- hereditary leader claiming descent from David -- was recognised by the Sasanian state as the legitimate representative of the entire Jewish community, responsible for justice, tax collection, market supervision, and defence. This arrangement was the direct precedent for the dhimmi system under Islam.

Critically, after the Muslim conquest, the Quran itself (Sura 5:43-48, 62:5) recognised the Torah and Gospels as sources of divine law for Jews and Christians, establishing the theological basis for communal legal autonomy. The Islamic government confirmed the exilarch's authority to appoint judges and heads of schools.

**Scene potential:** Ishaq bar Yohannan would understand this system intimately, since the Nestorian Christian community operated an analogous structure under its own Catholicos. A conversation between Ishaq and Yusuf about how minority communities govern themselves -- courts, scribes, tax collectors, market inspectors, all running in parallel to the Islamic state -- would reveal the institutional complexity of the world while characterising Ishaq's marginal but indispensable position.

### The Rise of the Gaonate and the Erosion of the Exilarchate (pp. 319-325)

The most important internal development in the Jewish community during the novel's period was the transfer of real authority from the exilarch (secular, hereditary) to the gaonim (scholarly, meritocratic). This process began in the late Sasanian period when the exilarchate was suppressed during persecutions, and the heads of the rabbinical academies at Sura and Pumbaditha filled the vacuum. By the early eighth century, the gaonate had become the real governing authority, with the exilarchate reduced to a ceremonial role. The crucial moment came around 767 CE -- squarely within Yusuf's lifetime -- when the scholar Anan ben David challenged the gaonate's authority. The Caliph al-Mansur intervened, imprisoning Anan, in the first recorded direct intervention by the Islamic government in internal Jewish affairs.

By the time of the novel (770-814 CE), this was settled: the gaonate held decisive power, the exilarchate was ceremonial, and judges were appointed jointly by gaon and exilarch. Semi-annual academic assemblies at Sura in the months of Adar and Elul continued to adjudicate legal questions sent by Jewish communities across the Islamic world.

**Scene potential:** The tension between hereditary authority and scholarly/meritocratic authority within the Jewish community mirrors the novel's own themes: Yusuf builds a meritocratic commercial house, but must eventually face the question of hereditary succession (Khalid). A Jewish merchant or sarraf could mention the exilarch-gaon tension as a way of illuminating these themes without stating them.

### Intercommunal Segregation and Social Boundaries (pp. 313-315)

Morony provides a vivid anecdote about intercommunal relations. At Sinjar, before the Muslim conquest, Christian and Magian shepherd boys would gather at a cistern for the midday meal, sitting and eating in separate groups. A Jewish boy named Ashir was forced to eat alone because the Christian boys drove him away. The story illustrates the layered segregation of late Sasanian Iraqi society -- communities lived side by side but maintained firm social boundaries, especially around food, burial, and worship.

Jewish communities used the legal device of the *erubh* -- converting public thoroughfares and courtyards into communal property -- to create Jewish enclaves that excluded non-Jews. This was extended to entire cities or districts at Pumbaditha and Mahoza. The Talmudic scholars discouraged social contact with non-Jews, opposed the sale of wine to outsiders, and objected to intermarriage.

Yet social isolation was never total. A Jew operated a wine press jointly with a pagan at Nehardea. Mata Mehasia near Sura had a mixed Jewish and pagan population in the fifth century. Jews were still selling wine to Christians in the 670s.

**Scene potential:** The tension between social segregation and commercial necessity is directly relevant to Yusuf's world. Ishaq bar Yohannan lives this tension daily -- a Christian who works intimately with a Muslim merchant, sharing meals and trust, yet belonging to a different community with its own courts, festivals, and burial customs. The wine-selling detail is particularly useful: shared commerce in products that cross religious boundaries (wine is permissible for Christians and Jews but forbidden for Muslims) creates exactly the kind of liminal commercial space where Yusuf operates.

### Messianic Movements and Sectarian Upheaval (pp. 326-330)

Morony documents a Messianic rising between 644 and 647 at Falluja, led by a Jew who gathered four hundred weavers, carpet makers, and linen bleachers. They burned three churches and killed a local governor before being suppressed by troops from Kufa. All rebels were killed with their families; the leader was crucified.

In the late seventh century, Abu Isa al-Isfahani proclaimed himself a prophet and forerunner of the Messiah, recognising Jesus and Muhammad as prophets each sent to their own people. He advocated that Jews read the Quran and the Gospels. His movement combined Islamic concepts of successive revelation, Christian attitudes toward divorce, and Jewish apocalyptic asceticism. He was killed in battle but his sect survived at Damascus into the tenth century.

Most relevant to the novel's period: around 767 CE, the scholar Anan ben David's challenge to the gaonate led to the formation of the Karaite sect, which rejected the Oral Law (Talmud) in favour of Scripture alone. By the ninth century, Karaites existed at Basra, Baghdad, in Khuzistan, Fars, the Jibal, and Khurasan. The Caliph al-Mansur's intervention against Anan in support of the gaonate was a precedent for caliphal power over minority communities.

**Scene potential:** The existence of Jewish sectarian diversity -- Rabbinic vs. Karaite -- adds texture to the novel's portrayal of religious life. A Jewish merchant in Baghdad might mention the Karaite controversy the way a character might mention any community dispute. The Falluja rising of weavers and bleachers is a reminder that religious minorities could be volatile, a fact that would concern any banker whose wealth depended on stability.

- c. 767 CE: Anan ben David challenged the gaonate; Caliph al-Mansur imprisoned him (p. 329)
- Anan's followers evolved into the Karaite sect, present in Baghdad, Basra, Khuzistan, Fars, the Jibal, and Khurasan by the ninth-tenth century (p. 330)

## [RESEARCH NEEDED]

- **Jewish sarrafs and jahbadhs in Baghdad specifically.** Morony's focus is on the Sasanian-to-Islamic transition period and the Sawad region. He does not cover the *sarraf* (money-changer) and *jahbadh* (state banker) roles in detail. Other sources (Goitein's *Mediterranean Society*, the Geniza documents) are needed for the specific mechanics of Jewish banking in Abbasid Baghdad.
- **Nestorian Christian communal institutions.** Morony treats Jews specifically; the parallel chapter on Christians in *Iraq after the Muslim Conquest* (not included in this extract) would provide the direct comparison needed for Ishaq's character. The Catholicos, the Church of the East's court system, and Nestorian scribal culture need separate research.
- **Jewish-Muslim commercial partnerships.** Morony documents the social boundaries but says little about the actual mechanics of cross-confessional business partnerships in the Abbasid period. The legal frameworks (e.g., whether a Muslim and a Jew could enter a mudaraba together, and under whose law disputes would be adjudicated) remain a gap.
- **The synagogues of Baghdad.** Morony focuses on the older Jewish centres (Sura, Pumbaditha, Nehardea). The Jewish community in Baghdad itself -- where it was located, its synagogues, its leaders -- is not covered here.
