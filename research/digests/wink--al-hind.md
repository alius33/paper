# Digest: Al-Hind: The Making of the Indo-Islamic World, Volume 1 -- Early Medieval India and the Expansion of Islam, 7th-11th Centuries

**Author:** Andre Wink
**Source:** `reference/Al-Hind_ The Making of the Indo-Islamic World, Volume 1_ -- by Andre Wink -- Al-Hind_ The Making of the Indo-Islamic World (1), 2002 -- Brill -- 9780391041257 -- f852343e0f1471e95769d00f1a99cac7 -- Anna's Archive.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-05
**Relevance:** Comprehensive scholarly study of trade between the Islamic world and India (7th-11th centuries), covering the Persian Gulf commercial system, overland and maritime trade routes, the goods flowing into Baghdad from the east, Muslim and Jewish trading diasporas in the Indian Ocean, and the political geography of Khorasan as a gateway between the caliphate and the Indian subcontinent. Directly informs the economic geography underlying Yusuf's commercial empire.

## Overview

Wink's central argument is that the Islamic Middle East achieved economic supremacy in the early medieval period by establishing new connections between the Mediterranean and the Indian Ocean. The India trade -- conducted through the Persian Gulf ports of Basra and Siraf, and overland through Khorasan, Afghanistan, and Sind -- became the foundation of the caliphate's international economy from the eighth century onward. The dinar served as the dominant global currency up to the eleventh century, and the caliphate functioned as the commercial hinge linking Europe, Africa, Central Asia, China, and India.

For this novel, the book provides three categories of essential material. First, it establishes the *scope and structure* of the India trade that enriched Baghdad during Yusuf's lifetime: what goods arrived from the east, by which routes, through which ports, and in what quantities. Basra was the Persian Gulf's commercial hub; Siraf on the Iranian coast was its partner, specialising in trade with India and China. Together these ports funnelled spices, textiles, precious stones, Indian steel, and teakwood into the caliphate's markets. Second, the book maps the *trading diasporas* -- Muslim, Jewish, and Parsi merchant communities scattered across the Indian Ocean and the overland routes -- that sustained these networks. The Jewish role in Abbasid finance and the India trade is documented in detail and enriches the novel's portrayal of cross-confessional commerce. Third, the political geography section on Khorasan establishes Yusuf's ancestral homeland as a liminal zone: partly Arab, partly Persian, partly Iranian dihqan aristocracy, with a significant Jewish population, and Central Asian trade passing through it in both directions.

The book is an academic monograph of 404 pages, drawing on Arabic geographical literature (al-Mas'udi, Ibn Hauqal, al-Istakhri, al-Muqaddasi, al-Biruni), Persian chronicles, and secondary scholarship. It is strongest on the economic and commercial dimensions and weakest on the day-to-day texture of ordinary life -- there are no sensory descriptions or anecdotal portraits of the kind that translate directly into fiction. Its value to this project is structural: it provides the map of the commercial world in which Yusuf operates.

## Key Extracts

### The Eastward Pull: Baghdad, the Persian Gulf, and the India Trade (pp. 25-27, 46-55)

When the Abbasid revolution replaced Damascus with Baghdad, the centre of gravity of the Islamic world shifted decisively eastward -- away from the Mediterranean and toward the Persian Gulf and the Indian Ocean. Wink identifies this shift as driven by the massive weight of Persia pulling the Abbasid empire toward the Gulf, down the Tigris and Euphrates, and toward the sea route linking Basra and Siraf with India and China. Throughout the eighth and ninth centuries, the Mediterranean coast diminished in relative importance compared to the eastern trade.

Basra was founded in approximately 638 CE with an explicit strategic purpose: to provide a stronghold on the Tigris that could protect Arab camps against naval attacks from Oman and India. After the conquest of Sind, Basra developed into the primary centre of the India trade, achieving a prosperity that lasted to the end of the ninth century. Siraf, on the Iranian coast, was second in importance only to Basra. Arab geographers described its buildings stretching in a continuous chain and emphasised that its wealth derived almost exclusively from trade with India, China, and Africa. Siraf's harbour offered advantages to large ships by enabling them to avoid the storms and navigational hazards of the delta system near Basra.

The Persian Gulf commercial system functioned as an integrated network. Ships from Siraf and Oman carried sailors and merchants belonging chiefly to the Azdi tribe. All shipping on both sides of the Gulf came under Persian dominance. The major ports of Oman, including Suhar, were frequented by merchants dealing with Sind, India, and China but were governed by a Persian governor working with an Azdi Arab client king. This pattern of Persian maritime organisation, inherited from the Sasanid period, was adopted and expanded under Islamic rule.

**Scene potential:** When Yusuf's trade network expands in Books 2-3 to include maritime routes, Basra is the essential hub. The detail that Basra was founded specifically to counter Indian and Omani naval threats grounds the city in military-commercial logic that Yusuf would understand. The Siraf detail -- wealth derived almost exclusively from eastern trade -- underscores why control of the Persian Gulf routes is the foundation of any serious commercial empire in this period.

### Goods from the East: What India Sent to Baghdad (pp. 60-64)

The products of India entering the long-distance trade were of enormous variety and commonly expensive or precious. The India-to-Islamic-world trade included:

- **Spices and aromatics:** pepper (from Malabar), ginger, aloe-wood, nard (spikenard), ambergris, camphor, myrobalans, cloves, nutmeg, sandalwood, musk, cinnamon, cardamom, mace, rhinoceros horn. In Arabic, words of Indian origin are predominantly terms for spices, perfumes, and medicines. Spices came from regions as far apart as Assam and Sri Lanka, Gujarat and Java.
- **Textiles:** silk, brocade, cotton, and jute, especially from Gujarat and Bengal. India's supremacy in the textile markets rested on early mastery of the technique of fast dyeing from natural sources.
- **Metals and metallurgy:** Indian steel and swords were legendary. Al-Idrisi wrote that Indian craftsmen made swords "surpassing those made by other peoples." Sindhi, Sri Lankan, and South Indian iron products competed for superiority based on local climate, skill, and polishing technique.
- **Precious materials:** jewels of gold and silver, gold bullion (especially from Sumatra), crystal, precious stones and diamonds, brass, bronze, elephant tusks, ivory.
- **Woods:** teak, ebony, bamboo, brazilwood. Teakwood from the Konkan coast was indispensable for shipbuilding in Iraq and Arabia.
- **Other:** hemp, indigo, lead, leather, rice and grains.

In return, the Islamic world sent India silk from Egypt and China, horses from Arabia and Persia, copper, lead, paper, carpets, glass, and chemicals from Iraq and Aden. Slaves from Ethiopia were also an import into India. Paper is specifically listed among the goods flowing eastward from Iraq to India.

India's inherent economic strength -- self-sufficient, with an export market for nearly all major commodities and relatively few import needs -- meant it attracted vast amounts of gold and silver in payment. This drain of precious metals toward India was notorious from antiquity and reached its peak in the ninth and tenth centuries.

**Scene potential:** The list of trade goods is a sensory catalogue for the novel. When Yusuf walks through Baghdad's markets or handles consignments at a caravansary, these are the commodities he encounters. The detail that paper specifically moved *from* Iraq *to* India reverses the usual direction and underscores that Yusuf's product has an eastern export market. The precious metals drain toward India is the macroeconomic current beneath every deal Yusuf negotiates.

### Central Asia and the Silk Route: Khorasan's Commercial Position (pp. 40-45)

Transoxania -- the area the Arabs called *ma wara' an-nahr*, "that which is beyond the river (Oxus)" -- became the Muslim bridgehead to Central Asia and China. The silk route ran along the oasis towns in the Takla Makan desert. Buddhism retained considerable importance north of the Oxus but was gradually being superseded by Islam along the silk route. Wink notes that the Islamic *madrasa* probably had its origin in the Central-Asian Buddhist monastery.

Al-Hajjaj and his general Qutayba advanced in 705-715 to conquer Bukhara and the Oxus valley, with expeditions into the Jaxartes provinces. The Arabs understood from the outset the commercial importance of Transoxania. The Battle of Talas in 751 removed the acute Chinese threat to the Arab position. A brilliant Islamic civilisation evolved among the Iranian peoples of Central Asia. Khorasan and Transoxania became the hereditary dominion of the Tahirid governors (821-873), then fell to the Samanids (819-1005). The Samanids pushed the frontier of Muslim Transoxania further northward and Central-Asian trade reached unprecedented heights. The lucrative China trade ran through these territories, as did the trade in Turkish slaves from the northern steppes.

**Scene potential:** When Yusuf journeys to Merv (Journey 6, age 19), he is travelling into the throat of this commercial funnel -- the point where Chinese silk, Central Asian goods, and Indian commodities converge before heading west to Baghdad. Merv's position as one of Khorasan's four capital cities, sitting at the junction of east-west and north-south trade routes, is what makes Yusuf's ancestral homeland commercially significant. The Samanid period -- just beyond the novel's timeframe -- represents the full flowering of the eastern trade that Yusuf, in founding his House, is positioning himself to exploit.

### Khorasan: The Four Quarters and the Arab Settlement (pp. 110-112)

From Abbasid times, the limits of Khorasan coincided roughly with the modern province plus north-west Afghanistan, bordered by Badakhshan in the east and the Oxus and the desert of Khwarazm in the north. The Arab geographers divided Khorasan into four quarters (*rub'*), named after the four capital cities: Nishapur, Merv, Herat, and Balkh.

The population came to consist of Iranians (including Zoroastrians and some Christians), Turks, Arabs, and numerous Jews. About 50,000 Arab families were transplanted to Khorasan shortly after the conquest, from Basra. The region, for this reason, came to be known as "a second Arabia" and "a colony of Basra." The Iranian aristocracy of the *dahaqin* (dihqans) negotiated treaties with the Arabs and largely succeeded in retaining their powerful position in local administration, paying fixed sums from taxes they collected independently.

The first Abbasid caliphs, dependent on their Khorasanian guards (*abna' ad-dawla*), succeeded to a considerable extent in politically and economically integrating Khorasan with the central Islamic lands. Through the political influence and financial support of the dihqans, the state was gradually Persianised. Harun al-Rashid's succession manoeuvres were intimately bound up with the political situation in Khorasan and aimed at drawing the province closer to the centre by judicious coalition management.

**Scene potential:** Yusuf's family history is embedded in this passage. His ancestors are Khorasanis -- part of the mixed Arab-Persian population that powered the Abbasid revolution and formed its military-administrative backbone. The detail that 50,000 Arab families from Basra were transplanted to Khorasan gives a demographic explanation for why Yusuf's family has both Arab and Persian cultural strands. The dihqan class -- Iranian aristocrats who maintained power by negotiating with conquerors -- is the broader social context for Abu Said's Sogdian heritage and for the pragmatic, deal-making culture Yusuf inherits.

### Jewish Bankers in Baghdad and the India Trade (pp. 87-90)

When Baghdad became the capital of Islam and trade surged in the eighth and ninth centuries, the Babylonian Jewry extended and intensified a long-held cultural and religious-juridical hegemony over the entire Jewish diaspora. Baghdad, capital of the caliphate, was also the seat of the two Gaons, the heads of the Babylonian Yeshivas -- academies combining the functions of a centre of scholarship, high court, and parliament.

In the benign climate of toleration of the early caliphate, a "Judaeo-Islamic" symbiosis evolved that went far beyond peaceful coexistence. The Jews produced an entire literature in Islamic languages, in Persian and Arabic, next to Hebrew. Jewish bankers (*jahabidha*) loomed large in the entourage of rulers, lending money to the government, consolidating state finances, becoming involved in the fiscal system and in tax-farming. Jewish bankers probably gained control of the Abbasid money market during the early tenth century and became instrumental in developing sophisticated financial techniques including the use of bills of exchange (*suftaja*) and cheques (*sakk*). The same bankers operated as traders (*tujjar*) or as financiers of other Jewish and Muslim traders. They supplied funds for slave expeditions in Africa, equipped caravans to Central Asia and China, and organised maritime expeditions in both the Mediterranean and the Indian Ocean. The great Jewish banking houses of Baghdad also financed the Jewish *radhaniya* trade extending from Western Europe to Sind, India, and China.

Wink cites Louis Massignon's argument that corporate international finance as it exists today, with a clear Jewish preponderance, appears to date back to the Abbasid caliphate of the late ninth and tenth centuries.

**Scene potential:** This passage directly informs Ishaq bar Yohannan's world -- not as a Jew specifically, but as a member of the broader cross-confessional financial ecosystem of Abbasid Baghdad. The detail that Jewish *jahabidha* were instrumental in developing suftaja and sakk techniques places Yusuf's financial instruments in a multi-confessional context. When Yusuf seeks a general manager with accounting skills and cross-cultural connections, Ishaq emerges from a community with deep institutional expertise in exactly these areas.

### The Radhaniya: Jewish Merchants Spanning the Known World (pp. 96-97)

The Jewish *radhaniya* merchants constituted the largest trading diaspora existing in the world at their peak, a single network with a shared cosmopolitan culture. Ibn Khordadbhih describes their routes: from the "country of the Franks" through the "country of the Slavs" to Khamlij (capital of the Khazars), across the Caspian Sea to Balkh, and sometimes onward across the Oxus to China. Another route ran from Frankish territory through North Africa and Egypt, then via the Red Sea to Sind, India, and China. A third went to Constantinople. A fourth went to Antiochia, down the Euphrates to Baghdad, then down the Tigris to al-Ubulla, and from there to Sind.

The radhaniya Jews spoke Arabic, Persian, Greek, and the Frankish, Spanish, and Slavic languages. India was reached either by the Persian Gulf and the Red Sea, or overland via Khorasan and Sind. The land route from Persia to Khorasan became the main route of Jewish immigration to China. It was also from Khorasan and Afghanistan that Jews penetrated deeply into India to trade.

**Scene potential:** The multilingual, multi-route structure of the radhaniya trade network is a model for the kind of commercial intelligence system Yusuf eventually builds. If a man with agents in five cities can honour a suftaja across a thousand miles, it is because of networks like this one. The radhaniya demonstrate that cross-cultural commercial trust -- the kind Yusuf builds his House on -- is not invented from nothing but grows from existing institutional patterns of diaspora commerce.

### Muslim Trading Colonies in India: Merchants from Basra and Baghdad (pp. 68-69)

Al-Mas'udi writes that the largest settlement of Muslims in western India numbered about 10,000 in the district of Saymur by the tenth century. These were a permanently established group with ancestors who had come from Siraf, Oman, Basra, Baghdad, and other cities in the Middle East, now "wearing the same dresses and having their beards grow in the same manner as the infidels." Among them were merchants of great distinction. One was customarily appointed by the local Hindu king as the head (*hamza*) of the Muslim community.

In the Gujarat-Konkan coast, flourishing Arab mercantile settlements dated back to the ninth, eighth, and in some cases perhaps the seventh century. The Konkan supplied teakwood that was indispensable to the Arabs for construction and shipbuilding in Iraq and Arabia. Hindu merchants from Siraf are described dining separately, each requiring his own dish, their numbers sometimes approaching or exceeding a hundred.

**Scene potential:** These details flesh out the far end of the trade networks Yusuf's House eventually taps. The merchants from "Basra, Baghdad, and other cities" who settled in India are the advance scouts of the commercial world Yusuf expands into in Books 2-3. The teakwood trade from the Konkan to Iraq for shipbuilding is a commodity Yusuf's agents might handle. The image of Muslim merchants who have lived in India so long they dress like the locals raises questions about what happens to identity when commerce takes a man far from home -- a theme central to Yusuf's journey.

### Jews in Khorasan and on the Eastern Frontier (pp. 94-96)

Jewish settlement in Khorasan increased considerably between the eighth and tenth centuries. Al-Muqaddasi, writing in 985 CE, states that "there are in Khorasan many Jews and only few Christians and various classes of Zoroastrians." By the tenth century, Jews could be located in many of the main cities: Nishapur, Maimanah, Herat, Kabul, Qandahar, Merv, with the largest concentrations in Balkh and Ghazna. Al-Tabari records a Jewish community in Merv as early as the eighth century. Judaeo-Persian inscriptions and business letters indicate established Jewish communities in Afghanistan and Chinese Turkestan from the eighth to twelfth centuries.

A revealing episode in the Tabaqat-i-Nasiri describes a Jewish merchant acquiring permission from the Shansabani ruler of Ghur (under Harun al-Rashid's arbitration) to settle immigrant Bani Israel in the territory of Ghuristan and Herat, in exchange for teaching the ruler Islamic court etiquette. This detail -- a Jewish merchant as cultural intermediary between a remote Afghan ruler and the Baghdad court -- illustrates the kind of multi-functional role diaspora merchants played.

**Scene potential:** When Yusuf travels to Merv, he would encounter a Jewish community already well established there. This enriches the portrait of Merv as a cosmopolitan crossroads -- not just Persian and Arab but also Jewish, with business letters in Judaeo-Persian moving along the same routes as Yusuf's paper. The anecdote about the Jewish merchant teaching court *adab* to an Afghan prince -- brokering culture as well as goods -- resonates with Yusuf's own role as a man who moves between worlds.

### The Persianisation of the Caliphate (pp. 17-21)

The Abbasid revolution began in eastern Persia and was heavily dependent on the dihqan aristocracy of the region, whose Persian identity was accommodated through a Shi'a version of Islam. Throughout the eighth and ninth centuries, the Mediterranean coast continued to diminish in relative importance as the massive weight of Persia pulled the Abbasid empire eastward. From its Arab roots, the Islamic conquest state shifted to a Persianised foundation.

Wink describes this process as gradual but profound. Early Islam rejected the imperial civilisation of Persia as illegitimate, but already in the seventh century, Persian principles of government were being applied in Iraq. The Sasanid bureaucratic system was reconstituted by Ziyad under Mu'awiya (661-680). Building projects introduced the architectural forms of authoritarian rule inspired by Sasanid antecedents: urban citadels, walls and gates, audience halls. Under the Abbasids, Damascus was abandoned for Baghdad, founded in 762 near the former Sasanid capital of Ctesiphon. The Umayyad dynasty was replaced by the Abbasids and their Islamised Persian supporters -- predominantly Khorasanis -- creating what Wink calls "a Persian empire in Islamic garb," most decisively in the reign of Harun al-Rashid (786-809).

Indian knowledge was also transmitted during this period: numerals, arithmetic, mathematics, philosophy, medicine, pharmacology, astronomy, and astrology. Chess was brought from India. Al-Biruni noted that Indian works translated into Arabic under the early Abbasid caliph al-Mansur (754-775) and the Barmakid viziers of Harun al-Rashid included the *Brahmasiddhanta* (known as the *Sindhind*) and the *Pancatantra*. The Barmakids specifically patronised this translation activity.

**Scene potential:** The Barmakid connection to Indian intellectual transmission adds a layer to the novel's portrayal of the Barmakid household as cultural patrons. When Yusuf enters the Barmakid orbit in Book 2, he enters a world that is actively importing Indian knowledge -- not just commodities. The "Persian empire in Islamic garb" formulation captures the political world Yusuf navigates: formally Arab, functionally Persian, commercially connected to India and China.

## [RESEARCH NEEDED]

- Wink's discussion of the Persianisation of the caliphate under the Abbasids provides a useful macro-framework, but the specific mechanisms of Harun's "judicious coalition management" in Khorasan are not detailed here. Cross-reference with Kennedy's works for the political narrative.
- The book's treatment of Merv is structural rather than descriptive -- it tells us Merv was one of the four capital cities of Khorasan but provides no physical description of the city, its markets, or its layout. The research gap on Merv city layout (noted in `research-index.md`) remains open.
- Wink cites Louis Massignon's argument about the Jewish origins of corporate international finance, which is now considered a controversial and outdated thesis. The factual details about Jewish *jahabidha* in Baghdad are well-sourced, but the claim about Jewish "control" of the Abbasid money market should be treated with caution. Cross-reference with Goitein (already digested) for a more nuanced view.
- The book's treatment of the India trade goods list (pp. 60-63) is drawn primarily from geographical literature of the 10th century and later (al-Idrisi, al-Muqaddasi). The degree to which these same goods and volumes applied in the late 8th century (Yusuf's period) is unclear. The trade was likely somewhat smaller and less diversified under the early Abbasids than in the 10th-century peak.
- The Parsi (Zoroastrian) trading diaspora section (pp. 104-108) was not extracted in this digest. It may be relevant to the novel's Zoroastrian background characters in Khorasan, particularly if expanded world-building is needed for the Merv journey.
