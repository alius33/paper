# Digest: Baghdad During the Abbasid Caliphate

**Author:** Guy Le Strange (1854--1933)
**Source:** `reference/baghdad-during-abbasid-caliphate--le-strange.pdf`
**Originally Published:** Oxford, Clarendon Press, 1900. Revised second edition: London, Oxford University Press, H. Milford, 1924.
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** Primary geographic reference for Baghdad's layout, districts, canals, and infrastructure -- essential for grounding all Baghdad scenes. The single most important scholarly reconstruction of the medieval city's topography.

## Overview

Guy Le Strange's *Baghdad During the Abbasid Caliphate: From Contemporary Arabic and Persian Sources* is the foundational scholarly reconstruction of medieval Baghdad's physical geography. First published in 1900, it remains the standard reference for understanding the layout, quarters, canals, bridges, palaces, and infrastructure of the Abbasid capital from its founding in 762 CE through the Mongol destruction in 1258. Le Strange was a British Orientalist who studied Arabic and Persian at the College de France in Paris and spent years travelling in Persia and the Middle East. His particular genius was in synthesising the accounts of medieval Arab and Persian geographers -- above all Ibn Serapion (c. 900 CE), al-Ya'qubi, al-Khatib al-Baghdadi, Yaqut al-Hamawi, and Ibn Jubayr -- into a coherent topographical picture, accompanied by eight reconstructed plans of the city.

The book is organised topographically rather than chronologically. After three chapters on the founding and design of al-Mansur's Round City, Le Strange works systematically through western Baghdad's canals and quarters (Chapters 4--12), then eastern Baghdad (Chapters 13--21), before closing with three recapitulatory chapters covering the early, middle, and late Abbasid periods. The approach is methodical and source-critical: Le Strange cross-references multiple authorities, flags contradictions, and traces the courses of canals and roads with a surveyor's precision. The result is an irreplaceable reference for any fiction set in this city.

For *The House of Paper*, Le Strange provides the geographic skeleton on which every Baghdad scene hangs. The book establishes precise relationships between the Khorasan Gate, the bridge of boats, the Tigris, the Karkh markets, the canal network, the Harbiyah military quarter, the palaces, and the eastern suburbs of Rusafah and Shammasiyah. It is the difference between writing Baghdad as a vague backdrop and writing it as a city whose streets, waterways, and sightlines the reader can feel.

## Key Extracts

### 1. The Round City: al-Mansur's Design (Chapters 1--3)

The Round City (*Madinat al-Mansur*) was founded in 762 CE and completed by 766 CE. Its design was a perfect circle approximately 2,500--2,700 metres in diameter, with a circumference of roughly four miles. Al-Ya'qubi states the distance from one gate to its opposite was 5,000 cubits, measured from outside the moat.

Three concentric walls defined the city's structure. The outermost was a defensive rampart surrounded by a deep, water-filled moat fed from the Tigris. The main wall, built of baked brick, rose to a reported height of 80--90 feet (sources differ), with a foundation 105 feet thick. It was crowned with battlements and flanked by 113 bastions. Between the outer and main walls lay the *fasil*, an empty killing ground wide enough for cavalry. A third, inner wall enclosed the central administrative precinct.

The residential population occupied the annular space between the main wall and the inner wall -- the "Residential Annulus." This ring was divided into four quadrants by straight avenues running from each gate toward the centre. The avenues were lined with vaulted arcades (*taqat*) originally intended for markets. These arcades were emptied of commerce in 773 CE when al-Mansur ordered the markets relocated to the Karkh suburb to the south, for reasons of security.

At the very centre stood two structures: the Great Mosque and the Palace of the Golden Gate (*Qasr Bab al-Dhahab*), also called the Palace of the Green Dome. Le Strange, drawing on al-Khatib al-Baghdadi, describes the palace as covering approximately 200 yards square. Its central feature was a great dome, green in colour, that soared to a height of 120 feet above ground level. Atop the dome stood the figure of a mounted horseman bearing a lance -- a weathervane said to turn toward the direction from which the Caliph's enemies would next appear. The dome was visible from miles across the plain and served as the city's defining landmark.

The central precinct was separated from the residential ring by a vast, open gravel plaza (*rahba*). No one was permitted to ride a horse across this space; all visitors dismounted and approached on foot. The emptiness was deliberate -- a declaration of absolute power rendered in open ground.

**Scene potential:** The Green Dome and its horseman figure are a powerful visual anchor for any scene set in or approaching Baghdad. Yusuf would have grown up seeing this dome from the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood -- turquoise-green tiles shifting from emerald at noon to dark jade at twilight. The gravel plaza and the dismounting rule offer a visceral way to convey the awe and fear of approaching the seat of power, useful for any later scene in which Yusuf is summoned to the palace.

### 2. The Four Gates (Chapter 3)

The four gates pierced the walls at regular intervals, each named for the road it commanded:

- **Bab al-Kufa** (southwest) -- toward Kufa and the pilgrimage road to Mecca
- **Bab al-Basra** (southeast) -- toward Basra and the Persian Gulf
- **Bab al-Sham** (northwest) -- toward Anbar, Syria, and Damascus
- **Bab al-Khorasan** (northeast) -- toward the Great Khorasan Road, Persia, and Central Asia

Each gate was a monumental defensive complex: twin towers of baked brick flanking an archway that soared some fifty feet high, with a bent entrance passage designed to slow attackers. Above the archway, a reception hall (*majlis*) with large windows allowed officials to oversee traffic. The doors were forged of solid iron, so heavy that dozens of men were needed to open and shut them. A legend held that the iron doors of the main gates were ancient work, crafted for King Solomon and transported by river from Wasit at al-Mansur's command.

The **Bab al-Khorasan** was the paramount gate. It faced northeast, toward the province whose soldiers had powered the Abbasid revolution. The Khuld Palace was situated near it, and the Great Khorasan Road -- the empire's vital artery to the east -- began here. The gate lay close to the Tigris, and the main bridge of boats crossed the river from near this point to the eastern suburb of Rusafah.

**Scene potential:** The Khorasan Gate is the geographic heart of Yusuf's childhood. Ali's tilework adorns it. Every departure and arrival in Book 1 passes through or near it. The iron doors groaning at dawn and dusk, the tax collectors on their stools, the multilingual chaos of arriving caravans -- these are the daily textures of Yusuf's world. The tile fragment Yusuf picks up during the siege (Book 4) falls from this gate.

### 3. The Khuld Palace (Chapter 3)

In 773 CE, al-Mansur began construction of the Khuld Palace (*Qasr al-Khuld*, "Palace of Eternity") on an elongated, mile-long stretch of land between the walls of the Round City and the western bank of the Tigris. The palace and its extensive gardens occupied the space between the Khorasan Gate and the western entrance of the main bridge of boats. Al-Mansur chose this site because the Tigris bank was at its highest here, protecting the palace from the river's ubiquitous insects.

Le Strange notes that while the Palace of the Golden Gate remained the official caliphal residence, Harun al-Rashid preferred the Khuld and lived there for the most part when staying in Baghdad. His son al-Amin, however, returned to the Palace of the Golden Gate. Both palaces suffered catastrophic damage during the siege of 812--813, struck by stone missiles from mangonels. The Khuld was likely in ruins by the siege's end, though at least one source suggests al-Ma'mun may have stayed there briefly when he finally reached Baghdad in 819 CE.

**Scene potential:** The Khuld Palace's proximity to the Khorasan Gate means Yusuf grew up in the literal shadow of the caliphal residence. Harun's preference for the Khuld places the Caliph close to Yusuf's neighbourhood -- important for the night-walk scenes in Book 3. The palace's destruction during the siege provides a powerful visual for Book 4: the building Yusuf has known all his life reduced to rubble.

### 4. The Canals of Western Baghdad (Chapters 4--7)

Le Strange devotes four chapters to the canal system of western Baghdad, reconstructed primarily from Ibn Serapion's account (c. 900 CE). Two great canals dominated the western city:

**The Nahr Isa (Isa Canal):** A navigable canal linking the Euphrates to the Tigris, dug at the time of Baghdad's founding by an Abbasid prince named Isa (identity disputed -- either al-Mansur's uncle Isa ibn Ali or his nephew Isa ibn Musa). This was the main commercial highway from the west. Trade caravans brought goods from Syria and Egypt to Raqqah in Upper Mesopotamia, where they were loaded onto barges and floated down the Euphrates and through the Isa Canal to the emporia of Karkh. The canal provided half of western Baghdad's water supply (the Dujayl Canal provided the rest). In the early tenth century, the Nahr Isa was crossed by nine masonry bridges (*qantarah*), each named for a nearby market or landmark.

**The Nahr al-Sarat (Sarat Canal):** A major branch of the Nahr Isa, splitting off at al-Muhawwal. The two canals ran in parallel into western Baghdad. The Sarat separated the quarters of Qattrabul (north) and Baduraya (south) before joining the Tigris directly south of the Basra Gate. Le Strange notes that while barges passed freely down the Nahr Isa from the Euphrates to the Tigris, the Sarat's weirs, dams, and waterwheels made it impassable for large boats. Both canals discharged into the Tigris at two separate ports: the Upper Fardah and the Lower Fardah (wharves/landing places).

A subsidiary canal, the **Karkhaya**, branched from the Nahr Isa and subdivided into smaller channels that criss-crossed the Karkh suburb, providing water to its markets, baths, and gardens.

**Scene potential:** The canal network gives Baghdad its distinctive geography. A paper mill requires abundant water -- the canals explain why workshops cluster where they do. The Isa Canal as a commercial highway means barges arriving from Syria and Egypt would be a daily sight. The sound of waterwheels on the Sarat, the smell of canal water (mud, reeds, refuse), the stone bridges with their market stalls -- all provide sensory anchoring for scenes set in western Baghdad.

### 5. The Karkh Suburb and Markets (Chapters 5--7)

Karkh was the great commercial suburb south of the Round City. The name derives from the Syriac *Karkha*, meaning "fortified town" -- it was a small pre-Islamic settlement around which al-Mansur built his capital. In 773 CE, al-Mansur ordered the markets expelled from the Round City to Karkh for security reasons, and the suburb became Baghdad's commercial heart.

Le Strange describes Karkh as a labyrinth of specialised markets (*suqs*). Among those he identifies:

- **Suq al-Attarin** -- the Perfumers' Market
- **Suq al-Warraqin** -- the Booksellers' and Stationers' Market
- **Suq al-Bazzazin** -- the Textile Market
- **Suq al-Kasrakin** -- a major book and paper market near the Lower Harbour, with over one hundred booksellers' shops concentrated along the roadway between two landmarks and even on the bridge itself
- The Date Market, Cotton Market, Tuesday Market
- The Needle-makers' Wharf
- The Market of Shawk-Sellers (thorn vendors selling kindling for ovens and hammam furnaces), located near the Thorn Bridge over the Nahr Isa

The Karkh fire is recorded as destroying seventeen thousand people, three hundred shops, many houses, and thirty-three mosques -- a detail that conveys the suburb's density and scale.

Le Strange identifies the Lower Harbour area as particularly relevant to the book trade. The *Suq al-Kasrakin* -- with its hundred-plus booksellers' shops -- was near the junction of the Sarat Canal and the Tigris, close to the wharves where goods arrived by river.

**Scene potential:** Chapter 5 (Suq al-Warraqin) is already set in this world. The hundred booksellers' shops at the Kasrakin market, the Thorn Bridge with its kindling vendors, the specialised markets arrayed along canal-side streets -- these details can deepen the texture of any scene set in Karkh. The fire detail (seventeen thousand dead, three hundred shops) conveys the terrifying density of a medieval commercial district built largely of wood and reeds.

### 6. The Harbiyah Quarter (Chapter 10)

The Harbiyah quarter lay to the northeast of the Round City -- the military suburb where the Khorasani soldiers, backbone of the Abbasid revolution, were settled. Le Strange describes it as including:

- The **Marwrudiyya** division (for settlers from Marw al-Rudh)
- A suburb of the **Furus** ("Persians," possibly people from Fars)
- A suburb for the **Khwarezmians**
- A mosque dedicated to the people of **Bukhara**

When the future caliph al-Mahdi moved from Rayy to Baghdad in 768 CE, a second wave of Persian military settlers arrived and was quartered here. Noble Iranian families, including the Barmakids (from Balkh), resided in or near this quarter.

The Harbiyah was thus a distinctly Persian-Khorasani neighbourhood, where Persian was the dominant language among the military elite. Its character was martial and ethnically defined -- a garrison suburb rather than a commercial district.

**Scene potential:** The Harbiyah quarter is the social world of Yusuf's half-brother Omar and the Khorasani military class. Its ethnic subdivisions (Marwrudiyya, Khwarezmian, Bukhari) give it texture beyond "military suburb." The fact that the Barmakids lived in this area connects Yusuf's neighbourhood to the family whose patronage and destruction drive Book 2. Omar's pride in his Khorasani identity is rooted in a specific place, not an abstraction.

### 7. The Bridge of Boats and the Tigris Crossing (Chapters 3, 13)

The Tigris was crossed by floating pontoon bridges -- large wooden barges moored side by side, linked by chains, and covered with a wide roadway of planks and packed earth. The main bridge stood near the Khorasan Gate, connecting the Round City to the eastern suburb of Rusafah via the Great Khorasan Road. Under Harun al-Rashid, there were three bridges in Baghdad.

Le Strange notes that these bridges could be cut from their moorings in times of attack, and the canals similarly served as natural barriers. During the siege of 812--813, the destruction of the pontoon bridges was a critical military event: Tahir ibn Husayn convinced Baghdad's merchants to destroy the bridges, severing communication between the defending forces and breaking the stalemate.

The bridge was a chaotic chokepoint -- a slow-moving procession of camels, mules, and pedestrians, constantly swaying with the river's current, managed by bridge-wardens trying to maintain order.

The river itself carried diverse traffic: large flat-bottomed barges poled upstream with grain and stone from the north; elegant *shadhawat* with carved prows and silken awnings ferrying the wealthy; and the distinctive *quffas*, perfectly round coracle-like boats made of woven reeds waterproofed with bitumen, spinning and bobbing in the current.

**Scene potential:** The bridge of boats is where Yusuf crosses between the western and eastern halves of his city. Its swaying, the press of bodies and animals, the bridge-wardens' shouts -- this is a recurring transit scene. The various boat types (barges, *shadhawat*, *quffas*) offer visual variety for any river scene. The destruction of the bridges during the siege is a pivotal moment in Book 4.

### 8. Eastern Baghdad: Rusafah and Shammasiyah (Chapters 13--15)

Le Strange describes eastern Baghdad as standing "opposite and as it were balancing the City of Mansur on the western side." The eastern bank developed around three suburbs: **Rusafah**, **Shammasiyah**, and **Mukharrim**, all founded during al-Mansur's reign. The catalyst was al-Mansur's construction of a palace for his son and heir al-Mahdi on the eastern bank in 768 CE, which drew a wave of settlers.

Rusafah soon rivalled the Round City in wealth, with markets, gardens, and palaces. The eastern quarters were more organic in layout than the geometrically planned Round City -- streets followed the curve of the river rather than radiating from a centre. Residential *mahallas* (neighbourhoods) were often organised by tribe, ethnicity, or profession, including distinct quarters for Christians, Jews, and various Arab and Persian groups.

The Shammasiyah quarter lay north of the main Rusafah area and was associated with the military cantonment of al-Mahdi's army. The Mukharrim quarter lay between Rusafah and Shammasiyah.

Le Strange notes that the caliphal residence did not shift permanently to the eastern bank until after the civil war and the period at Samarra (836--892 CE), when al-Mu'tadid established the "Abode of the Caliphate" (*Dar al-Khilafat*) on the east bank using the former Hasani Palace (originally a Barmakid pleasure house) as its nucleus.

**Scene potential:** Eastern Baghdad is where diversity is most visible -- the mosaic of ethnic and religious *mahallas*, each with its own character. The organic street layout contrasts with the geometric rigidity of the Round City. For the novel, Rusafah represents the commercial and residential world beyond the military-administrative western bank -- the world where Yusuf's business empire will eventually operate.

### 9. The Siege of Baghdad, 812--813 (Chapters 22--24)

Le Strange's recapitulatory chapters cover the siege as part of his account of the early period's end. The historian Hugh Kennedy, building on Le Strange's topography, characterised the siege as "an episode almost without parallel in the history of early Islamic society."

The siege lasted from August 812 to September 813. Most of Baghdad's population lived in suburbs that were not enclosed by walls, so the siege was not an assault on a fortified perimeter but rather street fighting, house-to-house invasion, and improvised fortifications. It was extremely destructive to the civilian population. Both sides used mangonels (stone-throwing siege engines). Al-Amin's commander al-Samarqandi mounted mangonels on boats and bombarded enemy positions from the Tigris, inflicting more damage on civilians than on the besiegers.

The stalemate was broken when Tahir ibn Husayn persuaded Baghdad's merchants to destroy the pontoon bridges, severing communication between the resisting forces. Al-Amin attempted to flee by boat, was captured, and was executed in September 813. His head was displayed on the Anbar Gate.

The Round City, the Khuld Palace, and the Karkh suburb all suffered severe damage. Le Strange's topographical chapters note the physical destruction of buildings, canals, and infrastructure. Baghdad would not fully recover; when al-Ma'mun finally arrived in 819 CE, the city's centre of gravity had already begun shifting to the eastern bank.

**Scene potential:** The siege is the climax of Book 4. Le Strange's topography makes the street fighting concrete -- which quarters were attacked, which canals served as defensive barriers, which bridges were cut. The detail about mangonels mounted on boats is vivid and specific. Al-Amin's attempted escape by boat and execution provide historical fixed points around which Yusuf's fictional actions must be woven. The destruction of the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood -- Omar's defence of it -- can be grounded in the actual pattern of damage Le Strange reconstructs.

### 10. The Palaces of Harun al-Rashid's Era (Chapters 17--19)

During Harun's reign (786--809 CE), the caliphal establishment was divided between the Khuld Palace near the Khorasan Gate (which Harun preferred) and the Palace of the Golden Gate at the Round City's centre. Harun also spent extended periods at Raqqa in Syria (from 796 CE) and at Anbar, restless and dissatisfied with Baghdad.

The Barmakid vizier Ja'far ibn Yahya built a pleasure house in the open country south of the populated quarters of eastern Baghdad -- a residence for his famous revels, including wine-drinking. After the Barmakid purge of 803 CE, this property was confiscated and eventually became the nucleus of the Hasani Palace, which later caliphs expanded into the main east-bank caliphal complex.

**Scene potential:** Harun's preference for the Khuld means Yusuf's most important patron-caliph lives in his neighbourhood. Ja'far's pleasure house across the river, where the revels take place, gives a physical location for Book 2's Barmakid scenes -- a location that will later be transformed into the seat of caliphal power, a geographic echo of how the Barmakids' world was appropriated after their destruction.

## Facts and Dates

- **762 CE:** Al-Mansur founds the Round City; construction begins 2 August 762 under four architects, with a reported workforce of 100,000 men and expenditure of 18 million gold dinars (or 100 million silver dirhams). (Ch. 1)
- **763 CE:** The Palace of the Golden Gate and the Great Mosque completed; al-Mansur takes up residence. (Ch. 1)
- **766 CE:** Round City construction completed. (Ch. 1)
- **768 CE:** Al-Mahdi's palace built on the eastern bank; Rusafah begins to develop. Second wave of Persian military settlers arrives in the Harbiyah. (Chs. 10, 13)
- **773 CE:** Markets expelled from the Round City to Karkh. Construction of the Khuld Palace begins. (Chs. 3, 5)
- **775 CE:** Khuld Palace inaugurated. (Ch. 3)
- **Round City diameter:** approximately 2,500--2,700 m (sources vary); circumference approximately 4 miles.
- **Main wall height:** 80--90 feet (24--27 m), depending on source. Foundation thickness: 105 feet.
- **Outer wall:** approximately 60 feet high, with 113 bastions.
- **Palace of the Golden Gate:** approximately 200 yards square; Green Dome rose to 120 feet above ground.
- **Khuld Palace:** occupied a mile-long stretch between the Round City walls and the Tigris bank.
- **Bridges of boats:** Under Harun al-Rashid, three pontoon bridges crossed the Tigris. The main bridge near the Khorasan Gate connected to the Great Khorasan Road.
- **Nahr Isa:** Navigable canal linking Euphrates to Tigris; crossed by nine masonry bridges in the early 10th century.
- **Nahr al-Sarat:** Major branch canal; not navigable for large boats due to weirs and waterwheels. Upper and Lower Fardah (wharves) at its junction with the Tigris.
- **Karkh fire:** Destroyed 17,000 people, 300 shops, 33 mosques (date unspecified in the extract -- likely 10th century).
- **Suq al-Kasrakin:** Over 100 booksellers' shops near the Lower Harbour.
- **Siege of Baghdad:** August 812 -- September 813. Al-Amin executed September 813. Mangonels mounted on boats. Pontoon bridges destroyed by merchants. (Chs. 22--24)

## Relevance to Chapters

- **Book 1, childhood (Khorasan Gate):** Le Strange establishes the precise geography of Yusuf's world -- the Khorasan Gate as the northeastern entry, the Khuld Palace stretching between the gate and the Tigris, the Harbiyah military quarter nearby, the bridge of boats visible from the gate area. The 773 CE market relocation to Karkh occurred when Yusuf was three years old, meaning his conscious childhood was spent in a post-commercial, military-administrative neighbourhood -- quieter, more regimented, dominated by soldiers rather than merchants. This shapes his early perception of power.

- **Book 1, apprenticeship (paper workshop):** The canal system explains the location of craft workshops that depend on water (paper-making requires large quantities). The Nahr Isa, the Sarat, and the Karkhaya canal network in Karkh provide the water infrastructure for Abu Said's workshop. The proximity of the Suq al-Warraqin (booksellers' market) and the Suq al-Kasrakin (100+ booksellers near the Lower Harbour) establishes the commercial ecosystem in which a paper mill would operate.

- **Book 1, journeys:** The Great Khorasan Road begins at the Khorasan Gate -- Yusuf's caravan departures start here. The Kufa road departs from the Bab al-Kufa. The Nahr Isa canal is the main avenue for goods arriving from Syria and Egypt, via Raqqah and the Euphrates -- Yusuf would see this traffic daily.

- **Book 1, Suq al-Warraqin (Chapter 5):** Le Strange's identification of multiple paper and book markets in Karkh -- including the hundred-shop Kasrakin market near the Lower Harbour -- gives depth to the commercial world Yusuf enters as a seller.

- **Book 2, the Barmakids:** Le Strange locates the Barmakids in the Harbiyah quarter (near the Khorasan Gate) and Ja'far's pleasure house on the eastern bank. This geography places the Barmakids physically close to Yusuf's world, making the connection plausible without contrivance.

- **Book 3, Harun's night walks:** Le Strange confirms Harun's preference for the Khuld Palace, which neighbours the Khorasan Gate area. The night walks can trace specific streets and cross specific bridges.

- **Book 4, the siege:** Le Strange's topography makes the siege three-dimensional. The destruction of the pontoon bridges, the street fighting in unwalled suburbs, the mangonels on boats, the damage to the Khuld Palace and the Round City -- all can be rendered with geographic specificity. Omar's defence of the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood is grounded in real patterns of destruction. The tile fragment falling from the Khorasan Gate gains physical reality.

## [RESEARCH NEEDED] Flags

- **Paper mill locations:** Le Strange does not specifically identify the location of Baghdad's paper mills. The first mill is dated to c. 794--795 CE, but its precise quarter is not established in this source. The project places Abu Said's workshop in Karkh near the canals, which is plausible given the water requirements, but this should be cross-referenced against al-Tha'alibi and other sources on early paper production in Baghdad.

- **Karkh fire date:** The devastating fire that destroyed 17,000 people and 300 shops in Karkh is cited but the exact date needs verification. If it falls within the novel's timeline (770--814 CE), it could be a significant event; if later, it still conveys the suburb's character and vulnerability.

- **Wall measurements:** Sources give conflicting figures for the Round City's wall heights (60 ft vs. 80 ft vs. 90 ft for different walls; 24 m vs. 30 m). Le Strange cites multiple authorities. The project's existing research files use 90 feet for the main wall, which aligns with one set of Le Strange's sources. The discrepancy should be noted but is not a fiction-breaking problem -- the point is that the walls were extraordinarily tall.

- **Number and location of bridges under Harun:** Le Strange states three bridges under Harun al-Rashid. The main bridge was near the Khorasan Gate. The locations of the other two should be confirmed -- one was likely near the Basra Gate/Sarat Canal junction, and a third possibly further north. This matters for scenes involving movement across the Tigris.

- **Sarat Canal navigability:** Le Strange notes the Sarat was not navigable for large boats due to weirs and waterwheels, while the Nahr Isa was fully navigable. This distinction could affect the plausibility of any scene involving boat traffic near Abu Said's workshop, depending on which canal it fronts.

- **Ja'far's pleasure house location:** Le Strange places it "in the open country, at some distance to the south from the populated quarters of East Baghdad." The exact site is uncertain but it eventually became the Hasani Palace. This needs more precise geographic pinning for Book 2 scenes.
