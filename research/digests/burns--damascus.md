# Digest: Damascus: A History (Cities of the Ancient World)

**Author:** Ross Burns
**Source:** `reference/damascus--burns.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-05
**Relevance:** The only comprehensive English-language history of Damascus, providing physical description, architecture, market layout, and the city's political atmosphere under early Abbasid rule -- essential for Yusuf's Journey 4 (~788 CE).

## Overview

Burns traces the physical development of Damascus from its emergence around 7000 BC through to the Syrian civil war, using the city's surviving monuments and archaeological evidence as the primary source material. The book is organised chronologically through 16 chapters, covering Aramaean, Persian, Greek, Roman, Byzantine, Umayyad, Abbasid, Crusader, Mamluk, Ottoman, and modern periods. Burns served as Australian Ambassador to Syria (1984-1987) and later completed a PhD at Macquarie University on Roman colonnaded streets, giving him both scholarly rigour and first-hand familiarity with the city's fabric.

For this project, the book's principal value lies in three areas. First, the physical setting of Damascus -- the Barada River, the Ghouta oasis, the approaches from the desert -- provides the sensory framework for Yusuf's arrival from the east after weeks of desert travel. Second, the Umayyad chapter (Ch 9) describes in rich architectural detail the Great Mosque and the surrounding urban fabric that would still dominate the city in 788 CE, only 38 years after the Abbasid takeover. Third, the Abbasid chapter (Ch 10) documents the deliberate degradation of Damascus under the new dynasty: the destruction of Umayyad tombs, the dismantling of city walls, the conversion of the caliphal palace into a prison, and the sullen, rebellious atmosphere of a city that never accepted its masters. This political mood is exactly what Yusuf would encounter on his trade journey.

The book is less useful for commercial detail (markets, caravansaries, trade goods) since Burns focuses on monumental architecture rather than everyday economic life. For those details, other sources will be needed. But as a guide to what a visitor would *see* and *feel* walking into Damascus in the late 8th century, it is indispensable.

## Key Extracts

### The Approach from the Desert (Physical Setting)

Damascus sits on the edge of a harsh steppe, sustained by two assets alone: water and human ingenuity. The Barada River, fed by snowmelt from the Anti-Lebanon mountains, rushes through a confined gorge and spills onto the plain, creating the Ghouta oasis. Burns describes the eastern approach as the most dramatic: after weeks of desert travel through monochrome wilderness, the eye is suddenly feasted by green fertility emerging through the mirage. The senses reawaken with the sound of water and its cooling humidity. The fertility becomes all-enveloping until the city is reached, nestled against the backdrop of the Anti-Lebanon range (pp. 1-2).

The climate is characterised by a short winter and a long summer bringing five months of unbroken sunshine. Average rainfall is only 220mm per year -- barely enough for agriculture -- but the Barada's spring thaw compensates generously. The river's channels were engineered over millennia to distribute water through the oasis, which the 12th-century traveller Ibn Jubayr described as enclosing the city "like the halo round the moon" (p. 5). By summer, with average temperatures over 35 degrees, the extensive marshes east of the city dried to patches of fetid moisture (p. 5).

**Scene potential:** Yusuf's first glimpse of Damascus after the desert crossing -- the shock of green after brown, the sound and smell of water, the sudden sensory overload of the oasis -- is one of the novel's set-piece moments. The contrast between desert silence and oasis abundance can parallel his inner transition from the known world of Baghdad to the wider empire.

### The Umayyad Great Mosque (Still Dominant in 788)

The Great Mosque of Damascus, completed under Caliph al-Walid I (r. 705-715), was the most richly decorated building since ancient Rome and would have been the overwhelming architectural presence when Yusuf visited. Burns details how the Umayyads acquired the site by stages: Mu'awiya took the eastern half of the old Cathedral of Saint John compound, while the western half remained Christian. Al-Walid negotiated (or imposed) full acquisition, demolishing the Christian church and building the mosque within the ancient Roman temple's temenos walls (pp. 138-141).

The construction adapted the Roman basilica plan to Islamic worship, creating a prayer hall divided by columns into three aisles running parallel to the qibla wall. A transept cut across the centre, creating a dramatic focal point. The building retained the towers from the Roman temple corners, which became forerunners of minarets (pp. 141-143). But what made the mosque extraordinary was its decoration: some 40 tonnes of glass and stone mosaic cubes (12 tonnes in green alone) covered every surface visible from the courtyard, depicting a fantastic garden of identifiable Ghouta vegetation -- figs, almonds, pomegranates, pears, cypresses -- interspersed with elaborate architectural fantasies (pp. 144-146). Each cube was carefully angled to catch the light. The effect was a vision of Paradise: exotic yet familiar, drawing gasps from viewers. Burns notes that nothing to rival it had been seen until then, and nothing since (p. 147).

The mosque complex extended south to the caliphal palace, al-Khadra, which Mu'awiya had established using the confiscated Byzantine governor's residence. A covered processional way linked palace to mosque, allowing the caliph to pass directly from his residence to the prayer hall (p. 143).

**Scene potential:** Yusuf entering the Great Mosque courtyard for the first time -- the shimmer of 40 tonnes of angled mosaic catching the light, the trees of the Ghouta rendered in glass, the scale of imperial ambition made visible in stone and gold. For a papermaker's apprentice from Baghdad, this is a confrontation with a different kind of craft: craft deployed not for commerce but for awe. The mosque also embodies the Umayyad world that the Abbasids destroyed -- a physical reminder of political impermanence that would resonate with the novel's themes.

### Abbasid Degradation of Damascus (750 Onward)

The Abbasid takeover was devastating for Damascus. Burns documents a systematic campaign to humiliate the former capital. The Umayyad family was hunted down and massacred in Palestine in 750. The caliphs' tombs in the Bab al-Saghir cemetery were opened, the remains of Hisham, 'Abd al-Malik, and Mu'awiya desecrated and scattered. All references to their burial places were removed (p. 159). The city walls were torn down. Damascus was downgraded to a provincial town of minor importance (p. 159).

The caliphal palace al-Khadra was converted into a prison. The Abbasid governors established a new residence outside the south-western walls near the Jabiya Gate, protected by tribal allies deployed in the Ghouta and the Hauran (pp. 161-162). Later, in 831, Caliph al-Ma'mun built a residence at Deir Murran on the slopes of Mount Kassyun north of the city (p. 162). The Abbasid caliph al-Mutawakkil (r. 847-861) tried moving his capital to Damascus but lasted only 38 days, driven away by the city's summer winds, humidity, high prices, and fleas (p. 162).

The Great Mosque was spared -- it represented the triumph of Islam itself, not merely the Umayyads -- but virtually nothing else was built in Damascus for three centuries. The one exception is a small treasury pavilion (Beit al-Mal) erected in 788 by the Abbasid governor al-Fadl ibn Salih ibn 'Ali in the western end of the mosque courtyard. This hexagonal chamber stands on eight reused Roman columns with their original capitals intact, decorated with mosaics echoing the courtyard's decoration (p. 160).

**Scene potential:** The ruined city walls, the converted palace-prison, the defaced tombs, the new governor's compound outside the walls -- all of this tells Yusuf about the cost of political failure. Damascus in 788 is a humiliated city, a warning about what happens when power shifts. The Beit al-Mal, built in the very year Yusuf might visit, could serve as a detail he witnesses under construction -- new Abbasid authority marking itself on the only building the conquerors left standing.

### The Mood of Sullen Revolt

Burns characterises Damascus under the Abbasids as a hotbed of agitation. The policy of appointing commanders from Khurasan intensified the sense of alienation, underlining that Syria was a conquered province. In the 770s, prolonged fighting erupted around Damascus after something as trivial as a watermelon stolen from a Yamani's garden. White banners of opposition to the Abbasids were unfurled repeatedly. The mood of revolt continued for many decades, often taking on a tribal dimension (Qays vs. Yamani), though successive caliphs still passed through the city with court panoply (p. 161).

The Abbasids favoured the Yamanis over the Qays. The 744 civil war had starkly pitted the two factions against each other, and the Yamanis allegedly opened the gates of Damascus to the Abbasid general. But trying to play off tribal favourites only raised tensions further. The city never accepted its new masters -- it remained a place of sullen resistance throughout the 8th century (pp. 161-162).

**Scene potential:** This atmosphere of resentment and tribal tension is precisely what a young merchant from Baghdad (the Abbasid capital) would walk into. Yusuf arrives representing -- however unwittingly -- the power that destroyed this city's pride. He would need to navigate tribal loyalties, read the mood of markets where anti-Abbasid sentiment simmers, and understand that not everyone in the empire views Baghdad as the centre of civilisation. A formative political education.

### The Roman Street Grid Beneath the Arab City

Burns traces how the Roman grid plan of Damascus gradually disintegrated, beginning not with the Arab conquest but in the Byzantine period. City government weakened; building regulations fell into disuse; funds were diverted to defence. The leading social group shifted from idle gentry to merchants and craftsmen. The Roman drive for straight lines and broad thoroughfares lapsed when the emphasis was on sales and profits. The use of wheeled transport died out as the camel became the prime means of goods transport, and streets once required for carts could now be much narrower. Grand avenues were constricted by parasitic stalls (pp. 110-111).

The transformation from Roman cardo to Arab suq is documented in a diagram (p. 111) showing four stages: (1) original colonnaded walkways with wheeled traffic; (2) shops built into the colonnade spaces; (3) a double suq; (4) a triple suq. The Straight Street (Darb al-Mustaqim, the biblical "street called Straight") preserved the Roman east-west axis but by the 8th century was thoroughly encrusted with market stalls. The Greek Orthodox Patriarchate occupied ground at the midpoint of Straight Street where churches had stood for as long as records exist (p. 110). The outer compound of the old Roman temple (the "Gamma," shaped like the Greek letter) had become an extensive shopping complex with arcaded halls, dividing worshippers entering the mosque from the commercial precinct (pp. 111-112).

**Scene potential:** Yusuf walking the Straight Street -- still recognisably the Roman axis but buried under layers of commercial activity. The ancient columns supporting market awnings, the narrowing of once-grand avenues, the shops pressed into every available space. This layered quality of Damascus (Roman bones, Byzantine alteration, Arab overlay) contrasts with Baghdad, which was built from scratch on a geometric plan. Damascus teaches Yusuf that cities -- like empires -- are palimpsests.

### Christian Communities Under the Abbasids

In the early Abbasid period, the Christian community (divided between Orthodox adherents of Constantinople, Monophysites, and a restored Nestorian presence) was largely left to its own devices, as under the Umayyads. After 800, a perceptible dwindling began: the number of churches in Palestine in 813 was only half those in use in 602. The conversion process gathered pace in the 9th century as the greater part of the Arab population went over to Islam, driven primarily by tax advantages. Non-Arab base populations held out longer. Ironically, the increasing adoption by the Christian church of an Arab identity ensured its survival (p. 162).

**Scene potential:** Yusuf encountering Damascus's Christian quarter -- churches still functioning but diminishing, the sound of church bells competing with the adhan, the visible presence of a faith community that predates Islam in this city. This diversity reinforces the novel's themes of religious coexistence and the practical accommodations that commerce requires across confessional lines.

### The Umayyad Legacy as Living Memory

Burns notes that the ʿAbbasids' fear of a Umayyad return was not paranoia. A ten-year-old Umayyad prince, 'Abd al-Rahman, escaped his pursuers by swimming the Euphrates, eventually founding a dynasty in Spain that perpetuated Syrian traditions in architecture and culture. The belief that descendants of Mu'awiya would return to restore the caliphate persisted; in 811, "Sufyani" followers attempted exactly this when Abbasid control weakened after Harun al-Rashid's death (p. 159). The late Umayyad caliphs, especially Walid II, had drifted into libertine aesthetics, drinking wine and writing poetry: "Leave me Sulaymah, wine, a girl to sing, a cup: / That's wealth enough for me!" (p. 150).

**Scene potential:** In Damascus, Yusuf might hear whispered stories of the Umayyad exile in Spain, the hope of restoration, the nostalgia for a golden age. This is a city that remembers what it was. The Umayyad nostalgia could surface in conversations with local merchants who resent their Khorasani overlords -- adding a political dimension to Yusuf's commercial education.

### The Barada River and the Ghouta Oasis

The Barada is the lifeblood of Damascus. Burns quotes an ancient source: the river "lavishes her life in the creation of a single city and straightaway dies in the face of the desert" (p. 4). The river rushes down a confined gorge from the Anti-Lebanon, and human intervention over three millennia tamed its extravagance into a system of canals irrigating the Ghouta, a ring of orchards and fields surrounding the city. The oasis included figs, almonds, pomegranates, apples, pears, and cypresses -- the same species depicted in the Great Mosque's mosaics (p. 146). South and east of Damascus, the flatness of the steppe stretches endlessly to Arabia, broken only by ridges, lava-strewn wildernesses, and the occasional dead core of a volcano (p. 2).

**Scene potential:** The sensory experience of crossing from desert to oasis -- the first sound of running water after weeks of dust, the smell of wet earth and fruit trees, the sudden cool of irrigated air. The Barada channels running through the city itself would be a constant aural presence, unlike Baghdad's more sedate canal system. The oasis vegetation matches the mosque mosaics, creating an intentional echo between human craft and natural paradise.

- Umayyad Great Mosque construction: under Caliph al-Walid I, completed c. 715 CE (p. 140)

- Damascus fell to the Abbasids: 25 April 750 (p. 151)
- Umayyad tombs desecrated: remains of Hisham, 'Abd al-Malik, and Mu'awiya scattered (p. 159)
- City walls torn down under Abbasid rule (p. 159)
- Caliphal palace al-Khadra converted to a prison (p. 161)
- Abbasid governor's new residence: outside south-western walls near Jabiya Gate (p. 161)
- Beit al-Mal (mosque treasury pavilion): erected 788 CE by governor al-Fadl ibn Salih ibn 'Ali (p. 160)
- Al-Ma'mun built residence at Deir Murran on Mount Kassyun slopes, 831 CE (p. 162)
- Al-Mutawakkil's aborted move to Damascus: lasted only 38 days (c. 858 CE), driven away by summer winds, humidity, high prices, and fleas (p. 162)
- Number of churches in Palestine halved between 602 and 813 CE (p. 162)
- Damascus arms industry: inherited from Roman-era armoury (one of five in the Oriens group of provinces), became renowned through Arab and Turkish centuries for decorated swords and inlaid knives (p. 109)
- Watermelon revolt: prolonged fighting in the 770s triggered by a stolen watermelon from a Yamani garden (p. 161)
- 'Abd al-Rahman's escape: Umayyad prince swam the Euphrates c. 750, reached Spain after five-year odyssey (p. 159)
- Straight Street: Roman east-west axis (Darb al-Mustaqim), Greek Orthodox Patriarchate at midpoint (p. 110)
- The "Gamma" shopping precinct: arcaded halls in the outer temple compound, providing sheltered approaches to the mosque (pp. 111-112)
- Byzantine governor's palace: built after 495 in south-eastern corner of outer temple enclosure (p. 112)

## Relevance to Chapters

- **Book 1, Journey 4: Damascus (age 18, ~788 CE)** -- Primary relevance. The physical setting (desert approach, Ghouta oasis, Barada channels), the architectural landmarks (Great Mosque, Straight Street, ruined walls, governor's compound), and the political atmosphere (sullen revolt, tribal tensions, Khorasani garrison, anti-Abbasid sentiment) all directly serve this chapter. The Beit al-Mal construction date of 788 is a remarkable coincidence with Yusuf's likely visit date.
- **Book 1, general** -- Damascus as a lesson in political impermanence: a city that was the capital of the world 40 years ago, now a resentful provincial town. This supports the novel's themes about the fragility of power and the cost of ambition.
- **Books 2-3** -- Background knowledge of Damascus's continued decline and the Umayyad nostalgia movement provides context for any references to Syrian trade, politics, or the "Sufyani" revolts that destabilise the western provinces.
- **Book 4 (Civil War, 809-813)** -- The 811 Sufyani attempt to profit from Abbasid weakness after Harun's death could be referenced as a destabilising factor in the empire's western provinces during the civil war.
