# Digest: Postal Systems in the Pre-Modern Islamic World

**Author:** Adam J. Silverstein
**Source:** `reference/postal-systems--silverstein.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-05
**Relevance:** The definitive study of the *barid* (postal/intelligence service) -- the caliphal surveillance and communications network that is critical infrastructure throughout the novel, used by Yusuf for commercial intelligence and by the state for espionage.

## Overview

Silverstein traces the evolution of Near Eastern postal systems from the Achaemenid *angareion* through the Byzantine *Cursus Publicus*, the Umayyad and Abbasid *barid*, the Mongol *yam*, and the Mamluk *barid*. His central argument is that each successive state adapted postal technology to its own political needs, and that the particular form a postal system took reveals deep truths about the nature of the state that maintained it. The Abbasid *barid* is the book's fulcrum: it was the first postal system in Islamic history to be centralised under its own bureau (*diwan al-barid*), funded from the Treasury rather than by requisitions from the population, and staffed by civilian administrators rather than soldiers.

For this project, the critical chapters are Chapter 2 (the early Islamic postal system, pp. 53-89) and Chapter 3 (the Middle Abbasid *Diwan al-Barid*, pp. 90-140). Chapter 2 covers the Umayyad and early Abbasid periods, including the crucial role of the Barmakids in re-establishing the *barid* under Harun al-Rashid. Chapter 3 covers the mature Abbasid system: its routes, stations, personnel, intelligence functions, and -- critically for the novel -- the rise of private merchant postal networks that both complemented and competed with the official system. The Appendix on distances and speeds (pp. 191-193) provides concrete data on how fast information moved across the caliphate.

The book is a compact scholarly monograph (210 pages including apparatus) but extraordinarily rich in primary-source quotations, many translated into English for the first time. Silverstein draws on Arabic literary sources, papyri from Egypt, and the mid-tenth-century administrative manual *Siyasat al-Muluk*, which provides the most detailed surviving description of the *barid*'s day-to-day operations.

## Key Extracts

### The Barmakids and Harun's *Barid*

The traditional account, preserved by al-Umari, holds that the Abbasid *barid* was dormant from al-Saffah through al-Mahdi -- that "no saddle was strapped and no mount was bridled for the Barid" until al-Mahdi temporarily established relay stations between himself and his son Harun, who was campaigning against the Byzantines. When Harun became caliph, Yahya ibn Khalid al-Barmaki told him that re-establishing the *barid* "would be beneficial to his rule," and Harun ordered Yahya to organise it "in the manner that it functioned during the days of the Umayyads." Silverstein argues this account is misleading: papyri and literary sources show the *barid* functioning under early Abbasid caliphs, but the tradition correctly identifies the Barmakids as key figures in systematising the postal network under Harun. Yahya ibn Ja'far al-Barmaki was placed in charge of the *barid* throughout the caliphate (*barid al-afaq*). (Ch. 2, pp. 53-54, 88)

The sharp decline of the *barid* after the Barmakid fall from grace in 803 CE demonstrates how pivotal they were to its operations. During the civil war between al-Amin and al-Ma'mun (809-813), control of the *barid* was a strategic weapon: Harun's succession document stipulated that al-Ma'mun would retain control of the postal system in Khurasan, which enraged al-Amin, who tried to send his own postal agent to the region. When al-Ma'mun cut off the *barid* between Khurasan and Baghdad, he both deprived his brother of intelligence and openly signalled his disobedience. (Ch. 2, pp. 88-89)

**Scene potential:** The Barmakid management of the *barid* places Yusuf's patrons at the heart of the empire's intelligence apparatus -- and its collapse after 803 creates an information vacuum that a resourceful private operator like Yusuf could exploit.

### Physical Infrastructure: Routes, Stations, and Mounts

The *barid* operated along imperial highways measured in miles (*mil*), parasangs (*farsakh*, equalling 3 miles or roughly 6 km), and stages (*marhala*). Stations (*sikak*, sing. *sikka*) were spaced at intervals of 2 parasangs (12 km) in Syria and Khurasan, and 4 parasangs (24 km) in the desert and Iraq. Not all roads had postal stations; the *sikak* covered only a fraction of the routes described by geographers. By the mid-ninth century, Ibn Khurradhadhbih counted 930 stations across the caliphate, costing 159,100 dinars annually in mounts, equipment, and salaries. (Ch. 2, pp. 64-65; Ch. 3, pp. 93-97)

The stations themselves were simple constructions -- no clearly identifiable Abbasid *sikka* has survived archaeologically. Each station was required to maintain at least two saddled mules, ready on the road, so that when a courier-guide (*furaniq*) called out, the fresh mount could meet him and the mail-bag (*kharita*) could be exchanged without stopping. The maximum weight a postal mule could carry, besides its rider, was 40 *ritls* (about 16 kg, using the Baghdadi measurement). Mules were the standard mount because they combined the sure-footedness of donkeys with the speed of horses. (Ch. 3, pp. 97-98, 104)

Ordinary correspondence was despatched twice per week. Urgent items were sent immediately via express couriers (*banadira*, sing. *bundar*) -- a term and technique that al-Ma'mun appears to have imported from Khurasan. The regular mail-bag was black silk; its opening was sealed with a tassel in a palm-leaf bag. Special mail-bags were distinguished by colour: al-Mutawakkil once sent a yellow *kharita* to Mecca with news of the new moon sighting. (Ch. 2, p. 83; Ch. 3, pp. 104, 106)

**Scene potential:** The physical detail of the station -- two mules saddled on the road, the courier calling out, the black silk mail-bag with its palm-leaf seal -- is directly usable for any scene where Yusuf encounters the *barid* on the road or in a city.

### Speeds and Distances

According to the *Siyasat al-Muluk*, a mail-bag carried by relays of courier-guides could cover 60 parasangs (360 km) in a single day. This required passing through up to 30 stations. Silverstein's appendix compiles recorded speeds for specific routes:

- **Baghdad to Mecca** (*kharita al-mawsim*, the pilgrimage mail-bag): 4 days for approximately 1,650 km
- **Jurjan to Baghdad**: 13 days for approximately 2,000 km (al-Mahdi's journey, about 150 km/day)
- **Samarra to Baghdad**: 1 hour 20 minutes for approximately 180 km (during al-Mu'tasim's campaigns)
- **Damascus to Samarra**: 6 days for approximately 1,200 km overland (about 200 km/day)
- **Damascus to Baghdad** (merchants' letters): 3 weeks for approximately 1,200 km (60-80 km/day -- considerably slower than the official *barid*)

A contemporary source claims that during Harun al-Rashid's reign, pigeon-couriers between Baghdad and Mecca could cover 30 stages per day (about 720 km), compared to 8 stages for swift she-camels. However, most routine *barid* traffic moved at far more modest speeds; the extraordinary figures were noted precisely because they were exceptional. (Appendix, pp. 191-193; Ch. 3, pp. 97, 113)

**Scene potential:** The 3-week delay for merchants' letters versus the 6-day speed of the official *barid* is the key asymmetry that Yusuf could exploit: if he can find ways to get information faster than the merchant networks but without access to the official *barid*, he has a decisive commercial advantage.

### The *Barid* as Intelligence Network

The *barid* was never merely a postal service. From the earliest Umayyad period, it functioned as the caliph's intelligence apparatus. Al-Mansur made "excessive use of the Barid in spying on his subjects." Local postal chiefs (*ashab al-barid*) were required to gather information "both covertly and overtly" and to report on a wide range of matters: unscrupulous officials, uprisings, deaths of important individuals, rebel movements, the spread of rumours, weather conditions, local prices, changes in climate, and the state of cultivation. (Ch. 3, pp. 101-105)

Multiple overlapping intelligence systems existed. The *Siyasat al-Muluk* instructs the king to "plant spies overtly and covertly, amongst the general population and the elite, so that whatever happens publicly will reach him, and whatever happens privately will not be hidden from him." The wazir must appoint a special *sahib khabar* (intelligence chief). The *sahib diwan al-kharaj* (tax bureau) must independently report on local conditions. And the local *sahib al-barid* must compose independent reports on prices, climate, and security -- serving as a check on the tax officials' reports. (Ch. 3, pp. 100-101)

This created a web of competitive surveillance. Tanukhi preserves an account from al-Mu'tadid's court where the caliph rebuked his wazir al-Qasim ibn 'Ubaydallah based on informants' reports that the wazir was neglecting work for wine and slave-girls. The rattled wazir then dispatched his own spies to uncover the caliph's source, illustrating a web of intrigue where everyone spied on everyone else. (Ch. 3, p. 100)

**Scene potential:** The competitive espionage between caliph and wazir is the world Yusuf navigates in Books 2-3. The detail that postal chiefs must independently verify tax officials' reports mirrors the kind of cross-checking intelligence work Yusuf performs. The *sahib al-barid* as "the eye of the sultan" is a phrase that could appear in dialogue.

### The Postal Chief (*Sahib al-Barid*): Qualifications and Duties

Most administrative centres had a resident *sahib al-barid*. Silverstein documents postal chiefs in Mosul, Mecca, Medina, Kufa, Basra, Egypt, Falluja, al-Ahwaz, Dinawar, Khurasan, Qinnasrin, the frontier regions, and Baghdad. The primary qualification was trustworthiness. Some were distinguished scholars: Ibn Khurradhadhbih (the geographer), Ya'qubi (the historian), and the poet Abu Tammam all served as postal chiefs. Others received the post as a reward or a bribe: one poet was given it to blunt his satirical tongue. (Ch. 3, pp. 102-108)

Local postal chiefs served as official witnesses in politically sensitive situations. When Itakh was killed, the local *sahib al-barid* officially witnessed the death. When the deposed caliph al-Musta'in was placed under guard, the postal chief was his custodian. When al-Mutawakkil sent a craftsman to repair the Ka'ba after storm damage, the postal chief accompanied him inside the holy building. Postal chiefs also served as arbiters in tax disputes, examining claims alongside other upstanding community members. (Ch. 3, pp. 105-106)

The Postmaster-General (*Sahib Diwan al-Barid*) in the capital was responsible for receiving all incoming provincial reports, summarising them for the caliph, supervising the courier-guides, endorsers, and station-masters throughout the system, and ensuring payment of salaries. Abu al-Malik ibn Muhammad al-Zayyat held the position for thirty years under both al-Mu'tadid and al-Muqtadir. His successors were eunuchs: Shafiq al-Lu'lu'i served for ten years overseeing the *barid* as well as surveillance of the wazir, the army, all bureau chiefs, judges, and police. (Ch. 3, pp. 102-103, 108-109)

**Scene potential:** The *sahib al-barid* stationed in every city Yusuf visits is a character type waiting to be used -- a literate, well-connected official who knows everything that happens locally and reports it all to Baghdad. For Yusuf, befriending or compromising such a figure would be an intelligence coup.

### Four Thousand Unopened Mail-Bags: The Aftermath of Harun's Death

Al-Jahshiyari reports that four thousand unopened mail-bags were discovered in the aftermath of Harun al-Rashid's death in 809. This detail indicates the enormous volume of correspondence flowing through the *barid* during a caliph's reign. Even at the rate of twice-weekly dispatches from provincial postal chiefs, the figure suggests regular correspondence from dozens of cities over many years -- or a catastrophic backlog in Harun's final years when he was increasingly focused on the succession crisis and the campaign in Khurasan. (Ch. 3, p. 104)

**Scene potential:** The image of thousands of unopened mail-bags -- intelligence reports no one ever read -- is a powerful symbol of imperial overreach and the gap between information and understanding. A scene where Yusuf learns of this detail could crystallise his insight that information is worthless without the capacity to act on it.

### Merchant Postal Networks: The *Kutub al-Tujjar*

From the late ninth century onward, private merchant letter networks (*kutub al-tujjar*, "letters of the merchants") increasingly supplemented and even competed with the official *barid*. The phrase occurs in several key episodes:

When Tahir ibn al-Husayn, serving as governor in Khurasan, deliberately omitted the caliph's name from the Friday sermon, the local *sahib al-barid* warned him that even if the postal system did not report it, merchants' letters were "uninterrupted between here and Baghdad" and the news would reach the caliph from other sources. (Ch. 3, p. 118)

In 901, merchants' letters from Raqqa brought Baghdad news that Byzantines had attacked and carried off fifteen thousand Muslims. In 903, merchants' letters from Damascus arrived in Baghdad three weeks after dispatch, reporting Qaramita victories. Baghdadi merchants gathered with the letters and went to the *qadi*, who promised to inform the wazir. In another account, a courier (*fayj*) was intercepted by a depressed merchant travelling from Raqqa to Baghdad, who peeked at merchants' letters revealing that olive oil prices had risen sharply and made a profit of 10,000 dinars by acting on the information. (Ch. 3, pp. 118-120)

Merchants were known as "the *barids* from the outlying regions" (*al-tujjar burud al-afaq*) -- a phrase that appears in both the Byzantine envoy's warning to al-Mansur and in the *Siyasat al-Muluk*. The relationship between trade and intelligence was openly acknowledged: the wazir was advised to "treat merchants kindly, for they are the *barids* from the outlying regions." (Ch. 3, pp. 117-118)

**Scene potential:** This is the infrastructure Yusuf builds -- a private commercial intelligence network that rivals the official *barid*. The phrase *al-tujjar burud al-afaq* could be the motto of the House of Paper's intelligence operation. The olive-oil anecdote shows exactly the kind of information arbitrage Yusuf would practise. The three-week merchant delay versus the six-day *barid* speed is the gap he must close.

### Rebels and the Vulnerability of Routes

Rebels routinely sabotaged the postal network. Bedouin "cutting roads" -- especially pilgrimage routes -- is described as ubiquitous in the sources, even during periods of relative peace. During the Zanj revolt, al-Muwaffaq had to repeatedly repair roads and highways before mounting an effective defence. The Zanj leader himself, lacking a *barid*, was reduced to entrusting letters to peasants and sending elderly men as scouts -- one of whom never returned. (Ch. 3, pp. 92-93)

The *barid* was "considerably easier to disrupt than it was to maintain." This fragility was the system's defining vulnerability, and controlling or disrupting the postal routes was a strategic weapon during every major revolt. During the civil war of 809-813, al-Ma'mun's cutting of the *barid* between Khurasan and Baghdad was the first overt act of rebellion. (Ch. 2, pp. 88-89; Ch. 3, p. 92)

**Scene potential:** The vulnerability of the *barid* to disruption is directly relevant to the civil war in Book 4, where the breakdown of communications isolates Baghdad. For Yusuf, who depends on information flow, the cutting of routes would be an existential threat -- and the fact that merchant networks could still function when the official *barid* could not would be his survival advantage.

### Funding: A Caliphal Innovation

The Abbasid *barid* was funded from the Treasury, not by requisitioning mounts and labour from the local population. This was a genuine innovation. The Byzantine *Cursus Publicus* and the Sasanid postal system both relied on corve labour and special taxes that were deeply unpopular. Under Hisham (r. 724-743), four million dirhams of Treasury funds were first invested in the *barid*'s infrastructure. By the Middle Abbasid period, the system was entirely state-funded: local *'amil al-kharaj* (tax officials) drew up three separate accounts for the *barid* covering mule expenses, station maintenance, and courier-guide salaries. Grooms who looked after postal mounts earned 150 dinars per month (or 5 dinars per day) under al-Mu'tadid. (Ch. 2, pp. 87, 104)

**Scene potential:** The cost of maintaining the *barid* -- 159,100 dinars annually for 930 stations -- places it in the same financial universe as Yusuf's commercial operations. The detail about groom salaries (5 dinars/day) provides a concrete sense of the economics.

## Relevance to Chapters

- **Book 1, Ch 1-3 (Kufa journey):** The *barid* route from Baghdad to Kufa runs parallel to the commercial road. Yusuf would see *barid* stations and courier-guides on the road. The *sahib al-barid* in Kufa is a potential character -- an official who knows everything happening in the city. The station detail (two mules saddled on the road, the *furaniq* calling out) is scene-ready for any road sequence.
- **Book 1, all journeys:** Every route Yusuf travels has *barid* stations. The spacing (12 km in most regions) means he passes one every few hours. Couriers overtaking caravans -- moving at 150-360 km/day versus the caravan's 25-40 km/day -- would be a vivid recurring image. The contrast between the speed of state information and the slowness of commercial travel is a core insight Yusuf internalises.
- **Book 1 (founding the House):** The merchant postal networks (*kutub al-tujjar*) provide the model for what Yusuf builds. The phrase "the merchants are the *barids* from the outlying regions" could be his founding insight.
- **Book 2, Barmakid connection:** The Barmakids managed the *barid* under Harun. Yusuf's access to the Barmakids could mean indirect access to intelligence flowing through the postal system -- a huge commercial advantage and a dangerous one.
- **Book 2, The Great Gamble (803):** The Barmakid fall would have caused the *barid* to deteriorate sharply. Yusuf, forewarned, could have already built alternative intelligence channels. The 4,000 unopened mail-bags discovered after Harun's death (809) suggest systemic intelligence failure in the post-Barmakid years -- the kind of vacuum Yusuf would fill.
- **Book 3, House of Gold (803-809):** Yusuf's private intelligence network gains value precisely as the official *barid* weakens. The caliph's own inability to process intelligence (Harun's unopened mail-bags) contrasts with Yusuf's smaller, focused commercial intelligence operation.
- **Book 4, Civil war (809-813):** Al-Ma'mun's cutting of the *barid* between Khurasan and Baghdad is a pivotal strategic move. The collapse of road security during the civil war, with Bedouin and *'ayyarun* disrupting routes, would devastate the *barid* infrastructure. Yusuf's survival depends on maintaining alternative channels -- pigeons, merchants, personal contacts -- when the official system fails.

The *Siyasat al-Muluk* instructs the wazir to "treat merchants kindly," suggesting a symbiotic relationship, but formal access is not documented.
- **Pigeon networks in 8th-century Baghdad:** Silverstein notes widespread official use of pigeon-couriers from the late 9th century onward, but their availability for private use in Harun's era is unclear. The novel's timeline (770-814) falls before the major expansion of pigeon networks documented in the sources.
