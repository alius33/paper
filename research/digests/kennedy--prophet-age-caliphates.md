# Digest: The Prophet and the Age of the Caliphates

**Author:** Hugh Kennedy
**Source:** `reference/prophet-age-caliphates--kennedy.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** The standard political history of the Abbasid caliphate, covering the revolution, court factions, Barmakid ascendancy, Harun al-Rashid's reign, and the catastrophic civil war of 809-813 CE -- the entire political backbone of the novel's four books.

## Overview

Kennedy's *Prophet and the Age of the Caliphates* is the most widely used English-language survey of the political history of the Islamic Near East from the seventh to the eleventh century. Now in its fourth edition (2022), it benefits from decades of revised scholarship, including updated discussions of the early Islamic economy. The book is organized chronologically, with a narrative style that tracks power through the movements of armies, the choices of caliphs, and the ambitions of administrative families.

For this project, three chapters are essential. Chapter 5 ("The Early Abbasid Caliphate") covers the revolution, the founding of Baghdad, the caliphates of al-Mansur, al-Mahdi, al-Hadi, and Harun al-Rashid, the rise and fall of the Barmakids, and the civil war between al-Amin and al-Ma'mun. Chapter 6 ("The Middle Abbasid Caliphate") covers the post-civil-war settlement under al-Ma'mun and the rise of the Turkish military -- useful context for the novel's epilogue. Chapter 7 ("The Early Islamic Economy") provides the economic framework: the fiscal engine of the caliphate, Baghdad's food supply, the agricultural expansion of Greater Mesopotamia, and the monetary system that undergirds Yusuf's financial world.

Kennedy is particularly strong on the structural dynamics of Abbasid politics: the tension between the Khorasaniyya military and the *kuttab* (bureaucratic secretaries), the role of palace servants and chamberlains (*hajib*) in controlling access to power, and the factionalism that made succession arrangements so fragile. His account of the civil war is the most useful single narrative for the novel's final book.

## Key Extracts

### The Khorasaniyya: Military Foundation of the Dynasty

Kennedy makes clear that the Abbasid revolution was carried to victory by the Muslims of Khurasan, who formed a distinct military-political class -- the Khorasaniyya -- that became the backbone of the new state. After the revolution, these troops settled in Baghdad and other garrison cities. They were paid salaries and were available for military service, but also engaged in commerce and property (p. 150-151). Their descendants became known as the *abna' al-dawla* ("sons of the dynasty"), and by Harun's time they were a settled urban military class, distinct from both the Arab tribal aristocracy and the Persian administrative elite (pp. 148-149).

The Khorasaniyya were of mixed Arab and Persian heritage. Many leaders were of Arab descent but spoke Persian and had intermarried with local people (p. 141). They were experienced frontier soldiers, having fought long wars against Turkish raiders, and their military skill was demonstrated by the series of victories they won during the revolution (p. 141).

**Scene potential:** Yusuf's family identity as Khorasaniyya -- Persian-Arab frontier people who rode the revolution to Baghdad -- is precisely this class. Kennedy's description of their mixed identity, military heritage, and economic adaptation to city life directly informs the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood and Ali's status as a respected craftsman-veteran.

### The Founding of Baghdad

Al-Mansur began building Baghdad in 145/762 at the northern end of the Sawad, on the great road from Iraq to Khurasan. Kennedy emphasizes both strategic and economic motives: the site lay on the pilgrim route from eastern provinces, had excellent river communications with Basra to the south and the Jazira grain-growing areas to the north, and could import food in quantities impossible for a purely local hinterland to supply (pp. 149-150). The Round City was the administrative nucleus, containing the palace, mosque, and a garrison of picked troops, but most people lived in the proliferating suburbs beyond its walls, including the new quarters developed across the Tigris by al-Mahdi (p. 150).

Baghdad was founded explicitly to provide a base for the Khorasaniyya. Leaders were given plots of land to distribute to their followers and to rent to merchants. The caliph rewarded his followers with these economic opportunities tied to the court's presence (pp. 150-151). By the mid-ninth century, population estimates range from 300,000 to 500,000, at a time when London had fewer than 10,000 inhabitants (p. 149).

**Scene potential:** The economic logic of Baghdad -- a city built to reward revolutionary soldiers with proximity to power and commerce -- is the world Yusuf grows up in. His father's tile workshop, located near the Khorasan Gate, serves precisely the class Kennedy describes: Khorasani settlers building houses and mosques in a new city.

### The Barmakids: Rise and Fall

Kennedy's account of the Barmakids traces them from Buddhist shrine guardians near Balkh who converted to Islam in the Umayyad period and came west with the Abbasid armies. Yahya b. Khalid became tutor to the young Harun and was "virtually a father" to him (p. 152). The Barmakids provided political leadership for the growing class of *kuttab* (secretaries/bureaucrats), and it was through this alliance that the bureaucrats became a major force in the state (p. 152).

The first decade of Harun's reign (786-796) was "the decade of the Barmakids." Yahya, his brother Muhammad, and his two sons al-Fadl and Ja'far effectively monopolized the central administration. Their policy concentrated power at the centre in Baghdad: provincial governors were changed so frequently that chronicles often fail to record their names (p. 155). Fiscal centralization paralleled political centralization -- Egypt's governor was twice obliged to bring revenues and accounts to Baghdad in person for checking (p. 155).

The weakness of Barmakid policy was military. Many army leaders from al-Hadi's era had been disgraced with Harun's accession. When the Kharijite rebel Walid b. Tarif dominated the Jazira in 794, the Barmakids had no military force capable of opposing him. Harun was forced to recall the bedouin chief Yazid b. Mazyad al-Shaybani, one of al-Hadi's closest collaborators (pp. 155-156). After this, the Barmakid monopoly began to break.

The Barmakids also advocated a generous policy toward the Alids, reminiscent of al-Mahdi's approach. Their literary assemblies were notable for intellectual freedom. Kennedy suggests there may have been a political dimension: the Barmakids may have believed the dynasty should aspire to a more absolute religious authority of the kind Alid supporters advocated (p. 156).

The fall came in 187/803, linked to the succession arrangements. Harun had arranged at Mecca in 802 that his son Muhammad (al-Amin), backed by the *abna'*, would succeed as caliph, while his son Abd Allah (al-Ma'mun), under Barmakid influence, would rule an effectively autonomous Khurasan. The continuing power of the Barmakids was an obstacle to this arrangement: if Muhammad was to establish himself, they had to be removed. The purge occurred as soon as the court returned from Mecca (pp. 157-158).

**Scene potential:** The political logic of the Barmakid fall -- not personal caprice but structural necessity arising from succession politics -- is exactly what Yusuf must understand to profit from it. Kennedy's framing of the fall as the culmination of a slow decline (from 796 onward) rather than a sudden catastrophe gives Yusuf years to read the signals and prepare his "Great Gamble."

### Harun al-Rashid: The Man Behind the Legend

Kennedy is notably unsentimental about Harun. The caliph appears "as a curiously nondescript character" compared to his predecessors (p. 159). Unlike al-Mansur (famous for miserliness and cunning) or al-Mahdi (for openness and generosity), Harun had no clear distinguishing characteristics. He was content to let the Barmakids exercise power in his name for the first decade, and "even later on he seems to have taken little interest in the day-to-day business of government" (p. 159).

His later golden reputation is explained by contrast: the catastrophic civil war, the Turkish military takeover, and the economic decline of Iraq that followed his death. Harun's was the last reign when all the central Islamic lands were under caliphal control. There was a vast treasury surplus; no mutinies over unpaid troops; a magnificent court; golden dinars showered on poets. He led the *hajj* eight times -- the last reigning Abbasid caliph to do so (pp. 159-160). He also oversaw the Darb Zubayda, the pilgrimage road from Iraq to Mecca, the largest civil engineering project of the early Islamic period (pp. 160-161).

**Scene potential:** This portrait of Harun as a man whose greatness was partly retrospective illusion -- and whose personal role in governance was surprisingly passive -- is useful for Book 3's intimate scenes. The night walks, the proximity to power, the sense of an aging empire at its zenith just before collapse.

### Khurasan Under Harun: The Problem That Killed the Dynasty

After the Barmakids fell, the governorship of Khurasan was given to Ali b. Isa b. Mahan, a leader of the *abna'*, who exploited the province ruthlessly. Tax revenues were sent west to Baghdad to pay *abna'* salaries. Local notables complained. Kharijite rebellions escalated. In 805-806, a serious rebellion broke out in Samarkand led by Rafi' b. Layth (p. 158). Harun was forced to dismiss Ali b. Isa by stealth, sending his general Harthama b. A'yan to depose him. When the unrest continued, Harun decided to go east personally in 808 -- the only reigning Abbasid caliph ever to visit Khurasan. He died there, near Tus, in 809 (p. 159).

The core issue was control of provincial tax revenue: whether it should be spent locally or sent to Baghdad. This tension, present since the time of Uthman, was the immediate political cause of the civil war (pp. 158-159).

**Scene potential:** The Khurasan crisis is the geopolitical backdrop for Book 3. Yusuf, as chief *jahbadh*, would be directly involved in the financial infrastructure that funneled Khorasani revenue to Baghdad -- the very policy that provoked the rebellion. The tension between local spending and central extraction is a version of his own family's story.

### The Great Civil War: 809-813

Kennedy's account of the civil war is the most useful single narrative for Book 4. The key dynamics:

**The Factions.** Al-Amin, based in Baghdad, was backed by the *abna'* (the settled Khorasani military) and the *hajib* al-Fadl b. al-Rabi'. Al-Ma'mun, based in Merv, was backed by the Khorasani provincial magnates and guided by his *wazir* al-Fadl b. Sahl, a man of Persian origin from the Sawad of Iraq who had risen through Barmakid patronage (pp. 161-162).

**The Provocation.** Al-Amin, under pressure from Ali b. Isa b. Mahan and al-Fadl b. al-Rabi', demanded control of Khorasani revenue. In November 810, he had his own son Musa acknowledged as heir, removing al-Ma'mun from the succession. In January 811, Ali b. Isa was appointed governor of Khurasan with an army of 40,000 *abna'* -- the biggest army Baghdad had ever seen -- equipped with silver chains to secure al-Ma'mun's person (p. 162).

**The Reversal.** Against this massive force, al-Ma'mun sent Tahir b. al-Husayn with fewer than 5,000 men. Tahir came from a family of Arab origin settled in Khurasan since Umayyad times, governing the town of Bushang near Herat. In May 811, Tahir defeated Ali b. Isa at Rayy. The *abna'* prestige was destroyed beyond repair (pp. 162-163).

**The Siege of Baghdad.** By summer 812, Tahir and Harthama were preparing to attack Baghdad itself. Al-Amin, losing *abna'* support, armed the ordinary civilian population. These irregular fighters were contemptuously called *ayyarun* ("vagabonds") -- the urban proletariat, not property owners or merchants, but people who sold trifles on the streets. Some left remarkable poems glorying in their humble origins, with helmets of palm leaves and "armour" of wool (pp. 163-164).

**The Fall.** The siege lasted just over a year. In September 813, Tahir persuaded richer citizens to cut the pontoon bridges across the Tigris. Al-Amin tried to surrender to Harthama, but Tahir's men overturned the boat, and al-Amin was captured and executed. Kennedy notes: the prestige of the Abbasid family "never fully recovered from the murder of the caliph" (p. 164).

**Scene potential:** Every detail here feeds directly into Book 4 -- Tahir's improbable victory, the *ayyarun* defending Baghdad, the cutting of the pontoon bridges, al-Amin's capture and execution. The characterisation of the *ayyarun* as an urban underclass with their own poetry and anti-war sentiment is invaluable for the siege chapters. Omar defending his neighbourhood would be fighting alongside precisely these people.

### The Early Islamic Economy

Kennedy's economy chapter (Ch. 7) provides structural context. Baghdad's population of 250,000-500,000 was sustained by a monetary economy in which millions of gold dinars, silver dirhams, and copper *fulus* circulated to collect taxes, pay soldiers, and fuel commerce (p. 206). The fiscal structure -- taxation collected in coined money and distributed as salaries -- created the demand that drove economic diversification (p. 207).

Baghdad's food supply was a massive private-enterprise operation, not state-run like Rome's *annona*. Al-Mansur chose the site deliberately for access to Jazira grain (by river from the north) and Basra dates (from the south). India and China goods came up through the Gulf (p. 209). Labour wages consistently exceeded bare subsistence between c.700 and c.1000 CE, likely because of post-plague demographic decline (p. 209).

Iraq and adjacent Khuzistan supplied four times as much revenue to the Baghdad government as Egypt, the next most productive province, and five times as much as all of Syria and Palestine combined (p. 148).

**Scene potential:** The fiscal geography -- Iraq as the caliphate's breadbasket, Egypt as a secondary revenue source, Khurasan's contested revenues -- maps directly onto Yusuf's commercial world. His banking house would operate within this monetary infrastructure: collecting, transferring, and lending the coined money that the state apparatus generated and consumed.

## Relevance to Chapters

- **Book 1 (all):** Khorasaniyya identity, Baghdad's founding logic, economic opportunity for settler-craftsmen, political context of al-Mahdi's and early Harun's reigns. Barmakid rise provides backdrop for Abu Said's patronage connections.
- **Book 1, Ch 1-3 (Kufa journey):** Kennedy's note that Kufa was the original capital and centre of Alid sympathy informs the city's atmosphere and Umar's Shi'a connections.
- **Book 1 (political context):** The tension between military leaders and *kuttab*/secretaries that Kennedy traces from al-Mahdi's reign onward is the structural context for Yusuf's later career -- he rises through the bureaucratic/financial side, not the military.
- **Book 2 (Barmakid connection, Great Gamble):** Kennedy's detailed account of the Barmakid decade (786-796), their administrative centralization, their vulnerability after 796, and the succession politics that triggered their fall in 803. Essential for plotting Yusuf's intelligence-gathering and financial positioning.
- **Book 3 (Harun, Khurasan crisis):** Kennedy's portrait of Harun as a passive, nondescript ruler overshadowed by his legend; the Khurasan revenue crisis; Ali b. Isa b. Mahan's ruthless governorship; the rebellion at Samarkand. These are the political currents Yusuf navigates as chief *jahbadh*.
- **Book 4 (civil war, siege):** Kennedy's is the best single narrative source for the civil war sequence. The factions (abna' vs. Khorasani magnates), the battle at Rayy, the siege of Baghdad, the *ayyarun*, the pontoon bridges, and al-Amin's execution. Directly informs Omar's defence of the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood and Yusuf's double-dealing.
