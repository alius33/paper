## Overview

This is an A-Z encyclopedic handbook (369 pages, Brill, 2009) covering every aspect of the Arabic manuscript tradition: writing surfaces, inks, pens, scripts, bookbinding, illumination, scribal practices, colophons, watermarks, forgeries, and the physical archaeology of the codex. It is not a narrative history but a reference tool, organized as alphabetical entries with extensive cross-references, illustrations, and bibliography.

For this project, the book is invaluable in three ways. First, it supplies the exact Arabic terminology for every tool, material, and process in the paper workshop and the book trade -- the vocabulary that Abu Said, his apprentices, and the warraqin of Baghdad would actually use. Second, it provides granular physical detail about how paper was made, sized, burnished, cut, ruled, and bound in the early Abbasid period, detail that can be translated directly into scene-level sensory description. Third, its entries on watermarks, forgeries, and paper authentication are directly relevant to Yusuf's watermark innovation in Book 2, providing both the technical basis for the invention and the historical context that makes it plausible.

The book draws on primary Arabic sources including the 5th/11th-century *Umdat al-kuttab* attributed to Ibn Badis, al-Qalqashandi's *Subh al-a'sha* (9th/15th century), and al-Malik al-Muzaffar's 7th/13th-century *al-Mukhtara' fi funun min al-suna'*, which are the main surviving technical manuals on ink, paper, and bookbinding from the medieval Islamic world.

## Key Extracts

### Paper: Introduction and Spread

Paper was introduced to the Arab world around the mid-2nd/8th century. Arabic tradition associates this with the Battle of Atlakh on the river Talas in 133/751, when Muslim forces captured Chinese prisoners including papermakers, who were taken to Samarqand where the first paper mill was established. The use of paper spread quickly from Samarqand to Baghdad. In the reign of Caliph Harun al-Rashid (170/786-193/809), paper began to be used in government offices. Harun reportedly decreed that all documents in the chancery should be written on paper because it was much more difficult to erase writing from paper than from parchment without this being noticed (p.186). Paper was also considerably less expensive than parchment. Papermaking then spread to Damascus and Cairo in the 3rd/9th century. By the mid-4th/10th century, paper had supplanted papyrus in Egypt (pp.186-187).

Arabic literature records several terms for paper: *qirtas* (also *qirtas Samarqand*), *waraq* (*waraq Sini*, *waraq al-Sin*), and *kaghad*. The last term was probably borrowed from Chinese (*chuzhi* or *guzhi*) via Soghdian, from which it passed into Persian (p.186).

**Scene potential:** The Sogdian etymology of *kaghad* connects directly to Abu Said's Sogdian identity and the Central Asian origins of the craft; a moment where Abu Said explains the word's origin to Yusuf could ground the reader in the trade's living history.

### Paper: Materials and Techniques

The Chinese originally used mulberry bark. Arabic sources mention flax (*kattan*) and its products linen and rags, hemp (*qannab*) and its product rope, cotton (*qutn*), and the bast fibres of the fig tree (*mudakh*) as ingredients. Raw cotton was likely not used, but cotton products (rags) were. Silk products may have been used for the so-called *hariri* paper. One source mentions adding the pith of colocynth (*shahm al-hanzal*) during maceration of the pulp to protect the finished product against mice (pp.187-188).

Two types of moulds (*tabaq*, *tarhah*, *qalib*) were used: wove and laid. The wove mould had a coarsely woven cloth (or possibly silk) acting as a sieve attached to a wooden or bamboo frame. The laid mould was made with parallel rows of grass or reeds (producing laid lines, running vertically) tied together with horse hair lacings (producing chain lines, running horizontally). To the mould was attached a deckle. The best paper was often that in which the laid lines were close together and the fibres less visible. Moulds were invariably rectangular. Papermakers also commonly employed floating and supple moulds. To increase thickness, several layers of pulp may have been used (pp.187-188).

**Scene potential:** The physical details of mould construction -- grass, reeds, horse hair, bamboo -- and the distinction between wove and laid paper are perfect for a scene where Yusuf learns the craft. The vocabulary (*tabaq*, *tarhah*) gives Abu Said authentic speech.

### Paper: Sizing, Burnishing, and Finishing

Paper was usually sized (*saqy*, *'ilaj*, *taghriyah*) with rice or wheat or sorghum starch and well-burnished/polished (*tasqil*). Fish glue (*ghira' al-samak*) was also used. One source gives a recipe for a paste made of ground alum (*al-shabb al-Yamani*) mixed with sour milk (*laban*) used before sizing to prevent paper from attracting moisture (p.188).

The burnished or glazed paper was referred to as *masluh*, literally "made usable." Burnishing was done with a variety of instruments: a large glass bead (*kharazah*), smooth stone, wooden tablet (*lawh*), wooden handle of a tool or a folder (*nisab*). According to one source, burnishing could be done on a slab of marble (*marmarah*) using a piece of shell (*mahar*) (p.188).

Some papers, especially those for deluxe manuscripts and often of Iranian origin, were finished with egg-white or gum tragacanth applied with a brush. Some 9th/15th-century Ottoman manuscripts have a substantial amount of finish (coating) giving them a very glossy and creamy appearance (p.188).

**Scene potential:** The poisoned vat scene in Chapter 4 involves sizing; the specific substances (starch, fish glue, alum, sour milk) and the term *masluh* ("made usable") ground that scene in authentic practice. Burnishing with a glass bead or shell on marble is a vivid physical action for workshop scenes.

### Paper: Named Types and Sizes

Ibn al-Nadim's *Fihrist* lists the following paper names: *Fir'awni*, *Sulaymani*, *Ja'fari*, *Talhi*, *Tahiri*, and *Nuhi*. Al-Qalqashandi classifies papers by size, with the largest being *Baghdadi* paper, equated with *tumar*, used mostly for copying Qurans. Measurements for Baghdadi paper: *al-Baghdadi al-kamil* = 1099 x 733 mm; *al-Baghdadi al-naqis* = 977 x 699 mm. The smallest was *waraq al-tayr* ("bird paper") = 91 x 61 mm (pp.191-192).

The main characteristic of Arab/Middle-Eastern and Indian papers is the lack of watermarks, a feature so prominent in European papermaking. Arab paper was used from the second half of the 2nd/8th century until the end of the 7th/13th century. In Europe it was widely known as *charta damascena*, i.e. Damascus paper (p.188).

**Scene potential:** The named paper types (*Ja'fari*, *Sulaymani*, *Baghdadi*) give Abu Said's workshop a product vocabulary. The fact that Arab paper historically lacked watermarks is the key context for Yusuf's innovation in Book 2 -- he invents something that did not yet exist in the Islamic tradition.

### Watermarks: History and Significance

Watermarks are images on paper visible when held against light. The image is the thinner part of a sheet formed by the weight of the pulp pressing on an elevated wire design on the mesh of the mould. The wire design was originally placed in the middle of the mould. The earliest known watermark is the 1293 cross from Fabriano, Italy. Italian watermarked papers began to be used in Arabic manuscripts in the main Arab lands as early as the mid-8th/14th century. The main characteristic of Arab paper is the absence of watermarks (pp.290-292).

Imitation watermarks are marks made by pressing paper between a male die and flat polished steel, embossing the underside while the top remains smooth. Impressed watermarks are designs left by a rubber stereo placed against paper at press rolls -- of no security value as they can be applied at any time after manufacture (pp.130-131).

**Scene potential:** This is the critical extract for Yusuf's watermark innovation. Arab paper had no watermarks; the technology was European, arriving centuries later. Yusuf's invention of a wire design pressed into the mould -- a wave pattern representing the Tigris -- would be historically unprecedented in the Islamic context. The fact that the technique involves a wire design on the mould mesh gives a plausible mechanism: Yusuf could devise a thin wire shape attached to the mould surface, creating a thinner area in the finished sheet visible only when held to light. The distinction between genuine watermarks (formed in the mould) and imitation/impressed marks (applied after manufacture, with no security value) is relevant to the novel's theme of trust and authentication.

### Forgeries and Paper as Anti-Forgery Measure

Forgery (*tazwir*) was known from early Islam. Caliph Harun al-Rashid substituted paper for parchment in the chancery because it was more difficult to erase text written on paper without detection. Before paper, some caliphs preferred papyrus to parchment for the same reason (p.108).

The treatise *Umdat al-kuttab* attributed to Ibn Badis contains a section on how to make paper look old (*ta'tiq*). Forgery for financial profit thrived among book collectors. Pieces of calligraphy signed by famous masters would fetch high prices and were often forged. One notorious forger, Muhammad al-Ahdab (d. 371/981), was known as *al-Muzawwir* ("the forger") and could reportedly imitate any calligrapher's hand (pp.108-109).

**Scene potential:** Harun's decree on paper replacing parchment is a historical fact that could appear directly in the narrative -- Yusuf witnessing or hearing about this decree, understanding its implications for the paper trade. The existence of professional forgers and the *ta'tiq* technique (artificial ageing) enriches the world of the Suq al-Warraqin with an underworld of fraud.

### Ink: Types and Recipes

Early texts distinguish two terms: *midad* (carbon ink) and *hibr* (iron-gall ink). Ibn al-Nadim divides Quran scribes into those who wrote with *midad* and those with *hibr*. Over time this distinction blurred. Inks fall into four categories: carbon (soot) inks, iron-gall (tannin) inks, mixed (compound) inks, and incomplete inks (p.132).

The main ingredient of carbon ink was soot (*sukham*, *dukhan*), obtained by combustion of rice, olive, chick-peas, seeds of radish or flax, walnut, hazelnut, sesame oil, and even petroleum (*naft*). The soot was suspended in gum arabic (*samgh*), honey (*'asal*), and water. It was not permanent and could be washed off, so was not recommended for parchment (p.133).

Iron-gall ink was made by mixing gallnuts (*'afs*), vitriol (*zaj*), and gum arabic. Gallnuts are swellings produced by insects laying eggs under oak leaves. Vitriol was obtained from alum (*shabb*). When properly mixed, it produced a fine permanent black ink, but when improperly mixed produced a highly acidic ink which could burn through parchment or paper over time (*harq hibr*) (p.133).

Inks were sometimes perfumed using camphor (*kafur*) and musk (*misk*). Aloe (*sabir*) was added to ward off flies and worms. To dry freshly written text, scribes used sand or sawdust (*tarmil*, *tatrib*), sometimes mixed with gold or silver dust (p.134).

Special inks existed for specific purposes: inks for paper (*hibr al-kaghad*), inks for parchment (*hibr al-raqq*), inks of famous calligraphers, the professional scribe's ink (*hibr warraqi*), and the common people's ink (*hibr al-'ammah*). Secret inks existed too -- a turtle's gall bladder produced ink visible only at night (p.134).

**Scene potential:** The physical detail of ink-making -- grinding soot from sesame oil, suspending it in gum arabic and honey, the risk of acidic iron-gall ink burning through paper -- provides rich sensory material for workshop scenes. The hierarchy of inks (*warraqi* vs *'ammah*) reflects social stratification in the trade.

### The Reed Pen (Calamus / Qalam)

The traditional writing instrument was the reed pen, most commonly called *qalam*. Before use, the reed had to be pared or trimmed (*taqlim*, *bary*) to create a nib (*jilfah*, *anfah*). The nib was then slit at its end (*shaqq*), usually once in the middle, but for large scripts twice or three times. The nib was halved to create two half-nibs (*sinn*). To increase ink flow, a piece of reed bark skin (*litah*) was sometimes placed in the slit, a process called *taliyat* (pp.40-41).

The most important step was cutting the point of the nib (*qatt*, *qattah*). In the Muslim East, the point was cut either straight (*al-qatt al-mustawi*) or obliquely (*al-qatt al-muharraf*). Writing with an obliquely cut nib produced thinner strokes at angles. Nibbing was done on a nibbing block (*miqatt*, *miqattah*) made of ivory, hardwood, or animal bone. When not in use, some scribes rested their pens on a support called a *markaz*. For cleaning, a pen wiper of cloth (*daftar*, *mimsahah*) was used (pp.41-42).

Pens were kept in a writing case (*dawah*) or penbox (*miqlamah*). Ottoman penboxes were cylindrical brass tubes with screw-on lids. Iranian and Indian lacquer penboxes (*qalamdan*) consisted of two sliding pieces (pp.42, 293-294).

**Scene potential:** The sequence of preparing a reed pen -- paring, nibbing, slitting, cutting the point -- is a craftsman's ritual that could open or anchor a scene. The distinction between straight and oblique cuts, and the effect on the finished writing, reveals the kind of technical mastery Abu Said would teach.

### The Inkwell and Writing Case

The inkwell (*mihbarah*) had a round neck so ink would not dry in corners. In the neck was placed a tow of unspun silk or wool (*liqah*) to prevent spilling when the pen was dipped. The ink had to be stirred regularly with a spatula (*milwaq*, *mihrak*). According to Ibn Shith al-Qurashi (d. 625/1228), the scribe should inspect the inkwell every day, stir the ink, and wash the tow every 20 days (p.135).

A Mamluk royal writing case (Sultan Sha'ban I) was 30.5 cm long with four compartments: the largest for long implements (pens, ruler), two round ones for sand and paste/glue, and a semi-circular space for an inkwell. The writing case contained compartments for: reed pens (*miqlamah*), pen knife (*mudyah*), nibbing block (*miqatt*), ink (*mihbarah*), stirrer (*milwaq*), sand (*mirmalah*), paste (*minshah*), awl (*minfadh*), paper clip (*milzamah*), pen liner (*mifrashah*), pen wiper (*mimsahah*), water (*misqah*), ruler (*mistarah*), burnisher (*misqalah*), a piece of paper (*muhraq*), and whetstone (*misann*) (pp.293-294).

**Scene potential:** The writing case with its sixteen named compartments is a scene in itself. A moment where Abu Said opens his *dawah* and names each tool for Yusuf would combine craft instruction with character revelation.

### Bookbinding: Types and Techniques

The codex book form was most probably introduced to the Arabs by Abyssinian craftsmen, with Coptic influence visible in binding and sewing techniques. The bookbinding process is referred to by three terms: *tajlid* (Eastern), *tasfir* (Western/Maghreb), and *tashif* (Persian/Ottoman). The bookbinder is *mujallid*, *musaffir*, or *sahhaf* (pp.22-23).

Three major types of binding existed. Type I: early "box-books" with wooden boards and leather thong closures, used exclusively for oblong-format Qurans, disappearing when that format changed to vertical in the 4th/10th century. Type II: bindings with the characteristic Islamic envelope flap, the most common form. Unlike Western bindings, these are roundback (covers flow smoothly into the spine) and never hollowback (spine always stuck to quire backs). They are "case bindings" produced independently and then attached to the sewn quires. Covers are the same size as the textblock, with no protruding edges. Type III: bindings without flaps, appearing in the 11th/17th century (pp.23-27).

Boards were originally wooden (cedar, poplar, fig, tamarisk, pine, 4-11 mm thick) and later pasteboard (layers of reused paper glued together). Sewing used a link-stitch (chain-stitch/Coptic) technique with two or four sewing stations. Coloured threads -- blue, red, gold -- were sometimes used, with colour associated with the book's subject: green for the life of the Prophet, red for Islamic law (pp.29, 247-248).

**Scene potential:** The physical process of binding -- sewing quires with the chain-stitch technique, covering boards with leather, attaching the characteristic flap -- provides material for scenes in Abu Said's workshop or the later House of Paper's production line.

### Scribes, Copyists, and the Warraq

The most common term for a copyist is *nasikh*. The earliest term associated with scribal activity, bookselling, and related trades was *warraq* (*muwarriq*), but because of its multi-task nature -- copying, selling, dealing in paper -- the word's precise meaning varies by context. Professional scribes often specialized in other aspects of bookmaking including illumination and binding (pp.238-239).

A scribe (*warraq*) was asked what pleasure was. He answered: "parchments, papers, shiny ink and a cleft reed pen." Asked about his condition, he replied: "my livelihood is narrower than an inkwell, my body more slender than a ruler, my rank more delicate than glass, my face darker than vitriol, my lot more concealed than the slit of a nib, my hand weaker than a reed, my food comes from gall nuts, and bad luck clings to me like gum arabic" (p.240).

The daily output of famous scribes could be enormous. Ibn al-Jawzi (d. 597/1200) reportedly wrote or recopied 2,000 volumes in his lifetime -- 50-60 per year, approximately 4 quires per day (40 folia, 80 pages). The calligrapher Yaqut al-Musta'simi (d. 698/1298) reportedly copied two sections of the Quran daily, two complete Qurans monthly, and 1,001 Qurans in his lifetime (p.240).

**Scene potential:** The warraq's lament -- "my livelihood is narrower than an inkwell, my food comes from gall nuts" -- is a piece of period voice that could appear verbatim in dialogue at the Suq al-Warraqin. The sheer output figures contextualize the scale of the manuscript trade.

### Scribal Etiquette

The scribe's work was regarded as worship (*'ibadah*). Religious etiquette demanded pure intention (*niyah*), ritual purity (*taharah*), observing the *qiblah*, clean body and clothes. An exception was made for ink stains -- wearing them was a mark of manliness. The Kufan traditionist Ibrahim al-Nakha'i (d. c. 99/717) said: "an aspect of manliness is to see ink stains on man's clothes and lips." The vizier 'Ubayd Allah ibn Sulayman (d. 288/901) said: "saffron is the perfume of young maidens, whereas ink is the perfume of men" (p.236).

The Muslim scribe sat with one leg folded under and the other bent, holding a sheet of paper or a quire on his right or left knee. For support, he used the palm of his left hand or a pasteboard. His *qalam*, when not in use, rested behind his right ear. He often kept his writing case stuck in his girdle (p.236).

**Scene potential:** The posture of the scribe -- cross-legged, paper on knee, pen behind ear, writing case in girdle -- is a physical image that could anchor any scene in the workshop or the suq. The ink-stains-as-manliness detail is a character note: Omar al-Dimashqi would have permanently stained hands.

### Calligraphy: The Reform of Ibn Muqlah

Calligraphy took on a special character in the early Abbasid period with oblong Qurans in heavy, hieratic scripts. The constant preoccupation with script and decoration meant calligraphy became the most important art form in Islamic civilization. Calligraphers noted that the numerical value of the profession of faith (*shahada*) -- 691 -- was the same as the word *khattat* (calligrapher), reinforcing the connection between faith and writing (p.44).

Towards the end of the early Islamic period (beginning of the 4th/10th century), the calligraphic field was completely transformed. Arabic tradition links this to the Abbasid vizier Ibn Muqlah (d. 328/940) and the introduction of "proportioned" writing (*al-khatt al-mansub*). The new scripts emerging toward the end of the 4th/10th century are: *thuluth*, *tawqi'*, *riqa'*, *muhaqqaq*, *naskh*, and *rayhan*. Their standardization is attributed to Ibn al-Bawwab (d. 413/1022). In the early Abbasid period, scripts were grouped into three categories: Quranic scripts (*khutut al-masahif*), bookhands or scribal hands (*khutut al-warraqin*), and chancery hands (*khutut al-kuttab*) (pp.44-47).

**Scene potential:** The three-category classification of scripts (Quranic, scribal, chancery) reflects the market structure Yusuf encounters. Abu Said's workshop would produce paper suited to each category, and Yusuf would learn to judge paper quality against the demands of different scripts.

### Pricking and Ruling

Before text was penned, individual leaves were often pricked using a dry point (stylus or awl) at the four corners of the justification to delineate the text body. Ruling was done by dry point (stylus), lead plummet, ink, a ruling board (*mistarah*), or even a thumbnail (*tazfir*). Blind lines (indentations) were greatly preferred for aesthetic reasons. Ruling was employed by Muslim copyists as early as the 1st/7th century. Laid lines in paper were often clearly visible enough to guide the copyist's hand without ruling (pp.203-204).

There was a tendency to use uneven (odd) numbers of lines per page, as the middle line served as a strong organizational axis for the entire page (p.204).

**Scene potential:** A brief physical detail -- the apprentice ruling lines with a stylus before writing, the preference for odd numbers -- could appear naturally in any scene of manuscript preparation.

### Adhesives and Pest Control

Wheat, rice, or sorghum starch were the usual pastes used for sizing paper. Pastes were cooked in water or in an infusion of absinthe/wormwood (*afsantin*), or the roots of colocynth (*'alqam*) or aloe (*sabir*), all being strong purgatives and good protection against worms. The pith of colocynth was added during maceration of the pulp to protect against mice (pp.7, 187).

The word *kabikaj* (a plant, Persian buttercup) was used in invocations against worms and insects. It was seen as a jinn, a guardian/protector. It was thought sufficient to inscribe its name in manuscripts to secure their protection. In India, the word was "frequently inscribed on the first page of a book, under the superstitious belief that, out of respect for the name of their king, the cockroaches will spare it" (pp.137-138).

**Scene potential:** The practical and magical pest-control measures -- wormwood in the paste, *kabikaj* inscriptions against bookworms -- add texture to the workshop world, mixing craft pragmatism with the supernatural beliefs of the period.

- The date of Baghdad's first paper mill is given as c. 794-795 in CLAUDE.md but Gacek does not specify a date for the first Baghdad mill, only that paper was used in government offices under Harun al-Rashid (r. 786-809). The *Fihrist* and other sources should be checked for the specific founding date of the Baghdad mill. Jonathan Bloom's *Paper Before Print* (also in the reference library) likely has more detail.
- Gacek states that Arab paper historically lacked watermarks (the earliest watermark being Italian, 1293). Yusuf's watermark innovation in Book 2 (set c. 793) is therefore an anachronism by design -- a fictional invention predating European watermarks by 500 years. This is acceptable under the project's creative licence rules (financial mechanics are poorly documented and allow invention), but the digest should flag it clearly: the watermark is Yusuf's fictional innovation, not a historical practice.
- The entry on the *lam alif al-warraqiyah* (the scribes' ligature) mentions it was used in the Abbasid bookhand and later scripts. Cross-reference with Blair's *Islamic Calligraphy* (also in reference library) for more detail on early Abbasid scribal hands.
- The *Umdat al-kuttab* attributed to Ibn Badis is the main primary source for papermaking, ink, and bookbinding recipes but dates to the 5th/11th century -- two centuries after Yusuf's time. The techniques described may be broadly applicable to earlier practice but some details could be anachronistic. Gacek notes that our sources for early Arab papermaking are limited (p.187).
