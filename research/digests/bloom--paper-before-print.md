# Digest: Paper before Print: The History and Impact of Paper in the Islamic World

**Author:** Jonathan M. Bloom
**Source:** `reference/paper-before-print--bloom.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** The single most important scholarly treatment of paper's transformative role in the Islamic world -- covers papermaking technology, the Baghdad paper mills, the Suq al-Warraqin, scribal culture, the book trade, and paper-based financial instruments, all directly relevant to the novel's central themes.

## Overview

Bloom's central argument is that the introduction of paper to the Islamic lands in the eighth century catalysed a transformation from oral to scribal culture that was as significant as the later shift from scribal to typographic culture in Europe. Paper was cheaper, more abundant, and more durable than papyrus or parchment, and its availability enabled an explosion of books, the development of new scripts, the growth of bureaucratic record-keeping, and the creation of sophisticated financial instruments -- all without the printing press. The book traces paper from its Chinese origins through Samarkand to Baghdad and then across the entire Islamic world.

For this novel, Bloom is indispensable. He provides the technical, economic, and cultural scaffolding for Yusuf's entire career arc: the physical process of papermaking that Abu Said teaches, the bureaucratic demand that creates the market, the Suq al-Warraqin where Yusuf sells paper, and the financial instruments (suftajas, sakks) that become the foundation of the House of Paper's banking empire. Bloom also establishes the Barmakid family's connection to paper adoption, the caliphal bureaucracy's insatiable appetite for paper, and the shift from papyrus to paper in government offices -- all events that occur during Yusuf's lifetime.

The book is richly footnoted and draws on Arabic primary sources (al-Nadim, al-Maqrizi, al-Qalqashandi, Ibn Khaldun, the Geniza documents) as well as archaeological evidence. Bloom writes for a general academic audience, and his prose is clear and accessible. Where Pedersen (*The Arabic Book*) focuses on the book trade and Hunter (*Papermaking*) on technique, Bloom provides the broader civilisational context -- why paper mattered, not just how it was made.

## Key Extracts

### The Battle of Talas and the Myth of Captured Papermakers (pp. 42-45)

The traditional story -- that Muslim soldiers captured Chinese papermakers at the Battle of Talas in 751 and brought them to Samarkand -- is probably a convenient metaphor rather than literal history. The story first appears three centuries after the event in the tenth-century author Thaalibi's entertaining miscellany. Bloom notes that earlier Muslim historians ignored the Battle of Talas entirely, focusing on other events in West Asia. The Arab accounts contemporary with the battle suggest Arab forces at Talas numbered no more than thirty thousand troops. One Chinese source, Du Huan, taken prisoner and returned a decade later, mentions weavers, painters, goldsmiths, and silversmiths among the prisoners but does not specifically mention papermakers.

Bloom argues persuasively that papermaking would have been practised throughout Central Asia well before the Islamic conquest. Paper was used in Samarkand -- and probably made there -- decades before the battle. Mount Mugh documents from Tajikistan, dated to 722-23, include paper alongside cloth, leather, and wood. Anonymous Central Asian papermakers had likely invented rag paper well before the Islamic conquest. The key innovation was not the capture of craftsmen but the Islamic administration's decision, under Abbasid rule, to adopt paper for bureaucratic use.

**Scene potential:** Abu Said could tell Yusuf a version of this origin story in the workshop, and Yusuf -- already showing his analytical mind -- could question its literal truth, earning Abu Said's respect.

### The Abbasid Bureaucracy Demands Paper (pp. 47-49)

The bureaucratic decision to adopt paper was driven by practical necessity. The second Umayyad caliph, Umar I (r. 634-44), had established the first government office to organise payments, register the army, and regulate the treasury; these records were kept on papyrus. According to later secretarial tradition, the first Abbasid caliph al-Saffah's (r. 751-54) vizier Khalid ibn Barmak ordered loose sheets replaced with codices, following Persian precedent, though papyrus continued alongside parchment during the reign of al-Saffah's son al-Mansur, the founder of Baghdad. The codex format had exposed two of papyrus's greatest weaknesses: its edges were easily frayed and it was damaged by repeated folding. Paper was noticeably superior in both respects.

The thirteenth-century encyclopaedist Yaqut reports that the first paper mill in Baghdad was established in 794-95, during the reign of the Abbasid caliph Harun al-Rashid, and enough paper was made available for bureaucrats to replace their papyrus and parchment records with paper ones. Bureaucracy begat more bureaucracy: the War Office (*Diwan al-jaysh*) had two branches for recruitment and payment; the State Treasury (*Diwan bayt al-mal*) provided monthly (and later weekly) accounts to the vizier; the Board of Comparison (*Diwan al-muqadarin*) made duplicate orders for payment; the Post Office (*Diwan al-barid*) supervised the post roads and personnel, as well as a network of informants about conditions in the empire.

**Scene potential:** The sheer scale of the caliphal bureaucracy -- every office consuming paper -- is the economic engine that makes Abu Said's workshop profitable and that Yusuf later exploits. The list of government offices consuming paper could be dramatised through a scene where Yusuf delivers paper to different government departments.

### The Barmakid Connection to Paper (pp. 49-50)

Ibn Khaldun's association of paper with the Barmakid family of viziers is intriguing. The charismatic Barmakids were descended from the chief priest (*pramuk*) of a large Buddhist temple at Balkh, capital of ancient Bactria. After the Arab conquest of Central Asia, the family threw in their lot with the Arab conquerors, and Khalid ibn Barmak (d. 781-82), apparently the first member to convert to Islam, served the first Abbasid caliph in the powerful role of administrator of army and land-tax records. In spite of court intrigues, his grandson al-Fadl ibn Yahya was foster brother to the future caliph Harun. The family revived interest in the literary masterpieces of Iran and India, as well as various eastern philosophical and religious doctrines. Their Central Asian and Buddhist antecedents, their experience in Khorasan, and their special interest in administration may well have familiarised them with paper and could have made them appreciative of its potential role for the burgeoning Abbasid bureaucracy.

**Scene potential:** Directly relevant to the novel's Barmakid plot arc. The Barmakids' eastern origins and cultural sophistication connect them to paper in a way that makes Yusuf's later relationship with Ja'far plausible -- they share a common appreciation of paper as a tool of civilisation.

### The Suq al-Warraqin (pp. 50-51)

When papermaking flourished in Baghdad, stationery was big business. The Stationers' Market (*Suq al-Warraqin*), in southwest Baghdad, was lined with more than one hundred shops selling paper and books. The population of the capital city would have provided a ready supply of rags, and its rivers and canals sufficient water for papermaking. By the tenth century, ship-mills -- floating mills powered by the flow of the current -- were moored on the Tigris above the city, although the sources do not specify what product the mills processed (grain or pulp).

Stationers like Ahmad ibn Abi Tahir, a teacher, writer, and paper dealer, were men of learning. Abu'l-Faraj Muhammad ibn Ishaq, known also as Ibn Abi Yaqub al-Nadim al-Warraq ("the Stationer"), used his extensive professional knowledge to compile the *Fihrist*, his encyclopaedia of contemporary books and writers, which remains a mine of information about medieval books and writing.

At that time an ordinary book cost ten dirhams, and a fine one ten times as much.

**Scene potential:** This is the world of Chapter 5 (The Suq al-Warraqin) already drafted. The detail about 100+ shops, the pricing (10 dirhams ordinary, 100 fine), and stationer-scholars like al-Nadim can enrich existing scenes. The ship-mills on the Tigris are a vivid visual detail.

### Paper Sizes and Standards (pp. 52-53)

The Egyptian historian al-Qalqashandi (d. 1418) enumerated nine sizes of paper, of which the two largest were the standard full-sized Baghdadi sheet, measuring one Egyptian cubit (approximately 23 inches, or 73 centimetres) high by one and a half cubits (approximately 43 inches, or 110 centimetres) wide, and the reduced Baghdadi sheet (26 by 39 inches, 65 by 98 centimetres). The pages of an early fourteenth-century manuscript penned by Ahmad al-Suhrawardi measure approximately 20 by 28 inches (50 by 35 centimetres). This implies that they were folded from a sheet twice as large, or approximately 20 by 56 inches (50 by 70 centimetres) -- about half the full Baghdadi size.

The fine quality of Baghdad paper was celebrated despite the Mongols' reported destruction of the city in 1258. A book by the eleventh-century secretary Hilal al-Sabi, *Rusum dar al-khilafa* (The Rules and Regulations of the Caliphal Court), contains a chapter on paper sizes used in official correspondence. For letters from the Caliph, the Envelopes Used to Hold Letters, and the Seals Used in Writing, specific paper sizes were prescribed.

**Scene potential:** Abu Said could teach Yusuf to recognise paper grades and sizes -- the full Baghdadi sheet versus the half-sheet, their uses for different purposes. This technical knowledge becomes commercial advantage when Yusuf begins selling.

### Milling Technology: The Trip-Hammer (pp. 53-55)

Three basic types of waterwheel were known in the medieval Muslim world: the undershot wheel, the overshot wheel, and the horizontal or Norse wheel. The undershot wheel is a paddle wheel mounted vertically on a horizontal axle; its power derives almost entirely from the velocity of the water flowing past it. The overshot wheel is also mounted vertically on a horizontal axle, but it has buckets or compartments along the rim into which water is discharged from a reservoir above.

The history of the trip-hammer -- the heart of the watermill used for pulping fibre in the Islamic lands -- is largely a matter of speculation. Its prototype is the Chinese water-driven mallet for husking rice, used from the first century CE. The Chinese type of trip-hammer mill must have spread across Eurasia along with the cultivation of rice. In most of the Islamic lands, rice never enjoyed the popularity of other cereals (wheat, sorghum, and barley), perhaps because it was difficult to grow in many places. In the well-watered regions of southern Iraq and southwestern Iran, however, rice bread made from ground rice was the staple of the poor.

Most medieval Islamic paper mills used grain-type trip-hammers to beat rags into pulp for papermaking. Starch, used to size (or "stuff") the finished paper, was also made from grain. Rotary motion, however, would not transform rags into pulp suitable for papermaking. Rather, the rags had to be beaten by a mechanism known as a stamper. Unlike rotary mills, these trip-hammer mills require running water for power, and they produced a reciprocal (vertical) motion by means of cams -- pegs or projections attached to a rotating wheel or axle.

**Scene potential:** The workshop scenes in Chapter 4 (The Poisoned Vat) can draw on this: the rhythmic thump of trip-hammers, the canal feeding the water-wheel, the conversion of rags into pulp. The distinction between rotary mills (grain) and trip-hammer mills (paper) is a technical detail Abu Said would know.

### Paper Replaces Papyrus and Parchment (pp. 48-52)

Paper documents were more secure from forgery than those written on papyrus and parchment: because paper absorbs ink, writing on it could not be erased easily. Paper was also harder to erase than papyrus or parchment, an extra protection against dishonesty in financial transactions. Ibn Khaldun, the North African historian and philosopher, was even more specific: the manufacture of paper had been introduced by the Barmakid vizier al-Fadl ibn Yahya, who served the caliph Harun, when insufficient parchment was available.

By the middle of the ninth century -- barely a century since Muslims had first encountered paper -- many, if not most, lettered Muslims, as well as Christians and Jews, were using it for writing letters, keeping records, and copying literature.

The great Islamic theologian Ibn Hanbal (d. 855) is reported to have said that he preferred to write with "a reed pen, shining ink, and parchment," but by that time the triumph of paper was already well advanced.

**Scene potential:** The security argument -- paper absorbs ink and cannot be erased like papyrus or parchment -- is central to the novel's theme of "paper as trust." Yusuf could recognise this property early and use it to argue for paper-based financial instruments.

### The Book Trade and Publishing (pp. 113-116)

Islamic society fostered such a respect for book learning that rulers and the wealthy opened their doors to the learned and lavished large sums of money on them. Caliphs, governors, courtiers, gentlemen-scholars, and physicians sponsored new books as well as translations of Christian and Jewish works written in Syriac and Greek. The translators themselves sometimes took on disciples, scribes, and amanuenses.

People wrote books simply because they wanted to or because patrons or rulers suggested they do so. Writers expected to be paid with honors, presents, and often cash. Others, such as secretaries and judges in state chanceries and offices, wrote books in their spare time. The publication of books was itself an oral procedure, for a work was first recited and written down to dictation, usually in a mosque. The author would sit cross-legged with his auditors seated in a circle before him. The author normally wrote out his work and followed the draft manuscript in dictating, which was often done from memory.

A copy of a book could not be considered genuine unless it had been authorised by the lecturer. The copyist had to read the copy aloud in order to obtain the authorisation, and an author might save time by assembling an audience for check-reading in the same way he had assembled one for dictation.

In contrast to the situation in medieval Christendom, where a single scribe made a single parchment copy of the single parchment manuscript on the desk before him, one author in the Muslim world could generate a dozen paper copies from a single reading, and each of those authorised copies could generate another dozen. Within two "generations" of reading, well over a hundred copies of a single work might be produced. This ingenious and efficient system was extraordinarily effective in increasing the circulation of books.

**Scene potential:** A scene in the Suq al-Warraqin could show this dictation/publication process in action -- an author reading aloud while a circle of copyists write on paper Yusuf has supplied. The contrast between one-copy-at-a-time parchment and the Muslim paper-based system of mass reproduction is a key theme.

### The House of Wisdom (pp. 117-118)

The caliphal collection underwent its greatest development when Harun's son, the caliph al-Mamun, created the House of Wisdom (*Bayt al-hikma*), which served as the official translating institution in Baghdad. It and the associated library, Storehouse of Wisdom (*Khizanat al-hikma*), were created in imitation of the pre-Islamic Iranian academy at Jundishapur. The caliph sent Salman of Harran, the Sabian curator of the House of Wisdom, a translator of Aristotle who was also conversant with Middle Persian, with a delegation of scholars to purchase philosophical and scientific manuscripts in Constantinople.

Under al-Mamun's patronage, Greek scientific texts, including Ptolemy's *Syntaxis* (Almagest), were translated into Arabic, and the earliest known Arabic treatise concerning astrolabes was written at his court.

The House of Wisdom was apparently destroyed during the period of orthodox reaction under the caliph al-Mutawakkil (r. 847-61).

**Scene potential:** Directly relevant to Book 3 and the House of Wisdom patronage plotline. Harun's son al-Mamun's creation of the Bayt al-hikma connects to Yusuf's later involvement with intellectual life. The Sabian scholar Salman of Harran buying manuscripts in Constantinople parallels the novel's themes of knowledge as commerce.

### Financial Instruments: Suftaja, Sakk, and the Paper Economy (pp. 135-139)

Although Muslims tend to think of the credit economy as a strictly modern development, medieval Muslim merchants conducted most economic activities on credit and recorded them on paper documents. The range of these documents -- including but not limited to contracts, accounts, and letters of credit -- shows that writing and written documents, particularly those on paper, were common at a time when the idea of writing and written records were just beginning to enter the lives of Europeans.

The demands of the state bureaucracy established by the Abbasids encouraged the use of paper for keeping government registers and accounts. One tenth-century Khorasani tax collector kept accounts showing the amount of assessed taxes, the amount paid by each taxpayer, the journal containing daily income and expenditure, and the amounts totalled up at the end of every month. The yearly account was a register in which amounts paid were systematically entered for easy reference. The statements were shown in three columns: first, the amount taxed; second, the amount actually collected; and third, the difference between the two.

Trade in agricultural goods and the products manufactured from them, especially textiles, was the fuel that powered the medieval Muslim economy. Unification of the lands between the Atlantic and the Indian Oceans had created a vast common market. Although the Koran and Islamic legal literature denounced all forms of lending money at interest, Muslim merchants had several other ways to acquire investment capital, and members of religious minorities were not prohibited from borrowing and lending with people outside their faith.

Commercial "paper," in use from at least the eighth century, made possible the transfer of large sums of money over considerable distances without the use of specie. Paper had the inherent advantage of being more difficult to erase than papyrus or parchment, an extra protection against dishonesty. Another instrument of credit was the *suftaja*, a letter of credit or, technically, a loan of money to avoid the risks of transportation. In the vast stretches of the empire, there were bankers and banking but no banks: no specialised institutions exclusively dealing in money. Well-known bankers usually issued letters of credit for a fee, and the suftaja could be drawn on like a modern money order, although they normally could not be transferred to another party. Around 1050 an Alexandrian merchant wrote to his correspondent in Cairo that he had sent the amount owed in cash because he had been unable to find someone willing to issue a suftaja. Suftajas could be sent over long distances, but only where direct and permanent business connections already existed.

Merchants also sent notes written on paper, known by the Persian term *sakk* or *sukka*, from which the European principle (and name) of a check is derived. At Awdagust, in the western Sudan, the tenth-century geographer Ibn Hawqal saw an officially certified check for forty-two thousand dinars drawn by a man from Sijilmasa on one Muhammad ibn Ali Sadun in the same city. Credit permeated all levels of society: the Geniza documents show that one did not even pay the local grocer for daily supplies but sent written orders and settled after a certain figure had been reached. Cash discounts were granted when payment was made promptly, and these discounts hovered around 3 percent.

**Scene potential:** This entire section is the economic foundation of Yusuf's empire. The suftaja system, the sakk (origin of "check"), the three-column accounting registers, the credit economy without banks -- all of this is what Yusuf builds and what makes the House of Paper revolutionary. The detail about the Khorasani tax collector's triple-column accounts could be a specific technique Yusuf learns or invents.

### The Daftar: Merchant Account Books (p. 140)

Short accounts were often written on single sheets, like letters, and were sometimes made in several copies, either for safety or because several partners were involved in the transactions. Longer accounts were normally written in a narrow booklet format known as a *daftar*, which typically measured between 3-4 inches (7-10 centimetres) wide and either 5.5-6 or 7.5 to 11 inches (14-15 centimetres or 19-28 centimetres) tall. The format allowed the merchant to carry a daftar in his sleeve, which was normally quite wide and served as a pocket, to be readily available for reference. The daftar consisted of a single sheet folded into a bifolio, creating four sides for writing. It could be punched with holes near the fold, so that it could be assembled, presumably with cords, somewhat like a modern loose-leaf binder, to continue a long record.

The Geniza documents hardly provide a complete picture of the financial state of the complex businesses these merchants ran. Ledgers as such do not survive among them. Account books were files into which a merchant entered notes about his payments and receipts, as well as copies of any accounts submitted to a business correspondent.

**Scene potential:** The daftar carried in the sleeve is a perfect physical detail for Yusuf -- his personal ledger, always on his person, the precursor to his *Daftar al-Asrar* (Book of Secrets). The dimensions, the cord binding, the bifolio construction are all scene-ready.

### Al-Khwarizmi and Hindu-Arabic Numerals (pp. 129-130)

The Indian system of numerals seems to have been known in Baghdad as early as 770, or less than a decade after its founding, but it was principally diffused through the writings of the Abbasid mathematician and geographer Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi (or al-Khowarizmi), who died around 846. Al-Khwarizmi, a native of the Khorasan region of Central Asia, was attached to the caliph al-Mamun's House of Knowledge in Baghdad.

Al-Khwarizmi's efforts in ninth-century Baghdad should be understood in the broader context of the enormous interest in learning and science that was encouraged by the wider availability of paper. The earliest known copy of al-Khwarizmi's work is a twelfth-century Latin translation, which opens with the words "dixit Algoritmi" (Algoritmi says). In this manner, the mathematician's epithet of origin came in the West to denote the new process of reckoning with Hindu-Arabic numerals, called *algorismus* in medieval Latin and *algorism* in English.

**Scene potential:** Al-Khwarizmi is a Khorasani -- like Yusuf's family. The arrival of Hindu-Arabic numerals in Baghdad during Yusuf's lifetime is directly relevant to the *siyaq* accounting script and Yusuf's commercial education. The connection between paper, numerals, and calculation is thematic.

### Ink Types and Paper (p. 107)

Black ink, prepared from lampblack bound with plant gum and known in Arabic as *midad*, was appropriate for use on papyrus, but it had notoriously poor adhesion to parchment and tended to flake off. Kufic manuscripts of the Koran were normally copied on parchment in the brownish ink known in Arabic as *hibr*, which was made from metal tannins prepared with gallnuts. The metal tannin ink actually penetrated the surface of the parchment like dye. When used on paper, however, the mixture of metal salts and tannins in tannin ink produced acids that eventually destroyed the paper. Carbon ink, by contrast, had no chemical effect on the surface to which it was applied; it was the type of ink secretary-copyists normally used on papyrus and paper.

**Scene potential:** Abu Said or Ustad Umar could teach Yusuf that you must match ink to material -- carbon ink for paper, tannin ink for parchment. Using the wrong ink destroys the document. This is both technical craft knowledge and a metaphor for understanding that tools must match their purpose.
