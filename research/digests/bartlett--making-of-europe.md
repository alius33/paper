## Overview

Robert Bartlett's *The Making of Europe: Conquest, Colonization and Cultural Change, 950--1350* (Allen Lane, 1993; Princeton University Press, paperback) is a landmark study of how Latin Christendom expanded from its post-Carolingian core in northern France, the Rhineland, and northern Italy to encompass the peripheries of the European continent. Bartlett argues that Europe was itself a product of colonization --- that the processes of conquest, settlement, legal imposition, and cultural homogenisation that later characterized European overseas imperialism were first developed within Europe itself.

The book's chronological focus (950--1350) begins after the period covered by *The House of Paper* (770--814 CE), but it is directly relevant to the project in two ways: (1) it provides the comparative context for understanding how far behind Western Europe was in the 8th--9th centuries, when the Abbasid Caliphate was the world's most advanced civilisation; and (2) it documents the institutional and cultural mechanisms by which Europe eventually caught up --- mechanisms that include the adoption of financial instruments (like the commenda, likely derived from the mudaraba) and technologies (like paper) originally developed in the Islamic world.

Bartlett is Bishop Wardlaw Professor of Mediaeval History at the University of St. Andrews, a Fellow of the British Academy, and educated at Cambridge, Oxford, and Princeton. The book won the Wolfson Literary Prize for History.

## Key Extracts

### The Carolingian Core: Europe in 800 CE

Bartlett's starting point is the post-Carolingian world --- the world that emerged from the fragmentation of Charlemagne's empire. In the first half of the ninth century, "the Christian West and the Frankish empire had come close to being coterminous." Apart from the British Isles and the kingdom of Asturias, virtually all Latin Christians acknowledged Charlemagne and his son as overlords.

But this empire was, by the standards of Baghdad, primitive. The Frankish lands were overwhelmingly rural, with few cities of any size. Most of the population were peasants on small farms. Trade was limited, primarily with Scandinavia and the British Isles. There was no banking system, no bills of exchange, and limited coinage. While Harun al-Rashid's empire stretched from Tunisia to India and generated enormous commercial wealth, Charlemagne's empire was sustained by land rents, tribute, and plunder.

The contrast is illuminated by the famous diplomatic exchange between the two rulers: Harun sent Charlemagne gifts including an elephant (Abu al-Abbas), an elaborate water clock, fine silks, and spices. Charlemagne sent hunting dogs and woollen cloaks. The relative sophistication and extravagance of Harun's gifts reflected "the relative might and technological progress" of the two empires.

**Scene potential:** The novel does not need to depict Europe directly, but the characters' awareness of the Frankish world --- the "Ifranj" --- can provide a comparative frame. When Abu Said or Yusuf speaks of trade with the Byzantines (Constantinople venture, Book 2), the Islamic world's commercial superiority is taken for granted. An occasional reference to the Ifranj as primitive, backward, or economically unsophisticated would ground this attitude historically. The elephant anecdote could appear as a court rumour or a merchant's tale.

### The Abbasid--Carolingian Alliance

Despite the vast disparity in wealth and sophistication, the Abbasid and Carolingian empires maintained diplomatic contact driven by shared geopolitical interests. Both opposed the Umayyads (who had established an independent caliphate in Cordoba) and the Byzantines (who threatened both from different angles).

Embassies were exchanged in both directions: a Frankish embassy visited Baghdad in 765 and returned after three years with gifts. An Abbasid embassy from al-Mansur visited France in 768. Later, Charlemagne sent envoys to Harun, and Harun reciprocated. Louis the Pious received an embassy from al-Ma'mun in 831.

Commercial exchanges occurred alongside diplomatic ones. Arabic gold coins (dinars) circulated in Carolingian Europe, apparently in payment for European exports of slaves, timber, iron, and weapons.

**Scene potential:** The Abbasid--Carolingian diplomatic channel could appear peripherally in the novel. A mention of Frankish envoys in Baghdad, or of the strange gifts they bring, would be historically accurate and would underscore Baghdad's position as the centre of the world. The slave trade --- European slaves sold into the Islamic world --- is a darker connection that the novel might acknowledge in passing.

### Europe's Financial Backwardness

Bartlett's account of European expansion in the 10th--13th centuries implicitly reveals how financially primitive Europe was before that expansion. The institutional innovations he documents --- chartered towns, standardised coinage, commercial law, merchant guilds --- were developments of the 11th--13th centuries, centuries after the Islamic world had developed equivalent or superior institutions.

The commenda contract, which powered Italian maritime trade from the 13th century onward, is widely considered to be derived from (or at least influenced by) the Islamic mudaraba. The bill of exchange, which transformed European finance in the 14th century, has clear parallels with the suftaja. Paper itself, the medium on which European commerce and government increasingly depended, was manufactured using techniques transmitted from the Islamic world.

**Scene potential:** Yusuf's innovations --- the watermark, the suftaja network, the mudaraba-based financial house --- are not inventions ex nihilo but extensions of existing Islamic commercial practice. The novel's implicit argument is that the Islamic world developed these instruments first, and that Europe's later adoption of them was a form of technological transfer. This context gives Yusuf's work historical weight without requiring the novel to make the comparison explicit.

### Technologies of Expansion

Bartlett identifies several "technologies of domination" that enabled European expansion: castles, heavy cavalry, chartered towns, standardised law, and the institutional church. These were the tools by which the Carolingian core replicated itself in the peripheries.

The Islamic world had its own equivalent technologies: the barid (postal/intelligence system), the waqf (endowed institution), standardised coinage, commercial law (fiqh al-mu'amalat), and --- crucially --- paper. Paper was the administrative technology that made the Abbasid bureaucracy possible. Without paper, the scale of record-keeping, correspondence, and legal documentation that the Abbasid state required would have been impossible.

**Scene potential:** Ali's argument with Yusuf about paper --- "Paper is real... you can touch it" --- operates on multiple levels. Paper is not just a material product; it is the technology that makes empire administrable, commerce extensible, and knowledge preservable. Bartlett's analysis of technologies of expansion gives the novel's treatment of paper an additional dimension: paper is not just Yusuf's trade, it is the Islamic world's most consequential technological export to Europe.

### Comparative Population and Urbanisation

Bartlett's data on European urbanisation highlights the disparity with the Islamic world in the 8th--9th centuries:

- **Baghdad (c. 800):** Estimated population of 300,000--1,000,000 (figures vary widely). The largest city in the world.
- **Constantinople (c. 800):** Estimated population of ~300,000--500,000. The largest city in Christendom.
- **Rome (c. 800):** Population collapsed to perhaps 20,000--35,000 from its ancient peak of ~1,000,000.
- **Paris (c. 800):** Perhaps 20,000--25,000 at most.
- **London (c. 800):** Perhaps 10,000--12,000.
- **Cordoba (c. 800):** Under the Umayyads, growing rapidly. Would reach ~100,000--500,000 by the 10th century.

The contrast is stark. At the time of *The House of Paper*, Baghdad was a world apart from anything in Europe. Its markets, mosques, palaces, workshops, libraries, and hospitals had no European equivalent.

**Scene potential:** The novel does not need population statistics, but the author should carry in mind the sense of scale. Baghdad in Yusuf's time is not a large town; it is a megacity, teeming, noisy, layered, and overwhelming. Characters who arrive from smaller places --- from Kufa, from the provinces, from the caravan routes --- should register its scale with appropriate wonder.

### The Role of Islam in Defining Europe

Bartlett argues that European identity was partly defined in opposition to Islam. The Reconquista in Iberia, the Crusades, and the ideological framing of Christendom as a unified civilisation all drew energy from the Islamic Other. Europe defined itself by what it was not --- and what it was not, above all, was Muslim.

This is significant for the novel because it reverses the usual perspective. The characters in *The House of Paper* do not define themselves against Europe; they barely think about Europe at all. The Ifranj are a peripheral curiosity, not a rival civilisation. The real rival is Byzantium, and the real internal tension is between Arab and Persian-Khorasani factions within the caliphate itself.

**Scene potential:** The absence of Europe from the characters' consciousness is itself a statement. When the novel's characters think about the world beyond the caliphate, they think about China (source of silk and paper techniques), India (source of numerals, spices, and philosophy), and Byzantium (the Christian rival, the market for luxury goods, the military frontier). Europe, as the characters understand it, barely exists as a coherent entity.

## Facts and Dates

- **751 CE:** Battle of Talas --- Abbasid forces defeat Tang Chinese forces in Central Asia. Paper-making techniques transmitted westward.
- **762 CE:** Baghdad founded. Same decade as the Carolingian dynasty's consolidation of power in Francia.
- **768 CE:** Abbasid embassy from al-Mansur visits Charlemagne's father, Pepin the Short (or possibly Charlemagne himself, who became king in 768).
- **800 CE:** Charlemagne crowned Emperor by the Pope in Rome. The Abbasid Caliphate, at the same moment, is the world's largest and wealthiest empire.
- **802 CE:** Harun al-Rashid sends an elephant (Abu al-Abbas) to Charlemagne.
- **831 CE:** Al-Ma'mun sends embassy to Louis the Pious.
- **950--1350 CE:** Bartlett's period of study --- the High Middle Ages, during which Europe undergoes the expansion and institutional development that eventually closes the gap with the Islamic world.
- **13th century:** The Italian commenda (derived from or parallel to the mudaraba) powers Mediterranean commerce.
- **14th century:** European bills of exchange (derived from or parallel to the suftaja) transform European finance.

## Relevance to Chapters

### General
- **Comparative context:** Bartlett's book provides the macro-historical argument for why the novel's setting matters. The Abbasid world of 770--814 CE was the centre of global civilisation, and the novel's commercial and cultural innovations were at the cutting edge of human achievement. This context informs the confidence, ambition, and cosmopolitan outlook of characters like Yusuf, Abu Said, and Ishaq.

### Book 1: The House of Paper
- **Paper as technology:** The significance of paper --- as material, as commodity, as administrative infrastructure --- is enhanced by understanding its eventual role in transforming European governance and commerce.
- **Caravan trade:** The trade routes Yusuf travels connect Baghdad to a world-system that dwarfs anything in contemporary Europe.

### Book 2: The House of Silver
- **The Constantinople venture:** Yusuf's journey to Byzantium places him at the boundary between the Islamic and Christian worlds. The commercial dynamics of this frontier --- what the Islamic world exports, what it imports, how transactions are conducted across cultural boundaries --- can be informed by Bartlett's analysis of inter-civilisational contact.

### Books 3--4
- **The Carolingian comparison:** Harun al-Rashid and Charlemagne were contemporaries. The contrast between their empires underscores the tragedy of the civil war: the Islamic world's most advanced civilisation tore itself apart while the Frankish barbarians were just beginning to build.

- **[RESEARCH NEEDED: Bartlett's date range]** Bartlett covers 950--1350. For the novel's period (770--814), the more relevant comparative source may be works on the Carolingian empire specifically, such as *The Abbasid and Carolingian Empires: Comparative Studies in Civilizational Formation* (ed. D.G. Tor, Brill, 2017).
- **[RESEARCH NEEDED: Commenda-mudaraba link]** The claim that the Italian commenda derived from the Islamic mudaraba is widely discussed but not universally accepted. The literature (Udovitch, Pryor, and others) should be consulted for the current scholarly consensus.
- **[RESEARCH NEEDED: European slaves in Baghdad]** The trade in European slaves (Slavs, in particular) into the Islamic world is documented but the specifics --- routes, volumes, prices, legal status --- need additional sourcing for any scene that touches on this trade.
- **[RESEARCH NEEDED: Harun-Charlemagne embassy details]** The specifics of the diplomatic exchanges --- dates, personnel, route taken by the envoys, the famous water clock --- are well documented but should be cross-referenced with the primary sources cited by Kennedy and others.
- **[RESEARCH NEEDED: Paper transmission to Europe]** The route by which paper-making techniques passed from the Islamic world to Europe (via Iberia and Sicily, primarily) and the approximate dates of European paper mills (12th century in Iberia, 13th century in Italy) should be verified for any comparative discussion.
