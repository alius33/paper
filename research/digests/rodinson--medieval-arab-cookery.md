## Overview

This collection brings together the foundational scholarship on medieval Arab cookery. Its centrepiece is A.J. Arberry's 1939 translation of the *Kitab al-Tabikh* ("Book of Cookery") by Muhammad ibn al-Hasan ibn Muhammad ibn al-Karim al-Katib al-Baghdadi, an autograph manuscript dated 623/1226 -- the oldest surviving Arabic cookbook manuscript. Though written in the thirteenth century, the recipes it preserves draw on a tradition stretching back to the Abbasid golden age: the preface quotes tenth-century court poets reciting verses about specific dishes at caliphal banquets, and Perry's introductory notes identify recipes named after the prince Ibrahim al-Mahdi (d. 224/839), who was a celebrated gourmet of the early ninth century. The book thus serves as a window into the food world of Baghdad from roughly the eighth through the thirteenth centuries.

The collection also includes Charles Perry's short, brilliant essays on specific aspects of Abbasid food culture: what street food was actually available in ninth-century Baghdad; the social and religious elements of Arab feasting; the history of *buran* (the eggplant dish named after al-Ma'mun's wife); and the *Ma'muniyya* (a sweet dish named after al-Ma'mun himself). Perry's essays are sharp, historically grounded, and full of the kind of concrete sensory detail a novelist needs. Rodinson's longer scholarly essays trace the transmission of Arab culinary vocabulary into European languages, documenting the deep influence of Abbasid cuisine on the medieval Mediterranean world.

For the purposes of this novel, the most valuable material falls into four categories: (1) what specific dishes a character in Baghdad between 770 and 814 CE would eat, smell, and see being prepared; (2) the geography and sensory world of Baghdad's food markets; (3) the etiquette and meaning of feasting, hospitality, and shared meals; and (4) the Persian influence on Abbasid court cuisine, which maps directly onto the novel's Arab-Khorasani cultural tensions.

## Key Extracts

### Street Food in Baghdad's Markets

Perry's essay "What to Order in Ninth-Century Baghdad" reconstructs the food landscape of the city using a tenth-century literary text by Badi' al-Zaman al-Hamadhani, set in the al-Karkh market quarter. The narrator passes through the Sharqiyya quarter's fruit stalls, past butchers located between the Canal of the Fryers and the Canal of the Chickens -- names that indicate permanent cooking establishments along Baghdad's waterways. The food market offered fryers of sausage and fish, sellers of roast liver and roast sheep's heads, roasters of sliced meat, purveyors of hot stews, cold vegetable dishes, and a porridge flavoured with meat and spices called *harisa*. For dessert, one could choose from over fifty varieties at the confectioners', including a fritter called *zulabiya* (known in India as *jelebi*). (pp. 219-220)

The two most prestigious street dishes were *judhaba* and *lauzinaj*. *Judhaba* was not a skewer of kebab grilled over coals but something sliced off a large cut of meat roasted in a clay oven -- a *tannur* (tandoor) -- with a sweetened pudding of meat, sugar, starch, and fat set underneath to catch the running juices. It was served in a copper pan called a *judhabadan*. *Lauzinaj* was essentially a pastry of ground almonds and sugar wrapped in paper-thin dough, stored in jars with syrup, sesame, or almond oil to cover. The pastry was so delicate that the poet Ibn al-Rumi compared it to grasshoppers' wings. (pp. 220-223)

**Scene potential:** The Canal of the Fryers and Canal of the Chickens are perfect named landmarks for Yusuf's walks through al-Karkh; the *judhaba* roasting in a *tannur* with its pudding catching dripping fat is an extraordinary sensory image for a market scene.

### The Baghdad Cookery Book: Ingredients and Methods

The *Kitab al-Tabikh* opens with detailed instructions for the cook's workspace: pots should be of stone, or second-best earthenware; copper pots that have lost their tinning produce abominable food. Dry wood is essential -- olive-wood or ilex -- while fig-wood must be avoided because it produces acrid smoke. The cook's standard seasonings are coriander (fresh-gathered, green), cumin, caraway, cinnamon (thick-barked, luxuriant, strong-scented), mastic (large, bright grains, free of dust), pepper (fresh, not old, large-grained). Utensils must be rubbed clean with brick-dust, then powdered potash and saffron, and finally the fresh leaf of the citron. (pp. 38-39)

The sauce *murri* appears constantly in the recipes and is essentially a soy sauce made from rotted barley (*budhaj* is the Persian word *pudag*, "rotten"). What Arberry translates as "beet" is actually Swiss chard, grown for its thick stalks. "Celery" is actually wild celery (*karafs*), used for its fragrant leaves. "Date-juice" or "date-honey" (*dibs*) is a thick treacle of boiled-down date juice. "Good oil" was probably olive oil; "refined" or "purified" oil was refined by frying cumin or spices in it. (pp. 21-22)

The book is organised into ten chapters: sour dishes, plain dishes, fried dishes, simple dishes, dry dishes, *harisa* (meat-grain porridge), stuffed dishes, roast dishes, and sweetmeats. The first chapter alone gives recipes for *sikbaj* (a sweet-sour meat stew with vinegar and date-juice, raisins, almonds, figs, and saffron); *Ibrahimiyya* (named after Prince Ibrahim al-Mahdi, the Caliph's half-brother and gourmet); *jurjaniyya* (with pomegranate, black grapes, walnuts, almonds); and *hummadiyya* (with citron juice). (pp. 40-42)

**Scene potential:** The cleaning ritual for pots (brick-dust, potash, saffron, citron leaf) is a perfect sensory detail for any kitchen scene; the *murri* sauce made from rotted barley connects to the novel's theme of transformation (rot becoming essential flavour, rags becoming paper); the named recipes after princes and cities provide ready-made dishes for specific social settings.

### The Social and Religious Meaning of Feasting

Perry's "Elements of Arab Feasting" traces the theology of hospitality in Islam. The Prophet is reported to have praised his wife Aisha by saying that she "surpasses other women as *tharid* (bread mixed with stewed meat) surpasses other dishes." Islam, unlike some other religions, positively encourages meat-eating: on the last day of the Pilgrimage, every Muslim who can afford it must sacrifice a lamb. Vegetarians in Islam have been suspected of the impious presumption of forbidding what God has permitted. A meal represented not only hospitality but religious grace: traditions say to eat together and not separate, for blessing is in society. (p. 228)

The feasting tradition drew from al-Jahiz's comic *Book of Misers*, which mocked hosts who served cheap food while praising its health benefits -- in a ninth-century Baghdad context, this cheapness was transparently hypocritical. The Persian conquest brought elaborate display dining to Arab culture: the Sasanian court had slaughtered a thousand animals daily for the Shah's table, and the number of guests at the Shah's expense is given as 15,000. About half of Middle Persian literary writing called *The Story of King Khusrau and His Page* is a discussion of the finest dishes, and this work was later known to the Arabs. The many Persian dishes in medieval Arabic cookery books and the Persian-style gourmet contests of the Abbasid caliphs attest to this influence. (pp. 229-230)

A twelfth-century writer, al-Hariri, gives a classic description of the order of a feast using euphemistic nicknames for each dish: bread is the "Father of Pleasantness"; *sikbaj* (the vinegar-meat stew, served cold) is the "Father of Acuteness" and is always offered to a guest; *harisa* is the "Mother of Strengthening"; *judhaba* is the "Mother of Joy"; *khabis* (a flour-thickened pudding) is the "Father of Dignity"; and a starch-thickened pudding is the "Father of Loftiness." (pp. 230-231)

**Scene potential:** The euphemistic feast-names could be used by a cultivated host in the novel; the Persian influence on court cuisine maps directly onto the novel's Khorasani identity theme; the Islamic theology of hospitality -- eating together as religious grace -- gives depth to any shared meal scene, especially caravan meals.

### Buran: A Dish from the Novel's Timeline

Perry's essay on *buran* begins with a set-piece that falls within the novel's exact timeline. On the evening of 23 December 825 CE, a wedding palace was erected on the Tigris for Caliph al-Ma'mun's marriage to Buran, daughter of the vizier al-Hasan ibn Sahl. The vizier spent fifty million silver dirhams on the celebration. When the Caliph arrived and stood on a tray of woven gold, a thousand pearls were poured over his head -- a literary allusion, since the court poet Abu Nuwas had once compared wine bubbles to "pearls scattered like pebbles on a field of gold." Buran lit a fifteen-pound candle of pure ambergris in the wedding chamber, while in the great hall her father scattered balls of solid perfume containing slips of paper naming a village, estate, or horse that now belonged to whoever caught it. Gifts were provided to every member of al-Ma'mun's armed forces, including the lowly camel drivers and muleteers. The wedding took place during Ramadan, with all-night feasts. (pp. 241-242)

The dish *badhinjan buran* ("Buran's eggplant") first appears about 125 years after the wedding. The original recipe was simple: fried eggplant slices with olive oil, sesame oil, *murri* (soy sauce), pepper, caraway, and rue. Eggplant was still an exotic newcomer from India in the ninth century, called *badhinjan*, which gave rise to the word for eggplant in most European languages (Spanish *berenjema*, French *aubergine*, Italian *melanzana*). Physicians warned it was dangerous, and a Bedouin is quoted as saying of eggplant that "its colour is like the scorpion's belly, and its taste is like the scorpion's sting." (pp. 242-244)

**Scene potential:** The Buran wedding is just after the novel's timeline (825 CE vs. the novel's end at 814 CE), but eggplant as a controversial Indian novelty is perfect for the novel's period -- a character could distrust it or champion it as a marker of cosmopolitan vs. conservative taste. Abu Nuwas's pearl/wine metaphor is directly usable since he is a character in the novel.

### The Caliphal Banquet Tradition and Court Poetry of Food

The *Kitab al-Tabikh*'s preface preserves a remarkable scene from the court of Caliph al-Mustakfi (r. 944-946), in which courtiers compete by reciting poems describing elaborate dishes. One poet describes a tray containing bowls of *kamakh* (a relish) with capers, marjoram, cinnamon, garlic, olives, salted fish, horse-radish, and meat in slices -- all served on a wicker tray with symmetrically laid cups. Another poet describes *sanbusaj* (samosas) in detail: the finest meat, red, soft to touch, minced with onion, cabbage, cinnamon, rue, coriander, cloves, ginger, pepper, cumin, *murri*, and Palmyra salt, enclosed in dough and cooked over a blazing fire. A third poet describes a sequence of courses: roasted kid with tarragon and mint, chicken roasted with a fledgling partridge, *tardina* pastry, *sanbusaj*, fried meat strips in oil, lemons with *nadd* (ambergris-scented perfume mixture) and cheese, vinegar, dates like pearls on a necklace, *buran* sauce with eggplant, asparagus, and a lozenge soaked in butter and buried in sugar. (pp. 25-29)

Though this courtly tradition is set in the tenth century, Perry notes that its roots lie in the Abbasid golden age, when caliphs and princes like Ibrahim al-Mahdi competed as gourmets. The *Kitab al-Tabikh* itself preserves recipes attributed to the personal collections of the caliphs. (p. 35)

**Scene potential:** The competitive poetry-reciting about food at a caliphal banquet is a ready-made scene structure for a feast in Books 2-3; the specific dish sequence (roast kid, chicken, pastry, samosa, fried meat, cheese, vinegar, dates, eggplant, sweets) provides an authentic multi-course dinner for any elite setting.

### Cookery Manuscripts and Food as Written Culture

The *Kitab al-Tabikh*'s preface notes that cookery manuals were known to have been written in the golden age of the Caliphate, but none survived (p. 24). The author defends the pleasure of food with Qur'anic citations and philosophical argument, placing cooking within the hierarchy of lawful pleasures (food, drink, clothes, sex, scent, and sound) with food as the noblest because it preserves life. He states his principles as brevity, succinctness, and exact language -- "as though he were detailing some alchemical operation," writes the editor. The author's precision in specifying weights and measures is described as "admirable, as it is, in such a context, unexpected." (pp. 36-38)

A footnote records that al-Nadim's *Fihrist* (written c. 987) mentions ten lost cookery manuals, and a *Kitab al-Tabikh* by a writer who had access to the actual recipe-books of the Abbasid Caliphs was discovered in an Oxford library -- written by a writer active in the fourth/tenth century. (p. 36)

**Scene potential:** The idea of food manuals as a written literary genre connects directly to the novel's themes of paper and knowledge; the author's Qur'anic justification for food-pleasure could inform a scene where a character discusses the lawfulness of luxury; the "alchemical" precision of cooking instructions parallels the precision of paper-making.

### Persian Influence and Cultural Fusion in the Kitchen

The Persian conquest's influence on Arab feasting is pervasive. Perry notes that about half of the Middle Persian writing called *The Story of King Khusrau and His Page* was a gastronomic discussion, and that the work was later translated and known to the Arabs. Many dishes in medieval Arabic cookbooks bear Persian names (*sikbaj*, *judhaba*, *sanbusaj*, *isfidhabaj*), and the Persian-style gourmet contests of the Abbasid caliphs are well attested. The *lauzinaj* pastry, valued in the court cuisine of ancient Iran, is the only pastry described in pre-Islamic Persian literature. (pp. 222, 230)

This fusion was not merely culinary. The feasting tradition combined three distinct elements: Arab hospitality (rooted in desert survival and Prophetic tradition), Persian display and connoisseurship (rooted in Sasanian court culture), and Islamic theology (the shared meal as religious communion). A Bedouin's contempt for eggplant ("the scorpion's belly") versus a Persian-influenced court that celebrated it as a delicacy maps the same cultural fault-line that runs through the novel. (pp. 228-230, 243)

**Scene potential:** The three-layered tradition (Arab hospitality, Persian refinement, Islamic grace) can structure any meal scene by having different characters embody different attitudes to the same food; Ali's Khorasani heritage could mean the family eats Persian-inflected dishes that mark them as distinct from their Arab neighbours.

## Facts and Dates

- The *Kitab al-Tabikh* is an autograph manuscript written in Baghdad in 623/1226, discovered in 1934 at the Aya Sofya mosque library in Istanbul. (p. 35)
- The manuscript was written 33 years before the Mongol sack of Baghdad. (p. 35)
- Prince Ibrahim al-Mahdi (d. 224/839), half-brother of Harun al-Rashid, was a celebrated gourmet after whom recipes were named. (p. 40)
- The dish *Ibrahimiyya* is named after Ibrahim al-Mahdi. (p. 40)
- Standard cookware: stone pots preferred, earthenware second-best, copper only if freshly tinned. (p. 38)
- Standard seasonings: coriander, cumin, caraway, cinnamon, mastic, pepper. (p. 39)
- *Murri*: a fermented barley sauce functionally identical to soy sauce, ubiquitous in Abbasid cooking. (p. 22)
- *Dibs*: thick treacle or molasses of boiled-down date juice, used as sweetener. (p. 22)
- *Sikbaj*: sweet-sour meat stew with vinegar and date-juice; always served cold to guests. (pp. 40, 231)
- *Harisa*: a hearty porridge of whole grain and meat. (p. 231)
- *Judhaba*: meat roasted in a *tannur* over a sweetened pudding that catches the dripping fat; served in a copper *judhabadan* pan. (pp. 220, 231)
- *Lauzinaj*: almond-paste pastry in thin dough wrappers, stored in jars of syrup and almond oil. (pp. 222-223)
- *Sanbusaj*: samosa-like pastry filled with spiced meat, cooked over a blazing fire. (p. 29)
- *Kamakh*: a fermented relish. (p. 25)
- *Khabis*: flour-thickened pudding, considered a dish of dignity. (p. 231)
- *Tharid*: bread mixed with stewed meat, praised by the Prophet. (p. 228)
- *Zulabiya*: a fritter (known in India as *jelebi*), available at confectioners'. (p. 220)
- *Badhinjan buran*: fried eggplant dish named after al-Ma'mun's wife Buran. Original recipe: fried eggplant with olive oil, *murri*, pepper, caraway, rue. (pp. 242-244)
- Eggplant was an Indian newcomer (*badhinjan*) in the ninth century, distrusted by Bedouins and physicians. (pp. 242-243)
- Buran's wedding (23 December 825 CE): 50 million silver dirhams spent; pearls poured over al-Ma'mun; 15-pound ambergris candle; paper slips in perfume balls granting estates. (pp. 241-242)
- Measurement equivalents from the *Kitab al-Tabikh*: 1 *ratl* = 12 *uqiya* = 16 ounces = 1 pint; 1 *uqiya* = 10 dirhams; 1 dirham = 6 *danaq*. (p. 36)
- Baghdad's food-market geography: Canal of the Fryers, Canal of the Chickens, al-Karkh market quarter, Sharqiyya fruit stalls. (pp. 219-220)
- Over 50 varieties available at the confectioners' alone. (p. 220)
- Abbasid-era food poets: Ibn al-Rumi (d. 283/896), Kushajim (d. c. 350/961), Ibn al-Mu'tazz (d. 296/908). (pp. 25-29)
- The Prophet's tradition on hospitality: "The best among you is he who feeds people." (p. 228)
- Islam positively encourages meat-eating; vegetarianism is suspected of forbidding what God has permitted. (p. 228)

## Relevance to Chapters

- **Book 1, Ch 1-3 (Kufa journey):** Caravan meals: *harisa* (porridge), flatbread from *tannur*, dates, dried meat. The contrast between road food and city food.
- **Book 1, Ch 4-5 (Workshop and Suq al-Warraqin):** Baghdad market food for working-class scenes: *judhaba* from a *tannur*, *harisa* stalls, *zulabiya* fritters from confectioners', *sikbaj* served cold from a vendor.
- **Book 1, all journeys:** Each city has a distinct food identity. *Murri* sauce, flatbread, dates, and *sikbaj* are constants; local variations (Kufa's simpler fare, Damascus's fruit, Merv's Persian-inflected dishes) can differentiate.
- **Book 1, apprenticeship scenes:** Abu Said's household meals; the cleaning of workshop pots (brick-dust, potash, saffron, citron leaf) parallels the cleaning of paper-making equipment; shared meals as trust-building between master and apprentice.
- **Book 2, Barmakid connection scenes:** Elite banquet food: multi-course meals, *sanbusaj*, roast kid, *lauzinaj*, competitive food poetry, Persian-inflected dishes at court.
- **Book 2, marriage to Zahra:** Wedding feasting customs; Zahra's household management includes control of the kitchen.
- **Book 3, Harun's night walks:** Street food encountered during Baghdad walks -- *judhaba* vendors, fryers by the canals, confectioners' stalls.
- **Book 3, peak of power:** Court feasting with Abu Nuwas as character; food as display of wealth and status; Ibrahim al-Mahdi as historical gourmet (d. 839).
- **Book 4, siege of Baghdad:** Food scarcity during the siege; the contrast between earlier abundance and wartime deprivation; what people eat when the supply lines are cut.
- **All books, all shared meals:** The three-layered tradition (Arab hospitality, Persian refinement, Islamic theology of communion) structures every meal scene differently depending on which characters are present and what cultural register they occupy.
