# Digest: Papermaking: The History and Technique of an Ancient Craft

**Author:** Dard Hunter
**Source:** `reference/Papermaking_ the history and technique of an ancient craft -- Dard Hunter -- 2d_ ed_, rev_ and enl_, New York, Unknown, 1947 -- New York, A_A_ Knopf -- 1150996712 -- a75d59d903dd3d5249c13d5482f54ab8 -- Anna's Archive.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** The definitive English-language history of hand papermaking worldwide, providing the technical craft detail -- raw materials, beating, mould construction, dipping, couching, pressing, sizing, drying, finishing -- that underpins Abu Said's workshop scenes across Book 1.

## Overview

Dard Hunter (1883-1966) spent over forty years travelling the world to document the ancient craft of papermaking. This 714-page second edition (1947) is the most comprehensive single-volume treatment of hand papermaking ever published, covering every stage of the process from the invention of paper in China (c. 105 CE) through its transmission to Samarkand (751 CE), Baghdad (793 CE), and eventually to Europe (12th-13th century). Hunter is a first-hand observer: he visited paper mills in China, Japan, Korea, India, Siam, Indo-China, and Europe, collecting moulds, tools, and fibre samples. His descriptions of workshop interiors, craft techniques, and the physical properties of materials are drawn from direct inspection and experimentation.

For this project, the book is indispensable in two registers. First, it provides the technical vocabulary and step-by-step processes of hand papermaking that must underpin Yusuf's apprenticeship: the preparation and fermentation of rags, the beating of fibre in stone mortars and trip-hammers, the construction of bamboo and wire moulds, the vatman's dipping motion, the coucher's skill in transferring wet sheets, the pressing, drying, sizing with starch or gelatine, and finishing by polishing with smooth stone. Second, it narrates the transmission of the craft from China to the Islamic world with specific dates and mechanisms -- the Battle of Talas River in 751, Chinese prisoners put to work making paper in Samarkand, the role of flax and hemp as substitute fibres, and the establishment of Baghdad's first paper mill in 793 under Harun al-Rashid -- all of which form the historical substrate of the novel's world.

Hunter's weakness for this project is that he devotes relatively little space to Islamic papermaking per se; his primary focus is the Orient (China, Japan, Korea) and the Occident (Europe, America). The Islamic world appears as a transmission conduit rather than a subject in its own right. Nevertheless, the technical detail he provides for the universal processes of hand papermaking is directly applicable to an 8th-century Baghdad workshop, since -- as Hunter repeatedly emphasises -- the fundamental principles remained unchanged for centuries.

## Key Extracts

### The Invention of Paper and Its Raw Materials

Hunter dates the invention of paper to 105 CE, when the eunuch Ts'ai Lun reported to the Emperor Ho Ti. The original materials were bark of trees, discarded cloth, hemp waste, old rags, and fish nets (p. 52). The key innovation was reducing these materials to disintegrated fibre and forming sheets upon a porous mould -- distinguishing true paper from papyrus, tapa, and other laminated or beaten-bark substances.

The earliest Chinese paper was made from disintegrated cloth -- specifically, waste clippings trimmed from woven silk manuscripts. These small fragments of cloth were wetted, placed in stone mortars, and pounded with pestles until reduced to fibre. The tangled mass of beaten fibres was thrown into a tub of clear water, where the minute filaments floated on the surface. The challenge was to devise a tool that could lift this matted film intact while allowing water to drain through (pp. 77-78).

**Scene potential:** The origin story of paper -- waste cloth scraps beaten in mortars, fibres floating on water like windblown seeds -- could be told by Abu Said to young Yusuf as a teaching parable about how something worthless becomes something essential.

### The Spread to the Islamic World: Samarkand and Baghdad

Hunter provides the core transmission narrative. Paper may have been imported and sold in Samarkand before 751, but the craft of making it was not practised there until after the Battle of the Tharaz (Talas) River, when Chinese prisoners of war included skilled papermakers. Their captors set these craftsmen to work (p. 60). The manufacture was favoured in Samarkand by abundant crops of flax and hemp and by numerous irrigation canals providing the pure water essential to paper production.

From Samarkand, the craft spread to Baghdad and Damascus and finally into Egypt and Morocco. Hunter's chronology gives 793 CE as the date paper was first fabricated in Baghdad, noting that Harun al-Rashid "acquired skilled artisans from China for the purpose" (p. 469). Paper reached Egypt around 800 CE, probably imported from Samarkand or Baghdad before local manufacture began around 900 CE.

A critical detail: when the Chinese prisoners began making paper in Samarkand, mulberry bark -- the standard Chinese material -- was not readily available. The Arabs therefore employed linen as a substitute (p. 139). This substitution of rag for bark was a pivotal technological adaptation, since linen rags became the standard material for all subsequent Islamic and European papermaking.

Hunter also records that the earliest use of paper in Mecca was 707 CE, with the material brought from China (p. 469), and the earliest use in Samarkand was around 650 CE as imported Chinese paper (p. 468).

**Scene potential:** Abu Said's Sogdian heritage could include family memory of the Samarkand mills established after 751. The shift from bark to linen rags is a detail he could explain to Yusuf -- the craft adapting to local materials, the first principle of a good papermaker.

### The Arab Method of Preparing Rags

This is the most directly relevant section for workshop scenes. Hunter describes the Arab method of disintegrating linen rags:

1. **Fermentation.** Rags were placed in heaps, saturated with water, and left to ferment. The fermentation was manifested by increased temperature and the rags taking on a colour not unlike the dregs of wine. This process was wasteful -- fully one third of the material rotted beyond use -- but the surviving portion was tender and easily beaten (p. 153).

2. **Boiling.** The rags were boiled in wood ashes (an alkaline lye bath). In some mills lime was thrown upon the rags to assist disintegration, though this was detrimental to the paper's strength and longevity (pp. 140, 155).

3. **Washing.** The boiled rags were placed in cloth bags and suspended in a running stream. The water action removed the alkaline residue and much of the dirt (p. 140).

4. **Beating.** The original method was mortar and pestle -- stone mortars with heavy pestles operated by hand. Later, the Arabs developed the trip-hammer: workers treading upon the end of a horizontal tilt-bar caused the hammer to fall upon the material. This required far less labour but remained a step behind the water-powered stamping-mills that would later appear in Europe (pp. 140-142). In India, where methods derived from the Samarkand tradition, the material was cut to bits, soaked in lime for several days, then thrown into rough stone mortars sunk in the ground, where it was beaten by heavy pestles balanced on pivots. Men and boys stood on the long horizontal beams, letting the hammers fall over and over until the substance was beaten to pulp (p. 142).

Hunter notes that when papermaking was first introduced into Europe, the methods of the East were used for maceration, but that by about 1151 the water-powered stamping-mill was invented at Xativa (Jativa) in Spain (p. 153). This means Abu Said's Baghdad workshop, operating in the 780s-790s, would use trip-hammers powered by workers treading on tilt-bars, not water-power.

**Scene potential:** The sour, alkaline smell of fermenting rags. The rhythmic thud of trip-hammers. Boys treading on the tilt-bar beams, their weight driving hammers into stone mortars. Cloth bags of boiled rags suspended in the canal behind the workshop. These sensory details are the daily texture of Yusuf's apprenticeship years.

### The Mould: Papermaking's Essential Tool

Hunter devotes his longest chapter (62 pages) to the hand-mould, calling it the papermaker's most important implement. Two fundamental types exist:

**The "wove" mould** -- the earliest form, a square of coarsely woven cloth held within a four-sided bamboo frame. Paper stock was either dipped up from below or poured onto the surface, then dried on the mould in the sun. This method required many moulds, since each sheet needed about half an hour to dry (pp. 78-79).

**The "laid" mould** -- the crucial innovation. A flexible screen made of thin rounded bamboo strips placed side by side, stitched together at regular intervals with silk, flax, camel-, yak-, or horsehair. The strips left impressions called "laid-lines" in the finished paper; the stitching left "chain-lines." This mould allowed the wet sheet to be removed immediately after forming (couched), so the craftsman could form sheets continuously on the same mould (pp. 86-88). The cover sat loosely on a shallow wooden framework with supporting ribs. Two wooden sticks (deckle sticks), held one in each hand along the outer edges, kept the wet pulp within bounds during dipping (pp. 88-89).

For the novel's Baghdad setting, the moulds would be of the laid type, derived from the Chinese-Samarkand tradition. The mould-cover material in a Baghdad workshop would likely be bamboo strips laced with horsehair (pp. 86, 92), though grass was also used in India and Kashmir.

**Scene potential:** The construction of moulds is a senior craftsman's skill. Abu Said or his best workers would make the moulds themselves. The precision of spacing bamboo strips, the patient lacing of horsehair through them -- this is slow, exacting handwork, the kind of thing that separates a master's workshop from an ordinary one.

### The Vatman's Art: Dipping, Shaking, and Couching

The process of forming sheets is described in detail for European mills, but the principles are universal:

The vatman stood before the vat holding the mould by its two narrow edges. He plunged it at an almost perpendicular angle into the fibrous liquid, turned it face upwards when submerged, and lifted it horizontally. Surplus stock ran over the far edge back into the vat. He then shook the newly formed sheet -- first from right to left, then from back to front. These crossing motions matted the fibres, making the sheet almost equally strong in both directions, while expelling considerable water. The wires of the mould retained the fibres but allowed water to drain through as in a sieve (pp. 177-178).

The vatman then passed the mould to the coucher, who placed it against an inclined drainage-horn (or "asp") to drain. When the wet sheet solidified to the proper extent -- determined by a distinctive lustre over its surface -- the coucher turned the mould completely over and deposited the sheet upon a piece of felting. The couching was done with a quick, deft motion, rocking the mould from one long edge to the other, leaving the sheet flat and unwrinkled upon the woollen cloth (p. 178).

In Oriental practice, no felts were used between sheets. Each sheet was couched directly upon the previous one. The pile was then pressed to expel surplus water (p. 179). A post of 144 sheets (six quires) was standard in European mills; the number would vary by size and tradition.

The stirring of the vat contents to prevent settling was originally done by a pole in the hands of a workman (p. 155). In Oriental mills, and likely in Baghdad, this ancient method persisted.

**Scene potential:** The vatman's dipping motion is the physical heart of the craft -- the plunge, the turn, the lift, the shake. The lustre that tells the coucher the sheet is ready. The coucher's quick rocking motion. These are the movements Yusuf would learn over years, first watching, then assisting, then performing under Abu Said's exacting eye.

### Pressing and Drying

After couching, the pile of wet sheets was placed in a press -- originally a heavy wooden screw press -- to expel excess water (pp. 182-184). In Oriental mills, a lever press was more common.

Drying methods varied. In Europe, sheets were hung over cow-hair ropes strung in a loft, or simply on lines (pp. 189-190). In India, paper was dried by pasting it onto walls or laying it flat on the ground in the sun (pp. 191, 193). In the Orient, paper was often dried upon boards (pp. 190-191). The Chinese method depicted by Sung Ying-hsing (1634) shows sheets being dried against a heated oven or kiln surface (p. 192).

For Baghdad, drying would likely be done on walls, boards, or ropes in a loft or courtyard -- the intense sun and dry heat would serve well.

**Scene potential:** The drying-loft of Abu Said's workshop: thin sheets of paper hanging like pale ghosts in rows, the fine white dust of the finishing room, the smell of drying paper -- faintly sweet and grassy.

### Sizing and Finishing

Sizing was essential to make paper impervious to ink for writing. The earliest Oriental method was to cover the surface with a thin coating of gypsum. This was followed by lichen, then starch or rice flour (p. 194). The Chinese used starch as a size as early as 768 CE (p. 194) -- which places the technique squarely within the novel's timeframe. In India, the paper was coated with rice or wheat paste applied by brush (p. 195). European papermakers later used animal gelatine rendered from hides -- but this was not the Islamic practice.

For finishing, the earliest method was burnishing each sheet by hand with an agate or other glossy stone (pp. 196-197). The Orientals gave their paper a glass-like surface by this procedure. One side was burnished; the reverse, laid against a smooth slab of wood, also took on a certain finish. This polishing closed the pores and rendered the sheets more suited for writing. The tools from Kashmir show polished stones set in clay or wooden handles (p. 197).

**Scene potential:** The finishing room: a boy sitting cross-legged, rubbing each sheet with a smooth stone until it gleams. The repetitive, meditative labour. The sound of stone on paper. The sizing process -- brush-coating each sheet with starch paste, the faint sweetness of the paste. These are the tasks an apprentice would learn in his first years.

### Watermarks: Origins and Absence in Islamic Paper

Hunter is definitive: no watermarks in the form of symbols or devices are found in ancient Oriental papers (p. 260). The earliest use of watermarks occurred in Italy about 1282, consisting of simple crosses and circles (p. 450). The reason for their absence in Eastern paper is that bamboo mould-covers did not lend themselves readily to forming applied designs.

However, the novel's watermark innovation (Book 2, c. 793 CE) is a fictional device. Hunter's evidence that watermarks were technically possible -- Chinese moulds from the 19th century bear watermarks made of ramie (China grass) characters stitched onto the bamboo cover (p. 260) -- suggests the concept could have been invented earlier but was not. The earliest European watermarks were wire designs laced to the mould surface (pp. 262-264).

**Scene potential:** Yusuf's watermark innovation in Book 2 would involve stitching a design in thread or thin wire onto the mould-cover surface, so that every sheet formed on that mould bears the mark as a thin spot visible when held to light. The technical plausibility is confirmed by Hunter.

### The Secrecy of the Craft

A recurring theme is the jealous guarding of papermaking secrets. The Chinese kept the craft secret for over 600 years before the Battle of Talas. When Ulman Stromer established the first German paper mill in 1390, he forced all workers to swear oaths of secrecy, with Italian workers taken before a procurator. His diary records the earliest labour dispute in paper history: the Italian workers deliberately hampered production, hoping to force Stromer to lease them the mill (pp. 233-234).

Hunter notes that even in modern times, Japanese papermakers boiled seeds of mitsumata and gampi plants before giving them to him as a gift, ensuring they could never germinate in America (p. 149).

**Scene potential:** Abu Said's workshop secrecy -- the oaths his workers take, the closed doors, the prohibition on outsiders watching certain processes -- is historically authentic. Jabir's sabotage in Chapter 4 has a direct parallel in Stromer's experience with his Italian workers.

### Workshop Organisation and Labour

Hunter describes the standard division of labour in a European mill: the vatman (who dips the mould), the coucher (who transfers the sheet), and the layman (who separates dried sheets). In addition there were beaters, sorters (often women, who sorted rags by quality and colour), pressers, and dryers. Stromer's diary mentions a carpenter for repairing stampers and vats, and notes that the carpenter's wife sorted rags and hung paper to dry (p. 235).

In Indian mills, the vatman sits at his work rather than standing (p. 178). In Korean mills, two workers operate a single large mould together (p. 96).

**Scene potential:** The hierarchy of Abu Said's workshop: the master papermaker at the vat, senior journeymen at the hammers, apprentices sorting rags, carrying water, tending the drying-loft, polishing finished sheets. Women sorting rags in a separate area. The slow progression from menial tasks to the vat is the arc of Yusuf's apprenticeship.

### A Physician of Baghdad on Wrapping Paper

A vivid detail from the chronology: in 1140, a physician of Baghdad wrote about the source of wrapping paper used by grocers in the city's markets. He recorded that Bedouins and fellahs searched ancient burial sites to recover the cloth bands in which mummies had been swathed, and when these could not be used for clothing, they sold them to the paper mills, which made of them paper destined for the food markets (p. 405).

**Scene potential:** This is the kind of dark, earthy detail that grounds the paper trade in the material realities of the medieval Islamic world. The grocers' wrapping paper made from mummy cloth -- Yusuf could hear this rumour on a trade journey to Egypt (Book 2-3).

- **105 CE:** Ts'ai Lun reports invention of paper to Emperor Ho Ti. Made from mulberry bark, hemp waste, old rags, fish nets (p. 50)
- **c. 150 CE:** Earliest surviving paper, found in the Great Wall by Sir Aurel Stein. Made from rags (p. 466)
- **c. 610 CE:** Papermaking introduced to Japan from China via Korea (p. 53, 468)
- **c. 650 CE:** Earliest use of paper in Samarkand, imported from China (p. 468)
- **c. 700 CE:** Papers begin to be sized: first with gypsum, then lichen, then starch flour (p. 468)
- **707 CE:** Earliest use of paper in Mecca, brought from China (p. 469)
- **751 CE:** Battle of the Tharaz (Talas) River. Chinese prisoners, including skilled papermakers, captured in Turkestan. They are set to work making paper in Samarkand -- the first papermaking outside China (pp. 60, 469)
- **768 CE:** Chinese use starch as paper sizing (p. 194)
- **770 CE:** Earliest text printing on paper -- the Empress Shotoku's million printed dharani (p. 469)
- **793 CE:** Paper fabricated for the first time in Baghdad, introduced by Harun al-Rashid, who acquired skilled artisans from China (p. 469)
- **c. 800 CE:** Earliest use of paper in Egypt, probably imported from Samarkand or Baghdad (p. 469)
- **c. 900 CE:** Paper first made in Egypt using Chinese methods (p. 470)
- **c. 950 CE:** Earliest use of paper in Spain (p. 470)
- **1035 CE:** Persian traveller Nasiri Khosrau astonished to see Cairo vendors wrapping goods in paper (p. 471)
- **c. 1100 CE:** Papermaking begins in Morocco, introduced from Egypt (p. 472)
- **1109 CE:** Earliest existing European manuscript on paper -- a deed of King Roger, in Arabic and Greek, Sicily (p. 472)
- **1140 CE:** Baghdad physician writes about grocers' paper made from mummy cloth (p. 405)
- **1147 CE:** Legend of Jean Montgolfier taken prisoner by Saracens and forced to labour in a Damascus paper mill (p. 416)
- **1150 CE:** El-Edrisi describes Xativa (Spain) paper as the finest in the civilised world (p. 472)
- **1151 CE:** Water-powered stamping-mill for rags first used at Xativa, Spain -- adopted from Oriental methods (p. 472)
- **1282 CE:** First watermarks used in Europe -- simple crosses and circles, Italy (p. 450)
- **1337 CE:** Probable earliest use of animal (gelatine) sizing for paper in Europe (p. 475)
- Linen rags replaced mulberry bark as the standard material when papermaking moved to Samarkand, because mulberry was not readily available (p. 139)
- The Arab method of maceration: ferment rags in heaps, boil in wood ash lye, wash in running streams, beat in stone mortars with trip-hammers (pp. 140-142)
- Starch sizing was used in Baghdad-era paper; animal gelatine sizing was a later European development (p. 194)
- Oriental papermakers polished sheets with smooth agate or stone burnishers to create a writing surface (pp. 196-197)
- No watermarks existed in Oriental or Islamic paper; the first appeared in Italy c. 1282 (pp. 260, 450)
- Trip-hammers operated by foot-treading were the standard maceration technology in the Islamic world before European water-power innovations (p. 140)
- The craft was jealously guarded: oaths of secrecy were standard from the Chinese period through medieval Europe (pp. 233-234)

## Relevance to Chapters

- **Book 1, Apprenticeship (ages 9-21):** The core technical reference for all workshop scenes. The step-by-step process of rag preparation, beating, mould construction, dipping, couching, pressing, drying, sizing, and finishing provides the physical substrate of Yusuf's education. The sensory palette of the workshop -- the sour tang of fermenting rags, the rhythmic thud of trip-hammers, the fine white dust of the finishing room, the faint sweetness of starch sizing -- comes from these descriptions.
- **Book 1, Ch 4 (The Poisoned Vat):** The sizing vat sabotage. Hunter's description of the sizing process (starch paste applied by brush or dipping) and the critical importance of proper sizing for writing-quality paper directly informs this chapter. The "poisoned vat" could involve contamination of the sizing solution.
- **Book 1, Ch 5 (The Suq al-Warraqin):** Yusuf's knowledge of paper quality -- how to assess a sheet by holding it to light, checking for imperfections, testing sizing by tongue-wetting or crumpling -- derives from Hunter's descriptions of paper defects and quality indicators (pp. 225-227).
- **Book 2 (Watermark innovation):** Hunter confirms that watermarks were technically feasible by stitching wire or thread designs onto mould-covers, but were not historically used in Islamic paper. This validates the novel's fictional innovation while establishing its plausibility.
- **Book 1, background (Abu Said's origins):** Abu Said is Sogdian, from the Samarkand papermaking tradition. Hunter's account of the 751 transmission -- Chinese prisoners, the substitution of linen for mulberry bark, the role of irrigation canals -- provides Abu Said's ancestral professional heritage.
- **Books 2-3 (Paper trade expansion):** The spread of paper to Egypt (c. 800-900), Morocco (c. 1100), and the growing market for the material provides context for the House of Paper's commercial expansion across the caliphate.
