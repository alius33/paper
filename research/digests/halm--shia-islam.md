# Digest: Shi'a Islam: From Religion to Revolution

**Author:** Heinz Halm (trans. Allison Brown)
**Source:** `reference/shia-islam--halm.pdf`
**Date digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** Origins of Shi'ism, Karbala, Kufa as a Shi'a city, the Penitents movement, Shi'a under the Abbasids, taqiyya, the deportation of the seventh imam by Harun al-Rashid, Shi'a settlement in Baghdad's al-Karkh district.

---

## Overview

Halm's compact survey traces Twelver Shi'ism from the succession dispute after the Prophet's death through the Iranian revolution, but the material most relevant to this project is concentrated in Part One ("The House of Sorrow: The Twelve Imams"), which covers the first through seventh imams -- exactly the period whose aftershocks define the religious landscape of Yusuf's Baghdad. The book is a reliable secondary synthesis, written for educated non-specialists, drawing on early Arabic source material (particularly the Kufan chronicler Abu Mikhnaf, d. 774 CE). Its strength for this project is threefold: (1) it explains the emotional and theological core of Shi'ism as a lived religious identity rooted in guilt, repentance, and surrogate suffering -- not just as a political faction; (2) it establishes Kufa as the birthplace and spiritual capital of Shi'ism in an explicitly Arabic, Iraqi context (countering the common misconception that Shi'ism is an Iranian phenomenon); and (3) it provides specific details about Shi'a communities under Harun al-Rashid, including the deportation of the seventh imam and the settlement of Shi'ites in Baghdad's southern suburb of al-Karkh.

The book is strongest on theology and ritual meaning, less detailed on social history or daily life. For the texture of how Shi'a identity manifests in the streets and homes of eighth-century Kufa and Baghdad, this digest should be cross-referenced with the existing research files on Baghdad society and the Kufa journey.

## Key Extracts

### 1. Kufa: The Birthplace of Shi'ism

Kufa on the Euphrates is the origin point of Shi'ism as both a political and a religious movement. Ali ibn Abi Talib himself moved to Kufa when he became caliph in 656, making it his capital. He was assassinated there in 661, inside the entrance to a mosque. His comrades-in-arms from the Battle of Siffin lived there, and after his death, Kufa became the stronghold of "the partisans" (the Shi'a), who hoped that one of Ali's sons would reclaim his inheritance. Halm emphasises that this was initially a political party, not a religious sect -- it took on religious characteristics only after the disaster at Karbala.

Halm is emphatic that Shi'ism originated in a purely Arabic environment in Iraq, not in Iran. The Arab character of Kufa's Shi'a community persisted for centuries. The first Shi'a colony in Iran (Qom) was founded in 712 by Arab colonists expelled from Kufa for their Shi'i beliefs -- an Iraqi transplant, not an indigenous Iranian development. Iran was not systematically Shi'ised until the Safavid dynasty imposed it as state religion in 1501.

**Scene potential:** When Yusuf visits Kufa in 787, he is walking through the city where Ali was murdered, where the Penitents marched, where the Shi'a was born. The Great Mosque where Umar prays (Chapter 3) would carry these associations for any Shi'a-sympathising character. Kufa's identity as a city of grievance, of loyalty betrayed, of old wounds unhealed, is theologically grounded here.

### 2. The Tragedy of Karbala (680 CE) and the Guilt of the Kufans

In 680, Ali's second son al-Husayn was invited to Kufa by partisans who promised thousands of supporters for an uprising against the Umayyad caliph Yazid. Al-Husayn set out from Mecca with a small band -- family and friends, not more than fifty people. But the Iraqi governor executed the Kufan rebel leaders before al-Husayn arrived. Of the thousands of promised supporters, not a single one came to his aid. On the 10th of Muharram (October 680), al-Husayn's group was massacred at Karbala, 70 kilometres north of Kufa. The governor's troops had blocked their access to the Euphrates for three days. Nearly all the men were killed.

Halm argues that Karbala was the "big bang" that transformed the Shi'a from a political faction into a religious community. The theological core of Shi'ism is not the event itself but the guilt that followed -- the Kufan partisans who invited al-Husayn and then failed to come to his aid.

**Scene potential:** This collective guilt is the spiritual atmosphere Yusuf breathes in Kufa. Umar's private grief at the Great Mosque (Chapter 3) resonates against this history. The city itself carries the weight of a betrayal more than a century old but still alive in the hearts of its people. The emotional logic is: we called him, we failed him, we bear his blood on our hands.

### 3. The Penitents (Tawwabun): Collective Guilt Made Physical (684 CE)

The aftermath of Karbala produced the movement Halm identifies as the true origin of Shi'i religious practice. A group of Kufan partisans -- led by Sulayman ibn Surad, an old veteran of Ali's wars -- gathered in Kufa to engage in collective self-reproach for their failure to save al-Husayn. They debated collective suicide as atonement, citing the Quranic precedent of the Israelites who worshipped the golden calf and were commanded to kill one another. Since the Quran forbids suicide, they chose instead a collective death march against the Syrian army -- expiatory death on the battlefield as a substitute.

In November 684, the Penitents marched north from Kufa along the Euphrates. They paused at Karbala to spend one night weeping over the grave of al-Husayn. In January 685, they were destroyed as expected by Syrian troops. The survivors were ashamed to have lived.

Halm identifies this episode as the source of the Ashura rituals (flagellation, chest-beating, sword-wounds) that persist to this day. These are not mourning rituals but rituals of repentance -- an attempt to discharge a debt of guilt accumulated across generations. The logic: by shedding our own blood, we show that if we had been at Karbala, we would have stood with the imam.

The key theological terms that emerge here: *dhanb* (sin/guilt), *tauba* (repentance/atonement), and *uquba* (punishment).

**Scene potential:** The Penitents marched from Kufa. Umar's emotional response at the Great Mosque, his connection to Shi'a grief, gains depth against this history. A character in Kufa who holds Shi'a sympathies is not just expressing a political opinion -- he is participating in a theology of guilt that is physically enacted, generation after generation. The concept of *tauba* (repentance through self-sacrifice) could inform how Umar understands duty.

### 4. The Shi'a Under the Abbasids: Betrayed Again

The Abbasid revolution of 749-750 that overthrew the Umayyads was partly powered by Iraqi Shi'ites who hoped to finally install an Alid imam. They were disappointed. The Abbasids -- descendants of the Prophet's uncle al-Abbas, not of Ali -- seized power for themselves and pushed the Shi'ites aside. The sixth imam, Ja'far al-Sadiq (702-765), was reportedly offered the caliphate by Kufan partisans during the chaos of the revolution and declined.

Ja'far al-Sadiq established the model of political quietism that defined mainstream Twelver Shi'ism for centuries. He maintained contact with the Iraqi Shi'a communities but refused any political activity, and was left in peace by the Abbasid caliphs. He is regarded as the founder of Shi'i law (the Ja'farite legal school). The Shi'ites thus named their faith after him.

This quietist model is critical for understanding the Shi'a world Yusuf encounters. By the 780s, mainstream Shi'ism (the imamiyya) did not advocate armed revolt. The imams lived in Medina as private scholars. Shi'a identity was expressed through loyalty to the family of the Prophet, through distinct legal practice, through grief for Karbala -- not through insurrection. This makes the Alid revolts that did occur (which were typically led by fringe Alid claimants, not by the recognised imams) politically complex events that did not necessarily represent the mainstream Shi'a community.

**Scene potential:** This is the tension Yusuf would encounter in Kufa: a population whose deepest loyalties are to the family of Ali, who grieve privately and enact their grief ritually, but who are not (mostly) revolutionaries. The Abbasids came to power partly on Shi'a hopes and then betrayed those hopes -- a second betrayal layered on top of the Karbala guilt. This gives Kufa its quality of suppressed sorrow.

### 5. Shi'a in Baghdad: The Karkh Connection

When Caliph al-Mansur founded Baghdad in 762, it rapidly became Iraq's dominant city, and a significant number of Shi'ites settled there -- concentrated in the southern suburb of al-Karkh, though they remained a minority of the total population. This detail is architecturally significant for the novel: Yusuf's family lives near the Khorasan Gate, and Abu Said's paper workshop is in al-Karkh. The neighbourhood where Yusuf apprentices has a Shi'a presence.

**Scene potential:** The Shi'a minority in al-Karkh would be a quiet fact of Yusuf's daily life during his apprenticeship. Some of his fellow workers, neighbours, or customers at the paper workshop might be Shi'a. This religious diversity is part of Baghdad's texture -- not a source of overt conflict in normal times, but a fault line that could crack open during political crises (Alid revolts, the civil war of 809-813).

### 6. Harun al-Rashid and the Seventh Imam (795-796 CE)

In 795-796, when Harun al-Rashid made his pilgrimage to Mecca and Medina, he brought the seventh imam, Musa al-Kazim, back to Iraq with him and held him under house arrest -- first in Basra, then in Baghdad. The imam's following had apparently grown large enough to frighten the caliph. Musa al-Kazim died in captivity in 799 and was buried in the elite cemetery north of Baghdad (the site later known as al-Kazimayn, after his shrine).

Halm notes that the Shi'ites describe this "Babylonian captivity" of the imams as a chain of martyrdoms. According to Shi'i tradition, Musa al-Kazim was poisoned by Harun's agents. The shrine at al-Kazimayn, with its two golden domes (the seventh and ninth imams are buried side by side), became a major pilgrimage site.

**Scene potential:** This event occurs during Yusuf's apprenticeship (he is 25-26 in 795-796). The deportation of a living imam to Baghdad -- a descendant of the Prophet held prisoner in the same city where Yusuf works -- is exactly the kind of event that would circulate as whispered news through al-Karkh's Shi'a population. The imam's death in 799 would be felt as a fresh martyrdom. Yusuf does not need to be Shi'a himself to be aware of this; it is part of the political weather.

### 7. Taqiyya: Dissimulation as Survival

Halm explains the concept of *taqiyya* (precautionary dissimulation) as central to Shi'i life under hostile rulers. Ali himself is said to have practised taqiyya during the twenty-four years he was denied the caliphate. The principle permits -- even requires -- Shi'ites to conceal their true beliefs when under threat. This is not considered hypocrisy but prudent self-preservation mandated by the imams.

**Scene potential:** Taqiyya explains why Shi'a identity in Kufa or Baghdad would not always be visible to an outsider like Yusuf. People who hold deep Alid loyalties might present as conforming Sunnis in public. The gap between surface and substance -- precisely the kind of thing Yusuf is trained to perceive -- is theologically structured into Shi'a life. A merchant in Kufa who seems unremarkable might reveal Shi'a sympathies only in private, in the safety of his own home, or through subtle signals (an invocation, a name, a reaction to the mention of Karbala).

### 8. The Eighth Imam and Al-Ma'mun (816-818 CE)

After Harun's death and the civil war (which falls in Books 3-4), al-Ma'mun took the extraordinary step of naming the eighth imam, Ali al-Rida, as his heir apparent in 816. The imam was brought from Medina to al-Ma'mun's court at Merv and married to the caliph's daughter. He died in 818 at Tus (modern Mashhad) while al-Ma'mun was marching west to deal with the Baghdad revolt. Shi'ites curse both Harun and al-Ma'mun at the shrine: Harun for imprisoning the seventh imam, al-Ma'mun for the death of the eighth.

**Scene potential:** This falls just after the series timeline (Book 4 ends in 814), but it demonstrates the volatility of the Alid question. Al-Ma'mun's gambit -- trying to co-opt Shi'a loyalty by naming an imam as heir -- is a radical political act that Yusuf (or Khalid) might anticipate or hear rumours of during the civil war period.

## Facts and Dates

- **656 CE:** Ali proclaimed caliph in Medina; moves to Kufa as his capital
- **657 CE:** Battle of Siffin (Euphrates) -- Ali vs. Mu'awiya; inconclusive
- **661 CE (January):** Ali assassinated by Ibn Muljam at the entrance to a mosque in Kufa
- **661 CE:** Al-Hasan abdicates the caliphate to Mu'awiya in Kufa's mosque
- **680 CE (October 10):** Massacre at Karbala -- al-Husayn and ~72 companions killed; his surviving son Ali (the fourth imam) spared because he was ill
- **684 CE (November):** The Penitents (Tawwabun) gather north of Kufa; march to Karbala for a night of mourning, then north along the Euphrates
- **685 CE (January):** Penitents destroyed at Ayn al-Warda in northern Mesopotamia
- **685 CE:** Shi'ite uprising in Kufa; brief Shi'i control of Kufa and parts of southern Iraq (~18 months)
- **702-765 CE:** Sixth imam Ja'far al-Sadiq -- established Shi'i law (Ja'farite school); model of political quietism
- **739-740 CE:** Zayd's revolt in Kufa; killed in battle against Umayyad troops
- **749-750 CE:** Abbasid revolution -- Iraqi Shi'ites helped pave the way but were pushed aside
- **762 CE:** Al-Mansur founds Baghdad; Shi'ites settle in the southern suburb of al-Karkh
- **765 CE:** Death of sixth imam; succession crisis splits the Shi'a into multiple groups
- **774 CE:** Death of Abu Mikhnaf, Kufan Shi'ite chronicler and earliest source for Karbala narrative
- **795-796 CE:** Harun al-Rashid brings the seventh imam Musa al-Kazim from Medina to Iraq; house arrest in Basra, then Baghdad
- **799 CE:** Death of seventh imam in Baghdad captivity; buried at what becomes al-Kazimayn shrine
- **816 CE:** Al-Ma'mun names eighth imam Ali al-Rida as heir apparent at Merv
- **818 CE:** Eighth imam dies at Tus (Mashhad); Shi'ites attribute his death to poison
- **850 CE:** Caliphal order to destroy al-Husayn's pilgrimage shrine at Karbala
- **873-874 CE:** Death of eleventh imam; beginning of the "occultation" of the twelfth imam

### Theological Vocabulary

- **Shi'a** -- "party" (of Ali); the collective community of Ali's partisans
- **Imam** -- leader of the community; from the same root as *umma* (community)
- **Khalifa** -- representative or successor (the Sunni term for the leader)
- **Taqiyya** -- precautionary dissimulation; concealing one's beliefs under threat
- **Tawwabun** -- "the Penitents"; Kufan Shi'ites who marched to their deaths in 684-685
- **Dhanb/khata'** -- sin/guilt (specifically the historical guilt of failing al-Husayn)
- **Tauba** -- repentance/atonement
- **Uquba** -- punishment
- **Shahid** -- martyr
- **Ghayba** -- occultation (of the Hidden Imam)
- **Ziyara** -- pilgrimage visit to the graves of the imams
- **Sayyid** -- honorific for descendants of the Prophet; distinguished by black turbans
- **Bait al-ahzan** -- "House of Sorrow"; the family of the Prophet, considered collectively as martyrs
- **Ma'sum** -- "infallible/protected"; applied to the Fourteen (Muhammad, Fatima, the twelve imams)

## Relevance to Chapters

**Book 1, Ch 1-3 (Journey to Kufa):** Directly relevant. Kufa's identity as the birthplace of Shi'ism, the city where Ali was murdered and the Penitents gathered, shapes its atmosphere. Umar's private devotion at the Great Mosque (Ch 3) and Kufa's quality of suppressed grief are grounded here. The theological concepts of *dhanb* (guilt) and *tauba* (repentance) inform the emotional texture of the city.

**Book 1, apprenticeship years (al-Karkh setting):** The Shi'a population of al-Karkh is a quiet background detail. The seventh imam's deportation to Baghdad (795-796) and death (799) occur during Yusuf's apprenticeship and would be whispered news in the neighbourhood.

**Book 1, political context:** Halm's account of the Abbasids' relationship with Shi'a supporters -- co-opting and then betraying them -- contextualises the Alid revolts that punctuate Yusuf's childhood and youth. The revolts are not random disturbances but eruptions of a betrayal that is over a century deep.

**Book 2 (Barmakid connection):** The Barmakids had Khorasani/Iranian connections. The Shi'a-Sunni dynamic at court, and the political danger of being perceived as sympathising with Alids, is relevant to the power games around the Barmakid purge.

**Book 3 (Harun's court):** Harun's fear of Shi'a influence -- demonstrated by his imprisonment of the seventh imam -- is part of his political character. His willingness to destroy the Barmakids partly reflects anxieties about loyalty and hidden allegiances.

**Book 4 (Civil war, 809-813):** Al-Ma'mun's base in Khorasan and his eventual attempt to co-opt Shi'a loyalty (naming the eighth imam as heir in 816) reflect the enduring power of the Alid question. During the civil war, both sides would court Shi'a support.
