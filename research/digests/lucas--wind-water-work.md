# Digest: Wind, Water, Work: Ancient and Medieval Milling Technology

**Author:** Adam Lucas
**Source:** `reference/Wind, Water, Work_ Ancient And Medieval Milling Technology -- by Adam Lucas [...] -- Brill Academic -- 9789004146495 [...].pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-05
**Relevance:** Technical mechanics of water-powered mills, including trip-hammer paper mills, in the early Islamic world -- directly informs Abu Said's workshop and all papermaking scenes.

## Overview

Lucas's book is a comprehensive scholarly history of milling technology from antiquity through the European Middle Ages, published by Brill in the "Technology and Change in History" series. Its central argument challenges the thesis that medieval Europe experienced a unique "industrial revolution" based on water-power, demonstrating instead that Roman, Chinese, and Islamic civilisations all made sophisticated use of water-powered milling well before European developments. The book runs to 464 pages across ten chapters, extensive appendices, and a bibliography.

For this project, the most valuable material is concentrated in Chapter Two ("Milling Technology in the First Millennium CE"), which devotes a section to Islamic societies (pp. 84-91), and Chapter Six ("Was There an Industrial Revolution in Medieval Europe Based on Water-Power?"), which includes the early history of industrial milling and its Chinese-to-Islamic transmission (pp. 230-235). Chapter One provides essential technical descriptions of how vertical-wheeled and horizontal-wheeled watermills actually work -- the physical mechanisms of gears, shafts, millstones, and trip-hammers that would have been present in Abu Said's workshop. Appendix A catalogues every documented industrial mill from antiquity to 1600 CE and includes specific entries for Islamic paper mills in Samarkand, Baghdad, Damascus, and Persia.

The book's limitation for this project is that Lucas is primarily a historian of European milling, and his coverage of the Islamic world, while well-sourced, is relatively brief and draws heavily on the secondary work of Donald Hill, Ahmad al-Hassan, and Arnold Pacey. He repeatedly notes that Islamic milling technology remains under-studied by Western scholars and that detailed work on the early manuscript sources "remains to be conducted or made known to western scholars" (p. 233). Nevertheless, the technical descriptions, the archaeological evidence, and the transmission narrative are all directly useful.

## Key Extracts

### How a Vertical-Wheeled Watermill Works (the "Vitruvian" Mill)

The standard vertical-wheeled watermill -- the type most likely used in Abu Said's Baghdad workshop for driving trip-hammers -- consists of a waterwheel rotating in a vertical plane, driving a horizontal axle-shaft. At the opposite end of the shaft sits a vertical cog-wheel whose pins engage with a horizontal cog-wheel, forming a right-angled gear that converts the horizontal rotation of the axle into the vertical rotation of a spindle. That spindle rises through the centre of the fixed lower millstone and drives the upper millstone. The relative diameters of the cog-wheels can be varied to control rotational speed -- larger wheel-to-smaller-wheel ratios gear up the speed, while the reverse gears it down (pp. 52-53).

Three subtypes existed: the **undershot** mill, where the wheel's lower perimeter is immersed in flowing water and driven by current; the **overshot** mill, where water is delivered to the top of the wheel and its weight drives rotation (more efficient but more expensive to build); and the **breastshot** mill, an intermediate design. Power output for early watermills is estimated at three to six horsepower on average (p. 56). The mill-race or leat -- an artificial water channel directing flow to the wheel -- could be regulated with sluice gates to control speed (p. 53).

**Scene potential:** The gearing mechanism (vertical cog-wheel engaging horizontal cog-wheel through wooden pins) is the heart of Abu Said's mill -- the rhythmic clicking and grinding of wooden teeth, the spray of water, the shudder of the whole apparatus as it transmits force from the canal to the hammers.

### How a Horizontal-Wheeled Watermill Works

The simpler alternative is the horizontal-wheeled watermill, consisting of a vertical spindle with radial paddles at its lower end, placed in a stream. The force of the water directly turns the spindle, which drives the upper millstone at its top -- no gearing is needed. The most efficient designs use a penstock (a funnel called an *arubah* in Arabic) to direct a concentrated jet of water against the paddles (pp. 57-58).

Donald Hill argues that in the Islamic world, "the relatively cheap, low-powered horizontal mills were used by small communities, whereas the vertical types were located on the larger rivers and served cities and major towns" (p. 84, citing Hill 1998). This is consistent with the archaeological evidence from both the Middle East and Islamic Spain.

**Scene potential:** Small rural mills encountered on journeys (Kufa, Wasit, the Zagros) would likely be horizontal-wheeled -- simpler, cheaper, operated by a single family. The contrast with Abu Said's larger, vertical-wheeled, geared installation in Baghdad is a marker of urban sophistication versus rural simplicity.

### Trip-Hammers: The Chinese Innovation That Made Paper Mills Possible

The key technology linking watermills to papermaking is the trip-hammer -- a cam-operated device that converts rotational motion into the rhythmic pounding action needed to pulp rags and plant fibres. Lucas traces the origin of water-powered trip-hammers to China, where vertical waterwheels without gearing were used to power cam-operated trip-hammers for rice-hulling and metallurgical bellows from as early as the first century BCE (pp. 78-79, 232). These were "gearless vertical waterwheels employing cam-operated trip-hammers" -- the waterwheel's axle had projecting cams that lifted and dropped heavy wooden hammers in sequence (p. 78).

The trip-hammer existed in two forms: the **recumbent** type, where the hammer pivots on a horizontal fulcrum and is lifted by cams on the rotating axle, and the **vertical stamp**, where the hammer is raised vertically and dropped. Both types were "cam-operated" -- wooden projections (cams) on the waterwheel's axle catch and lift the hammer shaft, then release it to fall under its own weight. The term "trip-hammer" is used as shorthand for both types in the scholarly literature (pp. 231-232, citing Reynolds 1983 and Lewis 1997).

Glick and Kirchner identify the industrial applications of water-power by Muslims in Spain as being "Chinese in origin," describing "vertical, trip-hammer mills, wherein hammers attached to the axis of the vertical water-wheel pounded the product to the point where the process could be continued manually. Thus paper and sugar-processing diffused simultaneously" (p. 91, citing Glick & Kirchner 2000). They argue that rice-husking mills and paper mills were "in effect, identical" machines -- if one existed in a region, the other almost certainly did too.

**Scene potential:** The THUMP-THUMP-THUMP of trip-hammers is the signature sound of Abu Said's workshop. The cam mechanism -- wooden teeth on a rotating axle catching the hammer, lifting it, releasing it -- is something Yusuf would learn to maintain and repair. A broken cam tooth or a warped hammer shaft would halt production.

### Water-Powered Milling in the Islamic World: Archaeological and Literary Evidence

Lucas documents clear archaeological evidence for watermills in the Islamic world from as early as the seventh century CE. Thirty-one mills dating from the seventh to thirteenth centuries have been located at two sites in Iraq and Iran, and twelve horizontal-wheeled watermills in Oman date to between the eighth and tenth centuries (p. 84, citing Wikander 1985, 2000). Both horizontal- and vertical-wheeled types were in widespread use from at least the ninth century (p. 84, citing al-Hassan & Hill 1986).

By the time of the Crusades, mills existed "in every province of the Muslim world from Spain and North Africa to Transoxiana [modern Uzbekistan]." These were engaged in grinding grain, fulling cloth, hulling rice, sawing timber, preparing pulp for papermaking, and crushing mineral ores and sugar-cane (p. 88, citing Pacey 1990, al-Hassan & Hill 1986).

**Scene potential:** When Yusuf travels to Kufa, Rayy, or Merv, he would encounter grain mills everywhere along the route -- small horizontal-wheeled mills on irrigation channels, larger vertical-wheeled mills on rivers near cities. Milling is part of the background soundscape of the Abbasid world.

### Islamic Innovations in Water Delivery

Islamic engineers developed at least three innovations in delivering water to mills that were largely unknown outside the Muslim world. In **Persia**, horizontal-wheeled watermills were placed in front of dams, with water conducted through large pipes from the dam's reservoir to the waterwheel (p. 85). The Persians also placed watermills **inside underground irrigation tunnels (qanats)**, exploiting the flow of water in the tunnel to drive the wheel (p. 88). In **Islamic Spain**, watermills were located on the main canals of valley-floor irrigation systems (p. 88).

Ship mills (built on barge-like platforms with wheels turned by river current) and bridge mills (built into bridge superstructures) were also used. The earliest Islamic record of ship mills describes them "moored to the banks of the Tigris and Euphrates from Mosul and Raqqa down to Baghdad" in the tenth century (p. 85, citing al-Hassan & Hill 1986).

**Scene potential:** Ship mills moored along the Tigris at Baghdad -- visible from the riverbank, grinding grain with the current -- are vivid background detail for any scene set near the river. The qanat-mill is striking for a Zagros/Rayy journey: a mill hidden underground, its sound rising through a shaft in the earth.

### Paper Mills in the Islamic World: The Appendix A Evidence

Appendix A catalogues specific documented paper mills. The entries for the Islamic world are (pp. 388-389):

- **8th century, Samarkand** (al-Hassan & Hill 1986, p. 54)
- **Syria** -- undated (ibid.)
- **Jativa, Spain** -- undated (ibid.)
- **Baghdad** -- undated (ibid.)
- **10th century, Persia** (Pacey 1990, pp. 10-11, 42)
- **c. 950, Baghdad** (Pacey 1990, p. 42)
- **c. 1000, Damascus** (ibid.)
- **1041, Samarkand** (ibid.)
- **1151, Jativa, Spain** (ibid.)
- **c. 1150, Catalonia, Spain** (Hill 1998, p. XVIII-10)

Lucas notes that Hill "repeatedly neglected to cite the documentary evidence" for claims about eighth-century paper mills in the Islamic world (p. 88, fn. 48). The water-powered paper mill in Baghdad is attested but not firmly dated in the sources Lucas cites -- the c. 950 date from Pacey is for a later period than the novel's setting. The eighth-century Samarkand entry rests on al-Hassan and Hill's general claim.

**Scene potential:** The ambiguity in dating is actually useful -- it allows Abu Said's workshop (c. 787-791) to be among the earliest paper mills in Baghdad, consistent with the novel's existing timeline of Baghdad's first mills around 794-795 CE. The technology was there; the exact date of adoption in Baghdad is uncertain.

### Mill Ownership in Islamic Societies

Drawing on research about Islamic Spain, Lucas reports that most watermills were built and operated by the tribal groups that settled a region, and were integral parts of larger irrigation systems owned collectively. In Majorca, almost all mills were owned by communal peasant farms (*alquerias*) or individuals associated with them, not by the state or feudal lords. As many as ninety percent of the mills recorded in the *Repartiment* of Valencia were owned by communities of peasant farmers (pp. 89-90, citing Glick & Kirchner 2000).

When the Christian conqueror James I organised the new kingdom of Andalusia, he did not grant mills within Muslim communities to individuals as feudal monopolies, "lending substance to the conclusion that mills were regarded as a public service by Muslims, whatever the details of their ownership may have been" (p. 90). This contrasts sharply with the feudal monopolies that dominated milling in medieval England and France.

**Scene potential:** Abu Said's workshop as a privately owned commercial enterprise (rather than a communal or state facility) marks him as unusual -- a Sogdian merchant-craftsman operating in a market economy, not a communal arrangement. This distinction could be dramatised through contrast with rural mills Yusuf encounters on journeys.

### The Chinese-to-Islamic Transmission Path

The evidence suggests that both Chinese milling innovations and Graeco-Roman watermill technology were inherited by the Islamic world. Chinese trip-hammer technology for rice-hulling and metallurgy was adopted for paper-making and sugar-processing. The transmission path ran through the Silk Road and Central Asian trade networks. Glick and Kirchner argue that "rice, sugar-cane, and paper formed a unified package of Chinese techniques that included processing by means of vertical, trip-hammer mills" -- the technologies travelled together because the machine was essentially the same for all three products (p. 91).

Lucas concludes that "it seems increasingly likely that it was through Islamic Spain and/or the Byzantine Empire that a number of Roman, Islamic, and possibly Chinese innovations in industrial milling technology were conveyed to Western Europe from the tenth or eleventh century onward" (p. 235). The Islamic world was not just a passive conduit but an active innovator in water-delivery systems and industrial applications.

**Scene potential:** Abu Said, as a Sogdian from Samarkand, embodies this transmission path in his person. He carries knowledge that originated in China, was refined in Samarkand, and is now being applied in Baghdad. His workshop is a node in a centuries-long chain of technological transfer across the Silk Road.

## Facts and Dates

- **7th century CE:** Earliest archaeological evidence for watermills in the Islamic world (p. 84)
- **31 mills dated 7th-13th c.** located at two sites in Iraq and Iran (p. 84, citing Wikander)
- **12 horizontal-wheeled mills in Oman** dated 8th-10th century (p. 84)
- **9th century:** Both horizontal and vertical watermills in widespread use across the Islamic world (p. 84)
- **8th century:** Water-powered paper manufacture supposedly begins in the Islamic world (p. 88, Hill 1998 -- but Hill "neglected to cite the documentary evidence")
- **c. 950:** Baghdad paper mills attested (Pacey 1990, p. 42)
- **c. 1000:** Damascus paper mills attested (ibid.)
- **10th century:** Ship mills on the Tigris and Euphrates from Mosul and Raqqa to Baghdad (p. 85)
- **10th century:** Fulling mills first referenced in Islamic sources (p. 88)
- **11th century:** Ore-crushing mills first referenced (p. 88)
- **Power output of early watermills:** 3-6 horsepower average (p. 56)
- **Vertical watermill mechanism:** Waterwheel (vertical) -> axle-shaft (horizontal) -> vertical cog-wheel -> horizontal cog-wheel (right-angled gear) -> spindle (vertical) -> upper millstone (pp. 52-53)
- **Horizontal watermill mechanism:** Water -> paddles on vertical spindle -> upper millstone. No gearing. *Arubah* (funnel/penstock) used in Arabic designs (pp. 57-58)
- **Trip-hammer mechanism:** Cams on rotating axle lift and drop hammer shafts. Chinese origin, 1st century BCE for rice-hulling (pp. 78-79)
- **Trip-hammer and paper mill are identical machines** to rice-husking mills -- if one existed, the other did (p. 91, Glick & Kirchner)
- **Paper, rice, and sugar-processing** travelled as a "unified package of Chinese techniques" through the Islamic world (p. 91)
- **Islamic water-delivery innovations:** Dam-fed mills in Persia; qanat (underground tunnel) mills in Persia; canal-fed mills in al-Andalus (pp. 85, 88)
- **Mill ownership in Islamic Spain:** 90% owned by peasant farming communities; mills regarded as "public service" not feudal monopoly (pp. 89-90)
- **Chinese Commissioners of Watermills:** Two appointed by 10th century to oversee eastern and western regions -- administrative innovation unique to China (p. 233)
