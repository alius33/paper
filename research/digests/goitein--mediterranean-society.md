# Digest: A Mediterranean Society: The Jewish Communities of the Arab World as Portrayed in the Documents of the Cairo Geniza, Volume I: Economic Foundations

**Author:** S. D. Goitein
**Source:** `reference/mediterranean-society--goitein.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-05
**Relevance:** The single most important source for reconstructing the daily mechanics of medieval Islamic commercial life -- partnerships, banking, letters of credit (suftaja), the role of the money-changer (sarraf/jahbadh), cross-confessional trust networks, bookkeeping, and the texture of merchant correspondence. Essential for grounding Yusuf's financial operations (Books 2-3) and Ishaq bar Yohannan's world as a non-Muslim operating within Islamic commercial structures.

## Overview

Goitein's *A Mediterranean Society* is a monumental six-volume reconstruction of medieval Mediterranean life based on the Cairo Geniza -- a repository of discarded writings preserved in a synagogue in Fustat (Old Cairo). Because pious Jews refrained from destroying anything written in Hebrew characters, even secular commercial documents in Arabic, the Geniza accidentally preserved tens of thousands of letters, contracts, court records, and accounts spanning the tenth through thirteenth centuries. Volume I, *Economic Foundations* (1967), treats the commercial and financial world revealed in these documents: the types of merchants, their forms of cooperation, banking and money-changing, business procedures, commodity trade, and travel.

For this novel, Goitein is indispensable on three fronts. First, the Geniza documents reveal the actual mechanics of medieval Islamic commerce as practised by real merchants -- not the prescriptions of legal treatises but the messy, human reality of partnerships that soured, accounts rendered grudgingly, goods lost at sea, and trust painstakingly built across religious and geographic lines. Second, Goitein provides the most detailed surviving evidence for how the commenda (mudaraba/qirad) partnership worked in practice -- the profit splits, the disputes, the layered arrangements where investors, middlemen, and managers each took their share. Third, his treatment of banking, the suftaja (bill of exchange), and the jahbadh (state banker) provides the documentary foundation for Yusuf's financial instruments and Ishaq's daily work as a scribe managing commercial correspondence.

A critical caveat: the Geniza documents date primarily from the eleventh and twelfth centuries, two to three hundred years after the novel's setting (770-814 CE). Goitein himself notes the close connection between Geniza-era banking practices and those known from literary sources for the earlier Abbasid period. The commercial structures documented in the Geniza -- partnerships, commenda, suftaja, the sarraf's clearing-house role -- existed in Yusuf's time, but the specific documentary evidence is later. This digest therefore treats the Geniza material as analogical: the practices described are the mature forms of institutions that were already developing in late eighth-century Baghdad.

## Key Extracts

### The Geniza as a Source: What Survives and Why (pp. 1-10)

The Cairo Geniza is not an archive but its opposite. Documents were deposited only after they had lost all value to their owners, often decades or centuries after they were written. Paper was expensive, so free space on a document was reused for drafts, short notes, accounts, or pen trials -- one release drawn up in 987 CE had pen trials on its reverse dated 1085 CE. The result is a haphazard but vast documentary trove. Unlike official histories written for rulers, these are the records of middle-class merchants, artisans, and their families. Letters to and from women survive in significant numbers; many were evidently dictated, preserving female voices guiding male pens.

The bulk of the commercial documents come from Tunisian merchants living in Egypt or travelling the Mediterranean, concentrated in the eleventh century. The India trade documents cover mainly 1080-1160. Local Egyptian documents dominate the later Fatimid and Ayyubid periods (1160-1250). Goitein emphasises that most of the middle-class Jews still lived in Fustat during the eleventh century, near the synagogue with the Geniza chamber, which is why that period is so richly documented.

**Scene potential:** The physical reality of document preservation -- paper reused, contracts kept for decades, pen trials on old deeds -- directly informs how Yusuf's House of Paper would operate. The cedarwood chest containing the *Daftar al-Asrar* would accumulate exactly this kind of palimpsest of commercial life: contracts, accounts, intelligence notes, all on paper that Yusuf's own workshop produced.

### The Merchant Types and Their Diversification (pp. 149-163)

Goitein distinguishes several categories of merchant. The *tajir* was the wholesale long-distance trader of highest status, dealing in expensive goods like silk, spices, and metals. Below him was the *baqqal* (grocer/retailer). Many merchants combined wholesale and retail, and diversification was the norm: a single merchant might deal in textiles, spices, metals, and dyestuffs simultaneously. Specialisation existed but was not rigid -- a merchant known as "the silk dealer" would also handle flax, indigo, or perfumes as opportunities arose.

The most striking feature is the prevalence of the broker (*dallal* or *simsar*). Brokers served as middlemen for virtually every transaction, whether a wholesale deal in silk or the sale of a slave girl. The commission on sales was called *dallala*. A *dallal* could be a wealthy person participating with a large sum in an overseas undertaking, or a small-time hawker earning a few dirhams a day. The auctioneer (*munadi*) was a related figure, sometimes applied even to large agents. Female brokers were economically important: the ladies of the Muslim upper class were prevented by their husbands from visiting the clothiers' bazaars, so women agents frequented the harems to offer precious textiles. One 1138 contract records a tax-farmer ceding his rights on silk taxes in a particular quarter of Old Cairo while reserving the right to do business with seven women brokers, four with Muslim names.

**Scene potential:** Yusuf's early commercial education would involve learning the roles of these different merchant types. The detail about female brokers operating in harems is directly relevant to Zahra's intelligence network in Books 2-4 -- she could use female textile brokers as informants, a historically grounded mechanism for gathering information from elite households.

### Partnerships and Commenda: How Medieval Commerce Actually Worked (pp. 169-185)

This is the most important section for the novel's financial mechanics. Goitein demonstrates that partnerships (*shirka*) were the dominant form of commercial organisation, substituting for both formal employment and interest-bearing loans. Medieval merchants strongly preferred the dignity of cooperation to the subordination of employment, and religious prohibitions against interest-bearing loans drove investment capital into partnership structures instead.

Two main types existed. In the first, contractors contributed capital, goods, or work in equal or unequal shares and divided profit or loss proportionally. In the second -- the commenda (*qirad* or *mudaraba*) -- one or more partners contributed capital while others did the work, receiving a smaller share of profits. The Geniza records use the terms *qirad* and *mudaraba* interchangeably. Jewish courts called the commenda "mutual loan according to Muslim law" (*qirad al-goyim*), sharply differentiating it from the Jewish partnership (*'isqa*), in which the manager received two-thirds of profits but was also responsible for losses. The Muslim commenda was more common because under the Jewish *'isqa*, a manager who invested work but no capital had limited ability to absorb losses.

The specifics are vivid and varied. In one contract, a notable invested 1,000 dinars in textiles to two partners who each contributed 50 dinars -- the managers sold and bought in Egyptian cities for one year, then divided profits equally with the investor. In another, a capitalist paid in 160 dinars, the manager 10, and profits were split equally, with the manager receiving an additional half-dinar per week. Newcomers to a partnership received smaller shares than established members. One partnership in washing the dead gave a new third partner only one-fourth (not one-third) of fees.

Partnerships commonly crossed religious lines. Jewish brothers in Qayrawan had a partnership with a Muslim in 1048. Several instances of four associates, one Muslim, appear in the Geniza. The legality of cross-confessional partnerships was specifically discussed by legal authorities.

The social role of prestige (*jah*) in partnerships is notable. In one case, a partner who contributed no regular work received an equal share of profits because his social position made a connection with him commercially valuable. In another, a local notable complained that two foreign brothers were mismanaging a store with a 4,000-dinar turnover -- his "sitting in the store" was regarded as vital to the brothers' success, though he evidently attended irregularly.

**Scene potential:** This section provides the documentary template for every partnership Yusuf enters. The detail about prestige (*jah*) as a form of capital directly parallels how Yusuf uses his Barmakid connections -- the Barmakid name on a partnership makes it more valuable, even without active participation. The cross-religious partnerships validate Yusuf's relationship with Ishaq bar Yohannan as historically normal, not exceptional.

### Business Friendship and Informal Cooperation (pp. 164-169)

Before formalising a partnership, merchants built relationships through informal cooperation called *suhba* (companionship). Merchants of lesser stature would be described as the *sahib* (companion) of a more prominent firm. Friendship (*sadaqa*) was also a common term for business relationships, as was *mu'amala* (mutual dealings), used specifically to describe relationships not based on a legal instrument. These informal bonds were remarkably durable -- some lasted decades. When terminating a relationship, merchants tried not to close the door entirely: after almost four thousand words of grudge in a liquidation letter, one Tunisian merchant wrote, "I know, my lord, that your love of me is not marred by any uncertainty," and placed a new order.

The informal system coexisted with formal contracts. Merchants simultaneously maintained formal partnerships in some ventures and informal cooperation in others. The emotional language of these commercial letters is striking: merchants address each other as "my lord," express love and devotion, and frame business relationships in the vocabulary of friendship. But this language was functional, not merely sentimental -- trust was the essential infrastructure of long-distance trade, and the cultivation of personal bonds was a business practice.

**Scene potential:** The emotional texture of merchant correspondence -- the blend of genuine affection and calculated relationship-building -- is exactly the dynamic between Yusuf and his network. The grudge-letter detail (four thousand words of complaint followed by a new order to maintain the relationship) is a perfect model for how Yusuf's commercial relationships would actually read in writing.

### The Banker, the Sarraf, and the Jahbadh (pp. 229-250)

Goitein provides the most detailed surviving account of how medieval Islamic banking actually worked. The banker's first and foremost role was as a clearinghouse for payments. Because coins varied enormously in weight and purity, every transaction required weighing and assaying -- tedious, time-consuming procedures. Money was therefore handled largely in sealed purses of coins whose exact values were indicated on the outside. Some purses bore the seal of certified money-assayers or a government exchange; others were known by the names of individual merchants whose reputation guaranteed the contents. A "purse of 50 dinars with the seal of the well-known banker in the Street of the Alchemists" (1086) was accepted at face value.

The *sarraf* (money-changer) served not only as a currency exchanger but as a banker in the modern sense. He held deposits, made payments on behalf of clients, and maintained running accounts. Bankers' accounts surviving in the Geniza list payments in and out against client names, recording even fractional amounts -- not a single *qirat* (one-twenty-fourth of a dinar) was overlooked. The exchange (*dar al-sarf*) was a formal institution, similar to a stock exchange. Admission was difficult; one prominent merchant-banker, Nahray b. Nissim, whose activities can be traced over several decades, never succeeded in becoming a member.

The *jahbadh* (state banker) was a higher-status figure. Goitein documents the term's Persian origin and its use for bankers who handled government funds. A 1124 document records a jahbadh succeeded by his son in the position, showing that the jahbadhship could be a family affair. All Geniza references to jahbadhs date from the eleventh and early twelfth centuries. By the thirteenth century, the term *naqqad* (assayer of money) appears instead, suggesting a shift in the institution's structure.

The exchange rate granted by a banker was called either *sarf* (from which *sayrafi* is derived) or *si'r* (price). The difference between the Egyptian dinar and foreign currencies of lower value was designated *waks* (loss). Banker commissions appear separately from the exchange rate.

**Scene potential:** This section is the operational manual for Yusuf's banking house. The sealed purse system -- where a banker's seal on a bag of coins substitutes for counting and weighing -- is exactly the kind of trust infrastructure that Yusuf's watermark performs for paper. The jahbadh as a family position validates Khalid's eventual succession. The exchange as a formal institution with restricted membership provides a concrete goal for Yusuf's ambitions in Book 2.

### The Suftaja: Bills of Exchange in Practice (pp. 242-246)

Goitein carefully distinguishes the suftaja from simpler orders of payment (*ruq'a*). The *ruq'a* was essentially a check -- a written order to pay a specified sum to a named person, drawn on a banker or debtor. Its form was similar to a modern check, approximately half the dimensions, with the sum specified in both numerals and words. The *suftaja*, by contrast, was specifically a bill of exchange: a loan of money in order to avoid the risk of transporting cash, where the borrower undertook to repay a third party in another city.

The Geniza evidence shows suftajas were real but their use was more restricted than literary sources suggest. They were not always easy to obtain -- a merchant in Alexandria complained that he could not find anyone prepared to issue a suftaja. Large bankers were cautious about issuing them on large sums because presentation required immediate payment. The Tustari banking family issued a suftaja on 100 dinars but was reluctant to go higher.

Suftajas were not transferable -- when a bill arrived, the named recipient had to present it personally. Bankers sometimes granted advances on suftajas whose issue had been promised. One remarkable reference describes suftajas sent from Fustat to Baghdad, establishing the geographic reach of the instrument. Suftajas were also drawn on commodities, not just cash -- one writer asks to make out a suftaja on 30 small pounds of silk.

Promissory notes issued by bankers were regarded as cash. When one merchant had to realise a suftaja of 44 dinars, it circulated as a payment instrument in its own right. The overall picture is of a sophisticated but cautious system where the personal reputation of the issuing banker was the instrument's only guarantee.

**Scene potential:** The suftaja mechanics described here are exactly what Yusuf builds his fortune on. The detail that suftajas were not always easy to obtain -- that even in major commercial centres, finding someone to issue one could be difficult -- provides the market opportunity that Yusuf exploits. His innovation is making suftajas reliable and available through his network of trusted agents across multiple cities. The reluctance of major bankers to issue suftajas on large sums explains why Yusuf's willingness to do so (backed by his intelligence network and diversified reserves) gives him a competitive advantage.

### Business Procedures: Accounts, Bookkeeping, and the Daftar (pp. 192-209)

Medieval merchants were ambivalent about rendering accounts. Goitein documents a striking cultural tension: business cooperation was regarded as a relationship of mutual trust and friendship, so requests for detailed accounts were perceived as expressions of mistrust. One merchant renounced his request for accounts, promising never to ask again, calling it "hypersensitivity." Another wrote: "God forbid that I should request exact accounts from you or anyone else. I simply wanted to know how much I still owe."

In practice, however, meticulous accounts were kept. Even when totalling hundreds of dinars, not a single qirat (one-twenty-fourth of a dinar) was overlooked. Shorter accounts were included in letters; longer ones were enclosed separately. Merchants requested statements about specific transactions or about business done over a year or more, though often indirectly -- asking the addressee to remind a third person of this duty. Accounts were sometimes written in multiple copies for safety.

The physical form of the account book (*daftar*) is revealing. Sheets were narrow -- three to four inches wide, five to eleven inches long -- because merchants carried their accounts in their sleeves for ready reference. One eleventh-century court deposition records a man taking a daftar out of his sleeve and declaring he would not enter anything whose correctness he had not personally verified. Longer account books were assembled not as bound volumes but as files: sheets folded and laid one above the other, perforated by two holes at a distance from the fold, so new sheets could be inserted over time.

There were no ledgers in the Geniza, no remnants of systematic bookkeeping in the modern sense. What existed was a great variety of accounts: season reports covering business from the spring arrival of ships to their autumn sailing; accounts for specific partnerships or transactions; personal accounts of payments, receipts, purchases, and sales. Methodical merchants listed all credits and sales first, then debits and purchases, and concluded with balances and actions to be taken.

**Scene potential:** The daftar carried in the sleeve is a perfect physical detail for any commercial scene. Yusuf or Ishaq pulling a narrow account booklet from a sleeve to check a figure is exactly the kind of specific, authentic gesture that grounds the historical world. The tension between trust-based relationships and the need for meticulous accounting is a recurring theme in the novel -- Yusuf's insistence on precise records (learned from Abu Said's *siyaq* training) would mark him as unusually rigorous.

### Debts, Loans, and the Problem of Interest (pp. 250-265)

The Geniza reveals an enormous volume of credit transactions. The most common legal instrument was the *iqrar* (acknowledgment): the debtor declared his debt, specified repayment terms (often in instalments), and stood security with all his possessions including "his garment on his shoulders" (an Aramaic expression). The creditor was freed from oaths and declared he had no other claims.

Lending at interest was both religiously shunned and economically limited. The Geniza documents confirm this: people avoided loans partly because "the borrower is a slave to the lender" (Proverbs 22:7). Borrowing manifested dependence, underlined by the fact that loans were often given as charity. Therefore, the economic role of financial investment was fulfilled by various forms of partnership rather than interest-bearing loans.

When merchants could not meet commitments, fellow merchants came to their assistance as a form of informal insurance. Loans of capital or goods (without interest) were the most frequent help. In cases of insolvency, communal authorities arranged settlements: typically one-third of the debt was remitted, another third paid in cash, and the final third paid in instalments. When even this failed, the communal authorities would assemble affluent merchants to make cash contributions. The declaration of bankruptcy ("oath of destitution") required approval by the highest communal authorities; when accepted by a Jewish court, it was apparently regarded as sufficient by Muslim judges as well.

**Scene potential:** The communal response to insolvency -- where fellow merchants collectively prevent bankruptcy through interest-free loans and negotiated settlements -- is directly relevant to how Yusuf's commercial network would function. The detail about cross-confessional recognition of bankruptcy declarations (Jewish court rulings accepted by Muslim judges) illustrates the practical accommodation between legal systems that Ishaq bar Yohannan navigates daily.

### Cross-Confessional Commerce and the Boundaries of Trust (pp. 70-74, 173)

Goitein argues that the Geniza people were "to a certain extent representative of their class in the Mediterranean world in general." Jewish merchants traded eagerly with Christians and Muslims, roamed the entire Mediterranean basin, and entered formal partnerships across religious lines. The documents show Jewish brothers partnering with Muslims, multi-party ventures with mixed religious composition, and commercial correspondence that crosses confessional boundaries without apparent friction.

Yet the boundaries were real. Legal authorities debated the permissibility of cross-confessional partnerships. The law governing disputes was ambiguous -- local custom and the specific aims of the contractors often determined which legal system (Muslim or Jewish) applied. Some contract terms appeared in Hebrew in the midst of Arabic texts, signalling that Jewish law governed those particular provisions. The result was a layered legal landscape where Muslim and Jewish commercial law coexisted, overlapped, and sometimes competed within a single contract.

**Scene potential:** This layered legal landscape is where Ishaq bar Yohannan operates. His expertise as a scribe lies precisely in navigating the overlapping legal systems -- drafting contracts that satisfy Islamic commercial law while incorporating protections from Christian or Jewish legal traditions. The detail about Hebrew terms embedded in Arabic contracts is a concrete technique Ishaq might use.

## Facts and Dates

- The Cairo Geniza contained material primarily from the 10th-13th centuries; most commercial documents date to the 11th century (pp. 148-149)
- Geniza documents were found in Fustat (Old Cairo), deposited in a synagogue lumber room; main transfer to European libraries occurred 1890s-1900s (pp. 1-6)
- The *tajir* (wholesale merchant) was the highest-status merchant type; the *dallal* (broker) mediated virtually every transaction (pp. 149-153)
- Female brokers operated in Muslim harems selling textiles; a 1138 contract names seven women brokers, four with Muslim names (p. 161)
- Partnerships (*shirka*) substituted for both formal employment and interest-bearing loans (p. 170)
- The commenda (*qirad/mudaraba*) was called "mutual loan according to Muslim law" in Jewish courts (p. 171)
- In a typical commenda, the investor contributed capital and the manager contributed work; profits were split equally or in specified ratios; the manager often received a small weekly payment (half a dinar = about 18 dirhams) as "compensation for work" (pp. 175-177)
- Cross-religious partnerships were documented: Jewish-Muslim partnerships in Qayrawan (1048), multi-party ventures with Muslim participants (p. 173)
- Sealed purses of coins, verified by a banker's seal, circulated as a form of money; purses ranged from 4 to 100+ dinars (pp. 231-233)
- The *dar al-sarf* (exchange) was a formal institution with restricted membership (p. 239)
- The *jahbadh* (state banker) was a family position; all Geniza references date to the 11th-early 12th centuries (p. 250)
- The *suftaja* (bill of exchange) was used for remittance between cities; not transferable; presentation required immediate payment; bankers were cautious about large sums (pp. 242-246)
- Suftajas were drawn from Fustat to Baghdad, establishing the geographic range of the instrument (p. 244)
- Promissory notes issued by bankers were treated as cash (p. 245)
- Account booklets (*daftar*) were 3-4 inches wide, carried in merchants' sleeves; no systematic ledger-style bookkeeping existed (p. 206)
- Insolvency was handled communally: one-third of debt remitted, one-third paid in cash, one-third in instalments; bankruptcy declaration required highest communal authority (p. 204)
- No formal insurance existed; mutual help among merchants served as informal insurance (p. 204)
- 20 dinars was a particularly common round sum for local purse transactions; 200 dinars was a standard partnership contribution (pp. 233, 170)
