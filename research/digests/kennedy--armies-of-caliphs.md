# Digest: The Armies of the Caliphs: Military and Society in the Early Islamic State

**Author:** Hugh Kennedy
**Source:** `reference/The Armies of the Caliphs_ Military and Society in the Early -- Hugh Kennedy, (Hugh N_) -- First Edition, 2023 -- Routledge -- 9780203458532 -- Anna's Archive.epub`
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** The definitive study of early Islamic military organisation, pay, equipment, and warfare -- essential for Omar's 25-year career and the siege of Baghdad in Book 4.

## Overview

Kennedy's study traces the military institutions of the Caliphate from the conquests through the Samarra period (c. 632-870 CE). For this project, the critical chapters are: Chapter 3 (military payment), Chapter 4 (early Abbasid warfare, 750-833), Chapter 7 (weapons and equipment), and Chapter 8 (fortification and siege warfare). The pre-Abbasid material (Chapters 1-2) and post-833 material (Chapters 5-6) have been omitted from this digest.

The book's central argument is that the Abbasid military represented an evolution from, not a revolution against, Umayyad practice. Soldiers were still paid salaries from the *diwan al-jund*, still fought largely on foot, and armies still numbered in the tens of thousands. What changed was the personnel: the *ahl al-Sham* (Syrians) were replaced by the *ahl Khurasan* (Khorasanis) as the backbone of the state's military power. The descendants of these Khorasani settlers in Baghdad became the *Abna al-dawla* -- the Sons of the Regime -- who are central to the civil war of 809-813.

Kennedy is particularly strong on the social structure of the military: how commanders recruited their own troops, how pay was distributed, how land grants (*qata'i'*) worked, and how the civil war bankrupted and destroyed the old military establishment. This is the most detailed English-language treatment of precisely the world Omar inhabits.

## Key Extracts

### The Khorasaniyya: Omar's People

The Abbasid revolution replaced the Syrian military establishment with Khorasani troops. Kennedy estimates that between 20,000 and 30,000 Khorasani soldiers came west during the revolution of 750. Many settled in Baghdad after its founding in 762; their descendants were known as the *Abna al-dawla*. Others remained in Khurasan or garrisoned the Byzantine frontier. The total early Abbasid military force was approximately 100,000 salaried troops -- far fewer than the 250,000-300,000 on the Umayyad rolls, but these were full-time professionals rather than part-time militia. (Ch. 4, pp. 1-6)

Al-Jahiz, writing in the early ninth century, distinguished five military groups: the Khorasaniyya, the Abna, the Mawali, the Arabs, and the Turks. The Khorasaniyya proper were heavy cavalry, equipped with armour (*tajafif*), curved swords, clubs (*kafirkubat*), and axes (*tabarzin*). They trained with polo, horse-leaping, and the *birjas* (a swinging target on a pole). The Abna, by contrast, emphasised infantry skills -- street fighting, scaling walls, amphibious warfare, and hand-to-hand combat with knives and daggers. They boasted of expertise fighting at city gates, in alleys (*aziqqa*), and in markets. (Ch. 4, pp. 34-37)

**Scene potential:** The distinction between Khorasaniyya cavalry and Abna infantry gives Omar two possible identities depending on when he enlists and where he serves -- and the Abna's street-fighting expertise directly prefigures the siege of Baghdad in Book 4.

### The *Quwwad*: Professional Commanders

The early Abbasid period saw the rise of a class of professional commanders (*quwwad*, sing. *qa'id*). These were not great landowners or tribal chiefs but career soldiers who depended on salary and royal favour. Their appointment was signalled by receiving a banner (*liwa'* or *'alam*). They were responsible for recruiting the troops who served under them -- effectively military contractors. Their status derived from *sabiqa* (precedence in the Abbasid revolutionary movement), family connections, personal ability, and luck. (Ch. 4, pp. 16-19)

Kennedy gives a detailed case study of Khazim b. Khuzayma al-Tamimi, a Khorasani from Marwrudh who rose through the revolutionary movement. His career illustrates the system: he recruited troops from his hometown, appointed fellow Marwrudhis as officers, spoke Persian to his followers, and negotiated the right to choose his own commanders. His tactics emphasised caution -- digging defensive *khandaqs* wherever he went -- and surprise, including naphtha-tipped spears and caltrops against cavalry. His family maintained their military status across three generations until it was destroyed in the civil war after Harun's death. (Ch. 4, pp. 20-28)

**Scene potential:** A *qa'id* like Khazim is the kind of commander Omar would serve under -- a man who commands personal loyalty from his troops, speaks Persian, and fights with practical cunning rather than glory-seeking. The detail about naphtha on spearpoints is vivid and usable.

### Military Pay and the *Diwan*

In early Abbasid times, soldiers had their names entered in the *diwan al-jund* and received regular *'ata'* (salary). This was now clearly a payment for military service, not a hereditary pension. Abu Muslim established a new system at the revolution's outset: instead of registering troops by tribal affiliation, the new Abbasid *diwan* listed men by the villages they came from -- deliberately breaking tribal rivalries. (Ch. 3, p. 83)

There was a gradual move toward monthly payment. Ibn al-Muqaffa' urged payment every three or four months on a stated day. The standard rate for regular troops was approximately 60-80 dirhams per month, comparable to skilled workers and teachers. Frontier troops received enhancements -- typically an extra 10 dinars (120 dirhams) above the standard rate. The garrison at al-Hadath received 40 dinars as *'ata'* plus a cash bonus of 300 dirhams and housing plots. (Ch. 3, pp. 84-88)

Beyond cash salaries, troops might receive *rizq* (rations, originally from Persian *rozik* meaning daily rations), *ma'una* (bonus payments for dangerous service), and *qata'i'* (land grants -- in Baghdad, these were housing plots, not revenue-generating estates). In Baghdad, Khorasani soldiers lived in the *Harbiya* quarter northwest of the Round City, grouped by their places of origin: Balkh, Marw, Khuttal, Bukhara, Isfijab, each with their own *qa'id* and *ra'is*. The Khwarazmians had their own suburb; the Bukharans had a mosque with a green minaret. (Ch. 3, pp. 99-109)

**Scene potential:** Omar's pay rate of 60-80 dirhams/month, his housing plot in the Harbiya quarter among fellow Khorasanis, the sense of a military district organised by hometown -- all directly usable for scenes where Omar visits Baghdad or discusses his life with Yusuf.

### Weapons and Equipment

Standard body armour was the *dir'* (coat of mail), expensive and inherited across generations. One soldier at the Battle of Tell Kushaf (750) wore mail inherited from his great-grandfather, over 100 years old. In Khurasan in 704, there were only 350 coats of mail for 50,000 men. A coat of mail cost 700 dirhams -- nearly a year's salary for a common soldier. (Ch. 7, pp. 5-6)

Other equipment: the *bayda* (egg-shaped helmet, sometimes with nose-piece), the *mighfar* (mail coif covering head and hanging to shoulders), shields of wood or leather (small, less than a metre diameter), the *sayf* (straight sword -- no curved scimitars until later), and the *rumh* (thrusting spear with iron point). Swords ranged enormously in price: a Yemeni sword cost 50-100 dinars, while an Egyptian sword could be had for 10 dirhams. A soldier on 60 dirhams/month could easily afford an Egyptian sword but would need 10 months' salary for a Yemeni one. (Ch. 7, pp. 5-27)

Iron stirrups were a relatively recent innovation, believed to have been introduced during fighting against the Kharijis in the late seventh century. Al-Muhallab is credited as the first to order iron stirrups made, replacing fragile wooden ones. (Ch. 7, pp. 15-16)

Archery was highly esteemed. Bows were composite with the characteristic double-S shape. Effective range was 180-240 metres. During the Umayyad and early Abbasid periods, archers mostly fought on foot; mounted archery became dominant only from the third/ninth century with the influx of Turkic soldiers. (Ch. 7, p. 38)

Incendiary warfare used *naft* (crude oil, probably from the fields at Khaniqin in Iraq or Baku on the Caspian). It was hurled or shot on spear-tips wrapped in rags. Used during the siege of Baghdad, when al-Amin ordered naphtha attacks on the Harbiya quarter. (Ch. 7, pp. 36-37)

**Scene potential:** Omar's inherited mail coat -- passed from father to son -- is a perfect physical detail connecting him to Ali and to the family's Khorasani military heritage. The economics of weaponry (a good sword costing months of pay) makes equipment a mark of status. The naphtha in the Harbiya quarter during the siege is a devastating detail for Book 4.

### The Civil War (809-813) and the Siege of Baghdad

After Harun's death in 809, the empire fractured between al-Amin (based in Baghdad, supported by the Abna) and al-Ma'mun (based in Merv, supported by eastern Iranian nobles). The decisive battle occurred near Rayy in May 811: al-Amin's general Ali b. Isa b. Mahan led 40,000 men against Tahir b. al-Husayn's force of only 3,800-5,000. Tahir chose to fight in open country rather than shelter behind Rayy's walls, fearing the citizens would hand him over. He arranged his men in mobile cavalry squadrons (*kata'ib* and *karadis*), concentrated his attack on the centre, and Ali was killed by an arrow. The massive infantry army was destroyed by a much smaller cavalry force -- possibly marking the end of large foot-soldier armies in Islamic warfare. (Ch. 4, pp. 46-52)

From August 812 to September 813, Baghdad endured a siege without parallel. The city's suburbs were unwalled. Fighting consisted of street-to-street combat, house-to-house struggles, and improvised fortifications. Siege engines (*manajiq* and *'arradat*) were used by both sides, causing terrible civilian casualties. One supporter of al-Amin even mounted *manajiq* on boats in the Tigris to bombard captured suburbs. (Ch. 4, pp. 56-58)

The *'ayyarun* (vagrants, urban underclass) became a major force. These irregulars carried no armour -- their shields were of reeds, their head-coverings of palm leaves. In one vivid anecdote, a well-equipped Khorasani cavalryman from Tahir's army encounters an *'ayyar* armed only with a reed mat and a sling. The Khorasani fires his arrows, but the *'ayyar* deflects them with his makeshift shield and collects the spent arrows to sell. When the Khorasani closes with his sword, the *'ayyar* hits him in the eye with a sling-stone, and the professional soldier retreats, humiliated. (Ch. 4, p. 64)

**Scene potential:** The Rayy battle and the siege of Baghdad are the military backbone of Book 4. The *'ayyarun* with their reed shields and palm-leaf helmets, selling collected arrows mid-battle, are extraordinary details for Omar's neighbourhood defence scenes. The siege engines on boats, the naphtha in the streets, the street-fighting expertise of the Abna -- all essential.

### Pay During the Civil War: The Economy of Collapse

On his accession, al-Amin offered troops a 24-month bonus (*mal al-bay'a*); al-Ma'mun offered only 12 months. Competition escalated: al-Amin offered 12 months' bonus to deserters from al-Ma'mun's side. The competitive recruitment rate settled at 80 dirhams per month. As Baghdad fell, al-Amin recruited untrained new commanders, giving each 500 dirhams and a bottle of perfume -- provoking resentment among unpaid regulars. (Ch. 3, p. 90)

After al-Amin's death, the economy collapsed. Tahir could not pay his own victorious troops and had to borrow 20,000 dinars from a friend for four months' back pay. Soldiers degenerated into bandits. Ibrahim b. al-Mahdi's bid for the caliphate could offer only 10 dinars per supporter. His troops were eventually paid in drafts (*written orders*) on the Sawad for wheat and barley, and they simply plundered everything they found. Salaries were paid in debased coin. The once-proud Abna eventually accepted 60 dirhams per month in debased currency -- reduced from the 80 they had enjoyed before the war. (Ch. 3, pp. 91-96)

**Scene potential:** The economic collapse is as devastating as the military destruction. Soldiers being paid in worthless drafts, then plundering the countryside; salaries in debased coin; professional soldiers reduced to banditry -- this is the world Yusuf must navigate financially in Book 4, and the world Omar experiences as a retired soldier watching his neighbourhood fall apart.

### Fortification and the Siege Environment

Baghdad's Round City had impressive defences: a ditch, two lines of walls with an open space between them, round interval towers approximately 20 metres high, and four elaborate gates each with passage, inner gate, and dome chamber above. But the vast suburbs where most people lived were unwalled at the time of the 812-813 siege. (Ch. 8, pp. 31-32)

By the second siege of Baghdad (865), the city had walls with a *khandaq* outside and shelters (*mizallat*) inside for horses. Gates had covered hallways (*dihliz*) where 100 infantry and 100 cavalry could be stationed. Gates were defended with wooden barriers (*shadakhar*) -- cross-beams and boards with protruding spikes -- plus a hanging gate (portcullis) armoured with iron sheets, suspended with ropes to drop on attackers. Siege engines were positioned on the gates: one gate had five large *manajiq* (one named "the Angry One") and six *'arradat*. Each engine had men assigned to pull ropes (confirming traction, not counterweight, propulsion). The entire fortification programme cost 330,000 dinars. (Ch. 8, pp. 32-33)

Caliphs often destroyed city walls deliberately. Harun had Mosul's walls demolished in 797 after tax protests, guaranteeing amnesty only if citizens demolished the sections nearest them with their own hands. Al-Ma'mun ordered destruction of fortifications across the Jazira and Syria once his authority was restored. The logic: city walls in a properly ordered state were unnecessary and potentially rebellious. (Ch. 8, p. 21)

**Scene potential:** The unwalled suburbs are critical -- Omar's Khorasan Gate neighbourhood has no city wall to hide behind in the 812 siege, only improvised barricades. The detail about Harun demolishing Mosul's walls is characteristic of the regime's paranoia about urban defences. The siege engines named "the Angry One" -- perfect for fiction.

- Bow range: 180-240 metres (Ch. 7, p. 38)

- Al-Amin's accession bonus: 24 months' salary (Ch. 3, p. 90)
- Al-Ma'mun's accession bonus: 12 months' salary (Ch. 3, p. 90)

- *Ayyarun* equipment: reed shields, palm-leaf head-coverings, slings (Ch. 4, p. 64)

- Al-Amin's accession: 193/809 CE (Ch. 3, p. 90)
