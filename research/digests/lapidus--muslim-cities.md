# Digest: Muslim Cities in the Later Middle Ages

**Author:** Ira Marvin Lapidus
**Source:** `reference/muslim-cities--lapidus.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-05
**Relevance:** The definitive study of social organisation, market structures, neighbourhood politics, and urban violence in medieval Islamic cities -- essential for depicting Baghdad's quarters, suqs, and the *ayyarun* during the civil war.

## Overview

Lapidus studies the social and political life of Damascus, Aleppo, and Cairo under the Mamluk Empire (1250-1517). Though the period is later than the novel's setting (770-814 CE), the book's analytical framework applies directly to earlier centuries because the structures it describes -- the relationship between military elites, urban notables, and common people; the organisation of residential quarters and markets; the role of lumpenproletarian gangs -- were all rooted in patterns established during the Abbasid period. Lapidus explicitly traces these patterns back to the Abbasid Caliphate and treats them as a continuous evolution.

The book's central argument is that Islamic cities were not the lifeless, bureaucratically administered wastelands that earlier European scholars imagined. Instead, they were governed through a complex three-way interaction between alien military rulers (the Mamluks, analogous to the Abbasid *jund*), local notables (ulama and merchants), and the common people. This interaction operated not through formal institutions but through patronage networks, overlapping solidarities, and the managed threat of violence. The analytical model maps almost perfectly onto Abbasid Baghdad, where the Khorasaniyya military caste, the Persian-Arab bureaucratic elite, and the urban populace (including the *ayyarun*) enacted the same dynamics.

For the novel, this book is indispensable in three areas: (1) how residential quarters like the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood actually functioned as self-governing micro-communities; (2) how markets were organised, taxed, and supervised without formal guilds; and (3) how urban gangs like the zu'ar (the Mamluk-period equivalent of the Abbasid *ayyarun*) operated in the grey zone between community defence and criminal predation -- essential for depicting the siege of Baghdad in Book 4.

## Key Extracts

### The Social Hierarchy of a Muslim City

Lapidus identifies four social strata recognised by medieval Muslim writers themselves. The *al-khassa* were the ruling military elite. Below them, *al-a'yan* -- the "notables" -- were primarily religious scholars (ulama) but also included wealthy merchants; their status derived from local respect rather than state power. The *al-'amma* were the respectable working classes: shopkeepers, artisans, and traders who "possessed neither office, learning, nor wealth" but were integrated into the communal fabric (p. 82). Beneath all of these was a lumpenproletariat of menials, criminals, beggars, rootless immigrants, and unassimilated rural migrants.

Crucially, merchant status was not degrading. Lapidus notes that leading merchants "were among the richest men of their times, often the equal of emirs" and "received many official honours" (p. 81). Trade was "not considered ignoble or derogating" in Islamic society. Social position was defined by three overlapping values: proximity to the state, religious dignity, and wealth combined with style of life. These criteria could conflict -- political power was prestigious but potentially corrupting; wealth could bring status but certain trades were tainted.

The market inspector's manual of Ibn 'Abd al-Hadi ranked trades morally. Perfumers, bakers, carpenters, and tailors were respectable. Gold and silk dealers were suspect because of usury associations. Tanners, barbers, donkey drivers, and leather workers were socially despised. Money changers fell among the disreputable trades on religious grounds, despite their economic importance (pp. 82-83).

**Scene potential:** The social hierarchy and trade rankings provide the exact texture needed for the Suq al-Warraqin scenes and for establishing where paper-makers and paper-sellers sat in the social order -- respectable but not prestigious, somewhere between the craft trades and the merchant class.

### The Organisation of Residential Quarters

Quarters (*harat*, *mahallat*) were small, village-like communities within the urban whole. In Damascus, about seventy quarters averaged five or six hundred people each. They were residential areas with small local markets and sometimes workshops, "characteristically isolated from the bustle of the main central city bazaars" (p. 85). They were communities where face-to-face intimacy among all residents was the norm.

The solidarity of quarters was built on several foundations. Religious identity segregated Christians and Jews into distinct (though adjacent, not ghetto-like) streets. Among Muslims, ethnic groups clustered together: Turkomans, Kurds, Persians each had their districts. Common village origin unified some urban quarters -- migrants gathered with their cousins, retained contacts with home, and "recreated the old life within the walls" (p. 86). Some quarters were economically specialised: mills, tanneries, and dye works drew workers into separate districts because they needed water and space, and other residents needed protection from noxious wastes. Quarters on main roads specialised in caravanning, transport, and animal marketing.

However, quarters were not segregated by class. Central quarters tended to be wealthier, but each contained a mix of ulama, merchants, craftsmen, and functionaries. "Economic, religious, and social life were not so differentiated from each other as to create the basis for any radical separation of classes by quarter" (p. 87). Quarters were communities of both rich and poor.

Each quarter was led by a sheikh or *'arif* of the *hara* -- the chief spokesman and administrator, selected by the governor but serving as intermediary between the state's fiscal demands and the residents. The sheikh's essential duty was mediating taxation: "essential to the regime for collection, essential to the people for evasion, linking government and subjects, belonging to both and to neither" (p. 92). Quarters were also responsible for police functions, crime prevention, and collective self-defence.

In times of crisis, quarters barricaded themselves behind great doors and sealed off thoroughfares. But permanent defensive fortifications were not standard. Gates had to be erected or repaired for every crisis. Defence by quarter was reactive, not permanent (pp. 94-95).

**Scene potential:** Directly applicable to the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood. The quarter's ethnic homogeneity (Khorasani families), its economic specialisation (caravanning, given its location on the road east), its self-governing sheikh, its barricading during the siege -- all are validated by Lapidus. The detail that quarters were mixed-class communities is important for showing Ali the tilemaker living alongside wealthier merchants and minor officials.

### The Organisation of Economic Life -- Markets Without Guilds

The central bazaar was a distinct social arena from the residential quarters. It contained rows of shops and workshops, khans and *qaysariyyas* for wholesaling, plus mosques, schools, baths, and the booths of scribes, tax collectors, and judges. Adjacent to the markets were the citadel and the residences of leading emirs. "Here the concerns of the whole city were carried on by the people of the quarters who came, separated from their families, differentiated from their communal roles" (p. 95).

The critical finding: there were no guilds in any meaningful sense. "Professional, merchant, and artisan guilds were virtually nonexistent, and what rudimentary forms did exist were created by the state for its own purposes rather than by the solidarity and self-interest of the members" (p. 96). Merchants were not organised into corporations. The few *ra'is* (chief) positions that existed were state appointments for liaison and tax purposes, not leaders of voluntary associations. Sheikhs of individual suqs were appointed by the governor from among notable merchants and "were not chiefs of merchant corporations" (p. 96). In one case, a merchant tried to refuse the post and was beaten into accepting it.

Trades and crafts were supervised externally by *muhtasibs* (market inspectors), whose duties blended moral regulation and tax collection. Each craft had an *'arif* appointed as its overseer -- selected from among craftsmen but appointed by the *muhtasib* to be a state agent. The *'arifs* advised on trade practices, organised markets for ceremonial duties, assisted with military auxiliary service, and most importantly, facilitated taxation (pp. 98-99).

The practice of *tahkir* -- requiring all traders in a particular commodity to gather in one place -- reinforced the spatial clustering of the bazaar and created true competitive markets where fair prices could be determined. Food trades (oil, butter, flour, dates, figs, charcoal) were especially subject to *tahkir*. This spatial requirement also benefited emirs as property owners, since it allowed them to raise rents (p. 100).

Taxes were assessed on individual transactions or goods, not as a global fee by craft. Brokers paid half their fees as a tax, acting as de facto sales tax collectors. The *muhtasib's* monthly market tax was repeatedly abolished and repeatedly reinstated; in Damascus it eventually yielded about 2,000 dinars a month (pp. 99-100).

**Scene potential:** Essential for the Suq al-Warraqin chapter (Ch 5) and all market scenes. The absence of guilds means Abu Said's workshop competes as an individual enterprise, not as part of a corporate structure. The *muhtasib* and *'arif* system, the spatial clustering of paper sellers, the taxation through brokers -- all are directly usable. The detail about a merchant beaten into accepting the post of suq sheikh is the kind of vivid specificity that could become a scene.

### Fraternal Associations and the Margins of Society

Lapidus finds only one religious fraternity with craft affiliation in the entire Mamluk period: a Sufi fraternity of silk workers in Damascus. This exception proves the rule. When the silk workers collectively protested a tax of 15,000 dirhams on looms in 897/1492, it was the only instance of a single trade organising self-defence rather than the community or markets acting as a whole (p. 102).

The prevailing attitude was hostile to independent worker associations. The ulama opposed them as sources of "conflict, injustice, and party strife" and as threats to the universal unity of the Muslim community. The state feared them as potential foci of resistance, especially among a working populace "whose taxes were essential, whose masses were powerful and capable of violent outbursts" (pp. 103-104). Worker associations were also feared as sources of heresy, since popular religious feeling often diverged from official orthodoxy.

The common people's religious life was imperfectly supervised. Efforts were repeatedly made to teach shopkeepers how to pray. In 790/1388, "the market inspector ordered the jurists to teach the common shopkeepers the introduction to the Koran and how to pray" (p. 103).

**Scene potential:** The absence of guilds and the state's hostility to worker organisation reinforces the individualistic, patronage-based world Yusuf navigates. The detail about common people needing to be taught to pray gives texture to the religiosity (or lack thereof) of market life -- useful for distinguishing the piety of different characters.

### Market Strikes and Food Riots

In the absence of formal associations, market closures (*strikes*) were the primary form of economic protest. Shopkeepers closed markets to protest arbitrary taxation, coinage manipulation, or Mamluk abuses. These were always defensive, never advancing new claims. The Mamluks could break strikes by threatening to plunder closed shops. Informal ties and feelings of common condition led to collective action even without prior organisation or "articulated objectives" (pp. 143-145).

Food riots crystallised around bread shops, ovens, and docks. Mobs sometimes surged through the city in search of food. In Cairo, the common people gathered outside the citadel gates to demand the Sultan relieve their hunger, curb speculation, and replace abusive market inspectors. They held *muhtasibs* responsible and attacked them physically. Market inspectors were "often stoned and beaten" and crises sometimes saw a succession of muhtasibs "offered as scapegoats to the masses" (p. 146).

The populace acted as if the ruler himself were innocent -- only his officials were culpable. "Although he might be deceived by advisers, the ruler was regarded as exempt from ill intentions" (p. 149).

**Scene potential:** The psychology of blaming officials rather than the ruler maps onto the Abbasid context: the caliph is sacrosanct, but his vizier or *muhtasib* is fair game. Market closures as protest provide a mechanism Yusuf could use or witness. The food riot pattern -- mobs at the citadel gate, stoning of officials, succession of scapegoat inspectors -- could inform the civil war chapters in Book 4.

### The Zu'ar: Organised Urban Gangs

The zu'ar of Damascus are the Mamluk-period counterparts of the *ahdath* (pre-Mamluk urban militias) and, by extension, of the Abbasid *ayyarun*. They were "self-consciously organised gangs of young men, probably bachelors, who wore a distinctive hair style called the *qar'ani* and robes worn over their shoulders as a kind of uniform" (p. 154). They were called scoundrels, rabble, and riff-raff in the sources, but many were actually artisans and shopkeepers -- a carpenter, a broker, a thread seller. One chief was the son of a cook.

The gangs were organised in quarters outside the city walls and in surrounding villages -- districts that still contained populations with rural backgrounds, not fully incorporated into the city. Each quarter's zu'ar had their own *kabir* (chief). Several chiefs bore the title *sharif* or *sayyid* (descendant of the Prophet), and at least one was a qadi. Ulama were also associated with the gangs, which seems paradoxical but reflected the double role of the zu'ar as both community defenders and criminals (pp. 154-155).

The zu'ar occupied a dual role. They defended their quarters against abusive taxation, assaulted rapacious tax collectors, led barricade resistance, and organised city-wide opposition. In 907/1501-02, they waged an all-out battle with the governor, resisting tax demands on the quarters: barricading streets, marching armed, refusing to attend prayers, and forcing the governor to negotiate with both the quarter sheikhs and the zu'ar chiefs separately. But the zu'ar also preyed on the quarters they claimed to protect: they were "criminals, assassins, thieves, and pillagers" who ran protection rackets, forced merchants to pay them, and installed accomplices in shops (pp. 156-158).

The quarters' zu'ar were "fundamentally hostile to each other," reflecting the feuds of their districts. Despite occasional alliances against the state, inter-gang violence was chronic. "The most powerful of popular forces dissipated their energies in futile bickering" (p. 157).

The state manipulated the zu'ar by enrolling them as auxiliary infantry, arming them, reviewing them in formal military parades, and using them in civil wars. Individual emirs cultivated personal followings among the gangs, hiring them as thugs and assassins. The result was a political equilibrium: the zu'ar resisted the state but also cooperated with it, and their revolutionary potential was broken by being channelled into criminality and inter-quarter feuding. "Political violence was converted into merely criminal violence" (p. 163).

**Scene potential:** The zu'ar pattern maps directly onto the *ayyarun* of Baghdad during the 809-813 civil war. The dual nature -- community defenders and criminal predators -- is exactly the texture needed for Book 4. Omar's neighbourhood defence of the Khorasan Gate could involve these dynamics: organised young men from the quarter barricading streets, fighting Mamluk (in Baghdad's case, *jund*) troops, but also turning to looting when discipline breaks down. The detail about zu'ar chiefs being ordinary tradesmen (carpenters, thread sellers) rather than professional soldiers is critical: it means the *ayyarun* Yusuf encounters would be his neighbours, people he has known for years, not alien outsiders.

### How Quarters Barricaded and Defended Themselves

When threatened, quarters closed off thoroughfares, erected barricades, and posted guards. But permanent defences were not standard -- gates and barriers had to be constructed anew for each crisis. In Damascus in 695/1296, after a series of murders, the city had to restore internal barriers and begin illuminating streets at night. Only during periods of extreme insecurity (like the late fifteenth century, when plunder by gangs was constant) did barricades become semi-permanent. Taxes were levied on quarters to pay for communal fortifications (pp. 94-95).

Collective responsibility was imposed: quarters were treated as liable for crimes committed within their boundaries, including payment of blood money. This forced the community to discipline itself in ways no outsider could effectively reach. But the quarters protested this collective responsibility, "affirmed the liability of each man and him alone for his offenses" (p. 94). The tension between collective and individual responsibility was a live political issue.

**Scene potential:** Directly applicable to the siege of Baghdad. The Khorasan Gate quarter would need to improvise defences, and the question of collective responsibility resonates with Yusuf's dilemma: is he responsible for the quarter, or only for his own house?

### The Abbasid Roots of the System

Lapidus explicitly traces his analytical framework back to the Abbasid period. He describes Abbasid society as "vertically organised": communities had no ties to each other but were bound directly to higher centres of coordination. Authority and privilege flowed from the Caliph. "Controls over financial and military resources were also vested in the Caliph or his designated substitutes" (p. 5). The Abbasid solution to governing diverse provinces was bureaucracy -- drawing socially powerful families (landowners, secretarial dynasties, tribal chieftains, religious leaders) into a hierarchy welded together by a common cultural idiom.

The breakdown of the empire in the tenth century permitted the patterns Lapidus studies to crystallise, but the "characteristic relations within the official classes and their relations to the rest of the population did not basically change" (p. 6). Whether power was centralised in Baghdad or held at provincial level, it was exercised in much the same way.

**Scene potential:** Validates applying the Mamluk-period observations backwards to the Abbasid setting. The "vertical organisation" model describes exactly how Yusuf's world works: patronage from the Barmakids and the Caliph flows downward; communities relate to each other only through higher authorities. The breakdown during the civil war (809-813) would disrupt these vertical connections and force horizontal, improvised solidarities -- exactly what the novel needs.

## Facts and Dates

- Damascus population: ~100,000 (travellers' estimates, late 14th-early 15th c.); ~57,000 (Ottoman census, 1520-1530) (p. 79)
- Damascus had ~70 quarters; al-Salihiyya suburb had ~30 more; average quarter size ~500-600 people (p. 85)
- Aleppo had ~50 quarters; average size ~1,000-1,200 people (p. 85)
- Cairo had 37 *harat* plus many smaller *akhtat* and streets (p. 85)
- Market inspector's monthly tax in Damascus: ~2,000 dinars/month (p. 100)
- Total weekly + monthly taxes raised by Cairo *muhtasib*: 76,000 dinars/year (p. 100)
- Damascus muhtasib's assistant post purchased for 16,000 dinars (p. 99)
- Silk workers protested a tax of 15,000 dirhams on looms (897/1492) (p. 102)
- In 895/1490, ~3,000 men of Damascus quarters mustered for infantry duty (p. 159)
- Trades ranked by respectability: perfumers, bakers, carpenters, tailors (high); money changers, tanners, leather workers, barbers (low) (pp. 82-83)
- Zu'ar of al-Shaghir reviewed in formal military procession, 893/1488 (p. 159)
- In 907/1501-02, zu'ar forced governor to negotiate; won cancellation of monthly market tax and house taxes (p. 156)
- Quarters taxed for military levies at 25 dinars per soldier (p. 93)

## Relevance to Chapters

- **Book 1, all Baghdad scenes** -- Quarter organisation, mixed-class communities, ethnic clustering, sheikh as intermediary. The Khorasan Gate neighbourhood can be modelled on Lapidus's quarter descriptions: ~500-600 people, face-to-face intimacy, common Khorasani origin, small local market, led by a neighbourhood sheikh.
- **Book 1, Ch 5 (Suq al-Warraqin)** -- Market organisation without guilds: the *muhtasib*, the *'arif*, spatial clustering of trades, taxation through brokers, individual competition rather than corporate structure. The paper sellers' market operates as individual shops under state supervision, not a guild.
- **Book 1, all apprenticeship scenes** -- Social hierarchy of trades: paper-making as respectable craft, money-changing as religiously suspect, the moral economy of the suq.
- **Book 1, Kufa journey (Ch 1-3)** -- Quarter hostilities, market closures as protest, communal tensions in smaller cities.
- **Books 2-3, commercial expansion** -- Merchant notables' dual role as state collaborators and community representatives. The *jahbadh* and *sarraf* function within the patronage system Lapidus describes.
- **Book 4, civil war and siege** -- The zu'ar/ayyarun pattern: young men of the quarters organising for defence, barricading streets, improvising gates, fighting troops but also looting; inter-quarter hostilities; the state's manipulation of popular violence; collective responsibility for crimes; the psychology of blaming officials rather than the ruler. Omar's defence of the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood should reflect these dynamics.
- **Book 4, Yusuf's political navigation** -- The three-way balance between military regime, notables, and common people. Yusuf is a notable whose power depends on maintaining relationships with both the regime and the populace. The civil war disrupts this balance.

## [RESEARCH NEEDED]

- Lapidus's zu'ar analysis covers the Mamluk period (15th-16th c.). The Abbasid *ayyarun* are mentioned as predecessors but not studied in detail. Kennedy's digests cover the *ayyarun* during the 809-813 siege; a direct comparison of zu'ar and *ayyarun* organisational structures would strengthen the novel's depiction. The *ahdath* (pre-Mamluk urban militias) may be a closer analogue and deserve separate research.
- The book focuses on Damascus, Aleppo, and Cairo -- not Baghdad. Baghdad's specific quarter structure (Round City, Karkh, eastern bank) differs from the Syrian model. The analytical framework transfers, but specific details (number of quarters, quarter names, defensive arrangements) need to come from Baghdad-specific sources.
- Lapidus says virtually nothing about paper-making or paper-selling as specific trades. The suq organisation is general; specific details about the warraqin must come from Pedersen, Bloom, and other sources already digested.
