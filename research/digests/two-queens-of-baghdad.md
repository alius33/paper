## Overview

Nabia Abbott's *Two Queens of Baghdad: Mother and Wife of Harun al-Rashid* (University of Chicago Press, 1946; 292 pp.) is a seminal work in Middle Eastern women's studies. Abbott reconstructs the lives of Khaizuran (Harun's mother, d. 789 CE) and Zubayda (Harun's wife, d. ~831 CE), drawing on Arabic chronicle sources to recover the political influence, economic power, and personal agency of these two extraordinary women.

Abbott's stated purpose is to correct both the exaggeration and the underestimation of these women's roles by previous biographers. The book is divided into two parts, one for each queen, and traces their lives from origin through death, embedding their personal stories in the broader political narrative of the early Abbasid caliphate.

For the project, Abbott's book is invaluable in three respects: (1) it provides documented examples of how elite Abbasid women exercised power, which grounds Zahra's characterisation in historical precedent; (2) it illuminates the household economics and patronage networks that women controlled; and (3) it offers specific sensory and social details about the private sphere of elite life --- the harem, the household, the ceremonies of marriage and mourning --- that are otherwise poorly documented.

## Key Extracts

### Khaizuran: From Slave to Queen

Khaizuran's life arc is remarkable. Born free in Yemen (most sources say Jurash), she was captured by Arab tribesmen and enslaved. Described as "slender and graceful as a reed" (khaizuran means "bamboo" or "reed"), she was sold in Mecca to the future caliph al-Mahdi, possibly by al-Mansur himself.

She rose from concubine to favoured wife, bearing al-Mahdi two sons: Musa al-Hadi and Harun al-Rashid. She was intelligent, well-educated in Quranic study, hadith, and law, and quoted poetry freely. Al-Mahdi formally married her and freed her.

Her political influence was exercised through control of access to the caliph, management of a vast personal fortune, and direct intervention in state affairs. Al-Tabari records that at the opening of al-Hadi's caliphate (her first son), "al-Khayzuran used to exercise her authority over him in all his affairs without consulting him at all ... assuming sole control over matters of ordaining and forbidding."

By the time of her death in 789, her annual income had reached 160 million dirhams --- roughly half the entire state revenue. This is a staggering figure that indicates the scale of economic power concentrated in the hands of a single woman.

**Scene potential:** Khaizuran's story provides historical precedent for Zahra's ambition and capability. While Zahra is not a queen, the mechanisms of female power --- intelligence gathering, household economics, strategic influence, independent financial reserves --- are the same at different scales. The detail about Khaizuran's income (160 million dirhams) also provides a benchmark for the novel's sense of scale: the House of Paper's revenue can be measured against the state's, and Zahra's private preparations represent a scaled-down version of a queen's independent wealth.

### Khaizuran's Political Methods

Abbott documents the specific channels through which Khaizuran exercised power:

1. **Control of access:** She determined who could approach the caliph in private, effectively filtering the information he received and the petitioners he heard.
2. **Patronage distribution:** She maintained her own network of clients, distributing gifts, appointments, and favours independently of the official court system.
3. **Intelligence:** She cultivated sources within the palace and the city, staying informed about political developments that the caliph might not share with her directly.
4. **Intervention in succession:** She successfully manoeuvred to have both her sons named as heirs, excluding al-Mahdi's sons by other women. When al-Hadi attempted to strip Harun of his succession rights, Khaizuran is widely suspected of having arranged al-Hadi's death (the circumstances are murky --- "two slave girls suffocated him" is one account).
5. **Property and commerce:** She owned extensive properties and commercial interests, managed by her own agents.

When al-Hadi objected to her political activity, telling her that "it is not in the nature of women to meddle in the affairs of state," she reportedly replied that she had done the same under his father without objection. The clash between mother and son over the boundaries of female authority is a recurring theme.

**Scene potential:** Each of these channels maps onto Zahra's behaviour in the novel. Zahra's independent preparations --- the emergency reserves, the escape routes, the cultivated contacts --- are not anachronistic inventions but historical practice. The confrontation between Khaizuran and al-Hadi over women's political role also provides a template for the tension between Zahra and Yusuf: she operates within channels he does not fully see, and when he does see them, he must confront the question of whether he trusts her judgment.

### Zubayda: Wealth, Patronage, and Public Works

Zubayda bint Ja'far was born into the Abbasid royal family --- her grandfather was Caliph al-Mansur. She was nicknamed "Zubayda" ("Little Butterball") as a child on account of her plumpness. Her marriage to Harun was a political alliance reinforcing the dynasty's internal bonds, and the wedding was legendarily expensive --- reported at 50 million dinars, against an average family's annual cost of living of about 240 dinars.

Zubayda's tastes were extravagant. Abbott documents her household: she insisted that her table vessels be made of gold and silver studded with gems, her clothing was the finest silk, and she maintained a household of hundreds of attendants.

But Zubayda was more than a consumer of luxury. She financed one of the greatest public works projects of the era: the construction of a 1,500-kilometre pilgrim road from Kufa to Mecca, complete with water stations, wells, and hilltop fire beacons. This road --- Darb Zubayda ("Zubayda's Road") --- remained in use for centuries, and pilgrims cried "God bless Zubayda" along the route for generations.

**Scene potential:** Zubayda's public works patronage demonstrates that elite women's power was not confined to the domestic sphere. The Darb Zubayda is a physical presence in the landscape that caravans in Book 1 might use or reference. Zubayda's combination of personal luxury and public generosity also provides a model for how wealth was deployed in the Abbasid world --- conspicuously, strategically, and with an eye to reputation.

### Zubayda and the Civil War

Abbott documents Zubayda's role during the civil war between her son al-Amin and her stepson al-Ma'mun. As al-Amin's behaviour became increasingly erratic and self-destructive, Zubayda faced an agonising choice. She eventually endorsed al-Ma'mun's accession, putting the interests of the state (and the dynasty's survival) above her own son.

This is described as "a personally painful decision" in which Zubayda placed institutional continuity above maternal loyalty. After al-Amin's death, she continued to live in Baghdad, respected by al-Ma'mun, who acknowledged her as a matriarch of the dynasty.

**Scene potential:** Zubayda's choice during the civil war parallels Zahra's moral position. Zahra, too, must make decisions that prioritise the House's survival over personal loyalty. The historical precedent of a mother choosing the state over her son gives weight to the novel's portrayal of women as political agents capable of hard decisions. Zahra's line in the outline --- "The man I married would have tried to save them" --- carries the same register of painful clarity as Zubayda's endorsement of al-Ma'mun.

### The Harem as Institution

Abbott is careful to distinguish the harem from its orientalist caricature. The harem (from the Arabic haram, meaning "sacred" or "forbidden") was the private domestic sphere of the household, not a prison. Elite women within the harem managed household economies, supervised servants and enslaved workers, maintained correspondence and social networks, received visitors, and participated in intellectual and religious life.

The caliph's harem was a small city within the palace: hundreds of residents, complex hierarchies (free wives, concubines, female relatives, servants, eunuchs, children, tutors), and its own internal governance. Women within the harem communicated with the outside world through trusted intermediaries --- eunuchs, female messengers, and male relatives.

Abbott notes that women could own property, engage in commerce, and appear in court as legal persons. The restriction was not on their agency per se but on their physical visibility and direct interaction with unrelated men. Within these constraints, women exercised considerable power.

**Scene potential:** The harem-as-institution provides the framework for Zahra's domestic sphere. Her household management, her intelligence network, her emergency preparations --- these are all historically grounded activities of an elite woman operating within the harem system. The novel should show the harem not as a prison but as a power centre with its own rules, hierarchies, and political dynamics.

### Material Culture of Elite Women

Abbott provides scattered but valuable details about the material world of elite Abbasid women:

- **Clothing:** Silk, brocade, gauze. Colours included white, red, yellow, and green (not black, which was reserved for official male court dress).
- **Jewellery:** Gold, silver, pearls, rubies, emeralds. Hair ornaments, arm bands, ankle bracelets.
- **Perfume:** Musk, ambergris, rose water, sandalwood.
- **Food:** Elaborate meals served on precious tableware. Sweets, fruits, game birds.
- **Music and poetry:** Women composed and recited poetry, played musical instruments, and patronised poets and musicians.
- **Religious practice:** Pilgrimage to Mecca was a significant event in elite women's lives. Zubayda's pilgrim road was partly motivated by her own hajj experiences.

**Scene potential:** These sensory details are essential for grounding scenes in Zahra's household. The smell of musk and rose water, the sound of an oud played in the women's quarters, the glint of gold tableware at a meal --- these details distinguish the domestic sphere from the commercial world of the suq and the workshop. They also provide Yusuf with a contrasting sensory palette when he moves between his public and private worlds.

## Facts and Dates

- **Khaizuran born:** ~740 CE, Jurash, Yemen. Captured and enslaved.
- **~758--765 CE:** Khaizuran sold to al-Mahdi (then crown prince) in Mecca.
- **775 CE:** Al-Mahdi becomes caliph. Khaizuran's influence grows.
- **785 CE:** Al-Hadi becomes caliph. Clashes with Khaizuran over women's political role.
- **786 CE:** Al-Hadi dies under suspicious circumstances. Harun al-Rashid becomes caliph. Khaizuran's influence restored.
- **789 CE:** Khaizuran dies. Annual income at death: 160 million dirhams.
- **Zubayda born:** ~762--766 CE. Granddaughter of Caliph al-Mansur.
- **~781--782 CE:** Zubayda marries Harun al-Rashid. Wedding cost reported at 50 million dinars.
- **~786 CE:** Al-Amin born (Zubayda's son).
- **Darb Zubayda:** 1,500 km pilgrim road from Kufa to Mecca, financed by Zubayda. Included water stations, wells, and fire beacons.
- **813 CE:** Al-Amin killed. Zubayda endorses al-Ma'mun.
- **~831 CE:** Zubayda dies in Baghdad.

## Relevance to Chapters

### Book 1: The House of Paper
- **Background context:** Khaizuran's death in 789 CE falls within Book 1's timeline (770--791). Her political influence and enormous wealth are part of the political backdrop that Yusuf grows up in. The Darb Zubayda may be referenced during caravan journeys.

### Book 2: The House of Silver
- **Zahra's introduction:** Zahra enters the story (~795 CE). Her characterisation --- sharp-eyed, direct, managing an independent intelligence network --- should draw on the documented behaviour of Khaizuran and Zubayda. Her wedding line ("If you want a wife who decorates your house, find someone else. If you want a partner who tells you the truth, I'm interested") is historically grounded in the tradition of politically assertive Abbasid women.
- **Household economics:** Zahra's management of the household economy mirrors documented practice.

### Book 3: The House of Gold
- **Zahra's independent preparations:** Her secret reserves, escape plans, and cultivated contacts are historically precedented by Khaizuran's independent wealth and Zubayda's patronage networks.
- **The succession question:** Zubayda's advocacy for al-Amin's succession and her later endorsement of al-Ma'mun provide historical context for the political dynamics.

### Book 4: The House of Wisdom
- **Zahra during the siege:** Her preparations pay off. Her actions during the siege should be grounded in the specific channels of female agency documented by Abbott: intelligence, logistics, household defence.
- **Zubayda's choice as parallel:** Zahra's difficult decisions during the civil war echo Zubayda's painful endorsement of al-Ma'mun over her own son.
