# Digest: Among The Umayyad, Abbasid, and Ottoman Empires: How Did The Islamic Economy Perform? A Historical Review from 600 AD

**Author:** Ali Akbar Anggara, M. Elfan Kaukab, and Ali Imron
**Source:** `reference/how-did-islamic-economy-perform.pdf`
**Digested:** 2026-03-04
**Relevance:** Comparative analysis of Islamic economic performance across three dynasties, with particular attention to the Abbasid "Golden Age" -- trade networks, financial innovations, institutional frameworks, and GDP-adjacent metrics that contextualise Yusuf's world within a broader civilisational arc.

---

## Overview

Published in *Zhurnal Belorusskogo Gosudarstvennogo Universiteta. Istoriya* (Vol. 7, No. 2, 2025), this historical-descriptive study traces the development of Islamic economic thought and practice across three major dynasties: Umayyad (661-750 CE), Abbasid (750-1258 CE), and Ottoman (1300-1922 CE). Written by researchers at Indonesian universities (Universitas Muhammadiyah Purwokerto and Universitas Sains Al-Qur'an), the paper applies a historical-descriptive method to survey how each dynasty managed economic affairs within its political and social context.

For our project, the Abbasid section is the primary interest, but the comparative framework is also valuable: it places the Abbasid economy within a developmental arc, showing what came before (Umayyad foundations) and what came after (Ottoman revival), allowing us to understand the Abbasid achievement not as an isolated miracle but as a particular historical configuration of institutional innovation, trade expansion, and state capacity. The paper's strength is breadth; its weakness is depth -- it surveys rather than drills down. Cross-reference with Shatzmiller and Oran for granular data.

## Key Extracts

### The Umayyad Foundation: What the Abbasids Inherited

The Umayyad dynasty (661-750 CE) was "the first Islamic government to institutionalize hereditary leadership" and laid essential administrative foundations: a structured bureaucracy, a standardised coinage system, and a postal service (*barid*). These institutional innovations -- particularly currency standardisation and bureaucratic structure -- created the stability that was a precondition for economic growth.

The Umayyads also oversaw the initial monetisation of the economy, replacing Byzantine and Sasanian coins with distinctively Islamic dinars and dirhams bearing Arabic inscriptions. This was not merely symbolic: uniform coinage reduced transaction costs across the empire's vast territory and created a common commercial language.

**Scene potential:** When Yusuf's ancestors made the journey from Merv to Baghdad with the Abbasid revolution in 750 CE, they were moving from a Umayyad world into an Abbasid one -- but the economic infrastructure they encountered (coins, bureaucracy, postal routes) was largely Umayyad in origin. The Abbasid innovation was to *expand* and *sophisticate* these systems, not to create them from nothing. Ali ibn Suleiman's generation inherited an already-functioning economic system; Yusuf's generation will transform it.

### The Abbasid "Golden Age": Economic Prosperity Under Harun al-Rashid

The paper identifies the Abbasid dynasty as the "Golden Age" of Islamic civilisation, characterised by "significant progress in trade, agriculture, and industry." Key findings:

**State economic policy:** The Abbasid state actively encouraged economic growth through irrigation projects, mining expansion, and agricultural development. These were not laissez-faire policies but deliberate state investments in productive capacity -- canal construction, water management, and the opening of new agricultural land.

**The Bayt al-Mal as fiscal engine:** The Bayt al-Mal played a "central role in revenue management and redistribution." Under Harun al-Rashid, economic revenues were "diversified and centralized," supported by multiple specialised departments (*diwans*). State wealth funded social services, education, healthcare, scientific endeavours, literature, and public works -- a remarkably broad portfolio of state expenditure.

**Harun al-Rashid's reign as the peak:** The paper explicitly identifies the period 786-809 CE as "the peak of Abbasid prosperity" -- precisely the years spanning Yusuf's adolescence through early adulthood. This is not coincidence; the story is set at the apex of a civilisation.

**Scene potential:** The prosperity of Harun's reign is the rising tide that lifts all boats, including Yusuf's. But the paper's implicit narrative -- a peak followed by decline -- also foreshadows the fragility of this prosperity. When Harun dies in 809 CE and the civil war begins, the institutional foundations that sustained the boom will crack. Yusuf's challenge in Books 3-4 is to build a private institution (the House of Paper) resilient enough to survive the collapse of the state institutions that enabled its creation.

### Trade Networks: The Abbasid Commercial World

The Abbasid economy was integrated into a global trade network extending from the Atlantic to the South China Sea. Key characteristics:

- **Maritime trade:** Arab vessels operated through the Persian Gulf, reaching India, Southeast Asia, China, Korea, and Japan. The port of Basra served as the primary maritime hub connecting Baghdad to oceanic trade.
- **Overland trade:** The Silk Road and its subsidiary routes connected Baghdad to Central Asia, Khorasan, and ultimately China. Southern routes crossed Arabia to the Red Sea and East Africa.
- **Financial infrastructure:** The suftaja (bill of exchange), hawala (value transfer), and sakk (cheque) systems enabled merchants to transfer funds across thousands of miles without physical transport of coin. A suftaja issued in Baghdad could be honoured in Samarkand or North Africa.
- **Commodity flows:** Manufactured goods (textiles, ceramics, paper, metalwork) flowed outward from core production centres, while raw materials (spices, precious metals, exotic woods, slaves) flowed inward.

The paper notes that "business partnerships would be made for many commercial ventures, and bonds of kinship enabled trade networks to form over huge distances." This kinship-and-trust model is the social infrastructure that makes Yusuf's financial system possible.

**Scene potential:** The trade network described here is the geographical canvas of the entire series. Yusuf's caravan journeys in Book 1 (Kufa, Wasit, Rayy, Damascus, Mosul, Merv) trace a subset of these routes. His expansion to five cities in Book 2 maps onto the documented network. The maritime trade through Basra connects to the Constantinople venture. Each journey is not a detour from the financial plot but an education in the physical infrastructure that financial instruments must serve. The suftaja works only because there are merchants at both ends of a route who trust the issuing house -- and that trust is built through exactly the kind of face-to-face relationship-building Yusuf conducts on his journeys.

### Financial Innovation: Banking and Credit Instruments

The paper surveys several key financial innovations of the Islamic Golden Age:

- **The Suftaja (Bill of Exchange):** A debt-transfer instrument allowing funds to be moved across vast distances. "A trader in Basra could write a suftaja payable in distant Samarkand." The suftaja predates European bills of exchange by centuries and was "the first and most important form of commercial credit paper in the Medieval Near East" (citing Udovitch via Chachi).
- **The Sakk (Cheque):** The English word "check" derives from the Arabic *sakk*. Muslim traders used cheques from the time of Harun al-Rashid. During the 9th century, "banks enabled the drawing of checks by a bank in Baghdad that could be cashed in Morocco" -- a span of roughly 4,000 kilometres.
- **The Hawala (Value Transfer):** A mechanism for transferring debt obligations without physical movement of funds, referenced in legal texts from the 8th century onward. The hawala system expanded through merchant networks across the Middle East during the Abbasid period.
- **The Sarraf and Jahbadh:** Professional money-changers (*sarrafs*) and state bankers (*jahbadhs*) performed most functions of modern banks: accepting deposits, extending credit, issuing payment instruments, and maintaining agent networks across the caliphate. Some maintained multigenerational family firms.

The paper notes that "many of these early capitalist concepts were adopted and further advanced in medieval Europe from the 13th century onwards." The Islamic *mudaraba* was used by Italian merchants who called it "Commenda" -- a forerunner to today's limited partnership company.

**Scene potential:** These are Yusuf's tools. The suftaja is the instrument that makes the House of Paper possible -- it is quite literally paper converting physical wealth into portable, transferable, trust-based value. The sakk is the mechanism by which Yusuf's customers access their accounts. The hawala is the infrastructure for transferring value between his branch offices. When Yusuf "builds a revolutionary financial house," these are the specific technologies he employs. The mudaraba-to-Commenda transmission path is also thematically rich: what Yusuf builds in Baghdad will, centuries later, become the foundation of European capitalism. The novel need not state this explicitly, but the reader of historical fiction will feel the weight of it.

### Comparative Economic Metrics

While the paper does not provide formal GDP estimates for the Abbasid period (the data does not support such precision), it offers several comparative indicators:

- **Urbanization:** The Islamic world was significantly more urbanised than Western Europe throughout the first millennium. Baghdad, at its peak, was the world's largest city outside China.
- **Monetary circulation:** Arabic silver dirhams have been found in hoards from Scandinavia to sub-Saharan Africa, indicating an integrated monetary zone of unprecedented scale.
- **Institutional sophistication:** The Abbasid state maintained a centralised treasury, specialised administrative departments, a standardised currency, a postal/intelligence system, and a codified system of commercial law -- institutional infrastructure that Western Europe would not match for centuries.
- **Trade volume:** The paper cites evidence of a "30% increase in trade volume" during the Abbasid period, driven by infrastructure investment (roads, bridges, irrigation) and institutional innovation (banking systems, *zakat* and *waqf* mechanisms).
- **Per capita income:** Drawing on Pamuk and Shatzmiller's work, Middle Eastern incomes were "2-3 times subsistence level" during the early Abbasid period, exceeding Roman and Byzantine peaks.

**Scene potential:** These metrics collectively paint a picture of an economy that is, by the standards of its time, astonishingly advanced and prosperous. When the reader enters Yusuf's Baghdad, they should feel -- through sensory detail, commercial activity, and material abundance -- that this is the richest, most sophisticated city in the world. The novel must convey this not through statistics but through the texture of daily life: the variety of goods in the suq, the complexity of a mudaraba contract, the existence of a functional postal system, the fact that a paper mill can operate on an industrial scale. The reader from a Western tradition may unconsciously imagine medieval Baghdad as a "developing" economy; the historical reality is that it was the *most developed* economy on earth.

### The Post-Abbasid Trajectory and Decline

The paper surveys the post-Mongol revival under the Ottoman, Safavid, and Mughal empires, noting that the Ottoman Empire "endured the longest, expanding from a small Anatolian principality into a powerful state." The Mughal Empire became "the world's largest economy" by the 17th century, producing "over 25% of world GDP."

The decline narrative is attributed to continuous warfare, institutional decay, and, eventually, European colonial disruption. The paper does not directly address the early Abbasid decline (9th-10th century political fragmentation), but the framework is implicit: the very centralisation that made Abbasid prosperity possible also made it fragile. When the centre broke (the civil war of 811-813), the system cracked.

**Scene potential:** The post-Abbasid trajectory provides thematic context for the series' ending. The House of Paper must survive not just the civil war but the transition to a new political order. Khalid's challenge in Book 4 is to adapt an institution built for Harun's unified empire to the fractured world that follows. The Ottoman/Mughal examples show that Islamic economic institutions *can* recover from political disruption -- but only with adaptation and new leadership. This is the historical argument for the series' "happy ending": survival is possible, but only through transformation.

## Facts and Dates

- **661-750 CE:** Umayyad dynasty -- established coinage system, bureaucracy, postal service; economic preconditions for Abbasid boom.
- **750 CE:** Abbasid revolution; economic centre shifts eastward from Damascus to Baghdad.
- **750-1258 CE:** Abbasid dynasty -- the "Golden Age" of Islamic civilisation.
- **786-809 CE:** Harun al-Rashid's reign -- "the peak of Abbasid prosperity."
- **9th century:** Cheques (*sakk*) drawn in Baghdad cashable in Morocco (c. 4,000 km).
- **Suftaja:** Predates European bills of exchange by centuries; "the first and most important form of commercial credit paper in the Medieval Near East."
- **Mudaraba to Commenda:** Islamic profit-sharing partnership adopted by Italian merchants as the *Commenda*, a forerunner of the limited company.
- **Trade volume:** 30% increase during the Abbasid period (cited from systematic literature review).
- **Urbanization:** Islamic world "much ahead of Western Europe" throughout the first millennium.
- **Per capita income:** 2-3x subsistence level in the early Abbasid period (Pamuk & Shatzmiller).
- **900,000,000 dirhams:** Reported contents of the state treasury under Harun al-Rashid.

## Relevance to Chapters

**Book 1 (770-791):**
- The Umayyad-to-Abbasid transition provides essential backstory for Ali ibn Suleiman's generation. The Khorasaniyya (Yusuf's family's political class) were the military-administrative force that made the transition possible. Ali's modest tile workshop exists in an economy that the Abbasid revolution has restructured.
- Trade network descriptions ground the caravan journey chapters in documented commercial geography.
- The financial instruments surveyed here (suftaja, mudaraba) are the technologies Yusuf will learn about on his journeys and eventually master.

**Book 2 (791-803):**
- Yusuf's financial innovations (watermark, suftaja system, five-city expansion) map directly onto the documented financial infrastructure. He is not inventing these instruments but systematising and scaling them within a single commercial house.
- The mudaraba-as-Commenda transmission path adds historical weight to the thematic claim that Yusuf is building something that will outlast him.
- The Barmakid connection is economically logical: the state needs financial intermediation, and Yusuf's house provides it more efficiently than existing alternatives.

**Book 3 (803-809):**
- Harun al-Rashid's reign is "the peak of Abbasid prosperity" -- and Yusuf is at the peak of his own power. The parallelism is intentional and should be felt in the prose: the abundance, the confidence, the faint unease of a summit that can only be followed by descent.
- The decline narrative foreshadowed in the paper's comparative framework parallels Yusuf's personal trajectory in Book 3: entrapment, the discovery that "the summit of achievement is a cage."

**Book 4 (809-814):**
- The civil war tests whether the financial infrastructure can survive political collapse. The paper's comparative examples (Ottoman, Mughal) suggest that Islamic economic institutions are resilient enough to survive dynastic disruption -- but only with adaptive leadership.
- Yusuf's decision to honour all obligations from both sides of the civil war is his ultimate assertion that trust-based finance can survive the collapse of the state that enabled it. This is the bet the series resolves.
