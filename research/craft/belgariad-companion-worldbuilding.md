# The Travelling Companion Group as World-Building Engine

## Craft Analysis: David Eddings, *The Belgariad* (1982--1984)

### Application to *The House of Paper*

---

## 1. The Companion as Cultural Ambassador

Eddings builds a party that functions as a portable atlas. Each companion carries his homeland with him -- not as exposition, but as instinct, preference, and reflexive behaviour. The reader never receives a lecture on Cherek culture. Instead, Barak removes his armour only under protest, refers to wrestling in the street as a commonplace winter pastime, and evaluates every situation in terms of whether swords should be drawn. The reader absorbs Cherek through Barak's body.

The technique operates on three levels simultaneously:

**a) Reflexive habits.** Barak sleeps in his mail shirt and smells of oiled steel. Hettar communicates with horses and interprets their emotional states with the seriousness of a diplomat reading a foreign head of state. Silk instinctively cases every room for exits and advantages. Durnik examines fireplaces, tools, and stables wherever they go. These are not decorative details; they are the visible surface of entire civilisations. When Barak winces at riding all day and Silk calls it a fine morning, the reader understands something about the difference between a seafaring warrior culture and a land of merchants and spies without a single word of explicit explanation.

**b) Values revealed under pressure.** The most efficient cultural exposition in the series comes during moments of friction, danger, or moral disagreement. When the party is ambushed in the Arendish forest, Durnik kills a man with his axe and weeps afterward. Barak and Hettar treat the dead with perfunctory indifference -- dragging bodies aside and moving on. Lelldorin retrieves his arrows from the corpses with workmanlike calm. In fewer than three pages, Eddings has shown the reader the gulf between Sendarian decency, Alorn warrior culture, and Arendish familiarity with violence. No character explains these differences. The reader simply witnesses them.

**c) Knowledge domains.** Each companion knows different things, and their knowledge corresponds precisely to their national background. Silk knows trade routes, border customs, the mechanics of bribery, and how to read a room for informants. Hettar knows terrain, animals, and distances. Mandorallen knows fortifications, feudal law, and the code of honour that governs aristocratic combat. Durnik knows how things are built: fireplaces, shelters, horseshoes, axles. The distributed knowledge means that the right person always provides the right information at the right moment, and it feels natural because it flows from established character.

---

## 2. Bickering as Exposition

The most distinctive feature of Eddings's world-building is that it arrives disguised as humour. The companions tease, argue, and needle each other constantly, and nearly every exchange carries freight.

**Cultural friction as information delivery.** When Silk tells Barak his armour smells like a rusty ironworks, the joke conveys that Chereks live in their armour, that Drasnians find this barbaric, and that these two peoples -- though allies -- regard each other with cheerful contempt. When Silk says Algars are always suspicious, the aside sketches an entire national temperament. When Durnik sells the wagons for half their value and Silk calls him the absolute soul of a Sendar, the compliment-insult simultaneously defines Sendarian thrift and Drasnian acquisitiveness.

**Prejudice that complicates.** Eddings is careful to set up national stereotypes and then undermine them. Mandorallen is introduced as a pompous, insensitive Mimbrate knight -- the embodiment of everything Asturians hate. Then, in a single revelation, Garion discovers that Mandorallen's blunt speech to the wounded Lelldorin was an act of compassion designed to save his life. The prejudice Garion absorbed from Lelldorin cracks apart, and with it the reader's assumptions about Arendish feudalism. This pattern -- plant the stereotype, let the companion embody it, then show the human being behind it -- is repeated for every major character.

**Humour as Trojan horse.** Silk's story about the election of the first Sendarian king (the rutabaga farmer who warned the kneeling nobles about the manure) is a comic set-piece that delivers, in under two pages, the entire political philosophy of Sendaria: populist, practical, suspicious of grandeur. Captain Brendig's stiff-lipped offence at the joke confirms the stereotype even as it humanises him. The reader laughs, and the world expands.

**The banter rhythm.** Eddings establishes a specific rhythm: a factual observation from one character, a sarcastic rejoinder from Silk, a blunt correction from Barak, and a weary intervention from Wolf or Aunt Pol. This rhythm becomes so familiar that the reader processes new information effortlessly because the delivery mechanism is trusted. The banter is comfort food; the world-building is the nutrition hidden inside it.

## 3. Complementary Knowledge: The Distributed Encyclopedia

No single character in the Belgariad knows everything. This is a deliberate structural choice that makes exposition invisible. Consider how the party handles a single problem -- crossing into Tolnedra:

- **Silk** knows the customs station will demand a bribe and handles the negotiation. He understands Tolnedran corruption as a system, not a failing.
- **Durnik** notices that the local soldiers are slovenly compared to legionnaires and disapproves. His Sendarian work ethic makes the distinction visible.
- **Mandorallen** asks whether trouble is likely and instinctively thinks in terms of combat readiness.
- **Barak** assesses the soldiers' fighting capacity at a glance.
- **Hettar** checks the horses.
- **Wolf** and **Aunt Pol** monitor for magical threats.

Each character is effectively reading the same scene through a different lens. The reader receives a composite, multi-angle view of Tolnedra without any single character functioning as an omniscient narrator.

**Ignorance is as useful as knowledge.** When Garion asks questions -- "Why are there Tolnedran soldiers in Sendaria?" "What are Murgos?" "Who was the Rivan King?" -- his ignorance is the reader's ignorance, and the other companions' answers reveal both the facts and their national attitudes toward those facts. Silk answers with cynicism and strategic thinking. Barak answers with warrior bluntness. Aunt Pol answers with enigmatic brevity. The same question, answered by different companions, yields different kinds of information.

**The naive observer.** Garion's role is crucial. As a Sendarian farm boy, he has no knowledge of the wider world, and his earnest questions give every companion licence to explain. This is a classic device, but Eddings makes it work by ensuring that Garion is perceptive enough to notice what matters: the absence of shadows under the dark rider, the way Silk's hands move in secret language, the quiet despair in Durnik's tears. His naivety is about geopolitics, not about human nature.

## 4. The Growing Group: New Lands, New Companions

Eddings uses a strict pattern: the party enters a new territory, encounters a representative of that culture, and that representative joins the quest. The culture then travels with the group.

**The pattern:**

1. **Sendaria** (Books 1): Garion, Durnik, and Aunt Pol are the Sendarian core. Their values -- practicality, decency, suspicion of grandeur -- are the baseline against which all other cultures are measured.

2. **Cherek** (Book 1): Barak joins in Sendaria but comes fully alive in Val Alorn, where his marriage, his doom-prophecy, and the wild Cherek warrior culture surround him. The palace with its crumbling, endlessly expanded wings tells the reader everything about Cherek kings.

3. **Drasnia** (Book 1): Silk also joins early, but Drasnian culture is revealed gradually through his behaviour -- the secret hand language, the network of agents, the assumption that everyone is lying. When they encounter other Drasnians (Delvor at the Great Fair, Kheran in the gold camp), they communicate in a private code that Garion and the reader must decode.

4. **Arendia** (Book 2): Lelldorin joins and brings the Asturian-Mimbrate conflict with him. Then Mandorallen joins and brings the Mimbrate perspective. Their mutual suspicion is Arendia's civil war in microcosm. When Lelldorin must be left behind among Mimbrates, the forced trust between ancient enemies dramatises the theme.

5. **Algaria** (Books 1--2): Hettar joins as a spare, silent figure and grows into the party's cavalry expert and tracker. His hatred of Murgos -- personal, murderous, barely controlled -- is the most extreme emotion in the group, and it carries the history of Algar-Murgo conflict in his body.

6. **Tolnedra** (Books 2--3): Ce'Nedra joins late and unwillingly, bringing her Tolnedran arrogance, political sophistication, and commercial instincts. Her clashes with Garion are a domesticated version of the Sendarian-Tolnedran cultural divide.

**What makes the pattern invisible.** Each new companion is introduced through action, not biography. Lelldorin is discovered singing a war song and is pulled off his horse. Mandorallen is encountered in the middle of a battle he started single-handedly. Ce'Nedra is found hiding in a tree. The reader meets a person first and a nation second. The biographical information -- family, grievances, customs -- trickles out over dozens of pages through conversation, reaction, and the banter pattern.

## 5. Application to *The House of Paper*

The House of Paper has a structurally similar situation: a young protagonist on an overland journey through diverse territories, accompanied by figures who represent distinct cultures, trades, and worldviews. The key difference is that Eddings writes comic-heroic fantasy where banter is expected, while *The House of Paper* operates in literary historical fiction where the world-building must feel earned and the humour must emerge from character rather than from genre convention. The adaptation must therefore be indirect.

### What to Take

**a) Distribute knowledge across companions.** Each of Yusuf's travelling companions should know things the others do not, and their knowledge should arise from their backgrounds.

- **Umar** (senior journeyman, Khorasani, Shi'a sympathies) should know the practical logistics of travel -- which wells are reliable, which towns are hostile to Khorasanis, where to find a *khan* that will shelter them. His knowledge is experiential and hard-won. He teaches by letting Yusuf fail. His taciturn delivery contrasts with Silk's volubility, but the function is identical: he is the voice of road-wisdom. His Shi'a sympathies should colour his reactions to specific places (the Great Mosque in Kufa, for example) in ways that reveal sectarian geography without exposition.

- **Musa** (musician, emotional intelligence) should know the social texture of every place they visit -- who is friendly, who is dangerous, what the local grievances are. Musicians hear things merchants do not. A man who plays the oud in a *khan* at night occupies a different social position from a paper trader. Let Musa's knowledge arrive through anecdote, song, and observation. He is the anti-Umar: where Umar withholds, Musa overflows.

- **Sa'dan** (camel handler) should know animals, weather, terrain, and the practical underside of the caravan -- water rationing, load distribution, the temper of specific beasts. His world is physical and immediate. When he speaks, it should be about things you can touch. He provides the sensory grounding that Hettar provides in the Belgariad, but through a different register: not the mystical horse-lord but the working man who keeps the enterprise moving.

- **Omar al-Dimashqi** (craftsman, later journeys) should know materials, quality, and the standards of the trade. His eye for craft gives him a different reading of every market they enter. He can assess the quality of local paper, local textiles, local metalwork at a glance. His knowledge is the knowledge of making, and it connects back to the workshop scenes.

**b) Use friction and disagreement for exposition.** The Belgariad's banter works because the companions disagree. In *The House of Paper*, the disagreements should be quieter but equally revealing. When Umar and Musa disagree about the right approach to a difficult town, the reader should learn something about the town from the nature of the disagreement. When Sa'dan objects to the pace Yusuf wants to set, the objection should teach the reader about camel endurance, desert distances, and the economics of overloading. The friction need not be comic; it can be tense, practical, and rooted in genuine stakes. But it must be between characters with different frames of reference, so that the collision illuminates.

**c) Let ignorance drive questions.** Yusuf at seventeen is, like Garion, a young man who has never left his city. His questions are the reader's questions. But unlike Garion, Yusuf is sharp -- Abu Said's training has given him eyes. He should ask questions that are more perceptive than the reader expects, and the answers he receives should sometimes be incomplete, evasive, or tinged with the answerer's prejudice. The drafted chapters already do this well, particularly with Umar, whose silences are more instructive than his speeches.

**d) Use the growing-group pattern for new territories.** As Yusuf's journeys expand (Rayy, Damascus, Mosul, Merv, Constantinople), each territory should ideally add a figure -- however briefly -- who carries that place with them. This need not mean a permanent companion. Even a secondary character who travels with the caravan for a few days can fill the role of cultural ambassador. The Zagros mountain guide, the Damascus papyrus dealer, the Nestorian translator in Mosul -- each can embody a place and leave an imprint on Yusuf's understanding.

### What to Leave Behind

**a) The comic register.** Eddings's banter is genre-appropriate for heroic fantasy but would feel out of place in *The House of Paper*. The friction between companions should be expressed through the voice and style established in the existing chapters: dry, precise, grounded in the physical world. Umar's gruffness is not Barak's bluster; it is the compressed reticence of a man who has seen too much and says too little. Musa's warmth is not Silk's wit; it is the spontaneous generosity of a musician who cannot help reaching toward people.

**b) The one-nation-per-companion simplicity.** Eddings maps one culture to one character with minimal overlap. The Abbasid world is more layered than that. Umar is Khorasani, but he is also a craftsman, a Shi'a sympathiser, and a man with personal history in Kufa. Sa'dan is an Arab, but his Arabness is inflected by his class, his trade, and his relationship to the desert. Companions should carry multiple identities, and those identities should sometimes conflict with each other. This complexity is what separates literary historical fiction from genre world-building.

**c) The innocent protagonist.** Garion is endearingly naive. Yusuf must not be. His perceptiveness is his defining trait. He reads people the way his father reads tile grain. His questions should reveal not innocence but analytical hunger. When he asks Umar about Kufa, it should not be the question of a boy who knows nothing; it should be the question of a young man who has noticed something specific and wants to understand the pattern behind it.

### The Core Principle

What Eddings understands, and what *The House of Paper* can adapt, is that **the best world-building is relational**. The reader does not learn about Cherek from a description of Cherek. The reader learns about Cherek from the way Barak reacts when Silk insults his armour. The reader does not learn about Sendaria from a history lesson. The reader learns about Sendaria from Durnik's tears after he kills a man.

Applied to the Abbasid world: the reader should learn about the Khorasaniyya not from a passage of exposition but from the way Umar goes silent in a particular mosque. The reader should learn about Bedouin culture not from a speech about desert honour but from the way Sa'dan refuses to leave a sick camel behind. The reader should learn about the paper trade not from a catalogue of techniques but from the way Omar al-Dimashqi runs his fingers across a sheet and winces.

The companion group is a lens array. Each member refracts the same world differently. When the party walks into a new city, the city is described not once but multiple times -- through each companion's eyes, reactions, and expertise. The composite image is richer, more dimensional, and more convincing than any single narrator could provide. This is the technique worth borrowing: not the jokes, not the pattern of one-culture-per-character, but the fundamental insight that a diverse group of travellers, each carrying their own world inside them, is the most powerful world-building tool a novelist has.
