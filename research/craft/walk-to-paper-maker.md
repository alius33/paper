The day had begun early for Ali. He was not a staunch follower of the five daily prayers, with the pre-dawn Fajr prayer being his most missed one. But on that day he had slept fitfully, anxious more than anything else at what he had to do that morning. He woke as the adhan pierced the night sky, calling all to wake, and reminding them that prayer is better than sleep. The muezzin's melodic yet piercing voice came drifting through the slightly open window in the room. Then another voice joined in, further away, starting his call to prayer. There were hundreds of masjids in the city, and the muezzin at each tried to outdo each other. Ali lay there in bed half asleep but listening to the call to prayer. By the end he could identify 4 separate adhans all happening at the same time.

He had woken, washed himself, and prayed that morning\'s fajr, but that wasn't the reason he had taken the trouble to wake up so early. He was up so he could perform the next prayer - the Istikhara - a practice handed down by the prophet himself. A prayer to ask the almighty for help in making a decision.

Ali had done all the pre-requisites. He had spoken to everyone he could think of who would be helpful. It was through a lot of consideration, discussion with people, and his own innate sense of an artisan and tradesman that he had decided on the course for his Yusuf.

The boy was a strange one, with a temperament and mind which Ali could like from afar, but would not have wished on his own son. He was willful and disliked constraints of any kind. Smart, but what he seemed to find most joy in was using his intellect to find ways to break the rules. There is an old saying that those who know how to break rules are often the best to make rules.

With this old wisdom in mind he had seriously considered the life of a qadi for his son. Being a man of law would certainly ensure he could provide a life for himself. But a decade or more of the strictest religious schooling would have either broken the boys spirit, or he would have run away. Neither possibility was something Ali was willing to gamble on. The boy loved books, and so an academic life would perhaps be best for him. But the thought of his son being a bureaucrat struck him the wrong way. He had dealt with officials all his life and the vast majority had been men of middle to low intelligence, relying on official ranks to project power and bully others into respecting them. That was not the kind of man he wanted Yusuf to grow up to be.

Finally he came to the third option, and the one he had been hoping for. The boy needs to learn a trade. He might decide to become a merchant later, or marry some rich widow and become a land owner, but if everything else fails, he will have a trade to fall back on.

Having collected his thoughts, and given himself the reassurance, he prayed.
