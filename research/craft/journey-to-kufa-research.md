There are two types of silence. The first is the quiet of rest, of the world slowing down, of the end of things. It was welcome, and it was what the boy was familiar with.

This was the other kind.

The pre-dawn cold of the caravan camp ground outside the city walls held a different silence. Vast, thin and brittle. It was a quiet that waited, holding its breath, and into it the world was leaking. It was being broken a hundred times by the snores and grunts of men and animals, but thankfully the open sky snatched away the sounds and let the quiet prevail.

Then the silence was pierced by a voice. The dawn call to prayer had started, and it had launched the complaints. Camels. It was not a simple noise, but a symphony of protest. A deep, wet, protesting groan from a hundred throats at once. They were the ships of the desert, indispensable and prized, and they hated the morning.

A bubbling, liquid belch from his own animal shocked Yusuf into fully opening his eyes. Hazily he saw ustad Umar get up and wrap himself in his shawl. As if in reminder, the wind started blowing harder and Yusuf pulled his blanket over himself tighter. He kept his eyes lowered, keen to avoid catching the ustad's eye and being told to go wash up for prayer. He watched his teacher get up, and walk away to the prayer area of the camp.

Breathing a sigh of relief, the boy allowed himself to drift off back to sleep.

What awoke him next time was the smell. Cutting through the thick animal reek of the camels was the smell of food. Not even fully formed food, first just the spices. He smelled a hint of cumin, a wisp of cardamom, burned sugar spoke of something sweet, all mixed together with the smoke of the fires. Breakfast would be ready soon, and to Yusuf that was a far greater reason to wake up than prayer.

As the delicious smells of the food spread across the vast field in which the caravan was camped, the sounds became louder, more frantic and urgent. Everyone wanted to finish the task at hand and start heading over to the cook fires. The biggest source of travellers along the road to Kufa at this time of year were the Hajj pilgrims. Those taking the route through Baghdad came from the eastern half of the Khalifa's vast empire.

Raqqa, Mosul, Isfahan, Rayy, all the great cities of the empire\'s heartland were represented. Pilgrims had come from as far away as Khorasan, from cities Yusuf had only heard of in his fathers stories; Merv, Nasa, Nishapur, and even further from lands Yusuf had not even heard of. Men from Kabul who spoke their Arabic with a thick accent and prayed differently than the others. They spoke of people from even far away, the land of Sindh, where friends they knew had set off from. They spoke of a city named Karachi where their friends had taken ship, and would land near Basra to take the overland route and join them in Kufa.

Sayeed grinned, his teeth very white in his dusty face. He held up another one. It was a dried, brown locust, its legs and wings still attached.

\"Protein!\" Sayeed said cheerfully. \"*Jarād*. Tastes like dusty shrimp. Want one?\"

Yusuf recoiled, a look of such profound horror on his face that Sayeed burst out laughing.

Sa\'dan just rolled his eyes, the corner of his mouth twitching. \"Don\'t waste them, Sayeed. The boy will be begging you for one in two days.\"

The sun climbed higher, a white, merciless eye. The caravan plodded on. The only sounds were the plodding of ten thousand feet, the *creak-squeak* of the harnesses, the shouts of the drivers---\"Hut! Hut! Yallah!\"---and the hot, empty wind. Yusuf, his mouth full of dust, his back on fire, his stomach churning, held on to his saddle and stared at the brown, endless road ahead.

The journey had truly begun.

"Is it\... dangerous?" Yusuf asked, trying to sound casual, though the rich stew suddenly felt heavier in his stomach.

Sa'dan sopped up the last of his porridge, his gaze distant for a moment, looking past Yusuf to the open desert beyond the camp. "Everything is dangerous. Being born is dangerous. But the road\... it has its own rules. You learn to watch the sky, not for the beauty, but for the way the clouds gather, or for the *absence* of clouds when the air feels too heavy. You learn that a yellow haze on the horizon at noon means a sandstorm by dusk. You learn which tribes are owed a 'gift' for passage and which will kill you for the insult of offering one. The Banu Kalb will accept silk, but the Banu Tamim want salt, and they\'ll slit your throat if you mix them up. You learn to tell the difference between a sandstorm kicking up dust and a bandit raid doing the same. One you can wait out, the other you must face."

"Bandits?" Yusuf's stomach tightened.

"He's trying to scare you," Sa'id said, rolling his eyes. "We travel in a *qafila*, a great caravan. Hundreds of us. We have guards, good ones. The master pays well. We are safe."

"We are *safer*," Sa'dan corrected, pointing his bread at Sa'id like a schoolmaster's rod. "Never say 'safe.' The desert hears you and takes it as a challenge. But he is right, mostly. The master is a good merchant. He travels in strong company. The real work isn't fighting bandits, Yusuf. It's this."

He gestured to their packed-up tent, the waiting camels, the organized chaos around them. "It's waking up when it's still night, when your bones ache with cold so deep you think you\'ll never be warm again. It's counting every bale and box, and counting them again because you\'re sure you missed one, and a missed box is a beating, or worse, a lost customer. It's making sure the camel-pullers aren't cheating you on feed, or mixing sand in the barley. It's knowing how to tie a diamond hitch in the dark, in the pouring rain, with fingers so cold and numb you can't feel the rope."

Sa\'dan leaned in a little. "It's boredom, Yusuf. That's the real enemy, worse than bandits. Hours and hours of walking. First, you\'ll count your steps. It\'s just the sun trying to burn the thoughts from your head, and the flies, and the smell of the camel, and the sound of your own feet in the sand. *That's* the real work. Not fighting, but enduring."
