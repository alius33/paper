# The Naive POV Character as World-Building Device: Lessons from The Belgariad

## Overview

David Eddings' Belgariad series (1982-1984) is one of the most effective deployments of the naive-POV technique in popular fantasy. Garion begins as a farm boy who knows nothing of sorcery, prophecy, or the wider political world, and over five books grows into the figure destined to confront a god. The series is essentially a masterclass in using ignorance as narrative architecture: the reader learns the world precisely as fast as the protagonist does, and the protagonist's questions become the reader's questions.

This analysis examines how Eddings builds that architecture, where it succeeds, where it creaks, and how its principles should be adapted -- not copied -- for Yusuf ibn Ali al-Khorasani in The House of Paper.

---

## 1. The Farm Boy Template: Ignorance as Narrative Engine

Garion's ignorance in Pawn of Prophecy is nearly total. He does not know that his aunt is a sorceress, that the old storyteller is the immortal Belgarath, that gods walk the earth, that he himself has power, or that a cosmic war hinges on his existence. He does not even know his own parentage. He cannot read. He has never bought food with money. He has never left Faldor's farm.

This totality is the key to the technique's power. Because Garion knows nothing, everything is new. The reader cannot be bored by exposition because every piece of information arrives as a discovery for the character. When the old storyteller recounts the myth of the Orb of Aldur in the dining hall, Garion is hearing it for the first time -- and so, effectively, is the reader. The myth becomes drama because it is filtered through a consciousness for whom it carries weight: Garion feels a "chill" at the mention of Torak, he holds his breath, he notices the reactions of others. The information is alive because it matters to someone on the page.

Eddings structures Garion's ignorance in concentric rings:

- **Innermost ring (personal):** Who are his parents? Why does Aunt Pol have that white lock? Why does the old storyteller seem to know her so well? These questions drive the early chapters and create intimate stakes.
- **Middle ring (social):** How does money work? What are Murgos? Why are the Arends fighting each other? These questions expand the world as Garion travels.
- **Outer ring (cosmic):** What is the Orb? Who is Torak? What is sorcery? These are the questions the entire series exists to answer, and they are planted early but resolved late.

The genius of this structure is that the personal questions keep the reader hooked while the cosmic questions build in the background. Garion asks about his mother before he asks about the Orb.

### When the Ignorance Feels Natural vs. Convenient

Garion's ignorance is most convincing in the early chapters of Pawn of Prophecy. A boy raised on an isolated farm in a medieval-analogue world would plausibly not know about international politics, sorcery, or even basic commerce. His inability to read is believable. His confusion about buying food at a tavern is charming and revealing.

The ignorance begins to feel more convenient when it extends to things Aunt Pol and Belgarath actively conceal from him. The story requires that Garion not learn his true identity too early, so the adults around him engage in a sustained campaign of evasion and misdirection. This works narratively -- it creates tension and dramatic irony -- but it occasionally strains credulity. Aunt Pol slaps Garion for saying Torak's name, then refuses to explain why. Belgarath tells the entire dining hall a kingly myth with obvious relevance to Garion's situation, then deflects every question. The adults' secrecy is the engine that keeps the naive POV running, but it requires the reader to accept that no one ever slips, and that a boy as perceptive as Garion never forces the issue.

## 2. How Garion Learns: Questions, Observations, and Forced Exposition

Eddings uses four distinct mechanisms to deliver world-building through Garion's POV:

### Direct Questions

Garion asks questions constantly. In the opening chapters alone, he asks Durnik why metalwork matters, asks Mister Wolf why the story of the Orb is unfinished, asks why he cannot keep the Angarak penny, asks about his parents, asks about Arends, asks about Murgos. This is the most straightforward world-building technique: the naive character asks, and a knowledgeable character answers.

Eddings manages the potential tedium of this approach by varying who answers and how. Durnik gives plain, practical answers. Mister Wolf gives evasive, teasing answers that create more questions than they resolve. Aunt Pol gives curt, authoritative answers that close down inquiry. Silk gives sardonic, knowing answers that reveal character as much as information. The variety of answering styles keeps the question-and-answer rhythm from becoming mechanical.

### Observations and Misinterpretations

Garion frequently observes things he cannot fully understand, creating dramatic irony. He notices that Aunt Pol and the storyteller exchange "a strange look" but does not know what it means. He sees the dark rider watching him but does not know who it is. He recognises that Mister Wolf's act in the spice shop is a performance but does not fully understand the danger. These moments let Eddings plant information that will pay off later while keeping Garion's limited understanding intact.

### Correct Observations (Growing Competence)

Even in the first book, Garion sometimes reads situations correctly. His first deliberate lie to the Murgo in Upper Gralt is a pivotal moment: he invents a false identity on the spot, complete with a plausible cover story, and Mister Wolf is "impressed." This is Eddings showing us that Garion is not merely passive. He has instincts -- quick, adaptive instincts -- that foreshadow his later competence. Similarly, Garion notices the Murgo's interest narrow and widen, correctly reading the interrogation's emotional temperature.

By Queen of Sorcery, this grows more pronounced. Garion's encounter with Lelldorin at Vo Wacune shows a boy who can now read social situations with some sophistication. He recognises Lelldorin's impulsiveness, sees through his revolutionary fervour to the friendship underneath, and manages a tricky diplomatic situation (the overheard conversation about the Asturian plot) with surprising maturity. The naive POV is beginning to evolve into something more complex: a character who knows some things deeply and is still learning others.

### Forced Exposition

The weakest moments in the Belgariad's world-building come when Eddings must deliver large blocks of information and has no natural way to do it through Garion's ignorance. The campfire scene in Queen of Sorcery, Chapter One, where Mister Wolf lays out the entire quest -- Zedar, the Orb, Ctuchik, the limitations of sorcery -- is essentially a briefing. Eddings handles it competently by having different characters ask different questions (Barak asks about sorcery, Silk asks about names, Lelldorin asks about Torak's death), but it remains a scene where characters sit in a circle and explain things to each other. The naive POV provides cover: Garion needs to hear all this, and the reader needs it too. But the seams are visible.

Eddings' best strategy for forced exposition is to embed it in action or conflict. The myth of the Orb is delivered as a performance -- the storyteller rises, draws his cloak about him, and declaims. The information about Murgos is delivered during a tense encounter in a spice shop. The rules of sorcery are explained while the group huddles in a ruin, surrounded by fog and danger. Context dignifies exposition.

## 3. The Transition from Naive to Knowing

The Belgariad handles the transition across its five books with a rough but effective three-stage structure:

### Stage One (Pawn of Prophecy): Everything Is New

Garion knows nothing. Every location, every character, every concept is introduced fresh. The reader is in lockstep with Garion's discovery. World-building and character development are almost indistinguishable because Garion is building his understanding of both simultaneously. This is the purest deployment of the naive POV.

### Stage Two (Queen of Sorcery, Magician's Gambit): Some Things Are Familiar, Some New

By the second book, Garion knows who his companions are, understands sorcery exists (if not how it works), and has basic competence with a sword. The world-building shifts from "what is this?" to "how does this work here?" Each new country (Arendia, Tolnedra, Nyissa) provides fresh naive-POV material -- different customs, different social structures, different dangers -- while Garion's relationships with his companions deepen through familiarity rather than discovery.

The critical technique in this stage is that Garion's naivety becomes selective. He is no longer ignorant of everything; he is ignorant of specific things. His moral naivety (his shock at Arendish serfdom, his inability to understand Lelldorin's revolutionary zeal, his discomfort with political compromise) replaces his factual naivety as the primary world-building tool.

### Stage Three (Castle of Wizardry, Enchanter's End Game): Garion Becomes a Source of Knowledge

By Castle of Wizardry, the naive POV has largely inverted. Garion is now in charge -- literally leading the group while Belgarath is incapacitated and Aunt Pol is shielding Errand. He makes tactical decisions, disperses magical attacks, and handles Silk's needling with growing confidence. When he thinks about the Orb's song and his growing power, the internal monologue is analytical, not confused.

Eddings compensates for the loss of naive-POV world-building by shifting to relationship dynamics (Relg and Taiba), by introducing the Orb's telepathic probing of Garion's memories (which functions as a recap device), and by placing Garion in new situations where his power is tested but his judgment is uncertain. The world-building challenge is no longer "what is this world?" but "what kind of man will Garion become in it?" -- a question of character rather than setting.

The transition is not always smooth. Castle of Wizardry's opening chapters show Eddings occasionally struggling with a protagonist who knows too much. The campfire briefings that worked when Garion was ignorant feel less natural when he already understands the stakes. Eddings solves this partly by giving Garion new challenges (leadership, magical combat) and partly by introducing new characters (Taiba, Errand) who bring their own forms of naivety.

## 4. Comparison to Yusuf: Adapting the Technique for The House of Paper

Yusuf ibn Ali al-Khorasani is a fundamentally different kind of naive POV from Garion, and the differences are as instructive as the similarities.

### What Yusuf Knows That Garion Does Not

Yusuf is 17 when the story begins -- not a child but a young man with deep craft knowledge. He has spent eight years apprenticing in Abu Said's paper workshop. He understands the chemistry of sizing, the physics of drying, the aesthetics of a well-made sheet. He knows his neighbourhood, his family, his religion, his city. He has a sophisticated inner life. He reads people instinctively -- his mother taught him the Bedouin arts of observation, and Abu Said has honed his eye for quality and deception.

This means Yusuf's naive POV cannot operate the way Garion's does. Garion's ignorance is a blank canvas; Yusuf's is a canvas with a detailed painting in the centre and blank edges. The reader arrives in a world that the protagonist already partially understands, and the world-building must honour that partial understanding while using the blank edges to introduce new material.

### Where Yusuf's Naivety Lives

Yusuf's naivety is commercial, geographical, and political. He has never left Baghdad. He does not know what a caravan journey entails (Chapter 1 opens with him waking in a caravan camp for the first time). He does not understand how trade works at scale -- the difference between making paper and selling it, between local reputation and distant markets. He does not grasp the political forces that shape commerce: the Barmakid faction, the tension between Arab and Khorasani elites, the machinery of the caliphate's tax and postal systems.

This selective naivety is both a constraint and an advantage. The constraint is that Yusuf cannot ask the childlike questions that make Garion's POV so efficient. He would not ask "what is money?" the way Garion does at the tavern. The advantage is that his questions are more interesting to an adult reader. When Yusuf encounters the failed paper sale in Kufa (Chapter 3), his confusion is not about what a market is but about why his excellent paper will not sell in this particular market. The world-building that follows -- about local tastes, existing supplier relationships, the politics of Kufa's scholarly community -- is delivered through a character sophisticated enough to ask sharp questions and interpret the answers.

### The Perception Paradox

The most important difference between Garion and Yusuf is that Yusuf's defining trait is perception. He reads people the way his father reads tile grain. This creates a paradox for the naive POV: how can a character who sees everything be ignorant of anything?

The answer, already visible in the drafted chapters, is that Yusuf's perception operates at the interpersonal level but not at the systemic level. He can tell that Sa'dan is watching him with amusement at breakfast, that Umar is testing him by withholding instruction, that Ibn Zubair's calm is the performance of a man who believes the world moves too slowly. But he cannot see the larger structures these individuals inhabit. He reads the person but not the system. His journey across Books 1-4 is essentially the education of a systems thinker -- a man who learns to see not just the individual tile but the entire mosaic.

This is a more sophisticated deployment of the naive POV than Eddings uses, and it requires more sophisticated world-building techniques. Instead of asking "what is this?" (Garion's mode), Yusuf asks "why does this work this way?" and "what am I not seeing?" His questions are diagnostic rather than descriptive.

### Techniques to Borrow from the Belgariad

1. **The concentric-ring structure of ignorance.** Yusuf's unknowns should also be layered: personal (what does Omar really think of me?), commercial (why do the Kufa scholars reject our paper?), political (what are the Barmakids planning?), and existential (what does it cost to build something that lasts?). The personal questions should hook the reader first.

2. **Variety in answering voices.** Eddings makes each mentor figure deliver information differently. Yusuf's world should do the same: Abu Said teaches by silence and demonstration, Umar teaches by letting Yusuf fail, Sa'dan teaches by practical example, and Ali (the father) teaches by proverb and craft metaphor. The variety prevents the question-and-answer pattern from growing stale.

3. **Correct observations as competence markers.** Just as Garion's lie to the Murgo shows growing cunning, Yusuf's moments of accurate reading -- noticing that Sa'dan checks on Sayeed from the corner of his eye, registering that the Sikbaj choice is a test of character -- should signal to the reader that this is a protagonist who will grow into formidable perception. The naive POV should leak competence at the edges.

4. **Embedding exposition in action.** Eddings' best world-building happens during tense encounters, not campfire lectures. The House of Paper should follow this principle ruthlessly. The mechanics of a suftaja should be revealed during a transaction with stakes, not explained in a quiet moment. The politics of the Barmakid faction should emerge from a scene where Yusuf observes their power in action, not from a character delivering background.

## 5. What Eddings Does That Will Not Work for The House of Paper

### Total Ignorance

Garion's blank-canvas ignorance is a fantasy convention that depends on the reader accepting a world where a boy can grow up knowing nothing. Historical fiction cannot afford this. A 17-year-old in 8th-century Baghdad would know his prayers, his neighbourhood, his craft, his family history, and the basic political structure of the caliphate. Yusuf's ignorance must always be plausible -- he should never not know something that a boy of his background, education, and intelligence would know.

### Comedy as Exposition Vehicle

Eddings uses comedy extensively -- Silk's wisecracks, Barak's bluster, Garion's earnest misunderstandings -- to make exposition entertaining. The House of Paper's more literary register cannot rely on this. World-building must earn its place through sensory immersion, narrative tension, and the intrinsic interest of the material (craft, commerce, political intrigue). A scene about paper-making should be as compelling as a scene about sorcery, but through different means: texture, stakes, and the reader's growing understanding that paper is power.

### The Prophecy Scaffold

The Belgariad's naive POV is supported by a prophecy structure that gives the quest an external logic. Garion does not need to understand what is happening because prophecy is directing events. The House of Paper has no such scaffold. Yusuf's journey must be driven entirely by human motivation -- ambition, curiosity, family obligation, commercial opportunity. His naivety is resolved not by revelation ("you are the chosen one") but by experience ("you have learned what the world is really like"). This means the world-building cannot rely on dramatic reveals; it must accumulate gradually, the way real understanding does.

### The Ensemble Exposition Model

Eddings distributes the burden of ignorance across his ensemble: Garion asks one question, Silk asks another, Barak asks a third. This allows a single briefing scene to cover multiple topics without Garion seeming unnaturally curious. The House of Paper's tight third-person-limited POV, locked to Yusuf, cannot do this. All information must reach the reader through Yusuf's consciousness. If Yusuf does not hear it, see it, or infer it, it does not exist for the reader. This is a harder constraint than Eddings faces, and it demands more disciplined world-building -- every piece of information must have a plausible reason to enter Yusuf's awareness.

### The Mentorship Conveyor Belt

Eddings essentially passes Garion from mentor to mentor, each one explaining a different aspect of the world: Belgarath handles history and sorcery, Silk handles espionage and politics, Barak handles combat, Mandorallen handles chivalry. This works in a quest fantasy where the party accumulates members. The House of Paper cannot cycle through mentors so freely. Abu Said, Umar, Ali, and the other teachers are grounded in specific relationships and specific places. The mentorship must feel organic to the social world -- a master instructing an apprentice, a father arguing with a son, a fellow journeyman sharing trade secrets -- not like a series of tutorial levels.

## Summary of Key Principles for The House of Paper

1. **Yusuf's naivety is a window, not a wall.** He sees through it clearly in some directions (people, craft, family) and not at all in others (commerce at scale, politics, the wider world). The window should widen gradually across Book 1.

2. **Questions should be diagnostic, not descriptive.** "Why won't they buy our paper?" is a better world-building question than "What is paper?" Yusuf's intelligence should be visible in the quality of his questions.

3. **Embed all exposition in scenes with stakes.** No campfire lectures. Every piece of world-building should arrive during a moment when something is at risk -- a sale, a relationship, a journey, a reputation.

4. **Let Yusuf's perception create dramatic irony.** He should notice things the other characters miss but fail to grasp their larger significance. The reader, armed with context Yusuf lacks, should feel the gap between what he sees and what it means.

5. **Track the evolution of understanding.** By the end of Book 1, Yusuf should be asking entirely different questions than he asks at the beginning. The shift from "How do I sell paper in Kufa?" to "How do I build a financial house that will outlast me?" should feel earned, not sudden.

6. **Never let Yusuf be ignorant of something he should know.** Every gap in his knowledge must be plausible for a 17-year-old Khorasani papermaker's apprentice who has spent his entire life within Baghdad's walls. When in doubt, give him the knowledge and find a different way to deliver the world-building.
