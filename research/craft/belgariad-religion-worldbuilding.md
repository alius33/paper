# Religion and Mythology Built Through Journey: The Belgariad

## How Eddings Constructs a Religious Landscape One Nation at a Time

### 1. The Structural Conceit: One God, One Nation

The Belgariad's religious architecture is deceptively simple: seven Gods created the world, each chose a people, and the map of belief is therefore identical to the map of nations. Belar belongs to the Alorns. Chaldan is the Bull-God of the Arends. Nedra presides over the Tolnedrans. Issa watches over the Nyissans. Torak claims the Angaraks. Each border crossing is also a theological crossing. And then there is UL, the father-God who refused to participate in creation and whose people -- the Ulgos -- had to beg for divine attention, becoming the most devout civilization in the world precisely because their God was the hardest won.

What makes this system instructive for The House of Paper is not the theology itself (which is flat by design) but the *delivery mechanism*. Eddings never explains the religious map in expository form within the narrative. Instead, he uses three interlocking techniques to build it through the journey.

### 2. The Prologue as Scripture, the Journey as Commentary

Each of the first three Belgariad novels opens with a pseudo-scriptural prologue written in a deliberately archaic register. Book 1 gives us the creation myth and the theft of the Orb, framed as a chapter from a holy text. Book 2 provides the Battle of Vo Mimbre as chronicle history. Book 3 offers the story of Gorim and UL, presented explicitly as scripture from The Book of Ulgo.

These prologues are not neutral. Each is written from the perspective of a particular tradition. The creation myth in *Pawn of Prophecy* is an Alorn-centric account -- Belar is "beloved" while Torak is "the most beautiful" but vain. The Gorim prologue in *Magician's Gambit* recasts the same cosmic events from the Ulgo perspective, where the younger Gods are irresponsible children who made "monstrous" creatures and then blamed UL for their own failures.

The key technique: Garion encounters these different versions of the same cosmic events as he crosses national borders. What he learned as a bedtime story in Sendaria (a nation so ecumenically mixed it has no single patron God) is contradicted, reframed, and deepened by the version told in each new land. The same events -- Torak stealing the Orb, the cracking of the world, the departure of the Gods -- look different depending on which God's people are telling the story.

This layered retelling through geography is directly applicable to The House of Paper.

### 3. Religious Practice as Cultural Texture

Eddings makes each nation's relationship with its God visible through everyday life rather than through temple visits or theological lectures. The technique is economical and effective.

**Sendaria: The Absence of Religion as Character.** Garion grows up on Faldor's farm in Sendaria, a country so practically-minded and ethnically blended that it has no single patron deity. The result is not atheism but a comfortable, unexamined agnosticism. This secular baseline is crucial because it allows Garion -- and the reader -- to encounter each subsequent religion with fresh eyes. When religious practice finally appears, it stands out.

**Arendia: Religion as Martial Ideology.** In southern Arendia, Garion's party passes a funeral procession for a serf. The priests of Chaldan, the Bull-God, chant a hymn that has "much to do with war and vengeance, but little to do with comfort." In a single sentence, Eddings characterizes an entire national faith. Chaldan worship is about martial honour, not consolation. The religion has calcified into an instrument of aristocratic violence, and the dead serf receives war hymns because Arendish religion has no other register. No temple is described; no theology is explained. The funeral procession, encountered on the road, does the entire job.

**Tolnedra: Commerce as Faith.** Nedra, the Tolnedran God, barely appears in the narrative, because the Tolnedrans have effectively replaced religion with commerce. The Imperial highways, the legions, the elaborate trade documents, the hostels spaced at precise intervals -- these are the true liturgy of Nedra. When Silk moves through the Great Fair, haggling and dealing, what Garion witnesses is not a secular activity but the highest form of Tolnedran devotion. Eddings never needs to describe a temple of Nedra because the entire Tolnedran state *is* a temple.

**Nyissa: Religion as Narcosis.** The snake-worshipping Nyissans serve Issa, the serpent-God, through drugs, poisons, and a queen who perpetually recreates herself through narcotics to resemble the legendary Salmissra. Religion in Nyissa is hallucinatory, sensual, decadent. Garion experiences it not through a temple visit but through his own drugged abduction -- forced to feel the religion in his body before he can understand it with his mind.

**Maragor: The Haunted Faith.** The most striking religious encounter in the series is also the most devastating. Mara, God of the destroyed Marags, did not depart the world as the other Gods did. He remained, weeping endlessly over the bodies of his slaughtered people. When Garion's company crosses Maragor, they encounter not a temple but a god -- raging, weeping, clutching the ghost of a murdered child. This is religion as raw grief, stripped of all ritual. The monks of Mar Terrin, Tolnedran penitents who walk through the haunted land in penance for their ancestors' genocide, provide a contrasting note: organised religion's attempt to address a spiritual wound too vast for ceremony.

The encounter with Mara also demonstrates how Eddings uses the journey to collapse the distance between mythology and reality. The myths Garion heard as stories become, at Maragor, physically present. The god is not an abstraction or a cultural tradition; he is standing in front of them, sobbing.

**Ulgoland: Religion as Total Way of Life.** The culmination of the religious journey in the first three books is the descent into the caves of Prolgu. Belgarath warns that "religion is the central fact of Ulgo life," and everything about the subterranean city confirms this. The architecture is pyramidal, mirroring the shape of the sacred mountain. A hymn to UL has been singing continuously for five thousand years, sustained by echoes that overlap each new chanting. The Gorim waits for a prophesied child. Even the meals begin with fruit "in remembrance of the years we spent wandering in search of UL." Every detail of Ulgo existence is saturated with their founding story -- the story of the Godless people who had to convince a reluctant spirit to become their God.

Ulgo worship stands in total contrast to every other religion encountered on the journey. The Alorns are boisterous warriors whose worship of Belar consists largely of feasting and fighting. The Tolnedrans worship Nedra through commerce. The Arends worship Chaldan through war. But the Ulgos worship UL through everything -- their architecture, their food, their music, their refusal to leave the caves. Their faith is not a compartment of life but its entire substance.

### 4. Mythology as Literal History

The Belgariad's most effective narrative strategy regarding religion is the gradual revelation that myth is literally true. Garion begins the series believing Belgarath and Polgara to be ordinary (if eccentric) people. The old stories are stories -- entertaining, culturally important, but not *real*. The journey systematically strips away this comfortable distance.

In Arendia, Garion learns that the Mrin Codex and Darine Codex -- prophetic scriptures -- are being consulted as operational intelligence. In Maragor, he meets an actual God. In the caves of Ulgo, he hears the Gorim recite scripture that Belgarath confirms as accurate history because he was there when it happened. By the time Garion enters Rak Cthol, the question is no longer whether the myths are true but what they *mean*.

The technique is one of progressive literalisation. Each step of the journey moves one layer closer to the divine, from bedtime story to cultural practice to living prophecy to the direct presence of a God. The reader makes this journey alongside Garion, and the effect is to make the fantastic feel earned rather than arbitrary.

### 5. Application to The House of Paper

Yusuf's world has something Eddings' world lacks: genuine religious complexity. The Abbasid Caliphate is not a tidy map of one faith per nation. Multiple religions coexist within the same cities, the same markets, even the same partnerships. Sunni and Shi'a Muslims differ on matters of profound political and theological consequence. Nestorian Christians, Jews, and Zoroastrians occupy distinct social and economic niches but share physical space with the Muslim majority. This complexity is vastly more interesting than Eddings' schema, but it is also harder to dramatise without slipping into exposition.

Eddings' techniques can be adapted as follows.

**The Secular Baseline.** Garion begins in religionless Sendaria. Yusuf begins in the Khorasan Gate neighbourhood of Baghdad, a practically-minded artisan district where religion is the water everyone swims in -- so familiar it is nearly invisible. The adhan marks the hours. Yusuf's father prays but does not theologise. Faith is structural, not examined. This gives the journey room to make each new religious encounter striking by contrast.

**The Funeral Procession Technique.** Eddings reveals Arendish religion through a single funeral procession glimpsed from the road. The same technique works for the Kufa journey. When Yusuf enters Kufa, the Shi'a character of the city should not be explained but felt -- through the particular cadence of the adhan (slightly different from Baghdad's), through the reverence at the Great Mosque (Ali's mosque, with all the weight of the succession crisis behind it), through the way people speak about the Prophet's family with an intensity that differs from what Yusuf knows. Umar's private visit to the mosque, his dead uncle, his barely acknowledged Shi'a sympathies -- these are the Arendish funeral procession rendered in a real-world key.

**Commerce as Liturgy.** Eddings makes Tolnedran highways and trade documents into a form of worship. This maps directly onto the financial instruments of the Abbasid world. When Yusuf first encounters a suftaja honoured across a thousand miles, when he watches Abu Said's watermark held to lamplight, when he learns the siyaq accounting script -- these are not secular activities. They are the liturgy of trust, as sacralised in their way as the Ulgo hymn. The novel should treat them with the same reverence Eddings gives the caves of Prolgu.

**The Monastery Visit.** Eddings uses the Ulgo caves to show religion as a total way of life -- architecture, food, music, social structure, all saturated with faith. The Constantinople section (Book 1, Part Five) offers an equivalent opportunity with the Stoudios Monastery and Brother Michael. Nestorian monasteries along the earlier trade routes offer smaller-scale versions. These are not tourism; they are Yusuf encountering entire civilisations organised around different answers to the same questions. The physical descent into the Ulgo caves -- the increasing strangeness, the alien architecture, the continuous chanting -- finds a parallel in entering a Zoroastrian fire temple in the mountains near Rayy, where the sacred flame has burned for centuries and the rituals are ancient, beautiful, and utterly unlike anything Yusuf knows.

**Myth as History, Inverted.** Eddings' trick is to reveal that myth is literally true. The House of Paper can do something more interesting: reveal that *history* is mythologised differently by different communities. The succession from the Prophet looks different in Kufa than in Baghdad. The fall of the Sassanids looks different to a Zoroastrian in Rayy than to an Arab in Basra. Yusuf, like Garion, should encounter these layered versions one at a time, each step of the journey adding a new perspective. The effect should not be relativism but complexity -- the growing realisation that the same events contain multiple truths.

**The Mara Moment.** The encounter with a weeping God in Maragor -- mythology become physically present, grief so vast it haunts the landscape -- has no direct equivalent in a realistic historical novel. But Kufa's Great Mosque carries something of the same charge. The weight of Ali's legacy, the sense of injustice that permeates the city's stones, the living grief of the Shi'a community over events that happened more than a century before Yusuf's birth -- this is a human equivalent of Mara's wailing. The mosque should feel haunted, not by ghosts but by history that refuses to become merely historical.

### 6. The Key Difference: Complexity Versus Simplicity

Eddings' one-God-per-nation system is a machine for clarity. Each border crossing changes the religious landscape completely, and the reader never has to hold more than one faith in mind at a time. The Abbasid world refuses such tidiness. In any given scene, Yusuf might be dealing with a Nestorian scribe, a Jewish money-changer, a Zoroastrian merchant, and a Sunni bureaucrat -- all within the same suq.

The solution is not to simplify but to use Eddings' gradual-revelation technique within a complex landscape. Each journey should foreground one religious encounter while the others remain in the background. The Kufa journey foregrounds Shi'a Islam. The Rayy journey foregrounds Zoroastrianism. The Constantinople venture foregrounds Eastern Christianity. Judaism and Nestorian Christianity enter through Ishaq and the financial networks. The reader accumulates understanding one layer at a time, just as Garion does, but the layers do not replace each other -- they accrete. By the end of the journey sequence, the reader should hold the full religious complexity of the caliphate in mind, not because it was explained but because it was lived through.

The other crucial difference is that in the Belgariad, the Gods are real but absent. In The House of Paper, God is real and present -- the adhan rings five times daily, and faith shapes every contract, every meal, every greeting. The challenge is not to prove the divine exists (as Eddings must) but to show how the same divine reality is experienced, interpreted, and politicised differently by different communities. This is harder, but it is also richer, and the journey structure -- which Eddings demonstrates so effectively -- is exactly the right mechanism for delivering it.
