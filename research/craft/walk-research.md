The year is 783 CE, and the Abbasid Caliphate, under Al-Mahdi, is thriving \[1-3\]. Baghdad, officially named \*Madinat al-Salam\* (City of Peace) \[4, 5\], is enjoying a golden age of stability and growing prosperity, having been founded just two decades earlier \[6, 7\].

\*\*\*

The morning air inside the walls of Caliph Mansur's \*\*Round City\*\* is still relatively quiet, carrying the atmosphere of imperial authority \[8\]. This architectural marvel, conceived and executed as a single, circular work \[3, 4, 9\], is the heart of the \*Dar al Islam\*, or Muslim world \[10\].

As the person walks toward the center, they pass the massive structure of the \*\*Golden Gate Palace\*\*, the caliph's residence, which dominates the central enclosure \[8\]. Above the main audience chamber, the Palace's most striking feature is its \*\*120-foot-high green dome\*\*, surmounted by the figure of a horseman with a lance, visible for miles around \[11\]. Sharing this central space is the prodigious \*\*Great Mosque\*\*, the first mosque built in Baghdad, emphatically conveying the message of Abbasid power \[8, 12, 13\]. This central precinct is strictly a \*\*royal preserve\*\*; no one, not even royal family members, is permitted to ride here \[8\]. The limited space is reserved for the palace of the caliph's children, barracks for the horse-guard, kitchens, offices for the treasury, armoury, and chancery, emphasizing the political and spiritual union at the city's core \[8\].

To leave the royal city and reach the commercial district, the traveler approaches one of the four equidistant gates, perhaps the Basra Gate or the Kufa Gate, which both lead to the canals southward \[9\]. The roads running from the center toward these outer gates may be lined with vaulted arcades \[8\]. However, the bustling commercial activity, originally part of the city plan, was ordered moved out of the Round City walls to the \*\*Karkh suburb\*\* years ago by Caliph Mansur, following the advice of an envoy who considered the bazaars within the royal residence to be enemies of the state \[14, 15\].

\*\*\*

Stepping outside the circular walls, the traveler heads south along the road towards Karkh, which is swiftly becoming the \*\*commercial hub of the most prosperous city on earth\*\* \[6, 14\]. The district, located beyond the Sarat Canal \[16, 17\], is recognized as the place where thousands have poured in to speculate and make their fortunes in a massive property boom \[6\].

The sights and sounds of this growing suburb are immediately apparent, a stark contrast to the formality of the inner city:

\* \*\*Sights:\*\* The road is lined with markets and quarters that are \*\*organized strictly by profession or merchandise\*\*, a tradition still alive in the Arab world \[17, 18\]. The air grows thick with the varied sights of global commerce: silks, gold, jewelry, precious stones, exotic fruit, exquisite carpets, and long-suffering \*\*camels\*\* laden with wares \[6\].

\* \*\*Sounds:\*\* The air is filled with the cacophony of \*\*merchants, traders, and artisans\*\* \[19\]. The bustling activity is particularly concentrated near the Dajaj Canal, a waterway that provides unloading points \[17\]. You might hear the clamor from the quarter of the \*\*coppersmiths\*\* \[17\], or the bustling activity around the quarters of the \*\*poulterers\*\* (fowl merchants) and the \*\*cooked-meat sellers\*\* \[17\].

\* \*\*Experiences:\*\* This immense concentration of trade means that \*\*Karkh is teeming\*\* \[6\]. The massive influx of people drawn by the boom ensures that the streets and markets are continuously crowded \[6, 20\]. This is the commercial center of a city whose economy yields staggering revenues to the Abbasid treasury \[6\]. Karkh is so successful it is fast becoming the largest and most splendid quarter of Baghdad \[21\].

Based on the nature of Baghdad\'s urban development in 783 CE, and the specialization of the Karkh neighborhood, a paper maker (or the site of early paper manufacture) would be situated in a district dedicated to craft or trade, likely near a crucial water source, even though paper was a relatively new industry.

\### Location of the Paper Maker in Karkh

In the Abbasid capital, the commercial life of the west bank was concentrated in the district of \*\*Karkh\*\*, which was rapidly expanding outside the circular walls of the Round City \[1, 2\]. Karkh was meticulously organized \[3, 4\]: streets, markets, and quarters were established \*\*strictly by profession or merchandise\*\* \[2, 4\]. Since Baghdad was famous for trading in \*\*books and paper\*\* \[5\], and given the nascent status of local paper production (the first dedicated paper factory in Baghdad was established slightly later, around 794--795 CE \[6\]), a paper maker\'s workshop would be segregated in a specific area, likely near resources vital for the craft.

The most likely location within Karkh would be in a quarter situated near one of the major waterways, such as the \*\*Dajaj (Fowls) Canal\*\* \[2\]. This canal and its branches served as a major commercial artery, providing \*\*wharves and unloading points\*\* \[2\].

Other industries that required water access or were segregated for practical/hygienic reasons, such as \*\*coppersmiths, soap-makers, and cooked-meat sellers\*\*, congregated near the Dajaj Canal \[2\]. Since paper making relies heavily on water for processing textile rags into pulp \[7, 8\], positioning the paper maker near this canal would be essential for both production and shipping materials.

\### Vicinity of the Workshop in Karkh

The paper maker\'s vicinity in Karkh would be one of \*\*intense commercial bustle\*\* and noise \[4, 5, 9\]. Karkh was the commercial hub of the most prosperous city on earth, teeming with massive crowds drawn by the property and trade boom \[1, 9\].

\*\*Sights and Sounds:\*\*

\* \*\*Orderly Chaos:\*\* Despite the throngs, the area maintained an air of order, as men of one business were not mixed with those of another \[3, 4\]. Therefore, the paper maker\'s immediate neighbors would likely be other specialized artisans or merchants dealing in related goods (like booksellers, if they were located near the production site, although the main book-selling quarter was often cited in Sharkiya \[10\]).

\* \*\*Canal Activity:\*\* If near the \*\*Dajaj Canal\*\*, the air would be filled with the sounds of \*\*shouting merchants and swearing boatmen\*\* \[11\], along with the clamor from nearby workshops, such as the \*\*coppersmiths\*\* \[2\]. The wharves would be busy with the unloading of goods supplied via the Euphrates-Tigris canal network \[11, 12\].

\* \*\*Materials and Traffic:\*\* The streets would be packed with long-suffering \*\*camels\*\* laden with wares \[5\] and foot traffic. The paper maker would regularly receive large bales of \*\*rags/textiles\*\* \[7\]---the raw material for paper---which were abundant given Baghdad\'s major textile industry that produced silks, cotton, and wool goods \[5, 13, 14\]. Depending on the specific location within the wide Karkh suburb, the vicinity might also host the \*\*camel stables\*\* (located west of the slave market area) \[5\].

\### Detailed Description of the Workshop

The paper workshop itself would reflect the industrial needs of the new technology while adhering to the typical architecture of the period:

1\. \*\*Construction and Layout:\*\* The building would likely be made of \*\*sun-dried brick\*\* \[15, 16\] (the main material for the city), possibly reinforced with kiln-baked brick for crucial structural elements like arches \[17\]. It would be designed in the traditional style of a West Bank building, likely being \*\*single-storey\*\* and surrounding a central \*\*courtyard\*\* \[18\], which provided essential light, fresh air, and space for activities \[19\].

2\. \*\*Water Access:\*\* Direct or easy access to the Dajaj Canal \[2\] would be mandatory, perhaps via a private or shared side street, or a dedicated wharf area \[2\]. This water was necessary for soaking the rags and grinding them to pulp \[7, 8\].

3\. \*\*The Manufacturing Space:\*\* Inside, specialized areas would be needed:

\* A wet area for \*\*soaking and fermentation\*\* of the textile rags.

\* A milling area where the fibers were pounded into pulp. This process would be continuous and noisy, generating the characteristic sounds of the craft, potentially audible over the general city din.

\* A clean, level surface where the wet pulp sheets could be laid out to dry. The courtyard and possibly the \*\*flat roof\*\* of the structure (a common feature used for sleeping during hot summer nights \[18\]) would serve as ideal drying spaces, exposed to the intense Mesopotamian sun.

4\. \*\*Storage:\*\* There would be significant storage capacity for raw materials (rags/textiles supplied from Karkh\'s industrial sector) \[7, 13, 14\] and for the final product---the sheets of paper---which contributed to the subsequent explosion of literature and bureaucracy in the Abbasid age \[7, 20, 21\].
